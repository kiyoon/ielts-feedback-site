# Topic: Society and Culture

## Sub-topics commonly seen on the IELTS exam

- Multiculturalism and cultural diversity in modern societies.
- Preservation of traditional values, languages and customs.
- The generation gap between young and old.
- Gender roles and gender equality at work and at home.
- Immigration: economic and cultural effects on host countries.
- Cultural homogenisation under globalisation.
- The role of religion in modern society.
- National identity and patriotism.
- Equality between rich and poor; social mobility.
- Whether modern societies are more, or less, cohesive than in the past.

## Sample real exam prompts

1. *Multicultural societies, in which there is a mixture of different ethnic groups, bring more benefits than drawbacks to a country. To what extent do you agree or disagree?*
2. *Some people believe that traditional customs and values are being lost as a result of modernisation. Discuss both views and give your own opinion.*
3. *In many cultures, older people are increasingly viewed as no longer useful. Why is this happening, and what can be done to address it?*
4. *Some people argue that men and women have different qualities, so certain jobs are more suited to one sex than the other. To what extent do you agree?*
5. *Some people believe that immigrants should fully adopt the culture of their new country, while others argue they should be allowed to preserve their own. Discuss both views.*
6. *Globalisation is leading to greater cultural homogeneity. Is this a positive or negative development?*
7. *Although many societies have become more diverse, some people argue this has made communities less cohesive. Do you agree or disagree?*
8. *Some people argue that gender equality has now been achieved, while others believe much more remains to be done. Discuss both views.*

## Core arguments FOR (e.g. multiculturalism, gender equality, immigration, social change)

| # | Argument | Supporting reason | Concrete example | Useful phrase |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Diverse societies are more economically dynamic | Different perspectives drive innovation and entrepreneurship | Studies repeatedly find immigrant-founded firms over-represented in start-up sectors in the US and UK | "harness the dividends of demographic diversity" |
| 2 | Cultural exchange enriches everyday life | Cuisine, music, language and the arts gain new ingredients | British cuisine and high streets have been transformed by post-war migration | "broaden the cultural repertoire of host societies" |
| 3 | Gender equality is morally and economically necessary | Excluding half the population from full participation is wasteful | Several World Bank reports estimate large GDP gains from closing the gender employment gap | "unlock latent productive capacity" |
| 4 | Migrants typically pay more in taxes than they receive in services | Younger, working-age migrants help fund pensions for the host society | OECD analyses confirm a generally positive net fiscal contribution from working migrants | "deliver a positive fiscal contribution" |
| 5 | Traditions evolve through contact rather than dying from it | Living cultures absorb and adapt foreign influences | The "tea ceremony" itself originated in cross-cultural exchange centuries ago | "evolve through reciprocal cultural contact" |
| 6 | Younger generations bring necessary modernisation | They inherit, but also update, social norms | Climate awareness and gender consciousness have shifted markedly between generations | "drive overdue normative reform" |
| 7 | Inclusive societies are more politically stable | Citizens who feel recognised support institutions | Comparative research links inclusive citizenship to lower civil unrest | "underpin long-term political stability" |
| 8 | Religious pluralism is consistent with civic peace | Plural societies have a long track record where rule of law holds | Cities such as Toronto and Singapore are routinely cited | "sustain peaceful coexistence under shared civic norms" |
| 9 | Equality of opportunity is the surest way to social mobility | Talent is randomly distributed; opportunity is not | Several Nordic countries combine equality with high mobility | "broaden access to upward mobility" |
| 10 | Multilingualism is a cognitive and economic asset | Bilingual children show measurable cognitive flexibility | Studies link bilingualism to delayed onset of certain cognitive declines | "treat multilingualism as an asset rather than a deficit" |

## Core arguments AGAINST / counter (e.g. concerns about integration, traditional values eroding)

| # | Argument | Supporting reason | Concrete example | Useful phrase |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Rapid demographic change can outpace integration capacity | Schools, housing and language services can be overwhelmed | Several European cities have struggled with sudden surges in arrivals | "outstrip the integration capacity of receiving institutions" |
| 2 | Cultural homogenisation under global media erodes local identity | Global brands and English content dominate | Many local-language film industries have lost ground to Hollywood streaming | "subjugate local cultures to a globalised commercial monoculture" |
| 3 | Traditional values offer continuity and cohesion that fully relativist societies lack | Shared rituals and norms aid social trust | Communitarian critiques of liberal individualism articulate this view | "preserve the connective tissue of shared meaning" |
| 4 | Some traditional gender norms reflect, rather than cause, deeper structures | Simply legislating equality may not change household realities | Workforce participation has risen faster than equal-housework statistics in many countries | "equality on paper has not always translated into equality at home" |
| 5 | Older generations preserve practical knowledge and craft skills | Disregarding the elderly loses transmissible expertise | Many traditional crafts in East Asia are now endangered as elders pass without successors | "lose intergenerational transmission of skill" |
| 6 | Identity politics can fragment a shared civic story | Hyphenated identities may compete with national ones | Several democracies have grown polarised along cultural lines | "fragment the shared civic narrative" |
| 7 | Immigrants concentrated in particular neighbourhoods may form parallel communities | Without contact, segregation can entrench | Several European housing markets show this pattern | "give rise to de-facto residential segregation" |
| 8 | Equating "modern" with "better" devalues legitimate continuities | Not every old practice is oppressive | Family meals, religious festivals and local dialects are mostly benign | "modernity is not synonymous with moral progress" |
| 9 | Some global cultural exports embed values not universally welcomed | Hyper-individualism, consumerism, sexualised media | Several conservative societies have pushed back against streaming content | "import value systems that may sit uneasily with host cultures" |
| 10 | Communities need shared language for everyday cohesion | Without a common tongue, civic life is harder | Most successful integration policies emphasise host-language acquisition | "common language as a precondition for civic participation" |

## Concession patterns (band-7 nuance)

1. *While multicultural societies bring undeniable cultural and economic benefits, the integration challenges they pose should not be dismissed as merely the preserve of bigots.*
2. *Granted that some traditional practices are oppressive, others sustain forms of belonging that pure individualism cannot replicate.*
3. *It is true that gender equality has advanced significantly; this advance, however, has been uneven across sectors and countries.*
4. *Critics rightly note that rapid immigration can strain housing and schools — a logistical concern, not in itself an argument against immigration.*
5. *Although global media has standardised much popular culture, local artistic traditions show a remarkable capacity for adaptation rather than extinction.*
6. *Even those most committed to social mobility tend to concede that talent and family connections still play an outsized role in life chances.*
7. *To be sure, identity-based politics can fragment a shared civic story, but ignoring identity altogether tends to entrench, rather than resolve, underlying grievances.*

## Ready-to-adapt thesis statements

1. **(Opinion)** *I largely agree that multicultural societies bring more benefits than drawbacks, although realising those benefits depends crucially on the quality of integration policy.*
2. **(Discuss both views)** *Although both views have merit, I would argue that the long-term benefits of cultural diversity outweigh the short-term frictions of integration, provided host institutions invest accordingly.*
3. **(Advantages / disadvantages)** *While the rapid pace of cultural change carries real costs for some communities, on balance these are outweighed by the social and economic openings that diversity creates.*
4. **(Problem / solution)** *Generational and cultural friction in modern societies is driven by uneven exposure to change; addressing it requires deliberate intergenerational and intercultural dialogue rather than legislative fixes alone.*
5. **(Two-part question)** *This essay will first examine why traditional values appear to be in decline and then consider whether — and how — they can be meaningfully preserved in a globalised age.*

## Sample fully-developed body paragraph #1 (band 7+)

> *(Topic sentence)* The strongest case for embracing cultural diversity rests not on sentiment but on the simple observation that diverse societies tend to be more economically dynamic. *(Explanation)* Where people of different backgrounds are brought together, they bring different professional networks, different consumer preferences, and different problem-solving habits, all of which feed into a more inventive economy. New businesses often emerge precisely at the seams between communities, where one group's skills meet another's unmet need. *(Concrete example)* Studies of start-up activity in cities such as London, Toronto and Sydney consistently find that immigrant-founded firms account for a disproportionate share of new ventures, despite immigrants themselves comprising a smaller share of the population. *(Mini-conclusion)* Far from being a burden, then, cultural diversity is one of the more reliable drivers of the economic dynamism that all modern societies claim to want.

## Sample fully-developed body paragraph #2

> *(Topic sentence)* Equally, however, it would be naïve to pretend that cultural diversity arrives without integration costs that must be honestly addressed. *(Explanation)* When demographic change outpaces the capacity of schools, housing and language services, the result is not the harmonious mosaic of policy speeches but rather pockets of disadvantage where the second generation can struggle to enter the mainstream. Public goodwill, on which integration ultimately depends, is itself eroded if these gaps are denied. *(Concrete example)* Several European cities that experienced rapid migration during the 2010s now run dedicated language and labour-market programmes precisely because earlier laissez-faire approaches left segregated communities behind. *(Mini-conclusion)* Recognising integration as a project that requires investment, rather than as something that happens by itself, is therefore not a concession to anti-immigrant sentiment but the very condition under which a diverse society can deliver on its promise.

## Common pitfalls / clichés to avoid

- "We are all human beings." — Empty platitude.
- "Variety is the spice of life." — Cliché.
- "East and West cannot meet." — Reductive and outdated.
- Treating *culture* and *country* as synonyms.
- Sweeping claims about "Asian values" or "Western individualism".
- "Old people are wise; young people are energetic." — Stereotyped.
- Using "the elderly" as if they were a single homogeneous group.
- Romanticising tradition without acknowledging its costs (e.g. gender roles).
- Confusing *immigration* (into) and *emigration* (out of).
- Phrases like "men are from Mars" — register-mismatched.
