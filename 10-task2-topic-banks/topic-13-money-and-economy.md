# Topic: Money and Economy

## Sub-topics commonly seen on the IELTS exam

- Consumerism and materialism in modern society.
- Saving vs spending; financial literacy.
- Inequality of income and wealth.
- Taxation and redistribution.
- Foreign aid and international development.
- Advertising's effect on consumer behaviour.
- Whether money brings happiness.
- The pursuit of economic growth at any cost.
- The cashless society and digital payments.
- The role of banks and financial regulation.

## Sample real exam prompts

1. *Some people believe that consumerism has had a positive effect on society, while others argue that it has been damaging. Discuss both views and give your own opinion.*
2. *Some people believe that the gap between rich and poor is widening, and the government should step in to address it. To what extent do you agree?*
3. *Some people believe that wealthy countries have a moral obligation to help poorer countries through foreign aid, while others believe each country should focus on its own people. Discuss both views.*
4. *Some people argue that money is the most important factor in achieving happiness, while others believe it is far less important. Discuss both views.*
5. *Some people believe that the government should provide free housing for poor people, while others believe individuals should be responsible for their own accommodation. Discuss both views.*
6. *Some people argue that high salaries should be limited by law. To what extent do you agree?*
7. *In many societies, people are encouraged to spend more rather than save for the future. Is this a positive or negative development?*
8. *Some people believe that economic growth is the only way to end poverty, while others argue that economic growth is harming the environment. Discuss both views.*

## Core arguments FOR (e.g. for redistribution, financial regulation, foreign aid, sustainable consumption)

| # | Argument | Supporting reason | Concrete example | Useful phrase |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Progressive taxation reduces inequality without crippling growth | Higher earners absorb higher rates more easily | Several Nordic states combine high taxes with sustained growth | "couple progressive taxation with sustained growth" |
| 2 | Foreign aid serves both moral and strategic interests | Stable poorer countries are better trading partners | UK and Scandinavian aid programmes are routinely cited | "advance both ethical and strategic objectives" |
| 3 | Inequality damages social cohesion and growth | Highly unequal societies have lower trust and mobility | OECD analyses link inequality to growth drag | "erode the social fabric on which markets depend" |
| 4 | Financial literacy reduces household-level vulnerability | People who understand interest and risk borrow less unwisely | Several school curricula now embed personal finance | "reduce household-level financial vulnerability" |
| 5 | Government can correct asymmetric information in financial markets | Consumers cannot price complex products themselves | The 2008 crisis and subsequent reforms illustrate this | "address informational asymmetries in financial markets" |
| 6 | Limits on advertising protect children from exploitation | Children cannot evaluate persuasion | Several countries restrict advertising to children | "protect those least able to evaluate commercial claims" |
| 7 | Cashless payments reduce certain crimes and improve tax collection | Cash supports tax evasion and theft | Sweden's near-cashless economy is widely studied | "narrow opportunities for tax evasion" |
| 8 | Sustainable consumption reduces environmental damage | Producing less means polluting less | Several "right-to-repair" movements articulate this view | "decouple welfare from rising material throughput" |
| 9 | Universal basic services lift the floor for all | Free schools, clinics, transport guarantee a baseline | The British NHS and universal-public-school systems are textbook cases | "ensure a universal floor of basic provision" |
| 10 | Long-term thinking is needed against short-term market pressures | Pension funds and infrastructure require horizons markets resist | Several states use sovereign wealth funds to manage this | "internalise long-term horizons that markets neglect" |

## Core arguments AGAINST / counter (e.g. against high taxes, against foreign aid, in defence of consumerism)

| # | Argument | Supporting reason | Concrete example | Useful phrase |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Heavy taxation can blunt entrepreneurial incentives | Capital and talent are mobile | Several high-tax economies have lost senior professionals abroad | "blunt the incentives that drive enterprise" |
| 2 | Foreign aid can entrench dependency rather than build capacity | Recurring grants displace local enterprise | Critics across the political spectrum raise this | "produce dependency rather than self-sustaining capacity" |
| 3 | Consumerism funds the innovation that ultimately benefits the poor | Mass markets allow prices to fall over time | Smartphones, once luxury items, are now near-universal | "extend access to once-luxury goods through scale" |
| 4 | Inequality may reflect differences in productive contribution | Equal incomes regardless of effort can disincentivise productivity | Comparative-economic research raises this concern | "reflect differential productive contribution" |
| 5 | Government redistribution can create welfare traps | Benefits withdrawn as earnings rise produce high effective marginal rates | Several welfare reforms target this issue | "create high effective marginal tax rates on low earners" |
| 6 | Excessive financial regulation can starve small business of credit | Banks become risk-averse | Post-2008 regulation has tightened SME lending | "constrain the supply of credit to small enterprises" |
| 7 | Advertising legitimately informs consumer choice | It is not, by default, manipulation | The case for free commercial speech rests partly on this | "perform an informational role in market exchange" |
| 8 | Cashless economies exclude the unbanked | The poorest can be locked out of payments | Several central banks have raised this concern | "exclude unbanked populations from the formal economy" |
| 9 | Limiting high salaries by law is unenforceable in mobile labour markets | Talent migrates | Critics of EU bonus caps cite this | "prove unenforceable in internationally mobile sectors" |
| 10 | Saving rather than spending is not always a virtue | Excessive saving can deepen recessions | The "paradox of thrift" articulates this | "deepen, rather than mitigate, downturns" |

## Concession patterns (band-7 nuance)

1. *While consumerism has driven innovation and choice, equating it with the good life conflates two questions that ought to be kept separate.*
2. *Granted that foreign aid has produced some failures, judging an entire policy by its worst examples gives a misleading picture.*
3. *It is true that high taxation can dampen incentives; this is an argument for thoughtful design, not for abandoning redistribution altogether.*
4. *Critics rightly note that welfare programmes can produce dependency; equally, the absence of such programmes typically produces visible destitution.*
5. *Even those who defend high executive pay tend to concede that the link between pay and productivity has weakened in some sectors.*
6. *To be sure, money enables much of what we value, but the empirical evidence linking it to happiness flattens above modest thresholds.*
7. *Although saving has obvious household benefits, an entire economy in which everyone saves at once tends to contract rather than thrive.*

## Ready-to-adapt thesis statements

1. **(Opinion)** *I largely agree that wealthy countries have a continuing obligation to provide foreign aid, although the form and accountability of that aid matter as much as its volume.*
2. **(Discuss both views)** *Although both views have merit, in my view the costs of inequality — to social cohesion, to mobility and to long-term growth — justify active redistribution within sensible limits.*
3. **(Advantages / disadvantages)** *While consumer culture brings genuine economic dynamism and choice, its disadvantages — environmental, psychological and financial — on balance call for a more measured relationship with consumption.*
4. **(Problem / solution)** *Rising household debt reflects a confluence of stagnant real wages, easy credit and aggressive marketing; addressing it requires action across financial regulation, education and labour-market policy.*
5. **(Two-part question)** *This essay will first examine the main drivers of widening inequality before considering what role government might most effectively play in addressing it.*

## Sample fully-developed body paragraph #1 (band 7+)

> *(Topic sentence)* The strongest case for foreign aid rests not on charitable sentiment but on a more sober recognition that prosperous neighbours are better neighbours. *(Explanation)* Countries trapped in extreme poverty tend to suffer political instability, mass migration and poor public-health surveillance — and these are not problems that politely respect borders. Aid that builds basic health, education and governance capacity in poorer countries reduces the spread of communicable disease, expands future trading partners, and lessens the pressure for distress migration. *(Concrete example)* Long-running development programmes funded by Britain, the Nordic countries and the World Bank have, on independent evaluation, produced measurable gains in immunisation rates, child mortality and primary-school enrolment in their target countries. *(Mini-conclusion)* Treating aid as either pure altruism or wasted money therefore misses the point: properly designed, it is part of the basic infrastructure of a connected world.

## Sample fully-developed body paragraph #2

> *(Topic sentence)* Critics of consumer culture are nevertheless right to point out that the equation of more spending with more happiness has worn dangerously thin. *(Explanation)* Above a modest income threshold, the empirical link between additional purchases and additional life satisfaction is repeatedly shown to flatten — and yet contemporary marketing, social media and credit availability all push relentlessly in the opposite direction. The result is rising household debt and a quiet sense of dissatisfaction even among objectively prosperous populations. *(Concrete example)* Long-running surveys in advanced economies have tracked GDP and self-reported wellbeing in parallel, and the divergence between the two over the last several decades is now a familiar finding in happiness economics. *(Mini-conclusion)* This does not mean rejecting the market economy — far from it — but it does mean that public policy, education and individual reflection should all be oriented around a more honest account of what additional consumption actually buys.

## Common pitfalls / clichés to avoid

- "Money is the root of all evil." — Cliché and a misquote.
- "Money cannot buy happiness." — Cliché; develop or replace.
- "Time is money." — Tired.
- Treating *poor people* as a homogeneous category.
- Using *cheap* (low-priced) and *cheap* (poor-quality) without thought.
- "All rich people are greedy." — Sweeping.
- Citing fabricated income statistics.
- Confusing *economic* (relating to the economy) with *economical* (thrifty).
- "The rich get richer; the poor get poorer." — Cliché.
- "We must close the gap." — Vague.
