# Topic: Crime and Justice

## Sub-topics commonly seen on the IELTS exam

- Punishment vs rehabilitation as the goal of the justice system.
- Deterrence: do harsh sentences actually reduce crime?
- Youth crime and how to address it.
- White-collar / corporate crime.
- Capital punishment.
- Prison reform and recidivism.
- Cybercrime and new forms of offending.
- Gun control and firearms regulation.
- The role of police in modern society.
- Whether criminal records should follow people for life.

## Sample real exam prompts

1. *Some people believe that the best way to reduce crime is to give longer prison sentences, while others think there are better alternatives. Discuss both views and give your own opinion.*
2. *In some countries, the number of crimes committed by teenagers is rising. What are the causes, and what can be done to address this issue?*
3. *Some people believe that the police should carry guns, while others argue that they should not. Discuss both views.*
4. *Many people argue that the death penalty should be abolished, while others think it is necessary for the most serious crimes. Discuss both views.*
5. *White-collar crimes, such as fraud, are usually punished less severely than violent crimes. To what extent do you think this is fair?*
6. *Some people argue that prison is the best way to deal with criminals, while others believe that education and rehabilitation are more effective. Discuss both views and give your own opinion.*
7. *In some countries, people are committing more crimes online. What are the causes, and what can be done?*
8. *Some people believe that having a criminal record should not affect a person's career prospects after they have served their sentence. To what extent do you agree?*

## Core arguments FOR (e.g. rehabilitation, restorative justice, alternatives to prison, addressing root causes)

| # | Argument | Supporting reason | Concrete example | Useful phrase |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Rehabilitation reduces re-offending more reliably than long sentences | Education and therapy address what prison does not | Norway's prison system reportedly delivers low recidivism | "address the underlying drivers of offending" |
| 2 | Treating root causes — poverty, addiction, abuse — is more cost-effective | Each prevented offence saves enormous downstream costs | Comparative criminology research consistently supports this | "tackle the structural drivers of crime" |
| 3 | Youth offenders respond especially well to rehabilitation | Adolescent brains are still developing | Several jurisdictions divert young offenders from adult systems | "intervene at a developmentally crucial stage" |
| 4 | Restorative justice produces higher victim satisfaction | Victims gain answers and acknowledgement | Evaluations across several jurisdictions report this | "deliver outcomes that better satisfy victims" |
| 5 | Community sentences keep offenders in work and family | Maintaining ties reduces re-offending | Probation systems in several countries are built on this insight | "preserve the social ties that aid desistance" |
| 6 | Long prison sentences can entrench criminality | Prisons can function as crime universities | Several official inspectorates have raised this concern | "produce institutionalised, more capable offenders" |
| 7 | Decriminalising minor drug use shifts addiction to public health | Treatment beats imprisonment | Portugal's reform is the canonical case | "reframe addiction as a public-health rather than criminal issue" |
| 8 | Investment in policing legitimacy reduces crime more than harsh penalties | Communities cooperate when they trust the police | Research at major universities backs this | "build the legitimacy that elicits voluntary compliance" |
| 9 | White-collar crime should be punished proportionate to harm | Fraud and tax evasion harm thousands at scale | Reformers point to systematic under-punishment | "calibrate punishment to actual social harm" |
| 10 | Cybercrime requires international cooperation rather than tougher domestic laws alone | Offenders operate across jurisdictions | Joint operations through Interpol illustrate the model | "respond to cross-border offending through international cooperation" |

## Core arguments AGAINST / counter (e.g. for harsher punishment, deterrence, public safety, victim rights)

| # | Argument | Supporting reason | Concrete example | Useful phrase |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Severe sentences deter would-be offenders | Rational calculation responds to expected cost | Several jurisdictions credit harsh drug-trafficking laws with low offence rates | "raise the expected cost of offending" |
| 2 | Custody removes dangerous individuals from the public | Some offenders pose ongoing risk | Repeat-violence cases illustrate the necessity | "incapacitate persistently dangerous offenders" |
| 3 | Punishment expresses moral condemnation society needs | Without it, victims feel ignored | Sentencing rituals serve a symbolic public function | "express the community's collective censure" |
| 4 | Soft sentences for white-collar crime reinforce inequality | The wealthy face less severe consequences | Critics across the political spectrum raise this | "compound rather than correct existing inequality" |
| 5 | Rehabilitation programmes have mixed evidence | Some studies report modest effects | Meta-analyses caution against over-claiming | "deliver disappointing returns in some programmes" |
| 6 | Hard-core repeat offenders are unlikely to be rehabilitated | A small minority commits a large share of crime | Comparative criminology often identifies this group | "concentrate offending in a small unrehabilitable group" |
| 7 | Capital punishment, where used, expresses absolute condemnation | Some crimes are seen as warranting it | Several democracies retain it for the most serious crimes | "reserve ultimate sanction for the gravest offences" |
| 8 | Victims have a legitimate interest in retribution | Justice is not only forward-looking | Restorative-justice critics make this case | "respect the legitimate retributive interests of victims" |
| 9 | Police forces need adequate resources, including in some cases firearms | Some threats cannot be addressed unarmed | Specialist armed units exist in most democracies | "ensure that police capability matches operational threat" |
| 10 | Criminal records serve a legitimate signalling function | Employers and the public have a legitimate interest | Reformers and businesses negotiate the appropriate scope | "balance rehabilitation with legitimate public-interest disclosure" |

## Concession patterns (band-7 nuance)

1. *While longer prison sentences satisfy a public desire for retribution, the empirical evidence that they reduce future offending is much thinner than is often assumed.*
2. *Granted that some violent offenders pose ongoing risk, this small group cannot be the model for an entire criminal-justice policy.*
3. *It is true that rehabilitation programmes have produced mixed evaluations; this does not mean they should be replaced with policies whose evidence is even weaker.*
4. *Critics rightly worry about leniency towards repeat offenders; equally, treating first-time offenders as if they were repeat offenders helps no-one.*
5. *Even those who oppose capital punishment in principle tend to accept that some offences merit the most severe sanctions a justice system can impose.*
6. *To be sure, victims have a legitimate retributive interest, but a justice system focused only on retribution will under-serve those very victims in the long run.*
7. *Although the police clearly need adequate resources, the relationship between firearm prevalence and effective policing is not as simple as advocates on either side suggest.*

## Ready-to-adapt thesis statements

1. **(Opinion)** *I largely agree that rehabilitation, rather than incarceration, should be the primary aim of the justice system, since the long-term reduction in re-offending it produces is in the public interest as much as in the offender's.*
2. **(Discuss both views)** *Although both punishment and rehabilitation have legitimate places, in my view the most effective response combines firm sanction with serious investment in rehabilitative programming.*
3. **(Advantages / disadvantages)** *While longer prison sentences offer the apparent advantage of incapacitation, the disadvantages — high cost, weak deterrent effect and high recidivism — on balance call for a more nuanced approach.*
4. **(Problem / solution)** *The rise in juvenile offending in recent years is driven by a confluence of family breakdown, weak schools and limited youth services; addressing it will require coordinated investment well beyond the criminal-justice system.*
5. **(Two-part question)** *This essay will first identify the principal causes of rising youth crime before examining which interventions are most likely to produce durable reductions.*

## Sample fully-developed body paragraph #1 (band 7+)

> *(Topic sentence)* The strongest argument against relying on long prison sentences as the principal tool of crime control is that the evidence for their deterrent effect is considerably weaker than political rhetoric implies. *(Explanation)* Most offending is impulsive or rooted in addiction and disadvantage rather than a calculated weighing of expected punishments; raising the maximum sentence from ten years to twenty does not, on the day of the offence, enter the offender's mind. What does seem to deter is the perceived likelihood of being caught, which depends on visible policing and reliable investigation rather than sentencing severity. *(Concrete example)* Comparative studies of jurisdictions that have lengthened sentences without changing detection rates have repeatedly failed to find substantial corresponding reductions in offending. *(Mini-conclusion)* This does not mean prison has no place; it does mean that treating sentence length as a primary lever for safer streets misallocates resources that would be better spent on detection and rehabilitation.

## Sample fully-developed body paragraph #2

> *(Topic sentence)* Equally, any honest case for rehabilitation must acknowledge that not every offender responds to it, and the public is right to expect protection from those who do not. *(Explanation)* A small minority of offenders commit a disproportionate share of serious crime, often persistently and despite repeated interventions. For this group, custodial sentences perform a function that rehabilitation alone cannot: they incapacitate, removing the immediate risk to victims while longer-term work continues. The challenge is to identify this group accurately rather than treating every defendant as if they belonged to it. *(Concrete example)* Most modern probation services use structured risk-assessment tools precisely to distinguish first-time offenders, who tend to respond well to community measures, from those whose history requires closer supervision. *(Mini-conclusion)* The objective, then, is neither pure rehabilitation nor pure punishment, but a calibrated system that matches each response to the specific risk and need of the individual before it.

## Common pitfalls / clichés to avoid

- "An eye for an eye." — Cliché and contested.
- "Crime doesn't pay." — Tired.
- "Lock them up and throw away the key." — Polemical, low band.
- Confusing *prison* (the building) with *imprisonment* (the experience).
- Using *judge* as a verb to mean *sentence*.
- "Criminals are bad people." — Reductive.
- "Education will solve all crime." — Overstated.
- Citing fabricated rates ("90 % of prisoners reoffend").
- Treating juvenile and adult offenders as equivalent.
- Confusing *the law* and *legislation*; *legal* and *legitimate*.
