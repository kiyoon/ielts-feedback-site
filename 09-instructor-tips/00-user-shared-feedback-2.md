# User-shared Feedback #2 (Task 2 — Education topic)

> Source: a second piece of personal IELTS feedback the repo owner received from a Korean instructor and found highly useful. Theme: Task 2 essay on education / universities / lectures / students. Same density target as `00-user-shared-feedback.md`. LLM agents marking essays should treat these as concrete band-6→7 upgrade patterns.

**Examiner's overall verdict (paraphrased):**
> 문제응답이 잘 전달되나, 제한적인 문법 사용, 특히 전반적으로 어휘 사용 선택이 많이 제한적이고 오류가 많음.
>
> *("Task response is delivered, but grammar is limited and — most importantly — vocabulary choices are very restricted overall and contain many errors.")*

This single comment IS the band-6 diagnosis. At band 6, ideas come through but lexical range is narrow and grammar is repetitive. The fixes below all attack that exact gap.

---

## 1. "anywhere and anytime" → drop entirely at band 7

| Don't (band 6) | Do (band 7) |
|---|---|
| anywhere and anytime | **regardless of its location and time restriction** |

**Why:** "anywhere and anytime" is a chatty colloquialism that examiners flag as informal. The replacement is more academic and demonstrates a complex prepositional phrase + nominalisation.

Variants:
- *irrespective of location or time*
- *without geographical or temporal constraints*
- *free from spatial and temporal limitations*

---

## 2. "many universities" → upgrade BOTH the quantifier AND the noun

| Don't | Do |
|---|---|
| many universities | **numerous / loads of¹ / a significant number of universities** |
| universities | **tertiary education systems / institutions of higher education / higher-education institutions** |

¹ "loads of" is informal — prefer *numerous* or *a significant number of* in academic writing. (The instructor listed it as a synonym to know, not necessarily to deploy.)

**Why:** Examiners specifically flag *many* as a "limited lexical resource" tell at band 6. Pair both upgrades together for compounding effect.

**Drop-in patterns:**
```
- A significant number of tertiary education institutions now offer ___
- Numerous higher-education institutions have shifted towards ___
- An increasing proportion of universities — particularly research-intensive ones — ___
```

---

## 3. "a bad idea" → academic register

| Don't | Do |
|---|---|
| a bad idea | **an ineffective idea / an inappropriate idea / a counterproductive approach / an ill-advised strategy** |

**Why:** *bad* is a band-5 word. The upgrades are evaluative + precise.

Calibrated alternatives:
| Severity | Phrase |
|---|---|
| Mildly negative | a less-than-ideal approach / a suboptimal strategy |
| Moderately negative | an ineffective / inappropriate idea |
| Strongly negative | a counterproductive / detrimental / ill-advised approach |
| Very strong | a damaging / pernicious policy |

---

## 4. "it helps" → "it contributes to" (with adverb)

`help` is a high-frequency verb that examiners want to see replaced. Use **`contribute to`** with a degree adverb.

**Pattern:** `[subject] contributes [adverb] to [outcome]`

**Examples from the feedback:**
> It **contributes considerably to** children's development.
>
> Lectures **contribute significantly to** students as they can receive immediate feedback.

| Common verb (band 6) | Upgrade (band 7) |
|---|---|
| help | contribute to / play a vital role in / facilitate / foster / underpin |
| help X to Y | enable X to Y / equip X to Y / empower X to Y |
| make X better | enhance X / improve X / bolster X |
| really help | contribute considerably/significantly/substantially to |

**Adverb bank for `contribute to`:** considerably, significantly, substantially, markedly, materially, decisively, meaningfully, modestly (hedged), marginally (hedged).

---

## 5. `staff` is already plural — never write "staffs"

| Don't | Do |
|---|---|
| many staffs | many **staff** / many **staff members** / numerous **personnel** |
| the staffs are | **the staff are** (BrE collective plural) / **the staff is** (AmE singular) |

**Why:** *Staff* is a non-count collective noun in BrE academic writing. *Staffs* only exists in the rare sense of "the staffs of multiple separate organisations."

Same trap nouns Korean/Chinese writers should know:
| Wrong | Right |
|---|---|
| informations | information |
| equipments | equipment |
| advices | advice / pieces of advice |
| furnitures | furniture |
| researches | research / research studies / pieces of research |
| feedbacks | feedback / pieces of feedback |
| knowledges | knowledge |
| homeworks | homework |

---

## 6. `amount` vs `number` — countability rule

| Don't | Do |
|---|---|
| the amount of students | **the majority of students / a large number of students / the proportion of students** |

**Rule:**
- **`amount of`** + uncountable noun (the amount of *information / water / time / money*)
- **`number of`** + countable plural (the number of *students / cars / cities*)
- For **people**, prefer: *the majority of, a significant proportion of, a substantial number of, the bulk of*.

Same trap with `much/many` and `less/fewer`:
| Wrong | Right |
|---|---|
| much students | many students |
| less students | fewer students |
| less mistakes | fewer mistakes |

---

## 7. `make` → `allow / enable` (causative upgrade)

`make` is the most overused verb at band 6. Upgrade based on the relationship:

| Common (band 6) | Upgrade (band 7) | When to use |
|---|---|---|
| It makes students learn | It **enables** students to learn | the cause provides capability |
| It makes them happy | It **allows** them to feel … / It **leaves** them feeling … | permitting an outcome |
| It makes the system better | It **improves / enhances / refines** the system | direct improvement |
| It makes people angry | It **provokes anger / triggers frustration** | emotional reaction |
| It makes us think | It **prompts us to consider / encourages us to reflect on** | mental action |

**Pattern:** `It allows / enables / empowers / equips / encourages [object] to [verb]`

```
- Online learning enables students to access materials at their own pace.
- Such policies empower citizens to participate in civic life.
- This approach equips graduates to compete in a globalised job market.
```

## 8. "getting feedback" → "receiving / gaining feedback"

| Don't | Do |
|---|---|
| getting feedback | **receiving feedback / gaining feedback / soliciting feedback / obtaining feedback** |
| getting a job | securing employment / obtaining a position |
| getting better | improving / progressing / advancing |
| getting older | ageing / advancing in years |
| getting | (almost always upgrade — *get* is band-5 territory) |

**General rule:** any sentence with `get / got / getting` should trigger a self-check at band 7. There is almost always a more precise verb.

Common `get` upgrades by context:

| Context | Upgrade |
|---|---|
| get information | obtain / acquire information |
| get a result | achieve / attain a result |
| get a degree | earn / obtain a degree |
| get money | earn / receive / generate revenue |
| get sick | fall ill |
| get to a place | reach / arrive at |
| get an idea | conceive of / develop an idea |
| get permission | secure / be granted permission |

---

## Top-level diagnosis to remember

The examiner's two-line summary is the canonical band-6 verdict. Internalise it:

1. **Lexical resource is the #1 ceiling.** You can have decent ideas and still be stuck at 6 if your word choices are repetitive (*help, make, get, many, big, important, bad, good, people*). Each of these has a 5-item upgrade bank above.
2. **Grammar is repetitive.** Even if grammar is "accurate," lacking variety (no relative clauses, no passive, no participle phrases, no conditionals) caps you at GRA 6.

So: every band-6 essay should be re-read once with **only** these two questions:
- *Have I used `help / make / get / many / good / bad / big / important` more than once each? Replace.*
- *Have I varied sentence structure (simple → complex with relative/participle/conditional/passive at least 4 different ways)?*
