# Anatomy of IELTS Writing Feedback

This file documents the typical *structure* of a piece of IELTS Writing feedback as delivered by examiners, teachers, automated graders, and correction services. The aim is to produce a structural blueprint an LLM can follow.

---

## 1. The canonical 7-block structure

Across all the sources studied, IELTS feedback consistently follows some subset of this order:

1. **Header / orientation line**
2. **Overall band score**
3. **Per-criterion scores + commentary** (4 blocks, equally weighted)
4. **Inline annotations on the essay** (corrections, codes, suggestions)
5. **Strengths summary**
6. **Weaknesses / common errors summary**
7. **Actionable next steps** (top 2–3 priorities)

Optional 8th block: **Model rewrite** of one paragraph, OR a link to a band-9 sample.

### Real-world examples of this structure

- **Writing9** (https://writing9.com) presents:
  - Overall band → 4 sub-criteria each with 3–5 micro-rubrics scored /9 → highlighted in-text errors → tips like "Support ideas with relevant, specific examples" and "Use synonyms" or "show examples".
- **IELTS Advantage's "Band 6 Student Answer with Examiner's Report"** (https://www.ieltsadvantage.com/2015/09/09/band-6-student-answer-with-examiners-report/) follows the order: overall band → TR comment → CC comment → LR comment → GR comment → "what to do" advice.
- **IELTS Luminary** (https://www.ieltsluminary.com/ielts-essay-correction-by-an-examiner) advertises "6–8 pages of detailed feedback" structured as: highlight strengths → identify weaknesses → offer actionable advice.
- **IELTS Podcast** (https://www.ieltspodcast.com/free-essay-check/) frames feedback by paragraph plus per-criterion summary, then "Practical advice you can use in your next essay right away".

## 2. Block-by-block: what to put where

### Block 1 — Header / orientation

Usually one short line. Typical openings:

- "Here is your feedback on Task 2."
- "Below you'll find a detailed analysis of your essay against the four IELTS criteria."
- "Overall, this is a competent answer that addresses the prompt."

### Block 2 — Overall band score

Always a single line, prominent. Variants:

- `Overall Band Score: 6.0`
- `Estimated band: 6.5`
- `Probable score: 6.5/7.0` (Writing9 / IELTS Podcast hedging style)

### Block 3 — Per-criterion scores + commentary

Four mini-sections, in this conventional order: **Task Response → Coherence & Cohesion → Lexical Resource → Grammatical Range & Accuracy** (Task 1 substitutes "Task Achievement" for TR).

Each mini-section typically has:

1. The score (e.g., `TR: 6`).
2. **One or two strength sentences**, often quoting language from the candidate's essay in inverted commas.
3. **One or two weakness sentences**, again quoting offending language verbatim.
4. (Sometimes) **one improvement directive** — "you need to…", "try to…", "consider…".

Length budget per criterion: about 50–120 words for a normal correction service; 200–400 words for premium examiner reports (IELTS Luminary).

Worked example structure (synthesised from the IELTS Advantage report):

> **Task Response — Band 6**
> You address all parts of the task and cover both arguments. However, the question asked you to discuss both views and you spend much more time saying why children were happy and not adults. To improve, balance the two sides more evenly and develop each main idea with at least two supporting sentences.

### Block 4 — Inline annotations on the essay

Two dominant conventions (see `error-correction-conventions.md`):

- **Code-based**: Sp, Gr, WW, WF, Coll, P, T, A, Prep, R, WO, Ref written in margin or above the offending word; the student fixes it.
- **Inline rewrite**: ~~strikethrough~~ over the wrong word, replacement in *italics* or [brackets].

Writing9 highlights errors directly in the rendered essay and on hover shows the type ("word repetition", "grammar mistake", "linking word issue").

### Block 5 — Strengths summary

Typically 3–5 bullet points or a single paragraph. Common opening phrases:

- "What you did well:"
- "Strengths of this response:"
- "Positive points first…"

Typical entries are concrete and quote the candidate's text:

- "The writer's position is relevant to task prompt, the main ideas are also relevant" (ielts-blog.com).
- "You logically organise information and ideas; there is clear progression throughout" (IELTS Advantage).
- "Strong collocations such as 'pursue a course of study' and 'launch a career'" (ielts470.rssing.com).

### Block 6 — Weaknesses / common errors summary

Mirror of strengths. Common opening phrases:

- "Areas to improve:"
- "What needs work:"
- "Weaknesses identified:"

Typical entries:

- "Not all the parts of the task were covered" (ielts-blog.com).
- "Not all of them are developed well enough" (ielts-blog.com).
- "There are some attempts to use more sophisticated words but many of them are inaccurate" (ielts-blog.com).
- "Some cohesion is faulty or mechanical" (ielts.idp.com — band 6 descriptor language).

### Block 7 — Actionable next steps

Almost always 2–3 prioritised actions. Verbs are imperative or "you need to / try to / consider":

- "Reduce the amount you write and focus on brainstorming, planning, organising and checking grammar." (ieltsbuddy.com)
- "Plan, check and make sure you answer all of the question." (Pauline Cullen / keytoielts.com)
- "Develop ALL of your main ideas to get Band 7." (ieltscharlie.com)
- "Use simple words and make no mistakes rather than use advanced words in error." (ieltsadvantage.com — the "100% rule")

### Block 8 (optional) — Model rewrite

Two patterns:

- **Sentence-level**: "Original: *individuals should contemplate the ramifications of their vehicular utilization* → Better: *people should consider the effects of their car use*" (babycode.org).
- **Paragraph-level rewrite**: shown below the original; or a separate "Band 9 sample response" PDF (offered by ielts-up, ieltsanswers, ieltsluminary).

## 3. Length norms

| Source type | Total length | Per criterion |
|-------------|--------------|---------------|
| Free AI grader (writing9) | 150–300 words + numeric rubric | One short paragraph |
| Free human correction (forums) | 200–500 words | 1 paragraph |
| Paid correction service (ieltsanswers, ted-ielts) | 600–1,500 words | 100–250 words |
| Premium examiner report (ieltsluminary, ielts-up) | 6–8 pages | 200–400 words |
| Cambridge / British Council official sample | 100–250 words | 1 dense paragraph |

---

## 4. Reading order conventions

- **Strengths first, then weaknesses** is almost universal — it sets a constructive tone.
- **Score before commentary** (the score frames the comments, not the other way around).
- **Specific quotation before generic advice** — examiners ground critique in the student's actual sentences before issuing a directive.
- **Most-impactful weakness flagged first** within each criterion.
- **Final takeaway is forward-looking**: "next essay", "to push to Band 7", "in your next attempt".
