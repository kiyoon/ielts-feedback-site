# Part 3 (abstract discussion) — structure, expected register, common follow-up patterns

_Crawled by claude on 2026-05-19T00:00:00Z. Sources cited inline._

## Summary

Part 3 is a 4–5 minute two-way discussion that follows the Part 2 long-turn, in which the examiner asks the candidate 4–6 increasingly abstract questions thematically tied to the Part 2 cue card. Because it forces candidates off personal-narrative autopilot and demands speculation, comparison, evaluation, hedging, and complex grammar, examiners and prep coaches across all major IELTS markets agree it is the single section where Band 6 and Band 7+ candidates most visibly diverge ([IELTSsure](https://www.ieltssure.com/ielts-speaking-part-3/), [SpeakPrac](https://speakprac.com/ielts-speaking-course/part-3/)). For an LLM judge, Part 3 turns are the highest-signal portion of the transcript for Lexical Resource (LR) and Grammatical Range & Accuracy (GRA), because the cognitive load surfaces vocabulary stretching, conditional/passive/hedging structures, and discourse-marker control that Part 1 small talk hides.

## Authoritative material

### Format (official)

- IELTS Speaking has three parts totalling 11–14 minutes. Part 3 is the final 4–5 minutes ([ielts.org Speaking format](https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking)).
- "In Part 3, you and the examiner discuss issues related to the topic in Part 2 in a more general and abstract way and, where appropriate, in greater depth." — official IELTS task description ([ielts.org](https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking)).
- The British Council Speaking Part 3 overview PDF confirms Part 3 "tests your ability to express and justify opinions and to analyse, discuss and speculate about issues" ([takeielts.britishcouncil.org PDF](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/speaking_part_3_overview_and_practice.pdf)).
- IDP IELTS describes Part 3 as a "two-way discussion" with abstract questions; no cue card is provided ([ielts.idp.com Part 3](https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-ielts-speaking-test-part-3)).

### Band descriptors that are most visible in Part 3

From the public IELTS Speaking band descriptors ([British Council PDF](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf), summarised by [IELTSTutors](https://ieltstutors.org/speaking-band-descriptors/) and [IELTS Advantage](https://www.ieltsadvantage.com/2015/06/28/ielts-speaking-criteria/)):

- **Fluency & Coherence — Band 7**: speaks at length without noticeable effort or loss of coherence; may demonstrate language-related hesitation at times, or some repetition and/or self-correction; uses a range of connectives and discourse markers with some flexibility.
- **Fluency & Coherence — Band 8**: speaks fluently with only occasional repetition or self-correction; hesitation is usually content-related and only rarely to search for language.
- **Lexical Resource — Band 7**: uses vocabulary resource flexibly to discuss a variety of topics; uses some less common and idiomatic vocabulary and shows some awareness of style and collocation, with some inappropriate choices; uses paraphrase effectively.
- **Grammatical Range & Accuracy — Band 7**: uses a range of complex structures with some flexibility; frequently produces error-free sentences, though some grammatical mistakes persist.

Part 3 is the part of the test that "most clearly separates candidates who score Band 6 from those who score Band 7 and above" ([IELTSsure](https://www.ieltssure.com/ielts-speaking-part-3/)).

### Canonical Part 3 question-type taxonomy

The most widely repeated taxonomy across prep schools enumerates 7 question types ([pteielts.com](https://www.pteielts.com/ielts-speaking-part-3-question-types/), [IELTS Advantage 7 common questions](https://www.ieltsadvantage.com/2015/04/03/ielts-speaking-part-3-common-questions/), [IELTS Luminary](https://www.ieltsluminary.com/speaking-part-3), [IELTSMaterial](https://ieltsmaterial.com/speaking/ielts-speaking-part-3/)):

1. **Opinion** — "What do you think about X?" / "Do you agree that…?"
2. **Evaluate** — react to someone else's stated position ("Some people say X — what's your view?").
3. **Future / Prediction** — "How do you think X will change in the next 20 years?" / "What types of vehicles will people use for transportation in the future?" ([pteielts.com](https://www.pteielts.com/ielts-speaking-part-3-question-types/)).
4. **Cause & Effect** — "Why has X become more common?" / "What effect does X have on Y?"
5. **Hypothetical** — counterfactual or imaginary ("What would happen if everyone worked from home?").
6. **Compare & Contrast** — "How is village life different from city life?" / "What is the difference between staying in a hotel and staying in an outdoor camp?" ([pteielts.com](https://www.pteielts.com/ielts-speaking-part-3-question-types/)).
7. **Past vs. Present** — "How has the way the media reports news changed in the last 20 years?" / "Do children of today spend their free time in the same way you used to?" ([pteielts.com](https://www.pteielts.com/ielts-speaking-part-3-question-types/)).

Each type maps to a high-value grammar/lexis target: prediction → `will / is likely to / I would expect`; hypothetical → second/third conditionals (`if … would have …`); past vs. present → present perfect, comparative + `used to`; cause & effect → `because of / due to / as a result / which has led to`; comparison → `whereas, while, by contrast` ([SpeakPrac Part 3 guide](https://speakprac.com/ielts-speaking-course/part-3/), [IELTS Mumbai discourse markers](https://ieltsmumbai.com/blog/ielts-speaking-advanced-discourse-markers-for-natural-speech-flow)).

### Hedging / register

Hedging is the structural lexical-grammar feature most reliably present in Band 7+ Part 3 answers. The Mumbai prep blog notes that in Speaking Part 3 hedging "helps you sound like a university student discussing real issues" and lists concrete phrases: *"It could be argued that…", "To some extent…", "On the whole…", "Generally speaking…", "I'd be inclined to say…"* ([IELTS Mumbai hedging](https://ieltsmumbai.com/blog/ielts-vocabulary--grammar-using-hedging-language-appropriately-in-ielts)). The register target is "academic but conversational, not formal" — overly formal essay-style language is repeatedly flagged as a Band 6 mistake ([Speechful AI tips](https://speechful.ai/blog/ielts-speaking-part-3-essential-tips), [Oxford Online English Band 7 walkthrough](https://www.oxfordonlineenglish.com/ielts-speaking-band-7)).

### Coherence devices and discourse markers

Band 7+ answers show *flexible* use of cohesive devices. The Buddy and Mumbai lists ([IELTS Buddy discourse markers](https://www.ieltsbuddy.com/discourse-markers-for-ielts-speaking.html), [Mumbai advanced markers](https://ieltsmumbai.com/blog/ielts-speaking-advanced-discourse-markers-for-natural-speech-flow)) classify these by function — adding (`on top of that`, `what's more`), contrasting (`having said that`, `then again`), exemplifying (`take X for instance`), concluding (`all things considered`), hedging (`I suppose`, `as far as I can tell`). IELTS Advantage warns that "many poor IELTS candidates try to insert discourse markers into every sentence. This will actually lower your marks and do more harm than good" — accuracy and appropriacy matter more than density ([IELTS Advantage criteria](https://www.ieltsadvantage.com/2015/06/28/ielts-speaking-criteria/)).

## Country-specific prep material

### South Korea (한국)

Hackers / Keyzard (Korean cram-school giant): the published Part 3 template explicitly warns that personal-experience markers like *"I think"*, *"in my opinion"*, *"from my perspective"* — which work in Parts 1 and 2 — **drag the score down in Part 3** because they signal an experience-based answer rather than a generalised, evaluative one. Instead the school pushes generalisation frames such as *"Generally speaking, people tend to…"*, *"It is often the case that…"*, *"In many cases…"* ([Hackers/Keyzard 파트3 템플릿](https://www.keyzard.cc/hackers_lawschool/nb/224099301614)).

TestGlider (testglider.com) and ELT School Japan/Korea also push the **PEEL** structure for every Part 3 answer: Point → Explain → Example → Link ([TestGlider speaking 가이드](https://blog.testglider.com/ielts-speaking-%EC%99%84%EB%B2%BD-%EA%B0%80%EC%9D%B4%EB%93%9C-%EC%9C%A0%ED%98%95%EB%B3%84-%EB%B6%84%EC%84%9D-%EB%B0%8F-%EA%B3%A0%EB%93%9D%EC%A0%90-%EC%A0%84%EB%9E%B5/), [eltschool.jp](https://www.eltschool.jp/en/column/ielts-speaking-questions)). IDP Korea reinforces that Part 3 is where you must "express and justify your opinions, analyse, discuss and speculate" ([IDP 아이엘츠 스피킹 시험 준비](https://ieltskorea.org/korea/prepare/speaking)).

### China (中国大陆)

Zhihu / Xinhua-route material treats Part 3 questions as **集合大类词** ("collective category" words) that demand categorical breakdown — e.g. "people" should be split into `teenagers / adults / seniors` before being answered ([Zhihu — Omar's answer](https://www.zhihu.com/question/431206051/answer/1676012817), [Zhihu thread](https://www.zhihu.com/question/431206051), [zhuanlan 万能套路](https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/576105614)). Standard Chinese cram structure is **总-分-总** (general-specific-general) and an explicit three-perspective expansion: `个人→社会→国家` (individual → society → nation), `过去→现在→未来` (past → present → future), `优点→缺点→解决方案` (pros → cons → solutions) ([Zhihu 简化版思路](https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/579933518)). 老烤鸭 catalogues a 10-type question taxonomy that maps closely to the global list but adds **场景演练** ("scenario role-play") and **方式方法** ("methods/approaches") as distinct categories ([老烤鸭 Part 3](https://www.laokaoya.com/category/ieltsspeaking/part3)). Recommended answer length per question: 30–45 seconds in early practice, extending to 45–60 seconds for Band 7 targets ([新航道 part3 介绍](https://www.xhd.cn/m/ielts/kouyu/156139.html), [沪江 雅思口语 part3 最难十题](https://liuxue.hujiang.com/ielts/speaking/p295918/)). 高顿 ([m.gaodun.com 深度问答](https://m.gaodun.com/ielts/1628654.html)) frames Part 3 as the *"思维广度"* (breadth-of-thinking) test and recommends pre-stocking 5–8 transferable arguments per macro-topic (technology, education, environment, etc.).

### Vietnam

IDP Vietnam pushes three named answer skeletons for Part 3 that recur across local materials ([IDP VN how to answer part 3](https://www.idp.com/vietnam/blog/how-to-answer-ielts-speaking-part-3-questions/), [DOL English](https://www.dolenglish.vn/blog/cach-tra-loi-ielts-speaking-part-3), [IELTS Fighter](https://ielts-fighter.com/speaking/chien-thuat-tra-loi-ielts-speaking-part-3_mt1506077473.html)):

- **Linear thinking** — single line of argument extended.
- **P.I.E.** — Point, Illustration, Explanation.
- **A.R.E.A.** — Answer, Reason, Example, Alternatives — the "Alternatives" branch is specifically designed to surface counter-argument language and hedging.

ZIM and IZONE catalogue 8 question types and stress that "trying to talk about yourself or your family — the examiner will guide you away from these familiar topics" ([ZIM 8 dạng câu hỏi](https://zim.vn/ielts-speaking-part-3-1), [IZONE Part 3](https://www.izone.edu.vn/blog/ielts-speaking-part-3/)). British Council Vietnam's 5 mẹo specifically authorises asking the examiner to rephrase using *"Can you rephrase the question?"* — this is treated as **non-penalised**, unlike silence ([British Council VN 5 mẹo](https://www.britishcouncil.vn/thi/ielts/chuan-bi/blog/5-meo-lam-bai-thi-IELTS-speaking-part-3)). Edmicro and Smartcom both warn that Part 3 answers based on personal experience cap the candidate around Band 6 even with otherwise good language ([Edmicro Part 3](https://edmicro.edu.vn/ielts/ielts-speaking-part-3/), [Smartcom Part 3](https://smartcom.vn/blog/nam-vung-cach-tra-loi-ielts-speaking-part-3-cuc-ky-don-gian/)).

### Iran (ایران)

IELTS Iran (`ieltsiran.ir`) and IDP Iran lay out Part 3 as a 4–5 minute academic dialogue, with **3 to 6 questions**, each answer ideally ~1 minute, and warn that the typical Iranian-candidate failure mode is "giving one idea repeatedly" instead of *answer → reason → example → alternative/opposite* ([IELTS Iran tips](https://www.ieltsiran.ir/fa/%D9%86%DA%A9%D8%A7%D8%AA-%D9%85%D8%B5%D8%A7%D8%AD%D8%A8%D9%87-speaking-%D8%A2%DB%8C%D9%84%D8%AA%D8%B3/), [IDP Iran Part 3](https://ielts.idp.com/iran/prepare/article-ielts-speaking-test-part-3/fa-ir)). The Iran 2 / 7-type-question article reproduces the canonical 7 question types in Farsi and ties each to a target grammar pattern ([ielts2.com 7 نوع سوال](https://ielts2.com/7-%D9%86%D9%88%D8%B9-%D8%B3%D9%88%D8%A7%D9%84-%D8%B1%D8%A7%DB%8C%D8%AC-%D8%AF%D8%B1-%D8%A8%D8%AE%D8%B4-%D8%B3%D9%88%D9%85-%D9%85%D8%B5%D8%A7%D8%AD%D8%A8%D9%87-%D8%A2%DB%8C%D9%84%D8%AA%D8%B3/)). Deed Language and Exir Academy ([deedlanguage.ir](https://deedlanguage.ir/article-page/IELTS-Speaking), [exiracademy.org](https://exiracademy.org/ielts-elixir/ielts-speaking-part-3/)) reinforce that intonation flatness is a major local pitfall — they urge Iranian candidates to deliberately vary pitch on stressed content words because Persian prosody differs from English nuclear-stress patterns. Zandi English's recent-questions archive ([zandienglish.ir](https://zandienglish.ir/recent-ielts-speaking-questions/)) is a free, regularly-updated bank of recently-reported Part 3 questions — useful for sanity-checking what real candidates actually faced.

### Indonesia

IDP Indonesia lists the five canonical Part 3 mistakes with concrete Indonesian-language coaching:

1. Filler words (`umm`, `like`, `you know`) — replace with *"In my opinion…"*, *"It depends…"*, *"On top of that…"* ([IDP Indonesia kesalahan umum](https://ielts.idp.com/indonesia/about/news-and-articles/article-common-mistakes-in-ielts-speaking-test-you-should-avoid)).
2. Repeating the same vocabulary — learn paraphrase synonyms.
3. Flat intonation — even if pronunciation is clear, monotone speech is penalised.
4. Repetitive grammar — actively mix simple, past, and perfect tenses.
5. Personal-experience anchoring — Part 3 demands general claims ([IELTSpresso](https://ieltspresso.com/jangan-lakukan-5-kesalahan-ini-di-ielts-speaking/), [AUG Study Services](https://augstudy.com/5-kesalahan-umum-ielts-speaking/), [ElevaU Part 3](https://elevau.id/blog/speaking/cara-menjawab-ielts-speaking-part-3-2/)).

EnglishCafe specifically frames Part 3 as a confidence test: *"penguji tidak ingin mendengar jawaban sempurna, tetapi ingin tahu bagaimana kamu mengekspresikan ide dengan percaya diri"* — examiners aren't looking for perfect answers but for confident idea expression ([englishcafe.co.id](https://www.englishcafe.co.id/tips-for-ielts-speaking-section/)).

### India & Pakistan

IDP India ([Perform Well in IELTS Speaking Part 3](https://ieltsidpindia.com/blog/ielts-speaking-part-3-discussion-tips)), Eduwings Global ([Part 3 guide](https://eduwingsglobal.com/ielts-speaking-part-3/)), and PW Live ([pw.live Part 3](https://www.pw.live/study-abroad/ielts/exams/ielts-speaking-part-3)) emphasise the OREO/AREA-style scaffolds but add a culturally-specific warning: do not over-reference one's own country or use it as the only example bank, because that locks the candidate into repeating "in India / in our country" as a verbal tic. They push instead a **two-example rule**: one local example, one international or hypothetical, per extended answer. CareerWise English ([Part 3 strategies](https://careerwiseenglish.com.au/ielts-speaking-part-3-tips-and-strategies/)) — Indian-targeted but Australia-based — adds explicit sentence-frame practice: *"This is largely due to…"*, *"As a consequence…"*, *"Although it's true that…, I would also say…"*.

## Open-source tools and code

There is no IELTS-specific open-source Speaking scorer with significant adoption as of May 2026 (searches returned no GitHub matches with stars > ~20 for "IELTS speaking scorer"). The relevant building blocks come from adjacent NLP research:

- **DisfluencyFixer** (Khanna et al., 2023, arXiv:2305.16957) — open ASR→DC→TTS pipeline that detects and corrects disfluencies, useful for tagging filler/hesitation features on a candidate transcript ([arXiv PDF](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2305.16957)).
- **Phonetic and Prosody-aware Self-supervised Learning for Non-native Fluency Scoring** (arXiv:2305.11438) — supplies a fluency-score head and feature definitions transferable to IELTS-style assessment ([arXiv PDF](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2305.11438)).
- **"Disfluency doesn't happen in isolation"** (Cambridge SSLA) — psycholinguistic evidence that disfluency clusters with grammar errors and lexical-search pauses, supporting joint-feature judging ([cambridge.org/core](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/studies-in-second-language-acquisition/article/disfluency-doesnt-happen-in-isolation/CD61D198FCEE38E178225AE2245AC2B0)).
- **"What Do the Measures of Utterance Fluency Employed in Automatic Speech Evaluation Tell Us About Oral Proficiency?"** (Suzuki et al., *Language Assessment Quarterly* 2023) — empirical study on 60 Chinese learners doing IELTS Part 2; quantifies which ASE features correlate with human rater bands ([Taylor & Francis](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/15434303.2023.2283839)).
- **G-Eval / DeepEval / Prometheus** — generic LLM-as-a-judge frameworks; the G-Eval rubric (Coherence / Consistency / Fluency / Relevance) maps onto IELTS FC and LR with minor adaptation ([Confident AI G-Eval guide](https://www.confident-ai.com/blog/g-eval-the-definitive-guide), [Databricks LLM eval](https://www.databricks.com/blog/best-practices-and-methods-llm-evaluation)).
- **USPTO Patent 8,457,967** — "Automatic evaluation of spoken fluency" — older but the feature list (mean pause length, silence ratio, articulation rate, repair ratio) is a reasonable baseline feature set ([USPTO PDF](https://image-ppubs.uspto.gov/dirsearch-public/print/downloadPdf/8457967)).

## Practical signals for an LLM judge

When reading a Part 3 turn from a transcript, weight these signals (drawn from converging advice across the sources above):

1. **Personal vs. general framing.** If the candidate's Part 3 answers contain ≥50 % first-person personal-narrative content ("my mother", "when I was at school"), cap FC and LR at Band 6 regardless of vocabulary, unless the question explicitly invited it. Generalised frames (*"people tend to…", "in most countries…", "the majority of …"*) are a Band 7 floor signal ([IELTS Liz](https://ieltsliz.com/common-mistake-in-speaking-part-3/), [Hackers/Keyzard](https://www.keyzard.cc/hackers_lawschool/nb/224099301614)).
2. **Question-type ↔ grammar match.** Reward when a *future* question elicits modal/future forms (`will / is likely to / I would expect`), a *hypothetical* elicits conditionals (`if … would / would have …`), a *past-vs-present* elicits present-perfect or `used to`, and a *comparison* elicits `whereas / while / by contrast`. Mismatches (e.g. hypothetical answered in simple past with no conditionals) flag GRA at Band 6 ([SpeakPrac](https://speakprac.com/ielts-speaking-course/part-3/), [Mumbai discourse markers](https://ieltsmumbai.com/blog/ielts-speaking-advanced-discourse-markers-for-natural-speech-flow)).
3. **Hedging density.** Band 7+ Part 3 answers typically contain ≥1 hedge or modal-of-uncertainty per 30 seconds of speech (*"it could be argued", "to some extent", "I'd be inclined to say", "in most cases", "tend to"*). Zero hedges across a full Part 3 = Band 6 LR ceiling ([Mumbai hedging](https://ieltsmumbai.com/blog/ielts-vocabulary--grammar-using-hedging-language-appropriately-in-ielts)).
4. **Answer length window.** Healthy answers run 30–60 seconds / roughly 70–150 words ([Keith Speaking Academy](https://keithspeakingacademy.com/ielts-speaking-part-3-tips/), [Speechful AI](https://speechful.ai/blog/ielts-speaking-part-3-essential-tips)). <20 seconds repeatedly = under-developed (FC/LR penalty); >75 seconds with topic drift = coherence penalty.
5. **Discourse-marker control, not density.** Look for *appropriate* markers across functions (adding, contrasting, exemplifying, concluding). Mechanical repetition of one marker (e.g. "moreover" five times) or markers used inappropriately (`furthermore` followed by an unrelated point) is the Band 6 trap explicitly called out by IELTS Advantage ([IELTS Advantage criteria](https://www.ieltsadvantage.com/2015/06/28/ielts-speaking-criteria/)).
6. **Self-correction texture.** Per the band 7/8 descriptors, occasional repetition/self-correction is *normal* at Band 7; what distinguishes Band 8 is that hesitation is "content-related" (searching for an idea) rather than "language-related" (searching for the word). When the candidate self-corrects fluently mid-clause and moves on, that's Band 7+ positive evidence, not a deduction ([British Council descriptors PDF](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf)).
7. **Paraphrase of the examiner's prompt.** Reward (rather than penalise) candidates who restate the question in their own words at the start of the answer. Verbatim repetition is a Band 6 hesitation marker; clean paraphrase signals LR flexibility ([IELTS Jacky](https://www.ieltsjacky.com/ielts-speaking-part-3.html)).
8. **Counter-argument move.** A.R.E.A./O.R.E.C.-trained candidates often produce *"Although it's true that…, I would also say…"* or *"Having said that…"* in extended answers. Presence of at least one such concession-then-pivot move across Part 3 is a Band 7+ positive signal ([IDP VN A.R.E.A.](https://www.idp.com/vietnam/blog/how-to-answer-ielts-speaking-part-3-questions/), [CareerWise](https://careerwiseenglish.com.au/ielts-speaking-part-3-tips-and-strategies/)).
9. **Topic-vocabulary depth.** Each Part 3 question implicitly opens a topic field (education, environment, technology, etc.). Band 7+ candidates produce 2–4 topic-specific lower-frequency items per answer (e.g. for education: *rote learning, critical thinking, vocational training, peer pressure*) rather than relying on basic vocabulary. Absence of topic-specific lexis across a full Part 3 caps LR at Band 6 ([100 phrases PDF](https://www.ieltsadvantage.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/100-Essential-Words-and-Phrases-for-Band-7-9-Success-2.pdf), [IELTS Material topic answers](https://ieltsmaterial.com/ielts-speaking-part-3-topics-questions-with-answers/)).
10. **Register policing.** Both formality extremes are penalised. Essay-formal turns ("Firstly, it is undoubtedly the case that one must consider…") signal memorised chunks and hurt FC. Heavy slang/filler ("yeah like totally, you know, kinda thing") signals under-developed LR. Band 7 target: educated-conversational ([Speechful AI](https://speechful.ai/blog/ielts-speaking-part-3-essential-tips), [Oxford Online Band 7](https://www.oxfordonlineenglish.com/ielts-speaking-band-7)).
11. **Categorical breakdown.** The Chinese cram-school move of splitting "people" into *teenagers / adults / seniors* — or any analogous 2–3 way split — is a strong Band 7+ coherence signal because it produces parallel grammar and visible structure. Score positively when present ([Zhihu Omar](https://www.zhihu.com/question/431206051/answer/1676012817)).
12. **"Can you rephrase that?" is non-penalised.** If the candidate asks for clarification once or twice across Part 3, do not deduct — this is officially sanctioned ([British Council VN 5 mẹo](https://www.britishcouncil.vn/thi/ielts/chuan-bi/blog/5-meo-lam-bai-thi-IELTS-speaking-part-3), [IELTS Advantage Speaking](https://www.ieltsadvantage.com/ielts-speaking/)).
13. **Examiner interruption is a signal.** If the transcript shows the examiner cutting the candidate off, treat as a positive fluency signal (the candidate had enough to say that the examiner needed to manage time), not negative. Conversely, examiner re-prompts with *"anything else?"* / *"why is that?"* indicate the candidate gave an underdeveloped first pass — count as a mild FC negative ([IDP Part 3 article](https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-ielts-speaking-test-part-3)).
14. **Idiomatic-chunk pacing.** Band 7+ candidates produce 1–3 idiomatic/collocational chunks naturally embedded in Part 3 (*"a double-edged sword", "in the long run", "by and large", "to put it another way", "draw the line at"*). Zero idiomatic chunks across Part 3 = LR Band 6 ceiling; >5 forced/misused chunks = also Band 6 (overcompensation) ([Mumbai discourse markers](https://ieltsmumbai.com/blog/ielts-speaking-advanced-discourse-markers-for-natural-speech-flow), [100 phrases PDF](https://www.ieltsadvantage.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/100-Essential-Words-and-Phrases-for-Band-7-9-Success-2.pdf)).
15. **Memorised-chunk detection.** Watch for register-incongruent fluent bursts: sudden flawless multi-clause sentences with low-frequency vocabulary that don't match the surrounding speech texture. Examiners explicitly downgrade these — *"sounding memorised … overly prepared language often sounds stiff"* ([Speechful AI](https://speechful.ai/blog/ielts-speaking-part-3-essential-tips), [All Ears English](https://www.allearsenglish.com/ielts-energy-859-managing-mistakes-in-ielts-speaking-part-3/)).
