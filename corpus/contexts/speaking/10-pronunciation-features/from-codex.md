# Pronunciation criteria - segmental and suprasegmental features, common L1-specific errors

_Crawled by codex on 2026-05-19T07:32:52Z. Sources cited inline._

## Summary

IELTS Pronunciation is a 25% Speaking criterion and is assessed through intelligibility, phonological range, control, connected speech, stress, rhythm, intonation, and word/phoneme production, not through imitation of a British, American, or Australian accent (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking; https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf; https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-does-ielts-speaking-assess-your-accent). For a transcript-only judge, pronunciation evidence is indirect: the judge should rely on explicit transcript signals such as examiner repair, [inaudible] spans, repeated misrecognitions, marked pauses/chunking, and phonetic spellings, while avoiding unsupported assumptions from nationality or L1 alone (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf; https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-does-ielts-speaking-assess-your-accent).

## Authoritative material

- Official IELTS says the Speaking test is a recorded face-to-face interview with three parts lasting 11-14 minutes, and the four equally named criteria are Fluency and Coherence, Lexical Resource, Grammatical Range and Accuracy, and Pronunciation (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking). IELTS describes Pronunciation as the ability to speak in a way that can be understood without too much effort, so listener effort is the first scoring anchor for a judge (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking).

- The IELTS Speaking Key Assessment Criteria define Pronunciation as accurate and sustained use of phonological features to convey meaningful messages (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf). The same official document identifies five key indicators: meaningful chunking within spoken sentences, rhythm and stress timing, linking and elision for connected speech, emphatic or contrastive stress plus intonation, word stress, vowel and consonant production, listener effort, and the overall effect of accent on intelligibility (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf).

- The public Speaking Band Descriptors make Band 9 pronunciation "effortlessly understood", with a full range of phonological features, sustained connected speech, and no intelligibility effect from accent (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf). Band 8 requires a wide range of phonological features, appropriate rhythm, flexible stress and intonation across long utterances with only occasional lapses, easy understanding throughout, and minimal accent effect (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf). Band 6 permits variable control: a range of features is present, chunking is generally appropriate, rhythm may be affected by poor stress-timing or rapid rate, intonation/stress are sometimes effective but unsustained, and word/phoneme errors cause only occasional lack of clarity (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf). Band 4 has limited range, frequent rhythm lapses, limited stress/intonation control, frequent word/phoneme mispronunciation, and some patches that cannot be understood (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).

- The official descriptors note that a candidate must fully fit the positive features of a descriptor at a level and is rated on average performance across all parts of the test, so a judge should not over-score a single memorised or unusually polished answer if later speech loses control (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).

- IELTS's teacher-facing article "Demystifying the IELTS Speaking test" says Pronunciation covers comprehensible utterances and a range of pronunciation features used to communicate meaning, and it confirms each Speaking criterion is equally weighted (https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/demystifying-the-ielts-speaking-test). The same article says the test is designed to assess natural production rather than rehearsed display, which matters because over-rehearsed answers may sound fluent in a transcript but still fail if delivery is monotone or not interactive (https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/demystifying-the-ielts-speaking-test).

- British Council teacher material lists individual sounds, word stress, sentence stress, intonation, and chunking as the pronunciation features that IELTS candidates should understand and practise (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-pronunciation). British Council also warns that memorising long responses can lead to monotone delivery and lower scores, and recommends chunking ideas with short silences so the listener can process meaning (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-pronunciation).

- IDP's pronunciation guidance states that candidates are assessed on stress, intonation, rhythm, and chunking; examiners listen for clear word pronunciation and whether errors affect understanding; and stress/intonation should emphasise important words or contrast ideas (https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-improve-pronunciation-ielts-speaking). IDP's intonation article explains rising, falling, and fall-rise intonation as meaning-bearing pitch movement, and says pronunciation in IELTS Speaking includes stress and rhythm in addition to intonation (https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-i-is-for-intonation).

- IDP's accent guidance says IELTS assesses clarity, stress, rhythm, and intonation rather than native-like accent, and gives operational examples such as word stress distinguishing DESert from desSERT and slow/clear chunking improving intelligibility over compressed delivery (https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-does-ielts-speaking-assess-your-accent). IDP Canada similarly states examiners assess spoken English rather than accent, while stress, intonation, and rhythm can be influenced by accent and should be the focus of preparation (https://ielts.idp.com/canada/prepare/article-accents-ielts-speaking-scores).

- Cambridge English ELT's classroom guidance says pronunciation is 25% of the IELTS Speaking score and recommends practical activities such as recording and speech-recognition transcription to notice pronunciation features and intelligibility problems (https://www.cambridge.org/elt/blog/2019/03/27/ielts-speaking-improving-pronunication/). IELTS research by Isaacs, Trofimovich, Yu, and Munoz Chereau studied which linguistic factors discriminate upper levels of the revised IELTS Pronunciation scale and used segmental, prosodic, fluency, and lexicogrammatical measures rated by accredited IELTS examiners (https://ielts.org/researchers/our-research/research-reports/examining-the-linguistic-aspects-of-speech-that-most-efficiently-discriminate-between-upper-levels-of-the-revised-ielts-pronunciation-scale; https://cdn.ielts.org/Research/examining-linguistic-aspects-of-speech-isaacs-et-al-2015.pdf).

## Country-specific prep material

### Korea

- British Council Korea's Korean-language IELTS pronunciation page says the assessed features include word stress, sentence stress, intonation, and chunking, matching the global British Council pronunciation video (https://www.britishcouncil.kr/exam/ielts/advice-resources/ielts-videos/pronunciation). Korean prep listings for IELTS speaking academies describe practice with linking, rules, and intonation in Part 2 and Part 3 speaking, which shows local cram-school attention to connected speech rather than only vocabulary scripts (https://whereisyourwork.com/education-english-ielts-speaking-academy/).

- For Korean L1 transfer, Pronunciation Studio reports common English difficulties with /f/ and /v/ substitutions toward /p/ and /b/, plus problems with English word and sentence stress because Korean does not use English-style stress timing (https://pronunciationstudio.com/korean-speakers-english-pronunciation-errors/). TALK Schools similarly lists Korean-speaker issues with sentence rhythm, intonation, stress, diphthongs, consonant pairs, and long/short vowel contrasts (https://blog.talk.edu/learn-english/common-pronunciation-errors-korean-speakers/). Korean research summaries report Korean EFL learners' problematic areas include vowels, stress, pause, rhythm, and intonation, so a judge should treat repeated vowel-length confusions and flat rhythm as plausible evidence only when the transcript or ASR metadata shows listener impact (https://www.kci.go.kr/kciportal/landing/article.kci?arti_id=ART002219699).

### Mainland China

- The Chinese IELTS official site provides a Chinese translation of the Speaking Band Descriptors but states that the English version is authoritative, so local Chinese materials should be mapped back to the official English descriptors when used for scoring (https://backoffice.ielts.chinaielts.org/api/assets/ielts-cms/0c5929f5-faad-45ca-83f5-ce5c8384ec95/%E8%AF%84%E5%88%86%E6%A0%87%E5%87%86-speakingfinal.pdf?version=1). Chinese prep provider New Channel says IELTS pronunciation is not only word accuracy but also linking, appropriate pauses, word stress, and natural intonation (https://www.xhd.cn/ielts/kouyu/161942.html). Another New Channel article lists common IELTS speaking pronunciation errors as wrong word stress, ignored connected speech and weak forms such as reduced "and", and unnatural intonation (https://km.xhd.cn/m/info/ieltsSpeak/934657.html).

- For Chinese L1 transfer, TALK Schools lists tone/intonation transfer, consonant clusters and word-final consonants, "r" and "v", vowel confusion, and "l"/"n" confusion as common Chinese-speaker issues (https://blog.talk.edu/learn-english/common-pronunciation-errors-chinese-speakers/). CAL Learning says Chinese speakers often struggle with English stress, linking, lengthened syllables, utterance-level intonation, and final consonant clusters, which are all aligned with IELTS's pronunciation feature list (https://www.callearning.com/blog/2013/12/pronunciation-errors-for-chinese-speakers/). Duke's learner-English handout describes Chinese speakers tending to stress too many English syllables and to give weak syllables full vowels, which is relevant to transcript annotations that show word-by-word rhythm or missing reductions (https://sites.duke.edu/writingstudio/files/2013/12/Learner-English-Chinese.pdf).

### Vietnam

- Vietnamese IELTS provider DOL English defines IELTS Pronunciation as clear individual sounds, sound-linking into words, rhythm, sentence stress, chunking, and intonation, and notes that Vietnamese speakers may be disadvantaged by English stress because Vietnamese uses tones rather than English-style lexical stress (https://www.dolenglish.vn/blog/tieu-chi-pronunciation-trong-ielts-speaking). A Vietnamese IELTS pronunciation PDF from FLYER lists word stress, linking, effective pronunciation features, flexible sustained use, intelligibility, and limited L1 effect as features to watch for higher bands (https://flyer.vn/wp-content/uploads/2024/05/TIEU-CHI-P-IELTS.pdf?x30689=).

- For Vietnamese L1 transfer, TALK Schools identifies word stress, missing middle sounds in multi-syllable words, and final consonants as three primary English pronunciation errors for Vietnamese speakers (https://blog.talk.edu/learn-english/common-pronunciation-errors-vietnamese-speakers/). Eduling similarly highlights final consonant sounds, word stress and sound lengthening, and short /i/ as recurring Vietnamese learner issues, and notes that Vietnamese tone can cause misplaced pitch or stress in English words (https://www.eduling.org/post/three-common-pronunciation-features-vietnamese-learners-need-to-improve). An empirical Vietnamese pronunciation study reports final consonants, consonant clusters, vowel distinctions, stress, intonation, rhythm, and linking as recurring problem areas, which supports treating omitted final consonants like "fas" for "fast" as potentially intelligibility-reducing when the transcript preserves them (https://www.ijsshr.in/v7i5/Doc/74.pdf).

### Japan

- British Council Japan's IELTS pronunciation page says the assessed aspects include word stress, sentence stress, intonation, and chunking (https://www.britishcouncil.jp/en/exam/ielts/prepare/test-tips/speaking-pronunciation). Japanese learner guidance from Improve Your Accent says Japanese speakers should work on English stress because Japanese tends to give syllables more equal weight (https://improveyouraccent.co.uk/pronunciation-tips-japanese-speakers/).

- For Japanese L1 transfer, Pronunciation Studio lists equal syllable stress, consonant clusters, and segmental issues as common for Japanese speakers of English (https://pronunciationstudio.com/japanese-speakers-english-pronunciation-errors/). TALK Schools links many Japanese-speaker English errors to katakana assimilation of English loanwords, which can produce extra vowels and altered segmental patterns in English speech (https://blog.talk.edu/learn-english/common-pronunciation-errors-japanese-speakers/). A J-STAGE paper on Japanese speakers' processing of English consonant clusters examines vowel epenthesis in cluster production, which is relevant when transcript evidence shows forms like "sutorike" for "strike" or extra vowels after final consonants (https://www.jstage.jst.go.jp/article/ast/31/5/31_5_320/_pdf).

### Spanish-speaking regions

IDP Mexico publishes IELTS pronunciation guidance on word stress, sentence stress, and intonation, reflecting Spanish-market prep emphasis on prosody as well as segmental clarity (https://ielts.idp.com/mexico/prepare/article-pronunciation-skills-word-sentence-stress-intonation-ielts-speaking).

- For Spanish L1 transfer, Pronunciation Studio lists common Spanish-speaker English errors including connected-speech treatment of /h/ in function words and other segmental/prosodic patterns (https://pronunciationstudio.com/spanish-speakers-english-pronunciation-errors/). TEFL.net identifies initial /s/ clusters with an added vowel, absent or regional /z/, and other pronunciation differences that can affect clarity in English (https://www.tefl.net/elt/articles/teacher-technique/spanish-speaker-pronunciation-problems/). Packard Communications says the two primary factors for Spanish-speaker English pronunciation are word stress and speech-sound differences, and notes that English word-final consonant clusters contrast with Spanish patterns (https://packardcommunications.com/pronunciation-english-spanish/).

IDP's general IELTS pronunciation guidance is also relevant for Arabic-speaking candidates because it emphasises stress, intonation, rhythm, and chunking instead of accent replacement (https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-improve-pronunciation-ielts-speaking).

- For Arabic L1 transfer, TALK Schools lists "b"/"p", "f"/"v", silent-letter pronunciation, added vowel sounds, intonation, and word stress as common Arabic-speaker English pronunciation problems (https://blog.talk.edu/learn-english/common-pronunciation-problems-arabic-speakers/). - IDP Canada says examiners are trained to assess spoken English rather than accent, and says accent can influence stress, intonation, and rhythm, so candidates should focus on pronunciation features that affect the score (https://ielts.idp.com/canada/prepare/article-accents-ielts-speaking-scores). Cambridge English's classroom activities recommend speech-recognition transcription as a way to check whether pronunciation is being understood, which is especially relevant to automated corpus-building from diarized transcripts (https://www.cambridge.org/elt/blog/2019/03/27/ielts-speaking-improving-pronunication/).

## Open-source tools and code

- Speechocean762 is an open non-native English speech corpus for pronunciation assessment with 5,000 English utterances from 250 non-native speakers, expert annotation at sentence, word, and phoneme levels, free commercial and non-commercial use according to the paper, and a GitHub repository plus Hugging Face dataset metadata listing CC BY 4.0 (https://arxiv.org/abs/2104.01378; https://github.com/jimbozhang/speechocean762; https://huggingface.co/datasets/mispeech/speechocean762/blob/7b69e51b3d13bd5e7d3026174c5ca857ee71c1d5/speechocean762.py).

- GOPT is a BSD-3-Clause PyTorch implementation of the Goodness Of Pronunciation Feature-Based Transformer for multi-aspect, multi-granularity non-native English pronunciation assessment, and the repository reports phone-level, word-level, and sentence-level Pearson correlations on SpeechOcean762 (https://github.com/YuanGongND/gopt; https://arxiv.org/abs/2205.03432).

- Kaldi includes a gop_speechocean762 recipe referenced by the Speechocean762 paper and GOPT repository; it is useful for phoneme-level Goodness of Pronunciation feature extraction, but it requires audio and forced alignment rather than transcript-only scoring (https://github.com/kaldi-asr/kaldi/tree/master/egs/gop_speechocean762; https://github.com/YuanGongND/gopt).

- SpeechSuper's public API-sample repository is MIT licensed and documents scripted pronunciation assessment at phoneme, syllable, word, and sentence levels, fluency metrics, rhythm/speed, syllable stress, liaison, loss of plosion, rising/falling sentence tone, and IELTS-oriented spontaneous-speech scoring (https://github.com/speechsuper/SpeechSuper-API-Samples).

- Speechace is a commercial pronunciation and fluency assessment API that advertises phoneme/syllable feedback, lexical stress, intonation, pausing, pitch, speaking/articulation rate, IELTS/CEFR/PTE/TOEIC scoring, and spontaneous open-ended speaking assessment; it can provide audio-side signals that a transcript-only LLM lacks (https://www.speechace.com/api-plans/; https://www.postman.com/speechace/speechace-s-public-workspace/documentation/fwsskik/speechace-api).

- Recent automatic pronunciation assessment reviews and models cover Goodness of Pronunciation, mispronunciation detection, and multimodal LLM approaches, but these tools generally require the learner's audio and prompt text rather than only a normalized transcript (https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.13974; https://arxiv.org/abs/2407.09209; https://arxiv.org/abs/2101.06396).

## Practical signals for an LLM judge

- Do not award or penalise Pronunciation from ordinary spelling, vocabulary, or grammar in a clean manual transcript unless the transcript explicitly preserves phonetic spellings, inaudibility, repairs, or examiner misunderstanding; official IELTS defines the criterion by listener effort and phonological features, not by written form (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf; https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).

- Treat repeated examiner repair prompts such as "Sorry?", "Could you repeat that?", "Do you mean...?", or transcript tags like [inaudible]/[unclear] as strong evidence of reduced intelligibility; Band 8-9 speech should be easy or effortless to understand throughout, while Band 4 allows patches that cannot be understood (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).

- Separate accent from intelligibility: an L1 accent with clear pronunciation, natural rhythm, and effective stress/intonation should not be penalised simply for sounding Korean, Chinese, Indian, Spanish, Arabic, or Russian; IELTS and IDP state that accent matters only through its effect on intelligibility and pronunciation-feature control (https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-does-ielts-speaking-assess-your-accent; https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).

- If transcript annotations show word-by-word delivery, unnatural pause breaks inside phrases, or missing phrase boundaries, lower the likely pronunciation band because IELTS explicitly scores chunking, rhythm, stress timing, linking, and elision as key indicators (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf; https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-pronunciation).

- If ASR or human annotation marks monotone delivery, flat intonation, rising tone after most statements, or lack of contrastive emphasis, cap high pronunciation claims because Band 8 requires flexible stress and intonation across long utterances and IDP identifies intonation as meaning-bearing (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf; https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-i-is-for-intonation).

- If the transcript preserves connected-speech problems such as every function word being stressed, no contractions/weak forms in otherwise fluent informal speech, or repeated full-vowel productions that cause ASR misunderstanding, use this as evidence of weak rhythm and stress timing, especially for L1s whose prep materials flag equal stress or weak-syllable difficulty (https://sites.duke.edu/writingstudio/files/2013/12/Learner-English-Chinese.pdf; https://pronunciationstudio.com/japanese-speakers-english-pronunciation-errors/; https://pronunciationstudio.com/russian-speakers-english-pronunciation-errors/).

- Segmental errors matter most when they reduce clarity or change lexical identity: examples include Korean /f/-/p/ or /v/-/b/, Chinese final consonant loss, Vietnamese final consonant deletion, Japanese vowel epenthesis, Spanish /s/ cluster epenthesis, Arabic /p/-/b/, Hindi-Urdu /v/-/w/ or TH substitutions, and Indonesian final stop/sibilant elision (https://pronunciationstudio.com/korean-speakers-english-pronunciation-errors/; https://blog.talk.edu/learn-english/common-pronunciation-errors-chinese-speakers/; https://blog.talk.edu/learn-english/common-pronunciation-errors-vietnamese-speakers/; https://www.jstage.jst.go.jp/article/ast/31/5/31_5_320/_pdf; https://www.tefl.net/elt/articles/teacher-technique/spanish-speaker-pronunciation-problems/; https://blog.talk.edu/learn-english/common-pronunciation-problems-arabic-speakers/; https://hadarshemesh.com/magazine/english-for-urdu-and-hindi-speakers/; https://bridges.monash.edu/articles/journal_contribution/Errors_in_pronunciation_of_consonants_by_learners_of_English_as_a_foreign_language_whose_first_languages_are_Indonesian_Gayo_and_Acehnese/5063614).

- Do not infer those L1-specific errors automatically from the candidate's name, nationality, or topic content; use them only as diagnostic hypotheses when transcript evidence, ASR alternatives, or examiner repairs show a matching intelligibility problem (https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-does-ielts-speaking-assess-your-accent; https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).

- A candidate with occasional isolated word/phoneme errors but generally understandable speech with some effective stress/intonation fits Band 6 more naturally than Band 4, because official Band 6 permits occasional lack of clarity while Band 4 involves frequent mispronunciation and listener effort (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).

- A candidate cannot be safely rated Band 8+ for Pronunciation from a transcript alone unless there are positive audio-derived annotations showing sustained rhythm, stress, intonation, connected speech, and easy intelligibility; official Band 8 requires those features across long utterances, and transcript-only text usually cannot demonstrate them (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf; https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf).

- Use Part 2 and Part 3 as important pronunciation stress tests because they create longer utterances where chunking, rhythm, stress timing, intonation, and connected speech can be sustained or break down; IELTS says the final score is based on average performance across all parts (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking; https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).

- If pronunciation problems lead to semantic confusion in the transcript, count the listener-impact under Pronunciation even when the same surface issue also looks lexical or grammatical; official descriptors foreground whether individual words/phonemes require effort and whether mispronunciation reduces clarity (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).
