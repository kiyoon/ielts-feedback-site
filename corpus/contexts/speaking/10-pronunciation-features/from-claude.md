# Pronunciation criteria — segmental and suprasegmental features, common L1-specific errors

_Crawled by claude on 2026-05-19T00:00:00Z. Sources cited inline._

## Summary

Pronunciation is one of the four IELTS Speaking criteria, worth 25% of the band score, and is measured against a fixed set of phonological features rather than against a "British/American" target. Examiners listen for the candidate's **range** of features (segmental: individual phonemes, word stress; suprasegmental: sentence stress, weak forms, connected speech, rhythm, intonation, chunking), the **control** with which they are deployed, and the resulting **intelligibility** — i.e., how much listener effort the speech requires. L1 background is *not* penalised in itself, but L1 transfer typically surfaces as a predictable cluster of errors (Korean L+R, dark-L→/w/, epenthesis on final clusters; Japanese vowel insertion in clusters; Chinese final-consonant deletion and word-level pitch; Arabic /p/↔/b/, vowel undershoot; Spanish schwa-loss; Vietnamese final-consonant elision; Persian /w/↔/v/ etc.). The judge should score on the **descriptor language** ("wide range, flexibly, easy to understand"), not on accent.

## Authoritative material

### Official band descriptors (public version)

The official pronunciation column references four operational components in the descriptor language: **range of pronunciation features**, **flexibility/control**, **intelligibility (listener effort / mother-tongue effect)**, and **mispronunciation patterns** (frequency and whether they affect clarity). The headline phrases per band, as reproduced across British Council, Cambridge, and IDP versions:

- **Band 9** — "Uses a full range of pronunciation features with precision and subtlety. Sustains flexible use of features throughout. Is effortless to understand." (Cambridge / British Council PDF, [takeielts.britishcouncil.org](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf))
- **Band 8** — "Uses a wide range of pronunciation features. Sustains flexible use of features, with only occasional lapses. Is easy to understand throughout; L1 accent has minimal effect on intelligibility." (same source)
- **Band 7** — "Shows all the positive features of Band 6, and some, but not all, of the positive features of Band 8." (same source — Band 7 is the only band defined by reference)
- **Band 6** — "Uses a range of pronunciation features with mixed control. Shows some effective use of features but this is not sustained. Can generally be understood throughout, though mispronunciation of individual words or sounds reduces clarity at times." ([takeielts.britishcouncil.org](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf))
- **Band 5** — "Shows all the positive features of Band 4, and some, but not all, of the positive features of Band 6."
- **Band 4** — "Uses a limited range of pronunciation features. Attempts to control features but lapses are frequent. Mispronunciations are frequent and cause some difficulty for the listener."

Cambridge's examiner-training material packages this into the four phonological features examiners listen for, paraphrased on multiple training pages: **(1) individual sounds** (phonemes), **(2) word stress**, **(3) sentence stress** (incl. weak forms), and **(4) intonation**, with **chunking** and **connected speech** treated as parts of rhythm/sentence-stress ([British Council teach-IELTS](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-pronunciation), [IDP IELTS](https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-pronunciation-for-the-ielts-speaking-test-discover-our-new-video-series)).

### What "range of features" actually means

Across IDP, British Council, and Cambridge guidance the assessable feature set is consistently:

1. **Phonemes** — the ~44 sounds of English (20 vowels, 24 consonants), including pairs absent from many L1s: /θ ð/, /ɪ iː/, /ʊ uː/, /æ e/, /v w/, /r l/, /ʃ ʒ/, /tʃ dʒ/ ([IDP](https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-improve-pronunciation-ielts-speaking), [ieltsetc.com](https://ieltsetc.com/ielts-pronunciation-tips-speaking-test/)).
2. **Word stress** — the syllable that bears prominence, e.g. `PHO-to-graph` vs `pho-TO-graph-y` vs `pho-to-GRA-phic`; also stress-shift pairs like *DESert/deSERT*, *PREsent/preSENT* that change part of speech ([IDP](https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-improve-pronunciation-ielts-speaking)).
3. **Weak forms / vowel reduction** — function words reduced to schwa: *and→ /ən/*, *to→/tə/*, *for→/fə/*, *of→/əv/*, *was→/wəz/*. Loss of weak forms is the most reliable marker that pulls a candidate from Band 7 to Band 6 on rhythm ([eflmagazine.com](https://eflmagazine.com/pronunciation-ielts-speaking/)).
4. **Sentence stress** — prominence on content words to mark meaning; ability to *shift* prominence for contrast (e.g. "I lost **ALL** my money" vs "I lost all **MY** money") ([keithspeakingacademy.com](https://keithspeakingacademy.com/ielts-speaking-pronunciation-features-practice/)).
5. **Connected speech / linking** — C→V linking (*an apple* → /əˈnæpəl/), elision (*next day* → /neksdeɪ/), assimilation (*ten bikes* → /tem baɪks/), intrusion (*go on* → /ɡəʊwɒn/) ([ieltsetc.com](https://ieltsetc.com/ielts-pronunciation-tips-speaking-test/)).
6. **Chunking** — pausing at phrase boundaries rather than mid-phrase; "delivering chunks of information…with short silences in between" ([takeielts.britishcouncil.org speaking video](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-pronunciation)).
7. **Intonation** — falling for completed declaratives and wh-questions, rising for yes/no questions and list-non-final items, fall-rise for contrast/uncertainty.
8. **Rhythm** — English is stress-timed: stressed syllables roughly equidistant, intervening syllables compressed. Syllable-timed L1s (French, Spanish, Korean, Vietnamese, Italian, Mandarin) characteristically over-articulate every syllable, producing a "machine-gun" rhythm that pulls bands down ([eflmagazine.com](https://eflmagazine.com/pronunciation-ielts-speaking/)).

### Pronunciation marking principles

- **Accent is not penalised.** "No specific accent required. IELTS is international English… If your accent requires significant effort to understand, your score drops" ([ieltsliz.com](https://ieltsliz.com/ielts-speaking-accent-british-us-or-other/)).
- **Mother-tongue effect is explicitly invoked at Band 8** ("L1 accent has minimal effect on intelligibility") — i.e., L1 accent CAN exist at Band 8/9 but must not force the listener to back-translate ([British Council descriptors PDF](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf)).
- **Mispronunciation impact is graded.** Band 6 tolerates word/sound mispronunciations that "reduce clarity at times"; Band 4 has frequent mispronunciations that "cause some difficulty for the listener"; Band 2 mispronunciations "severely affect" understanding ([ieltsagape.wordpress.com](https://ieltsagape.wordpress.com/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors/)).
- **Range × control is what separates 7 from 8.** Band 7 *shows* features (some sentence stress, some weak forms, some linking) but not consistently and not all; Band 8 *sustains* a wide set across the whole test ([ielts.com.au Band 6 vs 7](https://ielts.com.au/australia/about/news-and-articles/article-speaking-band-6-vs-band-7)).

## Country-specific prep material

### Korean (한국어)

Korean L1 transfer produces a tightly-clustered error profile and is the most relevant for this corpus.

- **L vs R**: Korean has one liquid ㄹ that surfaces as a flap intervocalically and as a lateral in coda. Result: word-final /r/ and /l/ collapse ("four"→"fall", "berry"≈"belly"); /r/ often produced as a tap or with insufficient retroflex/bunched articulation ([linkedin.com — Pronunciation problems Koreans face](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/pronunciation-problems-koreans-face-when-learning-alison-tomlinson), [hadarshemesh.com — English for Korean speakers](https://hadarshemesh.com/magazine/english-for-korean-speakers/)).
- **F/V → P/B; Z → J/S; TH → S/T/D**: e.g. *very*→*berry*, *fine*→*pine*, *thanks*→*sanks*/*tanks*, *doesn't*→*doe-chn't* ([hadarshemesh.com](https://hadarshemesh.com/magazine/english-for-korean-speakers/)).
- **Final-stop devoicing/insertion**: Korean codas neutralise voicing (B/D/G→P/T/K) and prohibit released stops; speakers either devoice (*bag*→*bak*) or epenthesise an /ɯ/ (*bus*→*ba-su*, *milk*→*mil-ku*) ([academia.edu L1-L2 transfer](https://www.academia.edu/6019976/L1_to_L2_Transfer_of_Korean_Learners_of_English)).
- **Consonant-cluster epenthesis**: Korean syllables ban onset clusters, so *school*→*su-kul*, *strike*→*sŭ-tŭ-ra-ik* — increases syllable count and breaks rhythm ([linkedin.com](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/pronunciation-problems-koreans-face-when-learning-alison-tomlinson)).
- **Vowel mergers**: /iː ɪ/ → both ≈ /i/ (*sheep* ≈ *ship*, *leave* ≈ *live*); /uː ʊ/ → both ≈ /u/ (*pool* ≈ *pull*); /æ/ → /e/ (*pat*→*pet*) ([mountainscholar.org thesis — Korean stress & vowel reduction](https://mountainscholar.org/bitstreams/7933c70e-a329-4458-8463-c49096c6ef9f/download)).
- **Flat stress / syllable-timing**: Korean has no lexical stress and roughly equal-prominence syllables; speakers stress every syllable equally in English, killing weak forms and rhythm — a strong Band 6 ceiling marker for many candidates ([mountainscholar.org thesis](https://mountainscholar.org/bitstreams/7933c70e-a329-4458-8463-c49096c6ef9f/download)).
- **Korean cram-school perspective** ([ieltskorea.org/scoring](https://ieltskorea.org/korea/results/scores/speaking), [blog.testglider.com IELTS Speaking guide](https://blog.testglider.com/ielts-speaking-%EC%99%84%EB%B2%BD-%EA%B0%80%EC%9D%B4%EB%93%9C-%EC%9C%A0%ED%98%95%EB%B3%84-%EB%B6%84%EC%84%9D-%EB%B0%8F-%EA%B3%A0%EB%93%9D%EC%A0%90-%EC%A0%84%EB%9E%B5/)): Korean prep blogs emphasise (1) marking word stress in a dictionary for every new word, (2) shadowing for rhythm rather than for accent, (3) deliberately practising the /æ/–/e/ and /iː/–/ɪ/ minimal pairs, and (4) drilling final consonants without an epenthetic vowel.

### Chinese (中文)

- **Final-consonant deletion or schwa insertion**: Mandarin syllables end in vowel, /n/, or /ŋ/. Result: *wood*→/wʊdə/ or *glass*→*gla*; "dark L" /ɫ/ retracted to /u/ or /w/, *ball*→*bau* ([FluentU — 10 most common pronunciation mistakes for Chinese speakers](https://www.fluentu.com/blog/english/english-pronunciation-for-chinese-speakers/), [callearning.com](https://www.callearning.com/blog/2013/12/pronunciation-errors-for-chinese-speakers/)).
- **Tone interference**: Mandarin uses lexical pitch on each syllable; speakers transfer this onto English so each *word* carries pitch movement, blocking the sentence-level intonation contour. This is the dominant Band-6-vs-7 cue for Mandarin candidates ([blog.talk.edu — Top 6 errors Chinese speakers](https://blog.talk.edu/grammar/the-top-6-english-pronunciation-errors-made-by-native-chinese-speakers/)).
- **/v/–/w/**, **/n/–/l/** (regional, especially southern), **/θ ð/ → /s z/ or /f v/** ([magoosh.com — pronunciation resources for Chinese speakers](https://magoosh.com/ielts/english-pronunciation-resources-native-chinese-speakers/)).
- **新东方 (Xinhua/Xindongfang) 评分维度** ([ielts.xdf.cn 雅思口语4个评分维度](https://ielts.xdf.cn/201706/10683720.html)): explicitly states Chinese candidates' strongest dimension is vocabulary and weakest are FC and PR; the most common deduction is "only word-level stress, no sentence-level stress."
- **沪江/雅思口语扣分潜规则** ([liuxue.hujiang.com 13个扣分潜规则](https://liuxue.hujiang.com/ielts/speaking/p367455/)): lists "speaking too fast without pauses" and "stressing every word equally" as the top pronunciation-related deductions.
- **TestDaily 厚朴优学** ([testdaily.cn 雅思口语评分标准](https://www.testdaily.cn/ielts-speak-20201020/)) walks through descriptor language band-by-band, noting that Chinese candidates are typically stuck at Band 6 on PR because they treat each Mandarin character as a stressed unit.

### Vietnamese

- **Final-consonant omission** is the #1 issue: Vietnamese codas are limited and unreleased, so *fine* loses /n/, *five* loses /v/, *fights* loses /t/, *cold*→*co*, *right*→*ri* ([zim.vn — common Vietnamese pronunciation errors (Phần 1)](https://zim.vn/nhung-loi-phat-am-pho-bien-nhat-trong-bai-thi-ielts-speaking-phan-1), [pasal.edu.vn — under-6.0 pronunciation errors](https://pasal.edu.vn/cac-loi-phat-am-trong-ielts-speaking-cua-nguoi-trinh-do-duoi-60-n1523.html)).
- **/θ ð/ → /t d s z/**, **/dʒ/ → /z/ or /d/**, **/j/ dropped** (*year*→*ear*) ([zim.vn Phần 1](https://zim.vn/nhung-loi-phat-am-pho-bien-nhat-trong-bai-thi-ielts-speaking-phan-1)).
- **Mis-syllabification**: monosyllables split into two — *green*→*gờ-rin* ([ieltsmentor.edu.vn — Vietnamese pronunciation errors](https://ieltsmentor.edu.vn/blog/cam-nang-speaking/ielts-speaking-cac-loi-phat-am-phan-lon-nguoi-viet-deu-mac.html)).
- **Plural -s** mis-rule: Vietnamese candidates often default to /s/ for all endings, missing /z/ and /ɪz/ ([onthiielts.com.vn — top 3 errors](https://onthiielts.com.vn/loi-phat-am-tieng-anh-cua-nguoi-viet/)).
- **Tone transfer**: Vietnamese has 6 lexical tones; speakers sometimes give English words a tonal contour that fragments sentence intonation ([ccsenet.org — Optimality Theory in Vietnamese L2 English](https://ccsenet.org/journal/index.php/elt/article/download/0/0/50828/55087)).
- **ZIM Academy** (Vietnam's largest IELTS publisher) explicitly states linking ("nối âm") is the dominant Band-6→7 marker for Vietnamese candidates ([zim.vn — under-6.0 errors](https://zim.vn/cac-loi-phat-am-o-nguoi-hoc-ielts-trinh-do-duoi-6-0)).

### Japanese

- **R/L collapse to flap ɾ**: only one liquid in Japanese (ら-row); both English /r/ and /l/ realised as a tap ([hpu.edu — phonological analysis of Japanese English](https://www.hpu.edu/research-publications/tesol-working-papers/2014/new-with-metadata/tesol-wps-2014-lesley.pdf)).
- **CV epenthesis ("katakana English")**: every English consonant gets a moraic vowel attached — *strike*→/sɯ.toɾai.kɯ/, *first*→/ɸaː.sɯ.to/. This inflates syllable count and demolishes rhythm ([tofugu.com — katakanization rules](https://www.tofugu.com/japanese/katakanization/)).
- **/v/→/b/** (no /v/ in native Japanese), **/θ ð/→/s z/**, **/f/→/ɸ/ or /h/**, **/si/→/ʃi/** (*see*→*shee*) ([tefl.net — Japanese English pronunciation changes](https://www.tefl.net/elt/articles/home-abroad/japanese-english-pronunciation-changes/)).
- **Mora-timed rhythm**: Japanese is mora-timed (each mora equal), pulling speakers further from English stress-timing than even syllable-timed L1s.

### Spanish

- **No schwa** — Spanish has 5 clear vowels, no reduction; speakers articulate every vowel fully, killing weak forms (*for example* → /fɔɾ eksampl/ instead of /fər ɪɡˈzɑːmpl/) ([pronunciationstudio.com — Spanish speakers' errors](https://pronunciationstudio.com/spanish-speakers-english-pronunciation-errors/), [tefltrainer.com](https://tefltrainer.com/pronunciation/pronunciation-problems-for-spanish-learners-of-english/)).
- **/ɪ iː/, /ʊ uː/, /æ e/ mergers**; **/b v/ confusion** (Spanish /b/ and /v/ are allophones).
- **Prothetic /e/ before s+C onsets**: *Spain*→*Espain*, *student*→*estudent* ([languagelearnershub.com — practical guide](https://languagelearnershub.com/blog/english-pronunciation/)).
- **Voiceless stops un-aspirated**: *pat*/*bat*, *tin*/*din* can collapse word-initially because Spanish /p t k/ lack the English aspiration that listeners use as a voicing cue.

### Arabic

- **No /p/** — substituted with /b/, so *park*/*bark* collapse; the puff-of-air test fails ([blog.talk.edu — Arabic speakers' problems](https://blog.talk.edu/learn-english/common-pronunciation-problems-arabic-speakers/), [boldvoice.com — Arabic speakers](https://boldvoice.com/blog/english-pronunciation-arabic-speakers)).
- **/v/ confused with /f/** (no /v/ in Modern Standard Arabic): *very*→*ferry*.
- **Vowel inventory mismatch**: MSA has 3 short + 3 long vowels (/a i u/ × length); English 12 monophthongs collapse onto these. Particular trouble: /ɒ ʌ/ (*lock*/*luck*), /eɪ əʊ/ shortened to /e ɒ/ ([britishaccentacademy.com — Arabic speakers' errors](https://www.britishaccentacademy.com/most-common-english-pronunciation-errors-made-by-arabic-speakers/)).
- **Cluster-breaking via vowel insertion**: *spring*→*sipring*, *children*→*chiledren* ([researchgate.net — pronunciation difficulties for Arab learners](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/309290691_PRONUNCIATION_DIFFICULTIES_FOR_ARAB_LEARNERS_OF_ENGLISH)).
- **Dark-L and /ŋ/** often back-realised; **/r/** trilled or tapped.

### Persian (Farsi) — Iran

- **/w/→/v/** (no /w/ in Persian): *wonder*→*vonder*, *we*→*ve* ([boldvoice.com — English pronunciation for Persian speakers](https://boldvoice.com/blog/english-pronunciation-persian-speakers), [speechactive.com — Farsi tips](https://www.speechactive.com/our-top-5-english-pronunciation-tips-for-farsi-speakers/)).
- **/θ ð/→/t d/ or /s z/**, **/ŋ/→/ng/** (cluster realised) ([academia.edu — Iranian EFL pronunciation errors survey](https://www.academia.edu/81520546/A_Survey_on_Several_Potentially_Problematic_Areas_of_Pronunciation_for_Iranian_Efl_Learners)).
- **Prothetic vowel before s+C**: like Spanish, *school*→*eskool*, *sport*→*esport*.
- **Vowel substitutions**: /æ/→/e/, /ɑː/→/ɔː/, /ʊ/→/uː/, /ɪə/→/eə/, /aɪ/→/ɔɪ/ ([researchgate.net — Iranian EFL pronunciation errors analysis](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/260602889_An_Analysis_of_Iranian_EFL_Learners'_Pronunciation_Errors)).
- **IDP Iran's official 5 tips** ([ielts.idp.com/iran 5 tips](https://ielts.idp.com/iran/prepare/article-5-tips-for-pronunciation-ielts-speaking)): practice sounds missing from L1; karaoke for intonation; drill with diagrams of articulatory muscles; repeat for muscle memory; speak with native speakers outdoors.

### Hindi / Urdu — India, Pakistan

- **/w v/ collapse to bilabial /ʋ/**: *wine*/*vine* indistinguishable to many listeners ([hadarshemesh.com — English for Urdu/Hindi speakers](https://hadarshemesh.com/magazine/english-for-urdu-and-hindi-speakers/)).
- **Retroflex /ʈ ɖ/ for English alveolar /t d/**: gives the characteristic "Indian English" timbre but is *intelligible* — does not pull bands unless paired with other features ([en.wikipedia.org — Hindustani phonology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hindustani_phonology)).
- **No aspiration distinction = no /ph/ /th/ /kh/ contrast** in initial position the way English uses it; voicing perception in plosives can be unreliable for listeners.
- **Syllable-timing**: South Asian English is famously syllable-timed; vowels are not reduced, so weak forms are weak or absent → flat rhythm.
- **/θ ð/ → /t̪ d̪/** (dental stops) — usually intelligible.
- Indian-IELTS prep emphasises **stress placement** as the single biggest lift (e.g. coaching to say `de-VEL-op-ment` not `DE-vel-OP-ment`).

### Turkish

- **No /θ ð/, no /w/ vs /v/ contrast**, vowel-harmony habits push speakers toward articulating each vowel fully ([blog.talk.edu — Turkish speakers' errors](https://blog.talk.edu/learn-english/common-pronunciation-errors-turkish-speakers/), [boldvoice.com — Turkish speakers](https://boldvoice.com/blog/english-pronunciation-turkish-speakers)).
- **Diphthong simplification or doubling**: *pain* either flattened to /pen/ or over-pronounced as /pa.ɪn/.
- **Final-obstruent devoicing**: *dog*→*dok*, *bag*→*bak*.

### Russian / CIS

- **Word-final devoicing**: /d→t, g→k, z→s, v→f/ — *good*→*goot*, *advice* mis-stressed and devoiced ([pronunciationstudio.com — Russian speakers' errors](https://pronunciationstudio.com/russian-speakers-english-pronunciation-errors/), [engvid.com](https://www.engvid.com/english-pronunciation-for-russian-speakers/)).
- **Dark-L palatalised; clear-L palatalised before front vowels** → distinctive L-quality.
- **/w/→/v/**, **/θ ð/→/s z/**, **/ŋ/→/ng/**.
- **Heavy consonant articulation**, vowel reduction reasonable (Russian *has* unstressed vowel reduction, which is an advantage for English schwa).

### Indonesian / Malay

- **Phonemes absent**: /θ ð/, /ʃ ʒ/, /v/, sometimes /z/. Substitutions: *think*→*tink*, *very*→*fery* or *wery* ([globalenglish.co.id — 6 common pronunciation errors](https://globalenglish.co.id/6-kesalahan-umum-pelafalan-kosakata-bahasa-inggris/), [zenius.net — pronunciation basics](https://www.zenius.net/blog/belajar-pronunciation-bahasa-inggris/)).
- **No diphthongs**: /eɪ əʊ aɪ aʊ ɔɪ/ tend to be monophthongised.
- **Spelling-to-sound** is direct in Indonesian, so candidates pronounce English the way it's spelled (*vehicle*→/ve.hi.kel/ instead of /ˈviːəkl/) — [IDP Indonesia common mistakes](https://ielts.idp.com/indonesia/about/news-and-articles/article-common-mistakes-in-ielts-speaking-test-you-should-avoid).
- **Syllable-timed**, each syllable roughly equal.

For a transcript-only LLM judge these tools are reference-only; the judge cannot run acoustic models, but the *error catalogues* above are usable to predict likely phonetic errors from spelling-only hints in a transcript (e.g., a Korean candidate writing "berry" where context demands "very" is a hint of F→B transfer).

## Practical signals for an LLM judge

When the judge has only a transcript (no audio), use these operationalisable rules:

1. **Use the descriptor anchor phrases verbatim** when justifying a band. Band 6 ↔ "range of features with mixed control"; Band 7 ↔ "all positive features of Band 6, *some* of Band 8"; Band 8 ↔ "wide range, sustained flexibly, easy to understand"; Band 9 ↔ "full range, precision and subtlety." Avoid inventing language outside the official set.
2. **Without audio, do not score pronunciation in isolation higher than 7.** A transcript cannot verify a "wide range of pronunciation features." Cap pronunciation at 7 unless transcribed prosodic cues (caps for stress, ellipses for pauses) explicitly show stress, weak forms, chunking, or contrastive intonation.
3. **Treat repeated misspellings of common words in the transcript as L1-transfer evidence.** *berry/very, sink/think, ship/sheep, light/right, este/study, tank/thank* — when a transcript contains these substitutions in a way that suggests the candidate said them, infer that the candidate was *understood despite* the substitution → Band 6 territory at best.
4. **Flag flat-rhythm cues**: if the transcript shows every content word lexically full (no contractions, no `wanna`, `gonna`, `kinda`, `sorta`, no `dunno`), and no marked pauses, this is consistent with syllable-timed rhythm and weak forms missing — a Band 6 ceiling marker on prosody.
5. **Reward chunking signals**: commas placed at semantic boundaries, em-dashes, and explicit `[pause]` annotations correlate with Band 7+ chunking. Long run-on sentences without breaks correlate with Band 6 chunking.
6. **Reward contrastive-stress markers**: ALL-CAPS, italics, or `*emphasis*` markup on a content word in a transcript suggests the candidate used sentence-level stress to mark contrast — a Band 7/8 feature.
7. **Penalise word-stress errors only when they are listed in stress-shift pairs that change part of speech.** E.g., if a candidate uses *PREsent* (noun) where they mean *preSENT* (verb), that is meaning-level interference; arbitrary syllable-stress drift on content words is a Band 6 marker but does not by itself drop below 6 unless it impedes understanding.
8. **L1-aware weighting**: apply different priors per likely L1. For Korean/Japanese candidates, weight L/R, F/V, final-consonant clusters, and rhythm. For Mandarin, weight final consonants and word-level pitch transfer. For Arabic, weight /p b/ and vowel quality. For Spanish, weight schwa absence and prothetic /e/. For Vietnamese, weight final-consonant deletion. For Hindi/Urdu, weight /w v/ and rhythm.
9. **Do not penalise accent itself.** If the transcript indicates a strong L1 accent but the meaning is consistently clear and the candidate uses contractions, weak forms, linking, and rhythmic content-word stress, that profile is consistent with Band 8.
10. **Use the "mother tongue effect" rule as the Band 7↔8 hinge.** Band 7 may show L1 features that *occasionally* require listener effort; Band 8 must show L1 features that have only *minimal* effect on intelligibility. Operational test: would a non-trained native English listener need to re-listen / back-translate to understand any utterance? If yes, ceiling is 7.
11. **Look for evidence of weak forms** in the transcript: contractions (*don't, won't, I'll, gonna, wanna, lemme*), reduced spelling (*kinda, sorta, cuppa*), absence of capitalised full-form function words. Presence of these is positive for Band 7+.
12. **Linking proxies**: phrases that the candidate clearly produced as one chunk and the transcriber wrote without word boundaries (*lotta, gotta, outta, kindof, sortof, whaddya, gonna*) are direct evidence of connected speech.
13. **Range vs. control distinction**: "uses some weak forms but only on common collocations" = Band 7 (some features); "uses weak forms, linking, contrastive stress, and chunking flexibly across all parts of the test" = Band 8 (wide range, sustained).
14. **Cap pronunciation at 6 when**: the transcript shows persistent meaning-changing mispronunciations (homophone collapses), or shows the examiner repeatedly asking "sorry?" or "could you repeat?" — direct intelligibility failure.
15. **Floor of 5/6 typical for Korean candidates without targeted prep** per multiple Korean cram-school analyses and Chinese cram-school estimates (China average 5.5 on Speaking, [xdf.cn](https://ielts.xdf.cn/201706/10683720.html)); use this only as a calibration prior, never as a justification.
