# How experienced tutors phrase speaking feedback (voice & structure for the LLM judge)

_Merged from codex + claude crawls on 2026-05-19T17:00Z._

## Summary

This topic captures both **what** experienced IELTS Speaking feedback covers (a score or estimate, an overall judgement, criterion-by-criterion strengths and weaknesses, concrete evidence from the answer, and next-step actions) and the **register, structure, and recurring lexical chunks** that real examiners and tutors use when writing those reports (https://oneielts.com/ielts-speaking/evaluation-sample; https://www.edmclass.com/ielts/product/13; https://ielts.ch/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/assessing-IELTS-speaking.pdf). For an LLM judge the practical goal is to sound like a Cambridge-trained examiner: descriptive, evidence-led, aligned to Fluency and Coherence, Lexical Resource, Grammatical Range and Accuracy, and Pronunciation, hedged with "estimated," and careful not to over-score non-linguistic factors such as confidence, eye contact, or interesting ideas (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking; https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-fluency-coherence). Strong feedback explains what held the score down, what already works, and what would move the candidate toward the next band; it quotes the transcript ("Q9 'all over world'"), pivots on `but/however/though` from strength to limitation, and avoids vague AI-style advice such as "use better vocabulary" or motivational closers such as "Great job!" (https://oneielts.com/ielts-speaking/evaluation-sample; https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf; https://www.ielts-blog.com/ielts-speaking-reports/alina-speaking-report-band-5.pdf).

## Authoritative material

### Official scoring frame

- IELTS states that certificated examiners assess the whole Speaking performance across four criteria: Fluency and Coherence, Lexical Resource, Grammatical Range and Accuracy, and Pronunciation (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking).
- IELTS explains Fluency and Coherence as speaking at normal speed without too much hesitation, ordering ideas logically, and using cohesive devices such as linking words, pronouns and conjunctions so the answer is not hard to follow (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking).
- IELTS explains Lexical Resource as range, accuracy and appropriacy of vocabulary, plus the ability to paraphrase around vocabulary gaps (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking).
- IELTS explains Grammatical Range and Accuracy as the range and accuracy of grammar used, so feedback should mention both variety and control rather than only counting errors (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking).
- IELTS explains Pronunciation as being understood without too much effort, so feedback should focus on intelligibility, stress, intonation, rhythm and sound clarity rather than whether the candidate has a British, American, or local accent (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking; https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-does-ielts-speaking-assess-your-accent).

### Public band descriptors — the canonical phrasing pool

The wording examiners reuse most often is preserved verbatim in the published UCLES Band Descriptors. The four-criterion frame is fixed; examiners' written comments are built almost entirely by paraphrasing or quoting the descriptor table (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf; https://ielts.ch/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/assessing-IELTS-speaking.pdf).

- **Fluency & Coherence (Band 9 → 6):** "speaks fluently with only rare repetition or self-correction; any hesitation is content-related rather than to find words or grammar" / "speaks fluently with only occasional repetition or self-correction; hesitation is usually content-related and only rarely to search for language" / "speaks at length without noticeable effort or loss of coherence; may demonstrate language-related hesitation at times, or some repetition and/or self-correction" / "is willing to speak at length, though may lose coherence at times due to occasional repetition, self-correction or hesitation; uses a range of connectives and discourse markers but not always appropriately" (https://ielts.ch/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/assessing-IELTS-speaking.pdf).
- **Lexical Resource (Band 7 boilerplate):** "uses vocabulary resource flexibly to discuss a variety of topics; uses some less common and idiomatic vocabulary and shows some awareness of style and collocation, with some inappropriate choices; uses paraphrase effectively" (same source). Band 8 uses a wide resource flexibly with precise meaning; Band 6 can discuss topics at length but vocabulary may be inappropriate while meaning remains clear; Band 5 is sufficient but limited in flexibility (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).
- **Grammar (Band 7):** "uses a range of complex structures with some flexibility; frequently produces error-free sentences, though some grammatical mistakes persist" (https://ielts.ch/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/assessing-IELTS-speaking.pdf). Band 8 has a wide range used flexibly with a majority of error-free sentences; Band 6 mixes short and complex sentence forms with frequent errors in complex structures that rarely block communication; Band 5 controls basic forms but complex attempts nearly always contain errors (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).
- **Pronunciation (Band 7):** "shows all the positive features of Band 6 and some, but not all, of the positive features of Band 8" — note the recursive comparative phrasing, which examiners reproduce in candidate reports. Band 8 can be easily understood throughout with flexible stress and intonation across long utterances; Band 6 is generally understood without much effort but rhythm, stress-timing, intonation or word/phoneme errors may be variable; Band 4 includes frequent mispronunciation causing lack of clarity (https://ielts.ch/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/assessing-IELTS-speaking.pdf; https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).

Cambridge's teacher-training pack tells examiners-in-training to "**highlight words which make one band different from another**" and then "**note down examples of performance in terms of the listed criteria**" before writing feedback — i.e. evidence-first, prose second. The pack also reminds teachers that band predictions "won't necessarily give … an accurate prediction" because nerves intrude, which is why real examiner feedback hedges with "**estimated** IELTS Speaking band" rather than a definite number (https://ielts.ch/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/assessing-IELTS-speaking.pdf).

### IDP IELTS — descriptor restated for candidates

IDP rewrites the Band 8 descriptor as "speakers… demonstrate fluent delivery with minimal hesitation, speaking fluently and developing topics at length without needing prompts" — note the substitution of "minimal hesitation" for the official "occasional repetition," which propagates into tutor feedback worldwide (https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-understanding-the-ielts-speaking-band-descriptors).

### What official teaching guidance implies for feedback voice

- British Council teacher guidance says Speaking is assessed on language criteria and not on appearance, body language or creativity of ideas, so feedback should not tell a candidate that a boring opinion, weak eye contact or unoriginal content caused a lower score unless it directly reduced language evidence (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-fluency-coherence).
- British Council teacher guidance warns against memorising large chunks before the test, so feedback should avoid pushing candidates toward rehearsed scripts and instead recommend flexible answer development, signposting, reasons and examples (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-fluency-coherence).
- British Council describes Fluency and Coherence as speaking at length, organising ideas logically, using signposting, and expressing/justifying/discussing opinions without long pauses or repetition; this supports feedback that says what the candidate did in Part 1, Part 2 and Part 3 rather than a single generic fluency label (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-fluency-coherence).
- Cambridge English's IELTS test-format page repeats the same four criteria and definitions, which supports using the official labels exactly rather than renaming criteria as "confidence", "ideas", "delivery" or "speaking skill" (https://www.cambridgeenglish.org/exams-and-tests/ielts/test-format/).
- Cambridge teacher material gives example comments mapped to criteria, such as comments about range of structures for grammar, appropriate vocabulary range for lexical resource, stress and intonation for pronunciation, and little hesitation plus linked ideas for fluency/coherence; this is a compact model for evidence-tagged feedback (https://assets.cambridgeenglish.org/webinars/3y06-ielts-speaking-002.pdf).
- Cambridge ELT guidance on pronunciation says the band descriptors assess range of pronunciation features, control of those features, and intelligibility, so pronunciation feedback should name the feature and its communication effect, not simply say "pronunciation needs work" (https://www.cambridge.org/elt/blog/2019/03/27/ielts-speaking-improving-pronunication/).
- IELTS.org's 2026 automarking article says automated speaking assessment is harder than writing because learner speech can be heavily accented and interaction/nuance matter, so an LLM judge should be explicit about evidence limits, especially if it sees only a transcript and not audio (https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/what-automarking-means-for-language-test-validity-and-integrity).
- IELTS.org's 2026 conversational-AI article says AI examiners may handle clarification and dialogue but can be difficult to standardise and may "help" too much, so feedback generated by an LLM should avoid coaching during assessment and should keep the scoring rationale separate from improvement advice (https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/exploring-the-potential-of-conversational-ai-in-speaking-assessment).

### Official IELTS examiner clips

The IELTS organisation hosts examiner-commented speaking videos. The phrasing pattern in those clips is consistent: a one-sentence overall verdict, then four short paragraphs (one per criterion) where each paragraph starts with the *strength* and pivots on "**however**", "**but**", or "**though**" to the limitation (https://www.ielts.org/for-organisations/speaking-clips-examiner-comments).

### Worked verbatim examples from real examiner reports

These are the gold standard for voice and structure. The text below is reproduced verbatim from full PDF reports examiners returned to candidates.

#### "Misato" — Band 6 overall (Crossroads Languages)

> "Misato speaks at length about the topics and is quite fluent. Her speech is not slow and she does not usually correct herself, but she hesitates quite a lot while she thinks about words or grammar (um; er; and then)."
> "She sometimes manages to link and organise her ideas well, but not always (The weather is nice and I could go anywhere else). She uses good discourse markers (well; actually; to be honest; especially) and basic linking words (connectives) (so; but; when), but these are sometimes inaccurate (because of we have children/because we have children…)."
> "She can only get a Band 6 for Fluency and Coherence because of her hesitation and her occasional difficulty in linking ideas."
> "…To get a higher band she needs to hesitate less and to have more range and accuracy in vocabulary and grammar."

Voice markers: third-person, present tense, *bracketed* in-line examples (`(um; er; and then)`), *bracketed corrections* (`because of we have children/because we have children`), and the closing "she can only get a Band X because…" formula (https://www.crossroadslanguages.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/02/Examiners-comments-of-IELTS-sample-speaking.pdf).

#### "Alina" — Band 5 (IELTS-Blog)

> "You speak coherently and your speech flows well, though there is some over-repetition of expressions and fillers 'I don't know', 'it's about', etc."
> "You use an appropriate range of vocabulary … though there is some lack of precision when it comes to giving more detail. Q9 'not very useful', Q10 'not so huge'."
> "Q4 'family banding' should be 'family bonding'."
> "You tend to overuse 'a lot of' and 'it's about' — find alternatives like 'several' 'a large number' and 'it's to do with', 'it relates to', 'what I mean by this is'."
> Pronunciation comment: "Two main areas to work on: 1. Long vowels and diphthongs … pronounce these longer (Q5 'sweater' you pronounced as 'sweeter'). 2. Don't raise intonation after every clause."

Voice markers: **second-person** ("you"), question-number anchoring (`Q5`), `X should be Y` correction frame, and a closing two-block "Well done on / Suggestions for improvement" bulleted summary (https://www.ielts-blog.com/ielts-speaking-reports/alina-speaking-report-band-5.pdf).

#### "Rafael" — Band 8 (IELTS-Blog)

Examiner uses a three-column layout per criterion: **Well done / Errors / To improve**, then a side-by-side "Your estimated score: Band 8 | To get Band 9 (must get all points)" with bulleted descriptor checkboxes.

> Fluency: "Your speech is very fluent and coherent and all topics are well developed. At first you tended to overuse 'well' in the early questions. Partial repetition or paraphrase of the question might be appropriate in places, but make sure it continues to sound natural and **don't put in connecting phrases just for the sake of it.**"
> Lexis: "'the food was exactly as I asked them to do' — one of the very few expressions that does not sound like native English. The correct preposition/collocation should be 'make' not 'do' but other expressions would sound better — 'exactly as I had requested', 'exactly as I'd asked for' etc."
> Grammar: "Your grammar was very good in terms of range and accuracy, but you should pay close attention to preposition use. You could try recording yourself and I bet you would even catch your own small mistakes."
> Pronunciation: "With 't's push your tongue more firmly against the roof of your mouth and spit it out … you tend to slur some syllables especially those with 'i' or 't'."

Voice markers: **multiple-alternative rewrites** (`'X' or 'Y' or 'Z'`), articulatory-phonetics micro-coaching ("push your tongue more firmly"), and an offered upgrade path tied to the next-band descriptor (https://www.ielts-blog.com/ielts-speaking-reports/rafael-speaking-report-band-8.pdf).

#### "Joanne" — Band 8.5 (IELTS-Blog)

> Fluency note: "she spoke very fast and frenetically, and she only managed around 38 seconds of speaking, when she needed to produce between 1 to 2 minutes." (Pacing called out by absolute number, not adjectives.)
> Pronunciation note: "Joanne had no discernable Swedish accent and even had a slight U.S. accent."
> Lexis: "only rare instances of her using a mildly awkward expression" and "one slightly awkward collocation, 'so much effort.'"

(https://www.ielts-blog.com/ielts-preparation-tips/speaking-tips/ielts-speaking-band-8-5-full-test-with-examiners-feedback/)

#### IELTS-Blog Band 7 sample (Aleks)

IELTS-Blog's Band 7 sample feedback gives separate criterion scores and explains them with compact evidence: the candidate was fluent and coherent, answered capably, did not often use longer developed sentences, had good vocabulary access, made some grammar/collocation errors, and had a slight accent that did not affect communication. The phrasing pattern "X was good, but Y limited the score" balances credit and constraint together (https://www.ielts-blog.com/ielts-preparation-tips/speaking-tips/ielts-speaking-band-7-full-test-with-examiners-feedback-aleks/).

#### IELTS Assistance summary across all bands

Aggregated by ieltsassistance.co.uk:

- Band 8: "speaks fluently and is able to give quite long and detailed responses; **hesitation is usually content-related**; vocabulary resource is wide; uses a wide range of structures with a high level of accuracy; only occasional minor errors."
- Band 7: "can speak at length without noticeable effort or loss of coherence; language-related hesitation, repetition and self correction; good vocabulary and shows some awareness of style; sometimes makes the wrong word choices; good grammatical control and produces many error-free sentences; some problems with tenses and articles."
- Band 6: "willing to give extended responses but there is occasional loss [of coherence]; **overuses certain fillers**; grammatical control of more complex structures is variable; intonation is somewhat flat; mechanical rhythm."
- Band 5: "able to keep going and is willing to give long answers; coherence is occasionally lost through hesitation; vocabulary is the strongest feature of her performance; errors do not interfere with communication; control of verb tenses is variable; has recurring difficulty with subject/verb agreement."
- Band 3.5: "unable to keep going without noticeable pauses; speech is slow with frequent repetition; some breakdowns in coherence; grammatical control is weak; errors frequent; understanding requires considerable effort."

(https://ieltsassistance.co.uk/ielts-speaking-test/examples-with-examiners-comments/)

### Other examiner-style sample feedback patterns

- OneIELTS publishes a public speaking evaluation sample with the structure `overall score`, short examiner feedback, `To aim higher` action bullets, then a per-criterion score report; per-criterion sections follow `Strengths`, `Mistakes/Issues`, and `Improvements`, which forces evidence and advice to stay attached to the correct criterion. The sample cites concrete answer evidence including repeated wording, limited connectors, a subject-verb agreement error, an article error, and a pronunciation confusion between similar words (https://oneielts.com/ielts-speaking/evaluation-sample).
- IELTS Buddy's speaking correction page says candidates submit an audio Part 2-style response plus transcript and receive analysis based on the official criteria; its sample feedback PDF describes a major weakness as Fluency and Coherence and gives category comments for grammar, vocabulary and pronunciation (https://www.ieltsbuddy.com/ielts-speaking-correction-service.html; https://www.ieltsbuddy.com/support-files/ielts-speaking-correction-service-sample.pdf).
- IELTS-Academic's public feedback sheet uses checklist-style prompts under Fluency and Coherence, Lexical Resource, Grammar and Pronunciation, showing that many teachers operationalise feedback as observable yes/no or weak/strong items (https://ielts-academic.com/2016/01/01/ielts-speaking-feedback-sheet/).

### Ben Teaches English — five-bucket tutor template

UK tutor Ben's published template uses **five sub-criteria** (one beyond the four) and a one-line verdict for each: Communicative Effectiveness ("Very well done. I know the last section of the exam was difficult, but your hesitation was not a problem."), Pronunciation ("Very good. There were some isolated problems (foot and cook), but overall your accent is consistent, controlled and easy to understand."), Accuracy ("Good. Relatively few errors and they did not inhibit communication."), Range ("Very good. An impressive display of grammatical structures and vocabulary."), then Band Score: "7.5–8". Note the **adjective-band ladder** Ben uses internally: *Excellent / Very good / Good / OK / Needs work* (http://benteachesenglish.com/do-a-speaking-test/sample-speaking-feedback/).

### My IELTS Classroom — ex-examiner cue-card feedback areas

Jessica (ex-examiner) commits her cue-card feedback to four diagnostic buckets that recur across submissions: "problems with past tenses when describing a past event", "high frequency of grammar errors", "giving examples to support adjectives when describing a person", "problems pronouncing single words". The framing is *patterns across the population* rather than one-off corrections — useful when the judge needs to make general comments (https://blog.myieltsclassroom.com/ielts-cue-card-feedback/).

### Academic research on tutor feedback voice

A 2023 study on IELTS speaking teachers' attitudes (Francis Academic Press) found OCF (oral corrective feedback) is "**guided by consideration for students' feelings**" — teachers temper accuracy-driven corrections to protect motivation. The study also flags that most IELTS speaking teachers "**lack systematic training and knowledge**" on which corrective strategy to apply, which is why feedback voice varies far more across tutors than across examiners. Useful contrast: examiner-style feedback should be **dispassionate**, but tutor-style feedback usually **softens** the same content (https://francis-press.com/papers/11190).

## Country-specific prep material

### Korea

- Korean provider edm IELTS sells a Writing/Speaking correction product that promises a score using the same assessment criteria as the real test, then teacher-written feedback; its speaking sample layout lists Fluency, Lexical Resources, Grammatical Range and Accuracy, Pronunciation and Band Score, mirroring the official criterion breakdown (https://www.edmclass.com/ielts/product/13).
- edm distinguishes `Basic` correction (teacher writes feedback on a submitted task) from `Premium` correction (personalised video feedback lecture); this shows a common Korean cram-school feedback model of score first, then direct correction, then personalised strategy. Basic speaking feedback includes typed feedback documents for recorded answers and correction/evaluation for grammar, expression and pronunciation, so an LLM judge should produce written evidence comments rather than only a numerical band (https://www.edmclass.com/ielts/product/13).
- Korean online school Skybel says IELTS Speaking lessons use real-test-like practice, immediate oral/typed correction, band-score-based level feedback, and a post-class report; classes focus on answer structure, grammar, vocabulary and fluency in a balanced way, and student testimonials mention predicted scores plus lists of strengths and weaknesses (https://www.skybel.co.kr/sub/edpro_02.php).
- Korean cram-school reviews (시원스쿨랩, 슈퍼잉글리쉬, 파고다) describe their speaking feedback as "**스카이프로 수업 중에 채팅을 통해 선생님과 계속 피드백을 주고 받으며, 틀렸던 문구나 어색한 문장을 올바른 문장으로 만들어서 보여준다**" — the tutor pastes the candidate's wrong sentence in chat, then pastes a corrected version next to it. Korean tutors heavily weight the recording + 20× shadowing loop ("음독하면서 20번 반복"), so feedback is typically a script of clean rewrites rather than a discursive comment (https://m.super-english.co.kr/kwa-9550780-536; https://www.gohackers.com/?c=ielts/ielts_info2/ielts_method&type=url&uid=580926).

### China

- IDP China hosts a Chinese public IELTS Speaking band descriptor PDF, so Chinese prep feedback should still map candidate performance to the same global four criteria rather than local-only categories (https://www.idp.cn/special/subject/IELTS/pdf/kscj_f2.pdf).
- New Channel Wuhan explains that the four Speaking dimensions are equally weighted and warns against blind practice without understanding what examiners score; it emphasises balance across criteria and says candidates should not rely on one strong dimension to compensate for weak dimensions — supporting feedback that avoids saying "good vocabulary means overall high score" when grammar or pronunciation evidence is weak (https://wh.xhd.cn/info/ieltsSpeak/1059815.html).
- Gaodun's Chinese IELTS guide describes Band 7-style speaking as natural expression with occasional pauses that do not affect understanding, and it recommends connecting words plus supporting detail; this supports feedback that separates acceptable thinking pauses from answer-development gaps (https://www.gaodun.com/ielts/1629843.html).
- Talk915 reproduces lower-band descriptors in Chinese, including reliance on repetition/self-correction/slow speech and overuse of discourse markers at Band 5, so Chinese feedback often names repetition, slow delivery and marker overuse as fluency problems rather than simply saying "not fluent" (https://www.talk915.com/web/article-details/328).
- New Oriental Online explains pronunciation feedback in terms of clarity and natural intonation rather than requiring British or American accent (https://ielts.koolearn.com/20250710/866309.html).
- Chinese IELTS prep on Zhihu (知乎) and 新东方 (New Oriental) emphasises that students must "**研究考官评语**" (study the examiner's comments) for each official sample at every band from 3.5 to 9. Chinese tutor reports therefore tend to quote the public descriptors verbatim in English inside an otherwise Mandarin commentary. A typical 老烤鸭 (Lao Kaoya) Part 1 Teachers model answer ends with "**口语提分点** (scoring point): use idiomatic chunks like 'a tough cookie', 'on the same wavelength'." The expectation is that feedback names the *exact* idiom or collocation that would lift the score, not generic "use more idioms" (https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/197405982; http://www.laokaoya.com/55958.html).

### Vietnam

- ZIM's Vietnamese guide explains that IELTS Speaking has four criteria; Fluency and Coherence is described as speaking with normal continuity, rate and effort while linking ideas into coherent connected speech — so Vietnamese tutor feedback should talk about continuity, idea linkage and effort rather than only "smoothness" (https://zim.vn/tieu-chi-cham-diem-ielts-speaking-phan-1).
- ZIM says pronunciation feedback should pay attention to loud/clear articulation, word stress, sentence stress and final sounds where they distinguish meaning in context; this supports concrete notes such as "final consonants reduce clarity" rather than generic "work on pronunciation" (https://zim.vn/tieu-chi-cham-diem-ielts-speaking-phan-1).
- ZIM's Speaking checklist for Band 7+ is based on IELTS criteria and public descriptors, reflecting a Vietnamese prep habit of turning criterion descriptors into checklists that learners can self-monitor (https://zim.vn/checklist-bai-thi-ielts-speaking).
- ZIM's vocabulary preparation page lists flexible use across topics, idiomatic/less common expressions, collocation and paraphrase as Lexical Resource targets, so feedback should identify whether vocabulary was inaccurate, repetitive, non-collocational or insufficiently flexible (https://zim.vn/phuong-phap-chuan-bi-tu-vung-cho-ielts-speaking).
- IELTS LangGo warns that Part 2 can become unfocused and hard to follow even when grammar and vocabulary are good, which supports feedback that says "language is accurate, but topic development was difficult to follow" when coherence is the limiting factor (https://www.reddit.com/r/u_LanggoIELTS/comments/uxcmo6).
- ETP TESOL (Vietnamese teacher-training arm) prescribes a three-part **Sandwich Feedback** structure: *Lời khen (praise) → Góp ý mang tính xây dựng (constructive criticism) → Lời động viên (encouragement)*. Sample: praise = "You're using past time markers like 'yesterday' correctly," critique = "The verb 'have went' should be 'went'," encouragement = "Keep practicing; your sentence structure is improving." ETP also explicitly tells teachers to take notes silently ("không gián đoạn") rather than interrupt, and to align corrections with the student's individual goal (IELTS vs interview vs daily) (https://etp-tesol.edu.vn/4-chien-thuat-danh-gia-feedback-ky-nang-speaking/).

### Iran / Persian-language prep

- Andishe Parsian's Persian IELTS article explains Speaking scoring through the same four areas: fluency/coherence, vocabulary range, grammatical accuracy/range and pronunciation, and warns that vocabulary or grammar alone cannot produce a high speaking score (https://www.andisheparsian.com/article/171/index.html).
- IELTS2's Persian band-descriptor guide frames feedback around the four criteria and recommends complex/compound sentences for grammar range, but keeps the scoring labels tied to official terms (https://ielts2.com/understanding-the-ielts-speaking-band-descriptors/).
- Hafez Education's Persian speaking tips says each of the four criteria is worth 25% and uses an example criterion-score calculation, supporting feedback that gives separate criterion bands and explains how a weak criterion pulls down the final speaking band; it presents discourse markers as tools for connecting and organising speech, so Persian tutor-style feedback may say a candidate needs clearer signposting rather than simply "more fluency" (https://www.hafezedu.com/ielts-speaking-tricks/).
- LELB Society offers recorded IELTS interview samples and correction by an online English teaching specialist; this supports a feedback format that pairs the candidate's recorded performance with post-hoc correction and examiner-style comments (https://lelb.net/ielts-interview-sample-correction/).
- Persian-language prep (مکتب‌خونه, ielts2, filmozaban) builds its speaking feedback around **15-mock-test packages** ("تصحیح اسپیکینگ آیلتس (15 تایی)") with both a numeric band per criterion and an audio voice-note ("فیدبک صوتی") that walks through the recording. The blended written-plus-audio model means Iranian tutor reports are short on prose; what they ship is bullet errors plus a 5-minute voice walkthrough (https://maktabkhooneh.org/course/%D8%AA%D8%B5%D8%AD%DB%8C%D8%AD-%D8%A7%D8%B3%D9%BE%DB%8C%DA%A9%DB%8C%D9%86%DA%AF-%D8%A2%DB%8C%D9%84%D8%AA%D8%B3-15-%D8%AA%D8%A7%DB%8C%DB%8C-mk856/; https://filmozaban.com/ielts-speaking-mock-test/).

### India and Pakistan

- UpGrad India explains that IELTS Speaking receives a score for each of four criteria and that scores are averaged, so Indian prep feedback commonly reports a criterion table rather than only one band. Criteria are explained learner-side as smooth/logical speaking, vocabulary range and accuracy, varied/correct structures and clear stress/intonation (https://www.upgrad.com/study-abroad/exam/ielts/speaking-band-score/; https://www.upgrad.com/study-abroad/exam/ielts/speaking/).
- LeapScholar India says Fluency and Coherence covers smooth speech, limited pauses/hesitations and logical flow, while Lexical Resource covers vocabulary range and expression of abstract ideas; its Band 6/7/8 comparison supports next-band feedback such as "to move from 6 toward 7, reduce repeated hesitation and organise ideas more clearly" (https://leapscholar.com/exams/ielts/speaking-band-score).
- British Council Pakistan hosts an IELTS Speaking Fluency and Coherence preparation video page, so Pakistan-facing feedback should still use the same British Council/IELTS criteria and not local informal categories such as "confidence marks" (https://www.britishcouncil.pk/exam/ielts/prepare/videos/fluency-coherence).
- Preply's India IELTS tutor profile describes practical, results-oriented speaking lessons with structured answers, fluency feedback and step-by-step guidance, reflecting a common South Asian tutoring emphasis on clear answer structure plus confidence-building (https://preply.com/en/tutor/7668487).
- British Council India and Pakistan train tutors to give feedback "using public Band Descriptors" verbatim. Tutor reports follow a four-criterion grid with the descriptor quoted at the top of each box, then specific evidence underneath. The IELTS Coach product explicitly differentiates this from "language development" classes — the feedback is *score-oriented*, not pedagogic (https://englishonline.britishcouncil.org/ielts-coach/; https://www.britishcouncil.in/english/online/classes/ielts-preparation/faqs).

### Indonesia

- IDP Indonesia explains that Speaking tests the ability to communicate opinions and information on everyday topics, speak at length on a topic, and organise ideas coherently; Indonesian feedback should therefore mention whether the response met the part-specific communicative demand as well as the four criteria (https://ielts.idp.com/indonesia/prepare/article-ielts-speaking-tested-skills/en-gb).
- An Indonesian university paper on IELTS mock speaking errors uses the same four assessment areas and keyword labels, which suggests Indonesian academic-prep feedback also frames speaking weaknesses by criterion rather than by general "English ability" (https://icels.fkip.uniska-kediri.ac.id/index.php/icels/article/download/163/30/474).
- IDP's global pronunciation article is especially useful for Indonesian candidates because it separates accent from pronunciation; feedback should not tell Indonesian speakers to remove an Indonesian accent unless the accent reduces intelligibility (https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-does-ielts-speaking-assess-your-accent).

### Russia, CIS, and Kazakhstan

- IELTS-up, a Russian-language-oriented prep site, presents feedback-friendly definitions: FC is how clear and structured the speech is, LR is vocabulary, Pronunciation is how natural/easy the speech sounds, and GRA is grammar control; it also recommends linking ideas, extending answers, speaking smoothly and using pausing correctly (https://www.ielts-up.com/speaking/ielts-speaking-marking.html).
- IELTS Score 7's Russian guide explains that the final Speaking score is the average of four separate criteria, and describes Fluency and Coherence as how smoothly the candidate speaks and how logically ideas are connected (https://ieltsscore7.com/ru/guides/ielts-speaking-scoring-criteria/).
- MyIELTS.kz advertises a Kazakhstan IELTS Speaking intensive with Part 3 practice, answer analysis, improvement advice and examiner-expectation training, showing a local course structure of simulated response, analysis, and targeted improvement (https://myielts.kz/product/intensive-ielts-speaking).
- Russian prep (profi.ru, ielts-school.ru, anglofeel.ru) sells feedback on the strength of the tutor being a **бывший экзаменатор (former examiner)**. Reviews emphasise "**подробные письменные комментарии**" (detailed written comments) and feedback that gives "**не только обратную связь, но и рекомендации**" (not only feedback, but recommendations) — Russian-market expectation is prose feedback + actionable next steps, mirroring the Cambridge "To improve" column (https://profi.ru/repetitor/mezhdunarodnyie-ekzamenyi-i-testyi-angliyskiy/ielts/; https://ielts-school.ru/responses; https://anglofeel.ru/ielts/; https://highschool.su/blog/ielts-speaking).

### Philippines

- IDP Philippines explicitly tells candidates examiners are "highly trained to spot memorised answers" and the standout advice is to be "conversational." The recommended self-rescue phrases when stuck — "I'm so sorry, my mind just went blank" and "I'm not really sure, but if I had to say…" — are themselves a model of how Filipino tutors phrase example *student* output in feedback (they show, not tell). Filipino tutoring chains (e.g. 3D Academy Cebu) market "cultural sensitivity" and direct, non-face-threatening corrections (https://www.idp.com/philippines/blog/how-to-ace-your-ielts-speaking-test/; https://3d-universal.com/en/blogs/why-is-cebu-philippines-the-best-place-for-ielts-speaking-training.html; https://www.idp.com/philippines/blog/taking-feedback/).

### UK, Australia, and Canada — native-perspective material

- British Council UK teacher guidance provides the clearest native-perspective boundary for feedback: assess only the four language criteria, discourage memorised chunks, and teach answer development through signposting, reasons, examples and discussion (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-fluency-coherence).
- IELTS Advantage's UK accent article says accent does not matter as long as pronunciation is clear and accurate, so feedback should say "clarity was reduced by word stress/final sounds" rather than "your accent is too strong" (https://www.ieltsadvantage.com/2019/06/10/ielts-speaking-accent/).
- IDP, co-owner of IELTS and headquartered in Australia, says Speaking is marked on the same four criteria and explains accent is not graded if clarity remains high; this supports feedback that prioritises intelligibility over accent imitation (https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-does-ielts-speaking-assess-your-accent).
- IELTS Vancouver's Canadian prep article says Fluency and Coherence is one of the four marking criteria and that speaking clarity is linked with pronunciation features such as individual sounds, word stress, sentence stress, connected speech and intonation; this supports Canadian-style feedback that connects fluency and pronunciation to listener effort (https://ieltsvancouver.com/2023/01/20/what-is-fluency-and-coherence-in-ielts-speaking/).
- Native-market sources (Sydney English Teacher, IELTSTutors, IELTS Liz, How to do IELTS) tend toward an **informal-but-precise** voice. Liz's typical feedback line is "the examiner is not trying to undermine confidence" — defusing student anxiety while keeping the focus on descriptor evidence. How to do IELTS publishes feedback like: "'used to' incorrectly used ('I used to know him' should be 'I knew him' or 'He has been my friend')" — same `X should be Y or Z` frame as ielts-blog (https://www.sydneyenglishteacher.com.au/ielts-feedback; https://ieltstutors.org/; https://ieltsliz.com/why-the-ielts-speaking-examiner-stops-your-answer/; https://howtodoielts.com/ielts-speaking-test-and-lesson-band-6-6-5/; https://ieltsonlinetests.com/speaking-tips/ielts-speaking-test-part-1-analysis-band-65).

### Switzerland / Germany

- Continental European prep centres (e.g. ielts.ch) simply mirror the UCLES 2011 teacher pack, so feedback voice in this region is the most directly descriptor-aligned worldwide. Tutors there literally photocopy the band-descriptor table and write notes in the margin (https://ielts.ch/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/assessing-IELTS-speaking.pdf).

## Open-source tools and code

- SpeechSuper API Samples is a public GitHub repository under MIT license, with samples for a speech/pronunciation assessment API; its README says scripted assessment can return phoneme, syllable, word and sentence scores, fluency metrics, words-correct-per-minute, pause count and IELTS/PTE score estimates, and unscripted assessment can return overall, pronunciation, fluency, grammar, vocabulary and transcription outputs. At crawl time the repo showed 22 commits, 63 stars, 8 forks, MIT license and no published releases; no reliable last-updated date was exposed (https://github.com/speechsuper/SpeechSuper-API-Samples).
- Speechat is a GitHub Pages project for spoken language assessment that extracts prosody features, uses ASR transcripts, and sends text to a language model for pragmatic/sentiment/mechanical analysis; it links to `my-voice-analysis` and `myprosody` repositories, but the crawl did not verify their license or update dates. Its approach focuses on prosody, stress, intonation and structural speech features, so it may inspire pronunciation/fluency feature extraction but should not be treated as a validated IELTS scorer (https://shahabks.github.io/Speechat/).
- Speechace's commercial Fluency API estimates IELTS/PTE fluency and pronunciation scores and returns segment-level scores and metrics; it is an API rather than open-source code, useful as a reference for feedback granularity such as segment-by-segment weakness reporting (https://www.speechace.com/hello-fluency-api/).
- Speechful — AI IELTS grader producing four-criterion feedback; closed-source competitor reference for the *kind of* sentence-level commentary an LLM judge should produce (https://speechful.ai/).
- SmallTalk2Me — free IELTS speaking mock with AI band feedback; produces criterion-by-criterion JSON output you can inspect at submission time for voice patterns (https://smalltalk2.me/ielts).
- Cathoven AI IELTS Speaking — instant AI feedback; same descriptor-mirroring voice (https://www.cathoven.com/ielts/speaking/).
- GEL IELTS speaking feedback help docs describe the operational flow tutors use, which is roughly what a judge agent should emulate (https://help.gelielts.com/hc/en-us/articles/26442761179284-How-can-I-get-feedback-on-Speaking).
- No notable open-source repos (MIT/Apache) ship pre-trained IELTS-speaking judges that we found; the closest open work is in the corrective-feedback NLP space (e.g. ResearchGate reproductions of OCF studies), not band-scored speaking. IELTS.org warns that automarking must be at least as accurate as trained human examiners and that speaking is especially complex because of accent, interaction and nuance, so any open-source or API-derived features should be used as support evidence for an LLM judge, not as a replacement for descriptor-based reasoning (https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/what-automarking-means-for-language-test-validity-and-integrity).

## Practical signals for an LLM judge

Operational rules the judge should apply when scoring and writing feedback, ranked by directness of application to a transcript:

1. **Use the official four-criterion frame in fixed order: Fluency & Coherence → Lexical Resource → Grammatical Range & Accuracy → Pronunciation.** Never collapse, re-order, or invent labels such as "confidence", "ideas", "delivery", or "speaking skill" — examiners never re-order, and IELTS/Cambridge only score these four (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking; https://www.cambridgeenglish.org/exams-and-tests/ielts/test-format/).
2. **Default report shape:** estimated band → one-sentence overall verdict → one paragraph per criterion (strength clause, pivot on `but/however/though`, limitation with evidence) → "To get Band X" upgrade paragraph or bulleted next-band checklist. Mirrors OneIELTS, IELTS-Blog, Rafael/Alina, edm and UpGrad reporting (https://oneielts.com/ielts-speaking/evaluation-sample; https://www.ielts-blog.com/ielts-speaking-reports/rafael-speaking-report-band-8.pdf; https://www.edmclass.com/ielts/product/13; https://www.upgrad.com/study-abroad/exam/ielts/speaking-band-score/).
3. **Anchor every error claim to a specific transcript span** in the format `Q<n> '<verbatim candidate phrase>' should be '<correction>'` or `Q<n> '<verbatim>' → '<better>'`. For non-numbered transcripts, cite `Turn N`. No anonymous "you sometimes…" without an example — this is the single biggest divergence between examiner voice and ChatGPT voice (https://www.ielts-blog.com/ielts-speaking-reports/alina-speaking-report-band-5.pdf; https://oneielts.com/ielts-speaking/evaluation-sample).
4. **Pick a person and hold it.** Default to **second person ("you")** for candidate-facing reports (Alina, Rafael); switch to **third person** only when producing a report about the candidate for a teacher (Misato style) (https://www.ielts-blog.com/ielts-speaking-reports/alina-speaking-report-band-5.pdf; https://www.crossroadslanguages.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/02/Examiners-comments-of-IELTS-sample-speaking.pdf).
5. **Hedge band predictions with "estimated"** ("Your estimated score: Band 7") in the prose; commit to an integer only inside the JSON score field. UCLES warns predictions are unreliable under exam nerves (https://ielts.ch/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/assessing-IELTS-speaking.pdf).
6. **Quote fillers and discourse markers in brackets with semicolons** when calling them out: `(um; er; and then)` for hesitation, `(well; actually; to be honest; especially)` for good discourse marker use. This is the Cambridge house style (https://www.crossroadslanguages.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/02/Examiners-comments-of-IELTS-sample-speaking.pdf).
7. **Offer 2–3 alternatives, not a single rewrite,** when correcting a lexical inappropriacy: `"'very glad' is not quite right here. Better: 'very happy to' / 'more than happy to' / 'more than willing to'."` Mirrors Rafael's report and reads as expert range, not pedantry (https://www.ielts-blog.com/ielts-speaking-reports/rafael-speaking-report-band-8.pdf).
8. **Name the pattern, then the evidence,** using bracketed insertions to show where missing items belong: "She also often leaves out words such as articles and verbs (eg I'm currently [a] housewife; I used to work in [an] office)" (https://www.crossroadslanguages.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/02/Examiners-comments-of-IELTS-sample-speaking.pdf).
9. **For Lexical Resource,** prefer precise comments such as "vocabulary was topic-related but repetitive", "collocations were inaccurate", "paraphrase worked", or "less common items were attempted but not always natural" — these map directly to IELTS lexical descriptors. Name the *exact* idiom/collocation that would lift the score (Chinese-prep convention), not a generic "use more idioms" (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf; http://www.laokaoya.com/55958.html).
10. **For Grammar,** distinguish a candidate who uses mostly simple clauses accurately from one who attempts complex clauses but loses control, because official descriptors score variety and error impact together (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).
11. **For Pronunciation, give articulatory micro-instructions, not adjectives.** Rafael's report: "With 't's push your tongue more firmly against the roof of your mouth and spit it out." Never just "improve your /t/" and never write "change your accent" — write "the examiner can follow you easily" or "word stress/final consonants increase listener effort" (https://www.ielts-blog.com/ielts-speaking-reports/rafael-speaking-report-band-8.pdf; https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-does-ielts-speaking-assess-your-accent; https://www.ieltsadvantage.com/2019/06/10/ielts-speaking-accent/).
12. **For Fluency, cite measurable behaviour** (pause counts, length of Part 2 in seconds, repetition counts) wherever the transcript allows. Joanne's Band 8.5 report flags "she only managed around 38 seconds of speaking, when she needed to produce between 1 to 2 minutes" — a specific number, not "you spoke too briefly" (https://www.ielts-blog.com/ielts-preparation-tips/speaking-tips/ielts-speaking-band-8-5-full-test-with-examiners-feedback/).
13. **Cap praise at one clause per criterion**, and use Ben's adjective ladder for impressionistic comments: *Excellent / Very good / Good / OK / Needs work*. Avoid "amazing", "fantastic", "awesome" — these never appear in real examiner reports (http://benteachesenglish.com/do-a-speaking-test/sample-speaking-feedback/; https://www.ielts-blog.com/ielts-speaking-reports/alina-speaking-report-band-5.pdf).
14. **Forbid motivational closers** and meta-commentary. Real examiner reports never include "Keep up the great work!", "You're doing amazing!", "I believe in you!", and never ask questions back to the candidate. The closest acceptable form is Vietnam's neutral, observation-conditioned encouragement ("your sentence structure is improving"), not unconditional praise (https://etp-tesol.edu.vn/4-chien-thuat-danh-gia-feedback-ky-nang-speaking/).
15. **Use next-band language from the public descriptors** in the upgrade paragraph: for a Band 6-like answer, name what would move it toward Band 7 — longer turns without noticeable effort, fewer coherence losses from hesitation, more flexible discourse markers, more frequent error-free sentences, more controlled pronunciation features (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf; https://leapscholar.com/exams/ielts/speaking-band-score).
16. **Separate assessment from coaching.** First justify the band from the performance, then give next steps. IELTS.org warns AI assessment can distort standardisation if it "helps" too much during the interaction (https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/exploring-the-potential-of-conversational-ai-in-speaking-assessment).
17. **Cap advice at 2–3 priority actions** tied to the limiting criteria, following OneIELTS's `To aim higher` pattern and edm/Skybel's targeted-correction model — not an undifferentiated list (https://oneielts.com/ielts-speaking/evaluation-sample; https://www.edmclass.com/ielts/product/13; https://www.skybel.co.kr/sub/edpro_02.php).
18. **Do not reward memorised or ornate language for its own sake.** British Council warns against memorised chunks; IELTS descriptors reward appropriate, flexible and precise language. Prefer natural collocation and relevance over rehearsed "band 9 phrases". IDP Philippines explicitly warns examiners "are highly trained to spot memorised answers" (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-fluency-coherence; https://www.idp.com/philippines/blog/how-to-ace-your-ielts-speaking-test/).
19. **Make feedback part-aware when possible.** Part 1 may need fuller answers without becoming speeches; Part 2 needs sustained long-turn development; Part 3 needs more abstract discussion. British Council guidance separates the speaking parts and the skills they elicit (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-fluency-coherence).
20. **If the judge has only a diarized transcript and no audio,** state pronunciation evidence as limited unless the transcript includes phonetic notes, ASR uncertainty, repeated clarification, or explicit mishearing. IELTS.org notes speaking automarking is complicated by speech and interaction, and pronunciation is officially about comprehensible speech (https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/what-automarking-means-for-language-test-validity-and-integrity; https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking).
21. **Apply culturally neutral scoring.** Korean, Chinese, Vietnamese, Persian, Indian, Pakistani, Indonesian, Russian, Kazakh, Filipino, UK, Australian, Canadian and continental European sources all refer back to the same official four criteria; regional material should influence examples and phrasing but not create different standards (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking; https://www.idp.cn/special/subject/IELTS/pdf/kscj_f2.pdf; https://zim.vn/tieu-chi-cham-diem-ielts-speaking-phan-1; https://www.upgrad.com/study-abroad/exam/ielts/speaking-band-score/).
