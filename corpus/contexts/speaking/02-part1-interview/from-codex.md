# Part 1 (4–5 min interview) — format, topic categories, examiner question banks

_Crawled by codex on 2026-05-19T07:31:56Z. Sources cited inline._

## Summary

IELTS Speaking Part 1 is the opening interview: the examiner checks identity, then asks short question-answer prompts about the candidate's own life and familiar everyday topics such as home, family, work, studies, interests, weather, cooking, music, movies, hobbies, and daily routines (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking, https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-free-speaking-practice-questions). It matters for scoring because it gives early evidence of whether the candidate can answer directly, extend briefly but naturally, maintain continuity, use topic-specific everyday vocabulary, shift tenses when the question requires it, and stay intelligible under scripted examiner control (https://ielts.org/cdn/Guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf, https://cdn.ielts.org/Research/topic-development-in-ielts-speaking-test-seedhouse-et-al-2011.pdf).

## Authoritative material

- **Official format and timing.** IELTS.org describes Speaking as a three-part test lasting 11-14 minutes; Part 1 is "Introduction and interview", where the examiner introduces themself, checks identity, and asks general questions on familiar topics including home, family, work, studies, and interests; Part 1 lasts 4-5 minutes (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking). IDP's official preparation page adds that Part 1 starts with name and ID, then current life status such as where the candidate lives or whether they work/study, followed by familiar topics such as music, cooking, weather, or movies; it also states that examiner questions are scripted and that the examiner may prompt with "why?" or "why not?" if the answer is too short (https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-free-speaking-practice-questions).

- **How many topic sets/questions.** Public official materials are not perfectly uniform in how they present sample counts: British Council's public Part 1 practice test gives two topics with four questions each and calls this a fair indication of what to expect (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/take-ielts/prepare/free-ielts-english-practice-tests/speaking/part-1), IDP Australia says candidates usually cover one or two more topics after the initial personal/work-study questions, with two or three questions about each (https://ielts.com.au/australia/prepare/speaking), and IELTS Research Reports describe Part 1 examiner scripts as normally containing three "frames", each frame being a connected topic sequence such as "where you live" with four linked questions (https://cdn.ielts.org/Research/topic-development-in-ielts-speaking-test-seedhouse-et-al-2011.pdf). For corpus scoring, treat "about 3 topic frames / around 8-13 questions" as a realistic operational range, not a rigid scoring rule, because the official public examples and research descriptions differ in presentation (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/take-ielts/prepare/free-ielts-english-practice-tests/speaking/part-1, https://ielts.com.au/australia/prepare/speaking, https://cdn.ielts.org/Research/topic-development-in-ielts-speaking-test-seedhouse-et-al-2011.pdf).

- **Question-answer interaction.** IELTS research describes Part 1 as a succession of topic-based question-answer adjacency pairs; the examiner's script determines the topic sequence, and topic shifts are normally examiner-controlled rather than candidate-controlled (https://cdn.ielts.org/Research/topic-development-in-ielts-speaking-test-seedhouse-et-al-2011.pdf). The same report says candidates need to understand the question, answer it, identify the topic embedded in it, and develop that topic; it gives a low-scoring example where minimal "yeah" responses answer the question but do not develop the topic (https://cdn.ielts.org/Research/topic-development-in-ielts-speaking-test-seedhouse-et-al-2011.pdf).

- **Recording and examiner conditions.** IDP Australia states the Speaking test is conducted face-to-face with a certified examiner and recorded in case review is needed; IELTS Canada likewise describes Speaking as face-to-face, interactive, and the same for Academic and General Training (https://ielts.com.au/australia/prepare/speaking, https://www.ielts.ca/ielts-speaking-test/). IDP Australia also notes that some centres offer video-call Speaking, while keeping the test interactive with an examiner (https://ielts.com.au/australia/prepare/speaking).

- **Four scoring criteria.** IELTS's public key-assessment document states that Speaking uses nine bands and four criteria: Fluency and Coherence, Lexical Resource, Grammatical Range and Accuracy, and Pronunciation (https://ielts.org/cdn/Guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf). IELTS.org's public test-format page explains those criteria in test-taker terms: fluency/coherence includes normal-speed speech with limited hesitation plus logical ordering and cohesive devices; lexical resource includes vocabulary range, accuracy, appropriacy, and ability to paraphrase; grammar includes range and accuracy; pronunciation is judged by how much effort is required to understand the speaker (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking).

- **Fluency & Coherence indicators.** The IELTS key-assessment PDF defines Fluency and Coherence as continuity, rate, effort, and linking ideas into coherent connected speech; it flags excessive false starts, backtracking, functionless repetition, and word-search pausing as fluency problems, while coherence indicators include logical sequencing, appropriate pausing/discourse markers, relevance to the turn purpose, and cohesive devices such as connectors, pronouns, and conjunctions (https://ielts.org/cdn/Guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf). The official band descriptors distinguish high bands by very occasional repetition/self-correction and content-related hesitation, Band 7 by keeping going and producing long turns without noticeable effort though some mid-sentence hesitation may occur, Band 6 by willingness to produce longer turns but possible coherence loss from hesitation/repetition/self-correction, and Band 4 by inability to keep going without noticeable pauses (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).

- **Lexical Resource indicators.** IELTS defines Lexical Resource as vocabulary range, precision of meaning and attitude, appropriacy of style, collocation including idiomatic expressions, and paraphrasing around a vocabulary gap (https://ielts.org/cdn/Guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf). In the official band descriptors, Band 8 has a wide, flexible resource with skillful less-common/idiomatic use and effective paraphrase; Band 7 shows flexible topic vocabulary, some less-common/idiomatic items, collocation awareness, and effective paraphrase; Band 6 has enough resource to discuss topics at length but possible inappropriate vocabulary; Band 5 can discuss familiar and unfamiliar topics but with limited flexibility (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).

- **Grammatical Range & Accuracy indicators.** IELTS defines grammatical range through spoken-sentence length, subordinate clauses, verb phrase complexity such as aspect/modality/passive, phrase modification, and varied sentence structures; it defines accuracy through error density and the communicative effect of errors (https://ielts.org/cdn/Guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf). The official band descriptors separate Band 8 from Band 7 by the width and flexibility of structures and by most sentences being error-free, while Band 6 has a mix of simple and complex forms with frequent errors in complex structures that rarely impede communication (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).

- **Pronunciation indicators.** IELTS defines Pronunciation as sustained use of phonological features to convey meaning, including chunking speech into meaningful utterances, rhythm, stress timing, sound linking/elision, contrastive stress, intonation, word/phoneme production, listener effort, and accent's effect on intelligibility (https://ielts.org/cdn/Guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf). The public band descriptors frame high pronunciation bands around flexible phonological features, rhythm/stress/intonation control, and easy or effortless intelligibility; lower bands show limited stress/intonation control, frequent word/phoneme mispronunciations, and patches requiring listener effort (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).

- **Official sample question banks.** British Council's Part 1 practice page provides a public sample with "hometown/village" and "accommodation" frames, each with four questions, and recommends recording practice and listening back; it explicitly says the supplied transcript is not a model answer to copy (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/take-ielts/prepare/free-ielts-english-practice-tests/speaking/part-1). British Council's teacher-facing Part 1 lexical-resource worksheet lists possible everyday topics such as hobbies, work, studies, home/accommodation, town/country, learning English, festivals, food, music, ambitions, transport, TV, reading, and travelling, and tells learners to give natural replies, avoid one-word answers, avoid list-like answers, and avoid memorised/rehearsed answers (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/speaking_part_1_familiarisation_and_lexical_resource.pdf).

## Country-specific prep material

### South Korea

- ed:m IELTS Korea's speaking handout advises Part 1 candidates to answer only the examiner's question, keep answers short at roughly 1-3 sentences, answer directly, and include a reason to avoid extra follow-up pressure; its topic bank includes home/accommodation, hometown, breaks, work/study, travel, transport, history, animals, planning, tea/coffee, languages, concentration, numbers, laughter, science class, scenery, movies, art, reading, parks, local area, cake, running, haircuts, markets, music, public holidays, photos, water sports, walking, friends, voice, maths, and time alone (https://www.edmclass.com/ielts/lib/download/free_lecture/sp_lec_202002.pdf). For scoring, Korean prep highlights a practical Part 1 target: direct answer plus reason, without a long Part 2-style story (https://www.edmclass.com/ielts/lib/download/free_lecture/sp_lec_202002.pdf).

- Another ed:m Korea lecture by Stella Lee frames Part 1 answers as a core answer plus short elaboration within 2-4 lines, and teaches elaboration through reason, example, addition, and contrast; its shopping/fashion lesson emphasises collocations and chunks such as value-for-money, hand-me-down, tailor-made, dress code, luxury goods, and impulse buying (https://www.edmclass.com/ielts/lib/download/free_lecture/2021/sp_upfile_20210423082751.pdf). For the judge, this means locally trained Korean candidates may use compact answer templates; template use should be penalised only when it sounds memorised or mismatched to the actual question, which British Council warns against (https://www.edmclass.com/ielts/lib/download/free_lecture/2021/sp_upfile_20210423082751.pdf, https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/speaking_part_1_familiarisation_and_lexical_resource.pdf).

### Chinese-speaking prep: Mainland/Hong Kong

- IDP Hong Kong's Simplified Chinese official page mirrors the IDP English format: 11-14 minutes total, Part 1 4-5 minutes, first name/identity, then personal questions and familiar topics such as music, cooking, weather, and movies, usually one or two topics after the opening; it states the examiner asks prepared questions and may prompt "why/why not" when answers are too short (https://ielts.idp.com/hongkong/prepare/article-free-speaking-practice-questions/zh-hans). This is useful because Chinese-language candidates may have read official advice in Chinese that short unsupported answers trigger extension prompts, so the judge can treat repeated examiner "why?" prompts as evidence of underdevelopment (https://ielts.idp.com/hongkong/prepare/article-free-speaking-practice-questions/zh-hans).

- 开源雅思 / IELTSDay gives a Chinese Part 1 weather-season bank with many micro-prompts about today's weather, preferred weather, hometown climate, bad weather, weather forecast habits, unusual weather, snow, seasons, and seasonal sports in China (https://www.ieltsday.com/433.html). EasyIELTS.cn presents a large Chinese question-sample library with 500+ speaking tasks across Parts 1-3 and displays Part 1 topic cards such as talents, mirrors, old buildings, lost and found, emails, ads, photos, cars, street markets, websites, and watches, generally as four-question practice sets (https://easyielts.cn/speaking). For scoring, Chinese prep banks show that "familiar" can still include narrow everyday nouns; a good candidate should adapt vocabulary to an unexpected familiar topic rather than deliver a pre-learned generic answer (https://www.ieltsday.com/433.html, https://easyielts.cn/speaking).

- A Zhihu IELTS-prep answer states a common learner expectation in Chinese forums: Part 1 lasts 4-5 minutes, has 3-4 topics, roughly 9-12 questions, and answers around 3-4 sentences; this is not official, but it reflects a common Chinese test-prep heuristic (https://www.zhihu.com/question/269054280/answer/3181743459). Use it only as a local-prep signal, not as an IELTS rule, because official/public sources express the question count differently (https://www.zhihu.com/question/269054280/answer/3181743459, https://ielts.com.au/australia/prepare/speaking).

### Vietnam

- ECE Vietnam presents Part 1 as 4-5 minutes with three topics and 3-4 questions per topic, all personal and familiar, including hometown, family, study, work, and accommodation; its sample "where you live now" set includes house/apartment, favourite room, what makes home pleasant, and neighbours (https://ece.edu.vn/cach-hoc-ielts-speaking/). This Vietnamese academy framing is very aligned with the user's corpus scope and gives the judge a realistic transcript expectation: compact answers across multiple topic frames rather than a single extended monologue (https://ece.edu.vn/cach-hoc-ielts-speaking/).

- Vietop English describes Part 1 as 4-5 minutes with short questions on personal/family/work/study/hobby topics and recommends concise answers with small details or examples of roughly 10-20 seconds; this is local advice rather than official timing, but it supports using answer brevity and relevance as a transcript-level signal (https://vietop.edu.vn/blog/tong-hop-bai-mau-ielts-speaking/). Ace of English Vietnam teaches an Answer -> Reason -> Example -> optional Feeling pattern and explicitly warns against one-word answers, recommending 2-3 sentences for Part 1 (https://aceofenglish.com/blog/ielts-practice/cach-tra-loi-cau-hoi-part-1-2-3-ielts-speaking).

### Iran / Persian-speaking prep

- IELTS2.com, a Persian IELTS prep site, says Part 1 is mainly introduction/warm-up before harder parts, lasts no more than 4-5 minutes, and expects short 10-15 second answers to simple examiner questions; it provides 100 Part 1 audio samples, including work, accommodation, university, family, and Tehran/public-transport prompts (https://ielts2.com/ielts-speaking-samples-part-1/). For scoring, this source reinforces that short answers are normal in Part 1, but only if they still answer and develop the question enough for the examiner to evaluate language (https://ielts2.com/ielts-speaking-samples-part-1/, https://cdn.ielts.org/Research/topic-development-in-ielts-speaking-test-seedhouse-et-al-2011.pdf).

### India

- Physics Wallah / PW Live India describes Part 1 as a direct face-to-face interview lasting about 4-5 minutes and says it generally contains 12-13 questions from three topic areas; it lists topic types as personal everyday topics such as home, study, hobbies, and habits (https://www.pw.live/study-abroad/ielts/exams/ielts-speaking-part-1-topics). For an LLM judge, this source is useful as a South Asian prep heuristic: if a Part 1 transcript has only one topic and a handful of answers, the judge should be cautious about overgeneralising lexical range because the candidate had less topic variety to demonstrate it (https://www.pw.live/study-abroad/ielts/exams/ielts-speaking-part-1-topics).

### Pakistan / Urdu-English prep

- IDP Pakistan's Urdu-localised page gives the official format in an Urdu interface but retains the key Part 1 wording in English: 4-5 minutes, name/ID, general questions such as where the candidate lives or whether they work/study, familiar topics such as music/cooking/weather/movies, scripted questions, and "why/why not" prompts for too-short answers (https://ielts.idp.com/pakistan/prepare/article-question-types-ielts-speaking/ur-pk). This is authoritative for Pakistani candidates and should outweigh local academy timing shortcuts (https://ielts.idp.com/pakistan/prepare/article-question-types-ielts-speaking/ur-pk).

- Shahi Academy presents Part 1 as 4-5 minutes, 5-6 questions, 1-2 topics, and 15-20 seconds per answer; its do/don't list says extend answers, avoid yes/no answers, avoid going off topic, avoid no answer, and avoid speaking too quietly; its sample banks include friends, flowers/plants, food/cooking, gifts, games, telephoning, clothing, reading, accommodation, drinks, colour, neighbours, leisure, and television (https://www.shahiacademy.com/speaking). Treat the 5-6 question count as local practice format, not official IELTS format, but the do/don't list maps well to Fluency/Coherence and topic-development evidence (https://www.shahiacademy.com/speaking, https://ielts.org/cdn/Guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf).

### Indonesia

- IDP Indonesia's Indonesian page states Part 1 is 4-5 minutes, starts with name and ID, asks personal general questions such as where the candidate lives or whether they work/study, then asks familiar topics such as liked music, cooking, weather, and films, usually one or two topics; it also states scripted questions and "mengapa/mengapa tidak" extension prompts if answers are too short (https://ielts.idp.com/indonesia/prepare/article-question-types-ielts-speaking). This gives the judge a direct Indonesian-language official source for the same operational markers: identity check, everyday topics, scripted Q-A, and examiner extension prompts (https://ielts.idp.com/indonesia/prepare/article-question-types-ielts-speaking).

### Russian / Kazakhstan prep

- Lingust.ru, a Russian-language prep site, explains IELTS Speaking as 11-14 minutes with Part 1 lasting 4-5 minutes and covering introduction plus general questions about work/study, home, hobbies, and simple topics such as weather; it recommends full but not overlong 2-3 sentence answers and warns against memorising full texts for common topics such as work, study, hometown, family, and hobbies (https://lingust.ru/faq/english/2157-17). This is a useful Russian-language heuristic for the judge: a high-quality Part 1 answer can be only 2-3 sentences if it directly answers, gives a reason/detail, and does not sound scripted (https://lingust.ru/faq/english/2157-17).

- MyIELTS.kz, a Kazakhstan IELTS portal in Russian, describes Part 1 as a warm-up of 4-5 minutes with approximately 10-12 questions in three blocks: occupation/place of living/hometown, then two additional topic blocks; it emphasises everyday/non-academic vocabulary, topic-specific lexis over forced idioms, possible past/future grammar questions, and roughly 3-5 sentence answers as a conditional guideline rather than a strict rule (https://myielts.kz/archives/15434). For scoring, this source is especially useful for lexical-resource interpretation: natural phrasal verbs and topic-specific vocabulary are stronger evidence than forced idioms inserted into every answer (https://myielts.kz/archives/15434, https://ielts.org/cdn/Guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf).

### UK / Australia / Canada native-perspective material

- British Council's UK-hosted Take IELTS practice page supplies official Part 1 sample topics and transcripts and advises recording practice answers for self-review; it also warns that sample answers are not model answers to copy (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/take-ielts/prepare/free-ielts-english-practice-tests/speaking/part-1). British Council Canada describes Part 1 as questions about the candidate and family lasting about 4-5 minutes, and advises avoiding short answers, avoiding over-rehearsal, asking for clarification if confused, and not overcomplicating vocabulary (https://www.ielts.ca/ielts-speaking-test/).

- IDP IELTS Australia gives a detailed official Part 1 account: after identity and current-life questions, remaining questions are personal and familiar; candidates usually cover one or two more topics with two or three questions about each; accents are accepted if intelligibility is not impaired; examiner interruptions can simply reflect timekeeping and should not be treated as a negative signal by itself (https://ielts.com.au/australia/prepare/speaking). IDP Australia's Part 1 advice also recommends extending responses before the examiner has to ask "why?", preparing to talk about work/study, where the candidate lives and their country, using cohesive devices, and speaking clearly at a good pace (https://ielts.com.au/australia/prepare/article-ielts-speaking-test-part-1).

## Open-source tools and code

- **SpeechSuper API Samples (GitHub).** SpeechSuper's public repository provides code samples for pronunciation assessment and English spontaneous speech assessment; the README says the API returns pronunciation, fluency, grammar, vocabulary, transcription, pause markers, pause-filler frequency, speaking rate, speech length, word-level pronunciation, and has `test_type = "ielts"` for English spontaneous speech assessment (https://github.com/speechsuper/SpeechSuper-API-Samples). The repository page shows an MIT license, 22 commits, no visible last-updated date in the fetched page, and code samples in languages including C#, Java, Dart, HTML, Go, and Python (https://github.com/speechsuper/SpeechSuper-API-Samples).

- **Speechace API (commercial API, not open source).** Speechace advertises an API for IELTS/PTE/TOEFL/TOEIC speaking practice, pronunciation/fluency scoring, IELTS/CEFR/PTE/TOEIC speaking scores, vocabulary/grammar/coherence scores, relevance/task-achievement assessment, and language-use feedback metrics; it says its models are proprietary and evaluated on large real-user samples (https://www.speechace.com/api-plans/). Its Fluency API blog says it can score longer audio for pronunciation, speech rate, pausing, hesitation, length of run, and words-correct-per-minute, and estimate IELTS/PTE fluency on rubric-aligned scales (https://www.speechace.com/hello-fluency-api/).

- **speechocean762 corpus.** The speechocean762 paper describes an open-source non-native English speech corpus for pronunciation assessment with 5,000 English utterances from 250 non-native speakers, expert annotations at sentence/word/phoneme level, and an open-source baseline in Kaldi; the arXiv page says the corpus is free for commercial and non-commercial use and was last revised 2021-06-02 (https://arxiv.org/abs/2104.01378). This is not IELTS Part 1 data, but it is relevant for building pronunciation features that a transcript-only judge cannot observe reliably (https://arxiv.org/abs/2104.01378).

- **EvalYaks.** EvalYaks is a research paper on instruction-tuned datasets and LoRA-tuned Mistral 7B models for scoring CEFR B2 speaking assessment transcripts; it evaluates open-source and commercial LLMs, creates expert-validated synthetic conversational transcripts, and reports average acceptable accuracy for CEFR B2 scoring, with the arXiv page last revised 2025-05-30 and a Creative Commons license link visible (https://arxiv.org/abs/2408.12226). It is not IELTS-specific, but it is relevant to transcript-based automated speaking scoring across criteria where audio features are unavailable (https://arxiv.org/abs/2408.12226).

- **Automatic assessment of conversational speaking tests.** A Cambridge repository paper on automated assessment of conversational speaking tests describes a pipeline using diarization, speaker assignment, ASR, and assessment with Longformer text models plus wav2vec2 audio models; it uses Occupational English Test data for L2 healthcare professionals and is peer-reviewed, accepted-version, all-rights-reserved in the repository (https://www.repository.cam.ac.uk/items/003ce1d5-6fe7-4d27-bbb1-91020f474b6d). This is relevant to the diarized-transcript setting because it explicitly separates speaker diarization, ASR, and scoring challenges (https://www.repository.cam.ac.uk/items/003ce1d5-6fe7-4d27-bbb1-91020f474b6d).

## Practical signals for an LLM judge

- **Identify Part 1 scope before scoring.** Treat identity/name confirmation and examiner setup separately from scored interview answers; Part 1 begins with examiner-led personal/familiar-topic questions and lasts about 4-5 minutes (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking, https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-free-speaking-practice-questions).

- **Expect compact turns, not monologues.** A typical Part 1 answer often fits 2-4 sentences or about 10-30 seconds in prep guidance, while official sources require enough language to answer and develop the topic; one-word answers and list-only answers are weak evidence because they do not allow topic development or language sampling (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/speaking_part_1_familiarisation_and_lexical_resource.pdf, https://aceofenglish.com/blog/ielts-practice/cach-tra-loi-cau-hoi-part-1-2-3-ielts-speaking, https://ielts2.com/ielts-speaking-samples-part-1/).

- **Score directness first.** For each examiner prompt, check whether the candidate answers the actual question before adding reasons or examples; IELTS research says high-scoring performance requires answering the question and developing the topic inherent in it (https://cdn.ielts.org/Research/topic-development-in-ielts-speaking-test-seedhouse-et-al-2011.pdf).

- **Treat repeated "why/why not" prompts as underdevelopment evidence.** IDP official materials say examiners may ask why/why not if an answer is too short, so repeated examiner extension prompts after minimal replies suggest weak topic development or fluency/coherence evidence, not necessarily examiner hostility (https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-free-speaking-practice-questions, https://ielts.idp.com/indonesia/prepare/article-question-types-ielts-speaking).

- **Do not punish examiner interruptions by default.** IDP Australia says examiners may interrupt to keep time and move topics, so interruption alone is not negative; judge whether the candidate had already supplied a coherent answer and whether the interruption cut off language because time was up (https://ielts.com.au/australia/prepare/speaking).

- **Fluency: separate content pauses from language-search pauses.** Official descriptors reward content-related hesitation at high bands but penalise hesitation, repetition, self-correction, false starts, and backtracking when they disrupt continuity or coherence (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf, https://ielts.org/cdn/Guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf).

- **Coherence: look for short spoken structure.** In Part 1, a strong answer often has answer + reason/detail + example/contrast, not a long essay; local prep sources across Korea, Vietnam, Russia, and Australia converge on this pattern, and IELTS criteria value logical sequencing and relevant cohesive devices (https://www.edmclass.com/ielts/lib/download/free_lecture/2021/sp_upfile_20210423082751.pdf, https://aceofenglish.com/blog/ielts-practice/cach-tra-loi-cau-hoi-part-1-2-3-ielts-speaking, https://lingust.ru/faq/english/2157-17, https://ielts.org/cdn/Guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf).

- **Lexical Resource: reward topic-specific everyday vocabulary over forced idioms.** Official criteria value range, appropriacy, collocation, idiomatic language, and paraphrase; MyIELTS.kz specifically warns that thematic lexis is usually more useful in Part 1 than forcing idioms everywhere (https://ielts.org/cdn/Guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf, https://myielts.kz/archives/15434).

- **Grammar: test the tense demanded by the question.** Part 1 banks often ask present, past, future, preference, frequency, and hypothetical questions; the judge should notice whether the candidate shifts tense/aspect/modality appropriately when asked about childhood, recent events, future plans, habits, or preferences (https://ielts.com.au/australia/prepare/article-ielts-speaking-test-part-1, https://myielts.kz/archives/15434, https://ielts.org/cdn/Guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf).

- **Pronunciation needs audio or transcript annotations.** A plain transcript cannot directly reveal rhythm, stress, intonation, linking, phoneme accuracy, or listener effort, which IELTS explicitly includes under Pronunciation; if the transcript has ASR confidence, inaudible tags, repeated examiner repair, or human notes, use them cautiously as evidence of intelligibility, not accent bias (https://ielts.org/cdn/Guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf, https://ielts.com.au/australia/prepare/speaking).

- **Accent is not a penalty by itself.** IDP Australia states candidates do not lose marks for having an accent if pronunciation remains intelligible, and IELTS pronunciation criteria focus on listener effort and clarity rather than native accent (https://ielts.com.au/australia/prepare/speaking, https://ielts.org/cdn/Guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf).

- **Detect memorised-answer risk.** British Council tells learners not to rehearse or memorise answers, and British Council Canada says examiners may change questions if they sense rehearsed lines; in transcripts, memorisation signals include answering a nearby but different question, sudden overlong polished chunks, or failure to adapt pronouns/time/place to the examiner's wording (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/speaking_part_1_familiarisation_and_lexical_resource.pdf, https://www.ielts.ca/ielts-speaking-test/).

- **Use topic variety as evidence, not a requirement.** IELTS research says three frames are normally included, but official public pages also show two-topic samples and IDP Australia says one or two more topics after opening questions; a transcript with fewer topics may reflect examiner time management, so lexical range should be judged against the opportunities actually present (https://cdn.ielts.org/Research/topic-development-in-ielts-speaking-test-seedhouse-et-al-2011.pdf, https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/take-ielts/prepare/free-ielts-english-practice-tests/speaking/part-1, https://ielts.com.au/australia/prepare/speaking).

- **Part 1-only scoring is lower confidence than whole-test scoring.** The official public descriptors apply to the Speaking test as a whole, so a judge seeing only Part 1 should produce criterion evidence and a provisional estimate rather than pretending to have the same evidence as a full 11-14 minute test with Part 2 long turn and Part 3 abstract discussion (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf, https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking).
