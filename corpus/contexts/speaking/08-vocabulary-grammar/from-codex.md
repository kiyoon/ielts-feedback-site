# Speaking-specific lexical resource and grammar — idiomatic chunks, collocations, paraphrase banks, fillers to drop

_Crawled by codex on 2026-05-19T07:32:17Z. Sources cited inline._

## Summary

This topic covers the parts of IELTS Speaking where vocabulary choice, natural word partnerships, idiomatic chunks, paraphrase, and grammatical control affect the Lexical Resource and Grammatical Range & Accuracy criteria; IELTS states that Lexical Resource measures vocabulary range, accuracy, appropriacy, and alternative wording, while Grammatical Range & Accuracy measures the range and accuracy of grammar used in speech ([IELTS.org speaking format](https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking)). For scoring, the core distinction is not "advanced words versus simple words" but whether the candidate can express precise meanings across topics, use style and collocation appropriately, paraphrase when needed, and produce flexible structures without errors that damage intelligibility ([IELTS Speaking Band Descriptors PDF](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf); [IELTS Speaking Key Assessment Criteria PDF](https://ielts.org/cdn/Guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf)). Filler and self-correction are not automatically negative: official criteria allow appropriate fillers and discourse markers, but penalise excessive false starts, backtracking, language-search pauses, and overused markers when they interrupt continuity or reveal missing lexis/grammar ([IELTS Speaking Key Assessment Criteria PDF](https://ielts.org/cdn/Guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf); [IELTS Speaking Band Descriptors PDF](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf)).

## Authoritative material

### Official scoring anchors

- IELTS Speaking is marked by certificated examiners across four criteria: Fluency & Coherence, Lexical Resource, Grammatical Range & Accuracy, and Pronunciation, and the Speaking test lasts 11-14 minutes across three parts ([IELTS.org speaking format](https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking)). The judge should evaluate LR and GRA over the candidate's average performance across all parts, because the public descriptors state that a candidate is rated on average performance across the test and must fully fit the positive features at a band level ([IELTS Speaking Band Descriptors PDF](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf)).
- Lexical Resource means more than vocabulary size: IELTS key assessment criteria define it as range of vocabulary, topic coverage, precision of meaning, expression of attitude, appropriate style, collocation including idiomatic expressions, and paraphrase around a vocabulary gap ([IELTS Speaking Key Assessment Criteria PDF](https://ielts.org/cdn/Guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf)). IELTS.org's test-format page also defines it as the range, accuracy, and appropriacy of vocabulary plus the ability to express the same idea with alternative vocabulary when the exact word is missing ([IELTS.org speaking format](https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking)).
- Grammatical Range & Accuracy is not just "few errors": IELTS key assessment criteria list spoken-sentence length, subordinate clauses, verb phrase complexity including auxiliary use, continuous/perfect aspect, modality and passive voice, phrase complexity through pre- and post-modification, and sentence patterns that move elements for information focus ([IELTS Speaking Key Assessment Criteria PDF](https://ielts.org/cdn/Guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf)). Accuracy should be judged by error density and communicative effect, not by counting every slip equally ([IELTS Speaking Key Assessment Criteria PDF](https://ielts.org/cdn/Guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf)).
- Band 9 LR requires total flexibility and precise vocabulary use in all contexts with sustained accurate idiomatic language; Band 8 LR requires a wide resource used flexibly, skilful less-common and idiomatic items, and effective paraphrase; Band 7 LR requires flexible vocabulary over varied topics, some less-common or idiomatic items, style/collocation awareness, and effective paraphrase, though inappropriacies may occur ([IELTS Speaking Band Descriptors PDF](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf)). Band 6 LR is sufficient to discuss topics at length, but word choice may be inappropriate while meaning stays clear, and paraphrase is generally successful; Band 5 LR has limited flexibility and only sometimes successful paraphrase; Band 4 LR is mainly familiar-topic vocabulary, frequent wrong word choice, and rare paraphrase ([IELTS Speaking Band Descriptors PDF](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf)).
- Band 9 GRA requires natural, appropriate, and consistently accurate structures except native-speaker-like slips; Band 8 requires a flexible wide range with most sentences error-free and only occasional non-systematic errors; Band 7 requires flexible use of a range of structures with frequent error-free sentences and effective simple plus complex sentences despite some errors ([IELTS Speaking Band Descriptors PDF](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf)). Band 6 GRA combines short and complex forms with limited flexibility, and complex-structure errors are frequent but rarely impede communication; Band 5 attempts complex structures but they are limited, usually erroneous, and may require reformulation; Band 4 rarely uses subordinate clauses and relies on short, repetitive structures with frequent errors ([IELTS Speaking Band Descriptors PDF](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf)).
- Official fluency/coherence descriptors are relevant to fillers: Band 9 hesitation is content preparation rather than word/grammar search, Band 8 has only occasional language-search hesitation, Band 7 may show some language-related hesitation/repetition/self-correction without harming coherence, Band 6 may lose coherence through hesitation/repetition/self-correction, and Band 5 relies on repetition, self-correction, slow speech, and overused markers to keep going ([IELTS Speaking Band Descriptors PDF](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf)). IELTS key criteria explicitly include appropriate spoken discourse markers and fillers as coherence indicators, while also listing false starts, backtracking, functionless repetitions, and word-search pauses as interruptions to speech continuity ([IELTS Speaking Key Assessment Criteria PDF](https://ielts.org/cdn/Guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf)).

### Idioms, collocations, chunks, and paraphrase

- IELTS.org says idiomatic language is assessed from Band 7 upward and explains that idioms, phrasal verbs, and collocations are common in everyday communication, but learners must use them correctly so they do not sound unnatural ([IELTS.org idioms article](https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/how-to-use-idioms-in-the-ielts-speaking-test)). The same IELTS.org article warns candidates not to overuse idioms, not to force overused internet-list expressions, and to copy phrases from actual native-speaker input instead ([IELTS.org idioms article](https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/how-to-use-idioms-in-the-ielts-speaking-test)).
- The British Council teacher resource on Lexical Resource explains that candidates need vocabulary effectiveness, not memorised lists, and highlights collocation, connotation, and word form as assessable features; its examples include "make a phone call" rather than "do a phone call" and connotative differences such as positive "laid back" versus negative "lazy" ([British Council lexical resource video page](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-lexical-resource)).
- Cambridge's ELT article argues for teaching "chunks" such as collocations, fixed expressions, sentence starters, and other multi-word items because they support IELTS fluency and comprehension; it gives classroom use cases such as a learner buying thinking time before answering an IELTS Speaking question with a memorised but adaptable phrase ([Cambridge ELT chunks article](https://www.cambridge.org/elt/blog/2020/08/12/using-chunks-to-improve-your-ielts-students-band-scores/)).
- The British Council Part 1 classroom PDF treats LR/GRA improvement as answer-upgrading, not ornamentation: stronger answers replace repeated/basic lexis such as "children" with topic-appropriate alternatives such as "toddler/kids," use natural collocations such as "spend time around," use spoken expressions such as "I'm really into" and "loads of," and vary grammar with forms such as present perfect continuous, "used to," and future expressions ([British Council Part 1 LR/GRA PDF](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/speaking_part_1_grammatical_range_and_accuracy_lexical_resource.pdf)).
- The British Council grammar page says candidates should use varied complex sentence structures to show range, but it also states that IELTS does not expect 100% accuracy and that self-correction is acceptable when candidates notice an error ([British Council grammar page](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-grammar)). The same page frames Band 7 grammar as frequent error-free sentences with some grammatical mistakes, and it says errors that block communication are more serious than conversationally passable errors ([British Council grammar page](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-grammar)).

## Country-specific prep material

### Korea

- The Korean Hackers IELTS Speaking book listing says the course is organised by Speaking part and by 20 topic units, including job/study, accommodation, cities, leisure, art, fashion, sport, people, food/health, travel, weather/nature, events, daily life, experiences, education, communication, science/technology, media, history/tradition, and society; this supports a judge-side expectation that strong LR is usually topic-distributed, not concentrated in a single memorised theme ([YES24 Hackers IELTS Speaking](https://www.yes24.com/Product/Goods/41050360)). The same listing says the book includes expressions for adding detail, developing a Part 2 talk, giving introductions/conclusions in Part 3, crisis expressions for unexpected exam situations, common speaking error points, answer self-check points, and topic-specific ideas and expressions ([YES24 Hackers IELTS Speaking](https://www.yes24.com/Product/Goods/41050360)).
- A Korean IELTS cram-school page describes LR improvement through collocation, less-common vocabulary/expressions, and avoiding short-answer responses in IELTS Speaking, which aligns with official Band 7+ descriptors on style/collocation and topic development ([Union IELTS Korean cram-school page](https://www.no1ielts.com/bbs/board.php?bo_table=1_1to4&page=1&wr_id=40); [IELTS Speaking Band Descriptors PDF](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf)).

### China

- New Oriental Online advises that high-score spoken grammar should include usable complex structures such as non-defining "which" clauses, indirect reported thought, and third conditional/counterfactual forms like "If I hadn't..., I would have..."; the article associates accurate flexible use with higher bands and explicitly warns that the structures require practice rather than blind memorisation ([New Oriental high-score speaking structures](https://ielts.koolearn.com/20200109/835779.html)). This is useful for judging because a candidate who produces a natural counterfactual in Part 2 or Part 3 demonstrates more range than a candidate who only stacks present-simple clauses, provided the form is accurate enough not to impede communication ([New Oriental high-score speaking structures](https://ielts.koolearn.com/20200109/835779.html); [IELTS Speaking Key Assessment Criteria PDF](https://ielts.org/cdn/Guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf)).
- New Oriental's scoring-focused tip page frames fluency as reducing stalls and using connectors, but it recommends natural transition markers such as "I mean" and "well" instead of raw hesitation noises such as "erh" or "hmm"; the same page says vocabulary breadth should be paired with accuracy of context and collocation, and grammar accuracy is more important than showy complex structures that become tangled or erroneous ([New Oriental speaking scoring tips](https://ielts.koolearn.com/20211112/854112.html)).
- The IDP China public band-descriptor PDF mirrors the official English descriptors in Chinese and is useful for bilingual verification: Band 7 LR requires flexible vocabulary, some less-common/idiomatic vocabulary, style/collocation awareness, and effective paraphrase, while Band 6 LR permits occasional inappropriate word use if meaning stays clear ([IDP China public Speaking band descriptors PDF](https://www.idp.cn/special/subject/IELTS/pdf/kscj_f2.pdf)).

### Vietnam

- ZIM Vietnam explains that collocation begins to appear explicitly at Band 7 LR in the public descriptors and defines collocation as two or more words that naturally co-occur, such as "pay tribute to"; it warns that wrong collocations can confuse listeners and cost IELTS Speaking marks ([ZIM collocations in IELTS Speaking](https://zim.vn/collocation-thong-dung)). ZIM's practical examples show that opinion-bank language should go beyond repeated "I think" by using alternative collocational frames for giving views ([ZIM collocations in IELTS Speaking](https://zim.vn/collocation-thong-dung)).
- IELTS Complete Vietnam's Part 2 collocation article says candidates have 1-2 minutes to speak continuously and should use accurate natural collocations to reduce dependence on filler words such as "um" and "uh"; it provides topic-linked collocation banks for people, personality, places, and environment, such as "strong personality," "excellent communication skills," "positive outlook on life," "breathtaking views," "bustling city," and "tranquil atmosphere" ([IELTS Complete Vietnam Part 2 collocations](https://ieltscomplete.com/20-collocations-ielts-xin-cho-speaking-part-2/)).
- ECE Vietnam separates collocations, phrasal verbs, and idioms by function: collocations fit Part 2 and Part 3, phrasal verbs fit more natural Part 1/2 answers, and idioms should be used only 1-2 times and not overused because memorised insertions are easy for examiners to notice ([ECE Vietnam high-score speaking phrases](https://ece.edu.vn/cac-cum-tu-an-diem-trong-ielts-speaking/)). The same source maps bands to phrase behaviour: Band 6 prioritises clarity, Band 7 adds variety and paraphrase with collocations/phrasal verbs, and Band 8-9 emphasises natural flexible idiom/collocation use with few grammar errors ([ECE Vietnam high-score speaking phrases](https://ece.edu.vn/cac-cum-tu-an-diem-trong-ielts-speaking/)).

### Iran / Persian

- IELTS2, a Persian IELTS site, says informal idioms are generally not recommended for Writing but some idioms can be useful in IELTS Speaking when they communicate meaning well; it defines idiom as a group of words with a figurative meaning that cannot be guessed from the literal meanings of the component words ([IELTS2 Persian idioms for IELTS Speaking](https://ielts2.com/ielts-speaking-idioms-pdf/)). The site's idiom list includes many high-risk memorisation targets such as "break the ice," "piece of cake," "costs an arm and a leg," and "hit the nail on the head," so a judge should reward these only when topic fit, pronunciation, grammar, and surrounding discourse are natural ([IELTS2 Persian idioms for IELTS Speaking](https://ielts2.com/ielts-speaking-idioms-pdf/); [IELTS.org idioms article](https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/how-to-use-idioms-in-the-ielts-speaking-test)).
- A Persian collocation-prep page frames collocations as more important than impressive single words and contrasts natural combinations with translated/unnatural combinations, which is consistent with IELTS LR descriptors that explicitly include collocation awareness at Band 7+ ([Cafe IELTS collocation guide](https://cafeielts.ir/ielts-cue-card-vocabulary-collocations-guide/); [IELTS Speaking Band Descriptors PDF](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf)).

### India

- IDP India explains that examiners assign a band for each of the four Speaking criteria and then match performance to the public descriptors; its Band 7 LR summary requires flexible vocabulary across topics, some less-common and idiomatic vocabulary, style/collocation awareness, and effective paraphrase, while its Band 7 GRA summary requires a range of complex structures, frequent error-free sentences, and some persistent mistakes ([IDP India Speaking band scores](https://ieltsidpindia.com/information/ielts-band-scores/speaking)).
- IELTSMaterial, an India-facing IELTS prep site, advises using idioms in context rather than memorising lists, grouping idioms by theme, using them in personal examples, avoiding overuse, and practising them aloud inside sentence structures; the judge should treat this as prep advice, while still prioritising official IELTS criteria when scoring ([IELTSMaterial idioms for IELTS Speaking](https://ieltsmaterial.com/idiom-list-for-ielts-speaking/)). IELTSMaterial's advice that overuse can make speech unnatural is consistent with IELTS.org's warning against forced or overused idioms ([IELTSMaterial idioms for IELTS Speaking](https://ieltsmaterial.com/idiom-list-for-ielts-speaking/); [IELTS.org idioms article](https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/how-to-use-idioms-in-the-ielts-speaking-test)).

### Pakistan

- MyIELTS.pk, a Pakistan-based IELTS prep site, presents Lexical Resource as 25% of the Speaking score and recommends using at least 1-2 collocations, learning from BBC "The English We Speak," and replacing very basic words with more precise choices; treat the "1-2 collocations" target as a practice heuristic rather than an official IELTS threshold ([MyIELTS.pk vocabulary tip](https://myielts.pk/blog/2025/04/24/%F0%9F%A7%A0-use-a-wide-range-of-vocabulary/); [IELTS Speaking Band Descriptors PDF](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf)).
- A Pakistan Languages and Humanities Review article discusses collocations and idioms as distinguishable lexical phenomena and notes Pakistani-context work on Urdu-English collocations, which is relevant when diagnosing L1 transfer errors such as direct translation of word partnerships into English ([Pakistan Languages and Humanities Review article PDF](https://www.ojs.plhr.org.pk/journal/article/download/1252/1117/2172)).

### Indonesia

- An Indonesian university paper on IELTS Speaking idioms states that LR includes paraphrase, style, collocations, and idioms, and it reports IDP IELTS Indonesia's four scoring criteria as fluency/coherence, lexical resource, grammatical range/accuracy, and pronunciation ([UNNES Journal of English Language Teaching paper PDF](https://journal.unnes.ac.id/sju/elt/article/download/57604/22995/)). The paper argues that idiomatic vocabulary connects to communicative competence and context-sensitive interaction, which supports a judge-side preference for idioms embedded in relevant turns rather than isolated idiom drops ([UNNES Journal of English Language Teaching paper PDF](https://journal.unnes.ac.id/sju/elt/article/download/57604/22995/)).
- In an Indonesian IELTS discussion thread, a user reports that for Speaking, candidates should acclimatise to thinking in English and even make their filler words English, while another warns against treating writing/speaking as a display of complicated grammar; use this only as community perspective, not an authoritative rule ([r/indonesia IELTS discussion](https://www.reddit.com/r/indonesia/comments/ndmo62)).

### Russian / Kazakh

- A Kazakhstan pedagogical article in Russian reports an IELTS elective where lexical-thematic modelling was compared with translation-method vocabulary learning; it says the model considered semantic, situational, and collocational features and helped learners use antonyms, synonyms, collocations, and idioms more often and correctly across speaking, writing, reading, and listening ([Kazakhstan pedagogical bulletin PDF](https://bulletin-pedagogical.ablaikhan.kz/index.php/j1/article/download/1095/474/5188)). This supports a scoring diagnostic: a candidate who has topic clusters with synonyms, antonyms, and collocations is more likely to paraphrase flexibly than a candidate translating isolated words ([Kazakhstan pedagogical bulletin PDF](https://bulletin-pedagogical.ablaikhan.kz/index.php/j1/article/download/1095/474/5188); [IELTS Speaking Key Assessment Criteria PDF](https://ielts.org/cdn/Guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf)).
- A Russian article on the lexical approach explains lexical chunks and collocations as central building blocks in foreign-language teaching; this is relevant to IELTS Speaking because official descriptors reward collocation and idiomatic awareness rather than isolated word display ([CyberLeninka lexical approach PDF](https://cyberleninka.ru/article/n/leksicheskiy-podhod-v-obuchenii-angliyskomu-yazyku-kak-inostrannomu-teoriya-i-praktika/pdf); [IELTS Speaking Band Descriptors PDF](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf)).

### UK / Australia / Canada native-perspective material

- IELTS Advantage, a UK-based IELTS teacher site, analysed its Band 9 VIP students' practice sessions and found an average of 1.2 idioms, with many Band 9 students using none; it warns that trying to include many idioms is often inappropriate and can lower the score when used incorrectly ([IELTS Advantage idioms article](https://www.ieltsadvantage.com/idioms-for-ielts-speaking/)). This is a useful correction to prep-school advice that implies idiom quantity alone raises LR: the official criterion is natural accurate idiomatic language, not idiom count ([IELTS Advantage idioms article](https://www.ieltsadvantage.com/idioms-for-ielts-speaking/); [IELTS Speaking Band Descriptors PDF](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf)).
- IELTS.org's idioms article was first published by IDP and gives an Australian-owned IELTS perspective: Band 7+ candidates should show idiomatic language, but native speakers use idioms naturally and learners should avoid overused forced expressions ([IELTS.org idioms article](https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/how-to-use-idioms-in-the-ielts-speaking-test)).
- IELTS Vancouver, a Canadian IELTS centre, explains Lexical Resource as vocabulary range, word appropriacy, repetition avoidance, and careful substitution; it warns that forced synonym replacement can change meaning, so a judge should reward paraphrase only when the candidate preserves meaning and register ([IELTS Vancouver lexical resource article](https://ieltsvancouver.com/2022/07/21/ielts-speaking-what-is-lexical-resource/)).

## Open-source tools and code

- TAASSC (Tool for the Automatic Analysis of Syntactic Sophistication and Complexity) measures classic syntactic complexity indices plus fine-grained phrasal and clausal complexity, including measures such as adjectives per noun phrase and adverbials per clause, and outputs CSV from plain text files ([TAASSC tool page](https://www.linguisticanalysistools.org/taassc.html)). TAASSC is relevant for a judge-side feature extractor because official IELTS GRA includes sentence length, subordinate clauses, verb phrase complexity, phrase complexity, and information-focus structures ([TAASSC tool page](https://www.linguisticanalysistools.org/taassc.html); [IELTS Speaking Key Assessment Criteria PDF](https://ielts.org/cdn/Guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf)).
- The TAASSC GitHub repository is the landing page for TAASSC version 2.0+ code and resources and notes that recent versions use spaCy for POS tagging and dependency parsing; the crawled result did not expose a license or last-updated date, so verify repository metadata before redistribution ([TAASSC GitHub](https://github.com/kristopherkyle/TAASSC)).
- TAALES (Tool for the Automatic Analysis of Lexical Sophistication) provides automatic analysis of over 130 lexical sophistication indices, including frequency and range measures, and supports batch processing with CSV output; this is relevant for estimating LR breadth and sophistication from transcripts, but it should not be treated as a direct IELTS band scorer ([TAALES page](https://soletlab.asu.edu/taales/); [IELTS Speaking Key Assessment Criteria PDF](https://ielts.org/cdn/Guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf)).
- CALF is an automatic analytical tool for speech and coded written data that outputs measures for complexity, accuracy, fluency, and lexis; it is relevant to IELTS Speaking research because it spans all four language-production dimensions, but the page shows "Copyright 2018. HSUHK, All rights reserved," so it is an accessible tool rather than clearly open-source code ([CALF tool page](https://www.censpothk.com/calf/)).
- PodcastFillers is an arXiv-described dataset with annotated filler words and other vocal events in podcast speech; it is not IELTS-specific, but it is relevant if the judge needs a filler-word detector to distinguish discourse markers from hesitation events before applying IELTS fluency criteria ([PodcastFillers arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2203.15135); [IELTS Speaking Key Assessment Criteria PDF](https://ielts.org/cdn/Guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf)).

## Practical signals for an LLM judge

- Treat natural collocation as a Band 7+ LR signal only when the phrase is contextually right, because official descriptors require style/collocation awareness and IELTS.org defines collocation as natural word order such as "fast food," not "food fast" ([IELTS Speaking Band Descriptors PDF](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf); [IELTS.org idioms article](https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/how-to-use-idioms-in-the-ielts-speaking-test)).
- Penalise wrong word partnerships more than simple-but-correct wording when they make the answer sound translated or confusing, because official LR includes appropriacy, referential meaning, style, and collocation ([IELTS Speaking Key Assessment Criteria PDF](https://ielts.org/cdn/Guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf); [ZIM collocations in IELTS Speaking](https://zim.vn/collocation-thong-dung)).
- Reward paraphrase when the candidate preserves meaning and avoids lexical gaps without long hesitation; cap the LR benefit if paraphrase changes the claim, forces an unnatural synonym, or creates wrong register, because IELTS rewards effective paraphrase and IELTS Vancouver warns that forced substitution can change meaning ([IELTS Speaking Band Descriptors PDF](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf); [IELTS Vancouver lexical resource article](https://ieltsvancouver.com/2022/07/21/ielts-speaking-what-is-lexical-resource/)).
- Do not require idiom-count quotas for Band 7-9; IELTS Advantage reports Band 9 practice answers averaged 1.2 idioms and many used none, while IELTS.org says idioms should be natural, topic-matched, and not overused ([IELTS Advantage idioms article](https://www.ieltsadvantage.com/idioms-for-ielts-speaking/); [IELTS.org idioms article](https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/how-to-use-idioms-in-the-ielts-speaking-test)).
- Flag "idiom drops" as negative when they are semantically mismatched, grammatically detached, over-pronounced, or clustered, because official LR rewards accurate idiomatic language and IELTS.org warns that forced internet-list idioms sound unnatural ([IELTS Speaking Band Descriptors PDF](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf); [IELTS.org idioms article](https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/how-to-use-idioms-in-the-ielts-speaking-test)).
- Accept spoken fillers such as "well," "I mean," "to be honest," or "how shall I put it" when they organise a turn or buy brief content-planning time; penalise raw hesitation strings and repeated discourse markers when they replace missing lexis/grammar or interrupt continuity, because official criteria distinguish appropriate fillers from language-search pauses and overused markers ([IELTS Speaking Key Assessment Criteria PDF](https://ielts.org/cdn/Guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf); [New Oriental speaking scoring tips](https://ielts.koolearn.com/20211112/854112.html)).
- For GRA, count a complex sentence as useful range only if it is intelligible and mostly controlled; do not reward tangled subordinate clauses, conditionals, clefts, or inversion merely for existing, because British Council says control matters and communication-blocking errors are more serious than passable slips ([British Council grammar page](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-grammar); [IELTS Speaking Key Assessment Criteria PDF](https://ielts.org/cdn/Guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf)).
- Distinguish Band 6 GRA from Band 7 GRA by flexibility and error-free sentence frequency: Band 6 mixes short and complex forms with limited flexibility and frequent complex-structure errors, while Band 7 uses a range flexibly and produces frequent error-free sentences despite some errors ([IELTS Speaking Band Descriptors PDF](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf)).
- Treat topic-specific vocabulary banks as evidence only when the candidate can deploy them across Parts 1-3 without sounding memorised; Korean, Vietnamese, and Kazakh/Russian prep sources all organise vocabulary by topic clusters, but official IELTS LR still requires precision, style, collocation, and paraphrase ([YES24 Hackers IELTS Speaking](https://www.yes24.com/Product/Goods/41050360); [IELTS Complete Vietnam Part 2 collocations](https://ieltscomplete.com/20-collocations-ielts-xin-cho-speaking-part-2/); [Kazakhstan pedagogical bulletin PDF](https://bulletin-pedagogical.ablaikhan.kz/index.php/j1/article/download/1095/474/5188); [IELTS Speaking Key Assessment Criteria PDF](https://ielts.org/cdn/Guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf)).
- Award extra LR credit for natural phrasal verbs and semi-formal spoken chunks when they fit the topic and register, because IELTS.org treats phrasal verbs as idiomatic language and British Council examples mark "I'm really into" and "I've got loads of" as natural spoken expressions ([IELTS.org idioms article](https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/how-to-use-idioms-in-the-ielts-speaking-test); [British Council Part 1 LR/GRA PDF](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/speaking_part_1_grammatical_range_and_accuracy_lexical_resource.pdf)).
- Treat self-correction as neutral or mildly positive when it is quick and improves accuracy without breaking coherence; treat it as negative when it becomes a support mechanism for maintaining speech, because official descriptors allow some self-correction at Band 7 but describe reliance on self-correction as Band 5 behaviour ([IELTS Speaking Band Descriptors PDF](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf); [British Council grammar page](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-grammar)).
- If using automated features, treat TAASSC/TAALES/CALF outputs as supporting evidence only; official IELTS scoring is holistic across all parts and requires matching descriptors, not simply maximising lexical sophistication or syntactic complexity scores ([TAASSC tool page](https://www.linguisticanalysistools.org/taassc.html); [TAALES page](https://soletlab.asu.edu/taales/); [CALF tool page](https://www.censpothk.com/calf/); [IELTS Speaking Band Descriptors PDF](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf)).
