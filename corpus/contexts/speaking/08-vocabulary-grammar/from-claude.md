# Speaking-specific lexical resource and grammar — idiomatic chunks, collocations, paraphrase banks, fillers to drop

_Crawled by claude on 2026-05-19T07:31Z. Sources cited inline._

## Summary

In the IELTS Speaking rubric, Lexical Resource and Grammatical Range & Accuracy each carry 25% of the band score and from Band 7 upward both bands explicitly require **"less common and idiomatic vocabulary"**, **collocation awareness**, **paraphrase ability**, and **a range of complex structures** used flexibly (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf, https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf). This file maps what those descriptors mean operationally: which chunks examiners reward, which "memorised idiom" patterns trigger penalties, which complex grammar moves (cleft, inversion, modal hedging, conditionals) deliver Band 8+ signals, which fillers are acceptable as natural thinking-time vs which are penalised as hesitation, and the topical paraphrase banks Band 7 candidates from Korean, Chinese, Vietnamese, Iranian, Pakistani, Russian, and Anglophone cram material rely on. The judge should use these to distinguish genuine Band 7 from "memorised Band 5 with idiom decoration", which is a common failure pattern reported across regional academies.

## Authoritative material

### Official band descriptors — Lexical Resource (Speaking)

Reproduced from the public IELTS Speaking band descriptor PDF hosted by IELTS.org and British Council (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf, https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf) and Cambridge's webinar PDF (https://assets.cambridgeenglish.org/webinars/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf):

- **Band 9** — uses vocabulary with full flexibility and precision in all topics; uses idiomatic language naturally and accurately.
- **Band 8** — uses a wide vocabulary resource readily and flexibly to convey precise meaning; uses less common and idiomatic vocabulary skilfully, with occasional inaccuracies; uses paraphrase effectively as required.
- **Band 7** — uses vocabulary resource flexibly to discuss a variety of topics; uses some less common and idiomatic vocabulary and shows some awareness of style and collocation, with some inappropriate choices; uses paraphrase effectively.
- **Band 6** — has a wide enough vocabulary to discuss topics at length and make meaning clear in spite of inappropriacies; generally paraphrases successfully.
- **Band 5** — manages to talk about familiar and unfamiliar topics but uses vocabulary with limited flexibility; attempts to use paraphrase but with mixed success.

### Official band descriptors — Grammatical Range & Accuracy (Speaking)

- **Band 9** — uses a full range of structures naturally and appropriately; produces consistently accurate structures apart from 'slips' characteristic of native speaker speech.
- **Band 8** — uses a wide range of structures flexibly; produces a majority of error-free sentences with only very occasional inappropriacies or basic/non-systematic errors.
- **Band 7** — uses a range of complex structures with some flexibility; frequently produces error-free sentences, though some grammatical mistakes persist.
- **Band 6** — uses a mix of simple and complex structures, but with limited flexibility; may make frequent mistakes with complex structures though these rarely cause comprehension problems.
- **Band 5** — produces basic sentence forms with reasonable accuracy; uses a limited range of more complex structures, but these usually contain errors.

(Sources: https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf, https://ieltstutors.org/speaking-band-descriptors/, https://idc.edu/IELTS-Speaking-Writing-Band-descriptors.pdf, https://lovetolearnenglish.com/tips-for-ielts-and-toefl/2024/9/12/detailed-summary-of-ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.)

### IELTS.org — "How to use idioms in the IELTS speaking test"

The official article (https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/how-to-use-idioms-in-the-ielts-speaking-test) makes three operational claims:

1. Idiomatic language is rewarded **from Band 7 upward** — below 7, idioms are neither expected nor decisive.
2. Two specific failure modes lose marks: (a) topic mismatch (e.g., "raining cats and dogs" forced into a question about education), (b) over-used clichés that "sound forced". Examiners read forced idiom use as memorised script and downgrade lexical resource for "inappropriate choices".
3. The rubric word is **"idiomatic language"**, not idioms. Phrasal verbs, fixed expressions, conventional metaphors, and natural collocations all count — a candidate with zero textbook idioms but rich phrasal verbs and collocation can score Band 7 LR.

### British Council — Top 20 IELTS Speaking idioms

The official British Council list (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/blog/top-english-idioms) centres on safe, high-frequency, register-appropriate idioms: *break the ice, by the skin of your teeth, call it a day, a piece of cake, get the hang of, on top of the world, in the blink of an eye, once in a blue moon, cost an arm and a leg, hit the books, under the weather, miss the boat, the best of both worlds, blow off steam, cut corners, see eye to eye, speak of the devil, the ball is in your court, beat around the bush, let the cat out of the bag*. These are the "default" set examiners hear most often — using them is not penalised, but using only these gives no Band 8 lift either.

### IDP IELTS (multiple regional sites)

IDP groups idioms by topical category and pairs the advice **"match the idiom to the topic; never force it"**:

- Colour/emotion: *over the moon, in the red, green with envy, feeling blue, see red*
- Study/work: *pass with flying colours, drop out, hit the books, burn the midnight oil, learn the ropes*
- Family/childhood: *like a kid in a candy store, runs in the family, the apple of one's eye, like father like son*
- Health/COVID: *under the weather, on the mend, on its last legs, bounce back*
- IELTS-meta: *practice makes perfect, break a leg, take the plunge*

(Sources: https://ielts.idp.com/about/news-and-articles/article-use-idioms-ielts-speaking, https://ielts.idp.com/pakistan/about/news-and-articles/article-use-idioms-ielts-speaking, https://ielts.idp.com/iran/prepare/article-ielts-speaking-best-phrases, https://ielts.idp.com/nepal/about/news-and-articles/article-ielts-speaking-vocabulary.)

### Common high-frequency collocations expected at Band 7+

From the IDP Iran and British Council collocations articles (https://ielts.idp.com/iran/prepare/article-ielts-speaking-best-phrases, https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/blog/top-phrasal-verbs-for-IELTS):

- Verb + noun: *take an exam* (NOT "make an exam"), *do homework, solve a problem, raise awareness, make a decision, reach a conclusion, foot the bill, pay attention, meet a deadline, take responsibility, lead a healthy lifestyle, cause concern*.
- Adjective + noun: *bright idea, strong criticism, heavy traffic, severe weather, fundamental difference, deep recession, vivid memory, key factor, hidden agenda*.
- Adverb + adjective: *highly successful, deeply concerned, widely available, completely different, perfectly natural, increasingly common*.
- Verb + preposition phrasal: *bring up, look after, take after, hang out with, run out of, come up with, put up with, get along with, look forward to, deal with, end up*.

Candidates who pair a topic-specific collocation with a phrasal verb in the same Part 2 turn (e.g., *"I ended up taking responsibility for the project"*) consistently score Band 7+ for LR per the IELTS Podcast ex-examiner analysis (https://www.ieltspodcast.com/ielts-speaking/ielts-speaking-vocabulary/).

### Complex grammar structures rewarded at Band 7-9

- **Conditionals (all three types + mixed)** — second conditional for hypothetical ("If I were…, I would…"), third conditional for past regret ("If I had known, I would have…"), mixed ("If I had studied harder, I'd be working there now"). Examiners specifically listen for at least one second or third conditional in Part 3 (https://www.ieltspodcast.com/audio-tutorials/10-ielts-band-9-grammar-features-3/, https://ielts.net/what-grammatical-structure-should-be-use-in-ielts/, https://www.ieltsbuddy.com/complex-sentences.html).
- **Cleft sentences** — *"It is X that…"* / *"What I really enjoy is…"* / *"The thing that matters most is…"*. Cited as a Band 9 grammar feature (https://www.ieltspodcast.com/audio-tutorials/10-ielts-band-9-grammar-features-3/, https://keithspeakingacademy.com/ielts-speaking-complex-sentences-grammar/).
- **Inversion** — *"Not only did she write it, she also presented it"*, *"Never have I felt so…"*, *"Rarely do we see…"*. Optional even at Band 9; one well-placed inversion impresses, two or more in a 90-second turn usually reads as memorised (https://ieltsmaterial.com/advanced-grammar-for-ielts-emphatic-structures-and-inversion/, https://www.ieltspodcast.com/ielts-speaking/advanced-speaking/).
- **Subjunctive / modal perfect** — *"He must have left already", "She should have known", "If I were in charge…"* (https://www.ieltspodcast.com/audio-tutorials/10-ielts-band-9-grammar-features-3/).
- **Relative clauses (defining and non-defining)** — *"my hometown, which is in the south"*, *"a friend who works in tech"*. The single most common Band-6→Band-7 lift recommended by Keith Speaking Academy (https://keithspeakingacademy.com/ielts-speaking-complex-sentences-grammar/).
- **Modal hedging** — *"It could be argued that…", "Many would suggest…", "It might depend on…", "There's a reasonable case for…", "I'd be inclined to say…", "Arguably…"* — supports Band 7-8 by avoiding over-generalisation and softening claims (https://ieltsmumbai.com/blog/ielts-vocabulary--grammar-using-hedging-language-appropriately-in-ielts, https://ted-ielts.com/hedging-language/, https://www.astaachiever.com/mastering-the-art-of-tentative-language-in-ielts-writing-and-speaking).
- **Ellipsis / substitution** — *"She asked if I wanted to go, but I didn't"*, *"My brother loves it; so do I"*. Marker of native-like coherence at Band 9 (https://www.ieltspodcast.com/audio-tutorials/10-ielts-band-9-grammar-features-3/).
- **Layered complex sentence** — Keith's pattern: *"I live in Spain, which is a pretty big country, because the food is great, although I think they eat too much fish."* Three subordinators in one sentence is a fast Band 7 signal (https://keithspeakingacademy.com/ielts-speaking-complex-sentences-grammar/).

### Self-correction, fillers, hesitation — Liz Ferguson and TED IELTS

Self-correction (https://ieltsliz.com/ielts-speaking-self-correction/, https://practice9.co/self-correction-inielts-speaking/, https://ifi.qa/self-correction-in-ielts-speaking-why-how/, https://ted-ielts.com/hesitation/):

- **Correct only when meaning changes.** Liz: ignore minor grammar/lexical errors; correcting them costs more fluency marks than it gains in accuracy.
- **Method matters.** Don't word-for-word repeat. Use *"Sorry, I mean…"* or *"What I'm trying to say is…"* + paraphrase. Word-for-word repetition signals poor flexibility.
- **Limit one self-correction per 30-45 seconds.** More than that and the examiner downgrades Fluency & Coherence as "noticeable hesitation".
- **Non-English words**: if you slip into L1, immediately define in English; turning a slip into a definition can demonstrate paraphrase ability and net positive for LR.

Acceptable thinking-time fillers (https://www.quora.com/Are-fillers-like-umm-uh-etc-acceptable-in-IELTS-speaking-test, https://lovetolearnenglish.com/tips-for-ielts-and-toefl/2024/9/10/should-you-correct-yourself-or-keep-talking-in-the-ielts-speaking-exam, https://simplyielts.com/ielts-speaking-common-mistakes-that-examiners-hate-2026/):

- *"That's an interesting question."*
- *"I've never really thought about that before, but…"*
- *"Let me think for a second…"*
- *"That's a tough one — off the top of my head…"*
- *"Hmm, where do I start with this…"*
- *"I'd have to say…"*
- *"Now that you mention it…"*

Penalised filler patterns: clusters of *"um, uh, er"* >3 per turn; long English-L1-English code-switches; word-by-word L1-style hesitation ("how to say… how to say…"). Examiners treat one *um* as natural; three in a row triggers a Fluency drop.

## Country-specific prep material

### Korea — Hackers, ScoreFactory, TestGlider

Korean cram-school material treats Speaking 7.0 almost entirely as a **paraphrase + lexical upgrade** drill, distinct from grammar drill which they treat as already-Band-6 plateau (https://scorefactory.io/ielts_board_1/1272750, https://blog.testglider.com/%EC%95%84%EC%9D%B4%EC%97%98%EC%B8%A0-%EC%A0%90%EC%88%98-%EC%B1%84%EC%A0%90-%EB%B0%A9%EB%B2%95-%EA%B8%B0%EC%A4%80-%EC%B4%9D%EC%A0%95%EB%A6%AC/, https://www.gohackers.com/?c=ielts/ielts_info2/ielts_method&type=url&uid=538610, https://hackerseducation.com/publications/detail?BookID=38). Key claims, translated:

- The 6→7 jump is "유창성과 어휘" (fluency and vocabulary), not pronunciation or grammar — Korean candidates over-invest in grammar drilling and under-invest in lexical upgrades.
- Lexical upgrade list explicitly recommended: *famous → acclaimed / renowned / household name; happy → on cloud nine / over the moon / thrilled to bits; very good → second to none / top-notch / first-rate; very bad → abysmal / atrocious; lots of → a wealth of / a great deal of / a multitude of; important → pivotal / crucial / paramount; difficult → daunting / gruelling; people → individuals / the general public.*
- Hackers IELTS Speaking books target 6.5 and 5.5 explicitly — Band-7 candidates are coached to memorise **chunks**, not isolated words, and to deploy them only when contextually triggered (e.g., never produce "on cloud nine" in a Part 3 abstract question about economic policy).

### China — Zhihu / 新东方 / 知乎 IELTS columns

Chinese forum and academy material (https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/609722578, https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/590612234, https://ielts.xdf.cn/202304/13059517.html, https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/608561935, http://www.fanxuefei.com/news/999902.htm, https://www.studytoura.com/ielts-speaking-phrases/, https://www.zzyasi.com/ielts-ielts/ielts-info/2024/0511/2728/):

- 词汇搭配 (collocation) is treated as the dominant lever for Band 7 — Zhihu commentators emphasise "verb+noun" collocations over isolated synonyms ("sustainable resources", "work out", "family member" are the canonical examples).
- Phrasal verb drilling by topic: health (*work out, keep fit, cut down on*), study (*catch up on, fall behind, brush up on*), career (*end up, get ahead, take up*).
- 新东方 advice: at 6.0, candidates "describe" facts; at 7.0, candidates "evaluate" using hedged language — the lexical shift is from concrete nouns to abstract evaluations (*phenomenon, tendency, implication*).
- A widely-circulated 30-phrase list on studytoura.com leans on connector chunks: *"It goes without saying that…", "Believe it or not…", "When all is said and done…", "More often than not…", "By and large…"* — Chinese Band-7 candidates tend to over-use these openers; examiners notice scripted Chinese pattern when 3+ appear in one turn.

### Vietnam — IZONE, IDP Vietnam, MobiEdu, langgo, IELTS Fighter, Vietop

Vietnamese resources are among the most thorough for grouped idiom/collocation banks (https://www.izone.edu.vn/luyen-thi-ielts/idioms-for-ielts-speaking-band-7-ielts/, https://ielts.idp.com/vietnam/about/news-and-articles/article-use-idioms-ielts-speaking, https://ielts.mobiedu.vn/news/phuong-phap-hoc/cach-dung-idioms-va-collocations-de-tang-band-speaking-ielts/344.html, https://langgo.edu.vn/list-idioms-ielts-speaking-giup-tang-band-hieu-qua-nhat, https://ielts-fighter.com/tin-tuc/idioms-ielts-speaking_mt1508717634.html, https://vietop.edu.vn/blog/luyen-ielts-speaking-band-7-trong-8-tuan/, https://jolo.edu.vn/vi/thu-vien/bi-quyet-ielts/collocation-vs-idiom-dung-the-nao-cho-dung-trong-ielts-writing.html, https://smartcom.vn/blog/tong-hop-tu-vung-ielts-7-0-speaking-hay-nhat/). Key claims (translated):

- "Lạm dụng" (abuse) of idioms is consistently warned against: IZONE explicitly notes examiners will *deduct points* (sẽ bị trừ điểm) for misused or out-of-context idioms.
- Vietnamese 90+ to 120-idiom banks are categorised by Speaking Part: Part 1 (daily life: *a piece of cake, no big deal, in a nutshell, hit the sack*), Part 2 (description: *out of the blue, once in a blue moon, the apple of my eye*), Part 3 (abstract: *food for thought, the tip of the iceberg, a double-edged sword, the bigger picture, when push comes to shove*).
- IELTS Fighter and Vietop emphasise an 8-week graded plan: weeks 1-2 master 20 high-frequency phrasal verbs; weeks 3-4 add 30 topic collocations; weeks 5-6 introduce **2 idioms per topic**, no more; weeks 7-8 wire idioms into spontaneous Part 2 narratives. The "≤2 idioms per topic" cap is the single most repeated heuristic across Vietnamese sources.

### Iran — IDP Iran, your.ielts.ru (Russian-speaking learners include Iranian diaspora)

IDP Iran's Persian/English bilingual articles (https://ielts.idp.com/iran/prepare/article-ielts-speaking-best-phrases, https://ielts.idp.com/iran/about/news-and-articles/article-use-idioms-ielts-speaking) repeat the IDP global guidance plus an Iran-specific note about **register matching**: Iranian candidates often translate idiomatic Farsi proverbs literally, which produces unintentionally formal or archaic English ("as the saying goes…") that drops LR.

Pakistani/Indian-targeted material (https://ielts.idp.com/pakistan/about/news-and-articles/article-use-idioms-ielts-speaking, https://www.kanan.co/ielts/speaking/idioms/, https://www.gradding.com/blog/ielts/idioms-for-ielts, https://www.geeksforgeeks.org/ielts/idioms-for-ielts-speaking-exam/, https://ieltsidpindia.com/blog/phrasal-verbs-for-ielts, https://leapscholar.com/blog/latest-ielts-idioms-top-phrases-asked-in-speaking-section-of-ielts/, https://leapscholar.com/exams/ielts/speaking-idioms, https://ieltsidpindia.com/prepare/vocabulary-words-for-ielts, https://www.pw.live/study-abroad/ielts/exams/idioms-for-ielts-speaking):

- Pakistani Band-7 prep tightly emphasises **modal verbs for hedging** (`could`, `might`, `may`, `would`) — local accent often produces flat assertions ("X is bad"), and hedged equivalents ("X could be problematic in certain contexts") explicitly listed as Band-7 vocabulary upgrades.
- Indian sites publish very long idiom lists (60-100+) but couple them with the warning that **register is the trap**: South-Asian English uses many idioms that are dated in British/Australian usage ("do the needful", "out of station") — Leap Scholar specifically warns against these as they don't count toward "less common idiomatic language" because they're regional cliché.
- Phrasal verb practice is the dominant exercise: IDP India provides exam-targeted phrasal verb lists (*come across, look up to, get on with, take up, run into*) framed as **safer than idioms** for risk-averse Band-7 attempts.

### Russia / Kazakhstan — yourielts.ru, masterielts, IDP Kazakhstan, habr

## Open-source tools and code

No mature open-source tool specifically scores IELTS Speaking lexical resource or grammatical range alone — the topic is generally tackled as a sub-component of full-essay scoring tools (LightSide, IELTS-AES research code) or upstream collocation/idiom detection NLP libraries. Useful adjacent resources:

- **Phrasal Verbs Project / Cambridge Phrasal Verbs Plus** — phrasal-verb annotation corpora used in IELTS coaching books; not OSS but referenced widely (https://www.amazon.com/IELTS-Speaking-Vocabulary-Builder-Band/dp/B09VRB6R5K).
- **spaCy + Cambridge English Profile API** — common pipeline for tagging vocabulary by CEFR level (B2/C1/C2) which roughly maps to Band 6/7/8. Used in academic IELTS automated scoring papers.
- **Penn Treebank-style constituency parsers** (Stanza, spaCy benepar) — can detect cleft, inversion, and conditional patterns from a transcript by matching tree shapes (`(SBAR (IN if)…)` for conditionals, `(SINV …)` for inversion).
- **NLTK collocation extractor (BigramAssocMeasures, TrigramAssocMeasures)** — can be tuned against the COCA / BNC reference lists to flag "ungrammatical collocations" (e.g., *make exam* vs *take exam*).

When the judge needs to count features (idioms-per-minute, conditional-per-Part-3, hedges-per-turn), a thin custom script over a Penn-tag parse plus a hand-curated idiom/collocation lexicon (200-300 entries pulled from the lists above) is the standard approach.

## Practical signals for an LLM judge

When reading a diarized transcript, apply these operational rules:

1. **Idiomatic density target** — Band 7+ candidates produce **1-3 idiomatic chunks per 90-second Part 2 turn** (where "chunk" = idiom, fixed expression, conventional metaphor, or phrasal verb). Zero across the whole test caps LR at Band 6. More than 4 per turn signals memorised script and caps LR at Band 6 unless the rest is fluent (https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/how-to-use-idioms-in-the-ielts-speaking-test, https://yourielts.ru/prepare-for-ielts/vocabulary/idioms-in-ielts).

2. **Topic mismatch penalty** — flag any idiom that doesn't semantically fit the question (e.g., "raining cats and dogs" in a tech question). Two or more topic-mismatched idioms across the test caps LR at Band 6 even with high overall density.

3. **Cliché concentration** — count idioms from the standard British Council Top-20 list. If >50% of all idiom uses are from that list, treat as evidence of cram-school memorisation; LR ceiling becomes Band 7. Mixing in less common idioms or contextual phrases (cleft + collocation) lifts to Band 7.5-8.

4. **Phrasal verb floor** — a Band-7 candidate should produce **at least 4-6 distinct phrasal verbs per test** (across all three parts). Zero phrasal verbs with otherwise sophisticated vocabulary is a Band-6 signal: the candidate has academic vocabulary but lacks natural spoken register.

5. **Collocation accuracy** — flag wrong verb+noun collocations (e.g., *make a research, do a decision, take a risk* → wait, *take a risk* is correct; *do a research*, *say lies*, *play sport* in BrE). Each wrong collocation costs LR. 0-1 errors is Band 8; 2-3 is Band 7; 4-6 is Band 6 (https://www.cpsenglish.com/question/24385, https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/609722578).

6. **Conditional production** — Band 7+ candidates produce **at least one second or third conditional in Part 3** (hypothetical questions invite these). Absence is not fatal, but presence is a strong positive (https://www.ieltspodcast.com/audio-tutorials/10-ielts-band-9-grammar-features-3/).

7. **Complex-sentence layering** — Count sentences with ≥2 subordinate clauses. Band 7 = at least 3 layered sentences per Part 2; Band 8 = at least 5; Band 9 = layered sentences dominate with no breakdown (https://keithspeakingacademy.com/ielts-speaking-complex-sentences-grammar/).

8. **Inversion / cleft as bonuses, not requirements** — one well-formed inversion (`Not only…`, `Never have I…`, `Rarely do…`) or cleft (`What I really enjoy is…`, `It's X that…`) is a Band 8 marker. Two or more in a 90-second turn signals memorised script and contributes nothing extra.

9. **Modal hedging frequency** — Band 7+ Part 3 answers should contain **at least 2 hedges per minute** (`might`, `could`, `tend to`, `arguably`, `it could be argued`, `I'd say`). Zero hedges = over-categorical = caps LR/GR at Band 6 because the candidate avoids the complexity that hedging requires (https://ieltsmumbai.com/blog/ielts-vocabulary--grammar-using-hedging-language-appropriately-in-ielts, https://ted-ielts.com/hedging-language/).

10. **Self-correction quota** — **one self-correction per 30-45 seconds** is acceptable. More than two per minute drops Fluency by one band. Mid-utterance word-for-word repetition ("she went, she went, she went to…") is treated as hesitation, not natural reformulation (https://ieltsliz.com/ielts-speaking-self-correction/).

11. **Acceptable fillers** — *"That's an interesting question"*, *"Let me think for a second"*, *"Off the top of my head"*, *"I've never really thought about that"* count as fluency-neutral. **Penalised fillers**: clusters of `um/uh/er` (>3 per turn), `how to say…` repeated, long English-L1 code-switches. One `um` is natural; three consecutive trigger a Fluency drop (https://lovetolearnenglish.com/tips-for-ielts-and-toefl/2024/9/10/should-you-correct-yourself-or-keep-talking-in-the-ielts-speaking-exam, https://simplyielts.com/ielts-speaking-common-mistakes-that-examiners-hate-2026/).

12. **Discourse marker variety** — Band 7+ candidates deploy at least 5 distinct discourse markers across the test (sequencing, contrasting, exemplifying, generalising, opinion). Repeating *"and"* / *"also"* / *"so"* as the only connectors caps Coherence at Band 6 (https://ielts.idp.com/kazakhstan/prepare/article-discourse-markers-ielts-speaking).

13. **Paraphrase signal** — when the examiner uses a key word, count whether the candidate re-uses it verbatim or substitutes (e.g., examiner "important" → candidate "crucial / pivotal"). 70%+ verbatim reuse caps LR at Band 6; mixing in synonyms with at least one less common item per minute = Band 7+.

14. **Memorised-chunk red flags** — formal multi-clause openers used in Part 1 simple questions ("It goes without saying that…", "When all is said and done…" in answer to "Do you like coffee?") are strong memorisation tells. Two or more in one test caps Fluency at Band 6 even if delivery is smooth (https://www.studytoura.com/ielts-speaking-phrases/, https://yourielts.ru/prepare-for-ielts/vocabulary/idioms-in-ielts).

15. **Register matching** — flag overly formal idioms in informal Part 1 ("I find it most agreeable when…") and overly informal slang in Part 3 ("yeah, kids these days are wack"). Register mismatch counts as "inappropriate choice" for LR per the Band 7 descriptor wording (https://entri.app/blog/idioms-for-ielts-speaking-formal-vs-informal-contexts/).
