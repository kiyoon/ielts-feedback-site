# Fluency {{TOPIC_TITLE}} coherence — discourse markers, natural hesitation patterns, what counts as fluent vs hesitant

_Crawled by codex on 2026-05-19T07:16:12Z. Sources cited inline._

## Summary

This topic covers the transcript-visible side of IELTS Speaking Fluency and Coherence: continuity, rate, effort, hesitation type, repetition, self-correction, spoken discourse markers, and logical topic development, which IELTS defines as talking with normal continuity/rate/effort and linking ideas into coherent connected speech (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf). The key scoring distinction is not "no pauses"; it is whether pauses and fillers help mark spoken structure or whether they interrupt the turn because the candidate is searching for words or grammar (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf). For an LLM judge, the most useful evidence is clustered: pause placement and length, mid-sentence language search, repeated repairs, overuse or flexible use of markers, and whether the answer is relevant and developed enough for the examiner to follow (https://ielts.org/researchers/our-research/research-reports/the-relationship-between-speaking-features-and-band-descriptors-a-mixed-methods-study).

## Authoritative material

### Official construct definition

- IELTS Speaking has four criteria, and Fluency and Coherence is defined as the ability to talk with "normal levels of continuity, rate and effort" and to link language and ideas into coherent connected speech (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf).
- IELTS lists key fluency indicators as speech rate that is not too slow, plus speech continuity that is not excessively interrupted by false starts, backtracking, functionless repetition, or pauses while searching for words (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf).
- IELTS lists key coherence indicators as logical sequencing of spoken sentences, clear marking of stages in discussion/narration/argument with appropriate pausing, spoken discourse markers and fillers, relevance to the purpose of the turn, and cohesive devices such as logical connectors, pronouns and conjunctions (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf).
- IELTS explicitly treats a "spoken sentence" as a speech unit that may include verbless or elliptical structures and is often marked by a pause and final intonation, so a judge should not penalise spoken ellipsis merely because it would be incomplete in writing (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf).

### Public band descriptors: transcript evidence by band

- Band 9 Fluency and Coherence: the current British Council public descriptors say the candidate is fluent with only very occasional repetition or self-correction; any hesitation prepares the next content rather than searching for words or grammar; speech is situationally appropriate; cohesive features are fully acceptable; and topic development is fully coherent and appropriately extended (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf).
- Band 8: the candidate is fluent with only very occasional repetition or self-correction; hesitation may occasionally search for words or grammar, but most hesitation is content-related; topic development is coherent, appropriate and relevant (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf).
- Band 7: the candidate can keep going and readily produce long turns without noticeable effort; some hesitation, repetition or self-correction may occur, often mid-sentence and indicating problems accessing language, but these do not affect coherence; the candidate uses spoken discourse markers, connectives and cohesive features flexibly (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf).
- Band 6: the candidate can keep going and shows willingness to produce long turns, but coherence may be lost at times because of hesitation, repetition or self-correction; discourse markers, connectives and cohesive features are used, but not always appropriately (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf).
- Band 5: the candidate can usually keep going, but relies on repetition, self-correction or slow speech; hesitations are often mid-sentence searches for basic lexis or grammar; certain discourse markers/connectives/cohesive features are overused; simple speech may be fluent while complex communication causes disfluency (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf).
- Band 4: the candidate cannot keep going without noticeable pauses, speech may be slow with frequent repetition, self-correction is common, simple sentences can be linked but connectives are often repetitious, and there are some breakdowns in coherence (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf).
- Band 3 and below: Band 3 includes frequent, sometimes long, word-search pauses and limited ability to link simple sentences; Band 2 involves lengthy pauses before most words and little communication; Band 1 has no rateable communication (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf).

### Test format and teaching guidance

- IELTS says Fluency and Coherence assesses speaking at a normal speed without too much hesitation, logical ordering of sentences and ideas, and appropriate use of cohesive devices such as linking words, pronouns and conjunctions so the message is not difficult to follow (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking).
- British Council teacher guidance describes Fluency and Coherence as the ability to speak at length, organise ideas logically, use appropriate signposting, and express/justify/discuss opinions without long pauses or repetition (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-fluency-coherence).
- British Council warns that memorising large chunks is not a good Speaking strategy and says examiners assess criteria, not appearance, body language or creativity of ideas (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-fluency-coherence).
- British Council suggests building Part 1 answers by adding details, using the Part 2 task-card bullets to structure the long turn, and in Part 3 developing reasons, effects, comparisons, examples and personal experience with clear signposting such as "In my view...", "I agree..." or "I think we'll probably see..." (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-fluency-coherence).
- British Council's Part 1 lesson plan aims to help students speak at length using connectives/discourse markers and identify phrases for opinions, reasons, results, facts and uncertainty (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/speaking_part_1_developing_fluency_and_coherence_.pdf).
- In the same British Council classroom activity, a practice round stops when a student hesitates too long, operationalised for teaching as 10 seconds, or when ideas are repeated; this is useful as a training diagnostic, not an official IELTS band cutoff (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/speaking_part_1_developing_fluency_and_coherence_.pdf).

### Discourse markers and spoken English

- Cambridge Grammar describes discourse markers such as "anyway", "right", "okay", "as I say" and "to begin with" as words or phrases used to connect, organise and manage speech/writing or express attitude (https://dictionary.cambridge.org/grammar/british-grammar/discourse-).
- Cambridge notes that spoken English commonly uses markers such as "anyway", "like", "right", "you know", "I mean" and "well", and gives functional examples: "so" begins a new part of conversation, "well" changes focus, "right" responds/agrees, and "anyway" shifts topic (https://dictionary.cambridge.org/grammar/british-grammar/discourse-).
- Cambridge explains that "you know" can mark old/shared/expected knowledge, while "you see" or "the thing is" can mark new information that the listener may not know (https://dictionary.cambridge.org/grammar/british-grammar/discourse-).
- British Council TeachingEnglish defines discourse markers as words and phrases that signpost discourse by showing turns, joining ideas, showing attitude and controlling communication, and says skilful use often indicates higher fluency and authentic-language control (https://www.teachingenglish.org.uk/professional-development/teachers/knowing-subject/d-h/discourse-markers).
- IDP IELTS says connectives help join clauses and signal relations such as reason/result, while discourse markers such as "actually", "in fact", "well", "probably" and "unfortunately" keep speech going, signal tone/attitude, and can sometimes replace a silent pause; IDP also warns not to overuse them (https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-ielts-speaking-fluency-and-coherence).

### IELTS research evidence

- The IELTS-funded Seedhouse, Harris, Naeb and Üstünel mixed-methods study used 60 IELTS Speaking test recordings, with 15 tests each at bands 5, 6, 7 and 8, to study which speaking features distinguish bands (https://ielts.org/researchers/our-research/research-reports/the-relationship-between-speaking-features-and-band-descriptors-a-mixed-methods-study).
- That study operationalised fluency partly as pause length per 100 words and found significant differences between score bands 5 and 8, so a judge may use pause burden as one fluency signal but should avoid treating pause count alone as a complete band scorer (https://ielts.org/researchers/our-research/research-reports/the-relationship-between-speaking-features-and-band-descriptors-a-mixed-methods-study).
- The same study says qualitative analysis did not find one single speaking feature that separates all bands; instead, a cluster of assessable features tends to lead toward a score, which supports combining pause, repetition, self-correction, topic development and marker use rather than using one feature in isolation (https://ielts.org/researchers/our-research/research-reports/the-relationship-between-speaking-features-and-band-descriptors-a-mixed-methods-study).

## Country-specific prep material

### Korea

- IDP IELTS Korea's country page says coherent speech with natural pauses and fillers makes ideas easier to communicate, while long pauses to decide what to say or access vocabulary/grammar make listeners lose track (https://ieltskorea.org/korea/prepare/article-discourse-markers-ielts-speaking/en-gb).
- The same IDP Korea article treats discourse markers as tools to organise speech, connect ideas, express attitude, and buy thinking time; it says a stalling phrase such as "Let me think about that for a minute..." is better than seconds of silence or intrusive "umm/err" (https://ieltskorea.org/korea/prepare/article-discourse-markers-ielts-speaking/en-gb).
- IDP Korea gives functional marker categories for sequencing, adding information, opinion/attitude, comparison, contrast, examples, stalling and result, and it warns that repetitive "firstly/secondly/thirdly..." overuse becomes noticeable and inappropriate (https://ieltskorea.org/korea/prepare/article-discourse-markers-ielts-speaking/en-gb).
- A Korean-language Practiceme guide frames "well", "you know", "I mean" and "actually" as short expressions that make spoken English sound less textbook-like, with roles such as buying time, softening statements and keeping the listener engaged; it also says these should be added naturally rather than in every sentence (https://practiceme.app/ko/blog/english-filler-words-conversation).
- Korean online IELTS-speaking provider Skybel advertises IELTS Speaking training around logical/consistent answer structure, spontaneous natural answers without scripts, and repeated practice with correction and feedback; this reflects a local prep emphasis on structuring ideas while maintaining natural spoken delivery (https://www.skybel.co.kr/sub/edpro_02.php).

### China

- IDP China hosts a Chinese public version of the IELTS Speaking band descriptors; its Fluency and Coherence translation mirrors the official boundary that Band 9 hesitation is content-based rather than word/grammar search, Band 7 may include language-related hesitation but no coherence loss, Band 6 may lose coherence through hesitation/repetition/self-correction, and Band 5 overuses some connectives/discourse markers (https://www.idp.cn/special/subject/IELTS/pdf/kscj_f2.pdf).
- New Channel Wuhan explains the Fluency and Coherence dimension as natural speed plus logical expression, not "the faster the better"; it accepts brief content-related hesitation but flags long pauses, frequent self-correction, meaningless repetition and excessive fillers as risks (https://wh.xhd.cn/info/ieltsSpeak/1059815.html).
- New Channel Wuhan uses a local prep rule that pauses over 3 seconds are problematic; because the official IELTS public descriptors do not give a 3-second rule, an LLM judge should treat this as a coaching heuristic rather than an official cutoff (https://wh.xhd.cn/info/ieltsSpeak/1059815.html).
- Talk915's Chinese band-score guide reproduces the key FC distinctions: Band 5 relies on repetition/self-correction/slow speech and overuses discourse markers; Band 6 uses a range but not always appropriately; Band 7 uses spoken markers/connectives/cohesive devices flexibly; Band 8 hesitation is usually content-related (https://www.talk915.com/web/article-details/328).
- Global Education Beijing argues that speed is not directly equivalent to fluency and that occasional topic-related pauses can be acceptable if the candidate is willing to speak at length; it also notes that one-word or very short answers leave too little evidence for fluency assessment (https://m.beijing.gedu.org/contents/9158/333616.html).

### Vietnam

- IDP Vietnam explains discourse markers as phrases that link ideas and help transmit ideas fluently and coherently, and it explicitly links them to the Fluency and Coherence criterion, which accounts for 25% of the Speaking score (https://ielts.idp.com/vietnam/about/news-and-articles/article-discourse-markers-ielts-speaking).
- IDP Vietnam says discourse markers can act as fillers for thinking time; "Let me think about that for a minute..." is presented as better than silence or continuous "umm... err..." when the candidate needs a moment to start (https://ielts.idp.com/vietnam/about/news-and-articles/article-discourse-markers-ielts-speaking).
- IDP Vietnam groups useful markers by function: opening/closing, topic shift ("Anyway", "By the way"), addition ("What's more", "Besides", "Additionally"), contrast ("On the one hand", "That being said"), certainty ("Definitely", "Without a doubt"), honesty/admission ("To be honest", "I must admit"), short responses ("I see", "Really", "Exactly") and hedging ("Apparently", "Sort of", "Perhaps") (https://ielts.idp.com/vietnam/about/news-and-articles/article-discourse-markers-ielts-speaking).
- Vietnamese RES describes FC as involving ability to speak longer answers, use discourse markers/conjunctions, maintain coherent sentences and stable speed, and limit self-correction; it says a three-word Part 1 answer such as "Yes, of course" is too short because the candidate should explain or give an example (https://res.edu.vn/unit-2-tieu-chi-cham-diem-ielts-speaking/).
- IELTS LangGo says discourse markers help open, lead and connect speech, organise/develop ideas, and express attitude; it also warns that markers used as fillers should not be abused and that lower-band candidates often overuse simple markers while Band 7+ candidates use varied markers reasonably and flexibly (https://langgo.edu.vn/discourse-markers-la-gi-cach-su-dung-hieu-qua-trong-ielts-speaking).

### Iran / Persian-language prep

- Persian-language IELTS2 defines Fluency and Coherence as speaking without long pauses or excessive "um/uh" fillers and organising ideas logically, and it recommends avoiding long pauses/unnecessary repetition, using linking expressions such as "for example" or "in contrast", and practising conversation/shadowing (https://ielts2.com/understanding-the-ielts-speaking-band-descriptors/).
- IELTS2's Persian guide on asking the examiner to repeat a question says a polite clarification request such as "Could you please repeat the question?" or "I didn't quite catch that" is normal and should not harm the score if it is clear and appropriate, but repeated/baseless requests used just to buy time may affect Fluency (https://ielts2.com/can-i-ask-the-ielts-examiner-to-repeat-the-question/).
- For transcript scoring, the Persian prep advice is useful for separating interactional repair from fluency weakness: one clear clarification request is an interactional move, whereas repeated clarification requests that replace answering can show comprehension or fluency strain (https://ielts2.com/can-i-ask-the-ielts-examiner-to-repeat-the-question/).

### India and Pakistan

- IDP India reproduces public Speaking band descriptors: Band 7 includes speaking at length without noticeable effort or loss of coherence and using connectives/discourse markers with flexibility; Band 6 includes occasional coherence loss from repetition/self-correction/hesitation and markers that are not always appropriate; Band 5 includes marker overuse and fluency problems in complex communication (https://ieltsidpindia.com/information/ielts-band-scores/speaking).
- IDP's global preparation article, used across IDP markets, warns that pausing and hesitation are natural but should not dominate, that speaking too slowly can reduce the amount of language produced, and that speaking fast is not the same as good fluency (https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-ielts-speaking-fluency-and-coherence).
- British Council Pakistan hosts a Fluency and Coherence IELTS video page that says the video explains the FC aspects considered in IELTS Speaking and provides tips for improving English fluency (https://www.britishcouncil.pk/exam/ielts/prepare/videos/fluency-coherence).
- The Pakistan-facing British Council page is consistent with the global British Council teacher guidance: improvement targets should be tied to the IELTS criteria rather than to memorised content, accent imitation, or non-linguistic performance features (https://www.britishcouncil.pk/exam/ielts/prepare/videos/fluency-coherence; https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-fluency-coherence).

### Indonesia

- The search pass found limited high-quality Indonesian-language IELTS prep material specific to discourse markers and hesitation; the relevant Indonesian source found was an English-education journal item from Universitas Tanjungpura that summarises IELTS Speaking assessment criteria and notes fluency indicators such as speech rate/continuity and coherent connected discourse (https://jurnal.untan.ac.id/index.php/JEEP/article/viewFile/75380/75676602979).
- Because no strong Indonesian prep article on "well / you know / I mean" marker use was found in this crawl, the judge should not infer Indonesia-specific scoring norms; use the official IELTS/IDP/British Council criteria for Indonesian candidates just as for all candidates (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf).

### Russia and Kazakhstan

- Russian-language IELTS777 presents the IELTS Speaking official scoring criteria and includes the Fluency and Coherence descriptor sequence, including Band 7 use of connectives/discourse markers with flexibility and Band 5 overuse of some discourse markers/connectives (https://ielts777.com/speaking/speaking-assessment.html).
- Kazakhstan prep site MyIELTS.kz hosts an IELTS Speaking Band Descriptors PDF that follows the official wording on spoken discourse markers, connectives, cohesive features, repetition, self-correction, slow speech and word-search pauses (https://myielts.kz/wp-content/uploads/2023/06/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).
- The Russian/Kazakh material reinforces that local prep markets translate the same official FC boundary rather than introducing a separate regional standard: appropriate flexible spoken markers support Band 7+, while repetitious simple connectives and marker overuse are Band 4-5 evidence (https://ielts777.com/speaking/speaking-assessment.html; https://myielts.kz/wp-content/uploads/2023/06/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).

### UK, Australia and Canada native-perspective material

- UK British Council TeachingEnglish treats discourse markers as authentic native-speaker language for controlling turns, joining ideas and showing attitude, and says skilful use often signals higher fluency (https://www.teachingenglish.org.uk/professional-development/teachers/knowing-subject/d-h/discourse-markers).
- Cambridge Grammar, a UK-based reference, distinguishes spoken markers from written-style linking by showing functions such as "well" for focus change, "right" for response/agreement, "anyway" for topic shift and "you know" for shared knowledge (https://dictionary.cambridge.org/grammar/british-grammar/discourse-).
- IDP's Australia-linked preparation article stresses that natural pace is not fast speech, that hesitation is acceptable when it does not dominate, and that markers such as "actually", "in fact", "well", "probably" and "unfortunately" can maintain flow and signal attitude (https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-ielts-speaking-fluency-and-coherence).
- IELTS Vancouver, a Canadian IELTS test-centre blog, advises candidates to distinguish pauses for ideas from pauses for words/grammar, avoid repeatedly restarting to self-correct, record themselves for pauses/fillers/repetitions/self-corrections, and use discourse markers to connect topic-development steps (https://ieltsvancouver.com/2023/03/03/ielts-speaking-explained-fluency-coherence/).

## Open-source tools and code

These tools are relevant for building measurement features around transcripts/audio, but none should be treated as an IELTS band scorer without validation against the official descriptors and human-rated IELTS data; IELTS research found that no single speaking feature separates all bands (https://ielts.org/researchers/our-research/research-reports/the-relationship-between-speaking-features-and-band-descriptors-a-mixed-methods-study).

- WhisperX can provide word-level ASR timestamps and diarization/alignment hooks that help estimate pauses, speech rate and examiner/candidate turns; GitHub URL: https://github.com/m-bain/whisperX; license is listed in the repository; last-updated date was not verified in this crawl.
- pyannote-audio can support speaker diarization before transcript scoring, which is useful because the IELTS judge should isolate candidate speech from examiner prompts; GitHub URL: https://github.com/pyannote/pyannote-audio; license is listed in the repository; last-updated date was not verified in this crawl.
- openSMILE extracts acoustic/prosodic features that can complement transcript features for speech rate, pauses and connected-speech/prosody studies; GitHub URL: https://github.com/audeering/opensmile; license is listed in the repository; last-updated date was not verified in this crawl.
- Parselmouth exposes Praat functionality from Python and can help extract duration, pitch and pause-related acoustic features when audio is available; GitHub URL: https://github.com/YannickJadoul/Parselmouth; license is listed in the repository; last-updated date was not verified in this crawl.
- CLP Research's deep_disfluency project is directly relevant to detecting repairs, restarts and filled pauses in spontaneous speech transcripts; GitHub URL: https://github.com/clp-research/deep_disfluency; license/last-updated date were not verified in this crawl.

## Practical signals for an LLM judge

- Separate **content-planning hesitation** from **language-search hesitation**: Band 8-9 hesitation is mostly or entirely content-related, while Band 7 may show some language-related hesitation without coherence loss, Band 6 may lose coherence at times, and Band 5 often hesitates mid-sentence for basic lexis/grammar (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf).
- Treat clause-boundary pauses that mark stages in a narration/argument as potentially coherent, but treat pauses inside a basic phrase while the candidate searches for a word as negative fluency evidence; this follows IELTS's distinction between appropriate pausing/discourse marking and pausing to search for words (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf).
- Count false starts, backtracking, functionless repetition and repeated self-correction as disfluency evidence, especially when they interrupt continuity rather than clarify meaning (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf).
- Use **pause burden per candidate words** as one quantitative feature; IELTS research used pause length per 100 words and found significant band 5 vs band 8 differences, but the same research warns against one-feature scoring because bands are distinguished by clusters (https://ielts.org/researchers/our-research/research-reports/the-relationship-between-speaking-features-and-band-descriptors-a-mixed-methods-study).
- Do not use a rigid "3-second IELTS rule" as official scoring; some Chinese prep sources use 3 seconds as a coaching warning, while British Council classroom material uses 10 seconds as a practice-game stop condition, and neither is presented as an official band cutoff (https://wh.xhd.cn/info/ieltsSpeak/1059815.html; https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/speaking_part_1_developing_fluency_and_coherence_.pdf).
- Reward functional, varied spoken markers: "well" to preface a non-simple answer, "actually/in fact" to correct or add contrast, "I mean" to reformulate, "the thing is" to introduce a reason, "on top of that" to add a point, and "anyway/by the way" to shift topic when appropriate (https://dictionary.cambridge.org/grammar/british-grammar/discourse-; https://ieltskorea.org/korea/prepare/article-discourse-markers-ielts-speaking/en-gb).
- Penalise marker **overuse** when the same item is repeated mechanically ("firstly... secondly... thirdly..." in every answer, or "you know" every clause), because Band 5 descriptors mention overuse and IDP Korea gives repetitive sequencing as a bad example (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf; https://ieltskorea.org/korea/prepare/article-discourse-markers-ielts-speaking/en-gb).
- Distinguish spoken discourse markers from written essay connectors: "moreover" and "furthermore" can be valid, but natural spoken control often includes "well", "actually", "I guess", "it depends", "to be honest", "the thing is" and similar interactional markers (https://dictionary.cambridge.org/grammar/british-grammar/discourse-; https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/speaking_part_1_developing_fluency_and_coherence_.pdf).
- A transcript with only short bare answers gives weak evidence for higher FC because British Council and local prep sources emphasise developing answers with reasons, examples, comparisons and personal experience rather than stopping after a minimal answer (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-fluency-coherence; https://res.edu.vn/unit-2-tieu-chi-cham-diem-ielts-speaking/).
- Do not penalise a candidate for being interrupted by the examiner at the end of a timed Part 2 or after a sufficient answer; score the candidate's continuity, coherence and development before the interruption, not whether the examiner allowed a full conclusion (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-fluency-coherence).
- Treat one polite clarification request as interactional management rather than fluency failure, but repeated requests that appear to buy time or replace answering can contribute to fluency/coherence concerns (https://ielts2.com/can-i-ask-the-ielts-examiner-to-repeat-the-question/).
- Do not reward speed alone: IDP says speaking too slowly can reduce language produced, but speaking fast is not proof of fluency; Chinese prep also warns that fast speech is not the same as fluent speech (https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-ielts-speaking-fluency-and-coherence; https://wh.xhd.cn/info/ieltsSpeak/1059815.html).
- Treat occasional self-correction as compatible with Band 7 if coherence remains intact, but treat reliance on self-correction/repetition/slow speech to keep going as Band 5-type evidence and frequent self-correction with noticeable pauses as Band 4-type evidence (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf).
- When scoring from transcript only, avoid penalising elliptical spoken units that are coherent in context; IELTS explicitly recognises spoken sentences may be verbless or elliptical and still function as sentence-like units (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf).
- Use a cluster decision: high FC requires enough developed candidate speech, logical sequencing, appropriate cohesive devices, and manageable hesitation/repetition/self-correction; a single good marker list cannot compensate for long language-search pauses or coherence breakdowns (https://ielts.org/researchers/our-research/research-reports/the-relationship-between-speaking-features-and-band-descriptors-a-mixed-methods-study; https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf).
