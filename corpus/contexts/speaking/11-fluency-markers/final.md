# Fluency & coherence — discourse markers, natural hesitation patterns, what counts as fluent vs hesitant

_Merged from codex + claude crawls on 2026-05-19T07:30:00Z._

## Summary

Fluency & Coherence (F&C) is one of the four equally-weighted IELTS Speaking
criteria (25% of the band) and is the most sensitive to **how** speech is
produced rather than what is in it. IELTS defines the construct as talking
with "normal levels of continuity, rate and effort" and linking ideas into
coherent connected speech
(https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf).
The public band descriptors hinge on three diagnostic axes: (1) the *type* of
hesitation (content-related vs language-related), (2) the *quality* of
repetition and self-correction (occasional and natural vs frequent and
flow-breaking), and (3) the *flexibility* with which spoken discourse
markers, connectives and cohesive features are used
(https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf).
The key scoring distinction is not "no pauses"; it is whether pauses and
fillers mark spoken structure or whether they interrupt the turn because the
candidate is searching for words/grammar. IELTS research confirms that no
single feature distinguishes all bands — pause burden, repetition,
self-correction, marker flexibility and topic development must be combined
as a cluster
(https://ielts.org/researchers/our-research/research-reports/the-relationship-between-speaking-features-and-band-descriptors-a-mixed-methods-study).

## Authoritative material

### Official construct definition

- IELTS Speaking has four criteria, and Fluency and Coherence is defined as
  the ability to talk with "normal levels of continuity, rate and effort"
  and to link language and ideas into coherent connected speech
  (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf).
- IELTS lists key fluency indicators as speech rate that is not too slow,
  plus speech continuity that is not excessively interrupted by false
  starts, backtracking, functionless repetition, or pauses while searching
  for words
  (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf).
- IELTS lists key coherence indicators as logical sequencing of spoken
  sentences, clear marking of stages in discussion/narration/argument with
  appropriate pausing, spoken discourse markers and fillers, relevance to
  the purpose of the turn, and cohesive devices such as logical connectors,
  pronouns and conjunctions
  (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf).
- IELTS explicitly treats a "spoken sentence" as a speech unit that may
  include verbless or elliptical structures and is often marked by a pause
  and final intonation, so a judge should not penalise spoken ellipsis
  merely because it would be incomplete in writing
  (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf).
- IELTS's official Speaking format page reinforces that F&C assesses
  speaking at a normal speed without too much hesitation, logical ordering
  of sentences and ideas, and appropriate cohesive devices
  (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking).

### Public band descriptors (Fluency & Coherence, bands 4–9)

Quoted verbatim from the public version of the IELTS Speaking band
descriptors (Cambridge Assessment English / British Council / IDP).
Primary source:
https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf;
Cambridge mirror:
https://assets.cambridgeenglish.org/webinars/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf;
verbatim transcription:
https://ieltsagape.wordpress.com/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors/.

- **Band 9** — "Speaks fluently with only rare repetition or
  self-correction; any hesitation is content-related rather than to find
  words or grammar. Speaks coherently with fully appropriate cohesive
  features. Develops topics fully and appropriately."
- **Band 8** — "Speaks fluently with only occasional repetition or
  self-correction; hesitation is usually content-related and only rarely to
  search for language. Develops topics coherently and appropriately."
- **Band 7** — "Speaks at length without noticeable effort or loss of
  coherence. May demonstrate language-related hesitation at times, or some
  repetition and/or self-correction. Uses a range of connectives and
  discourse markers with some flexibility."
- **Band 6** — "Is willing to speak at length, though may lose coherence at
  times due to occasional repetition, self-correction or hesitation. Uses a
  range of connectives and discourse markers but not always appropriately."
- **Band 5** — "Usually maintains flow of speech but uses repetition, self
  correction and/or slow speech to keep going. May over-use certain
  connectives and discourse markers. Produces simple speech fluently, but
  more complex communication causes fluency problems."
- **Band 4** — "Cannot respond without noticeable pauses and may speak
  slowly, with frequent repetition and self-correction. Links basic
  sentences but with repetitious use of simple connectives and some
  breakdowns in coherence."
- **Bands 3–1** (codex paraphrase of the official PDF): Band 3 includes
  frequent, sometimes long, word-search pauses and limited ability to link
  simple sentences; Band 2 involves lengthy pauses before most words and
  little communication; Band 1 has no rateable communication
  (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf).

The descriptors collapse into a clean decision tree: **what kind of
hesitation is happening?** Content-related = upper bands; language-related =
mid-to-lower; "noticeable pauses" = Band 4 or below. See also the
descriptor explainer at
https://ielts-gpt.com/blog/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors-explained.

### Hesitation typology: content-related vs language-related

The single most diagnostic distinction in the descriptors. Examiner-side
sources reduce it to:

- **Content-related** = pausing to formulate or evaluate an idea ("Let me
  think… I suppose the biggest reason would be…"). Tolerated at Band 8;
  expected at Band 9 (since native speakers do this too).
- **Language-related** = pausing to retrieve a word, conjugate a verb, or
  reorder a clause ("My favourite… eh… my favourite thing to… er… to do…
  is…"). Acceptable "at times" at Band 7; characteristic of Band 6;
  pervasive at Band 4–5.

References:
https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/mastering-fluency-band-9-ielts-speaking-test-hesitation-shaji-ali;
https://simplyielts.com/ielts-speaking-fluency-coherence-score-band-8/;
https://ted-ielts.com/hesitation/. TED IELTS adds a third rule: **L1
fillers are penalised harder than English ones** (음, 那个, ano… all read
as L2 leakage to the examiner).

### Self-correction policy (Liz Tarp / ieltsliz.com)

Liz Tarp's self-correction guidance
(https://ieltsliz.com/ielts-speaking-self-correction/), widely cited by
examiners, gives two operational rules:

1. **Never stop to correct minor grammar or vocabulary errors.** "You will
   only show the examiner that you struggle with fluency and need to keep
   stopping to correct your English."
2. **Correct only if you misstated the meaning** — and when you do,
   *paraphrase*, never repeat verbatim. "Use different language as well as
   altering the meaning."

This matches the band descriptors: at Band 7+, self-corrections are
"occasional"; at Band 5 they "keep [the speaker] going" — i.e., they're
crutches, not flourishes.

### IDP's canonical functional inventory of discourse markers

From IDP's official discourse-markers article
(https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-discourse-markers-ielts-speaking),
the most authoritative non-descriptor list (IDP co-owns IELTS):

- **Sequencing**: first / first of all / firstly / to begin with / the next
  one / moving on to / secondly / thirdly / subsequently / on top of that /
  later / after this / finally.
- **Adding information**: another thing that comes to mind / also / and /
  besides / additionally / another good example of this is / another reason
  for this / and one more thing.
- **Opinion & attitude**: unfortunately / however / actually / to be
  honest / definitely / essentially / frankly / basically / clearly / I'm
  afraid / if you ask me / sadly / thankfully / in fact / seriously / as a
  matter of fact.
- **Comparing**: similarly / in the same way / equally / likewise / in a
  similar fashion / if I compare it to my country.
- **Contrasting**: however / although / instead of / despite / on one hand /
  on the other hand / in the opposite way / in contrast / whereas.
- **Giving examples**: a great example of this is / for example / for
  instance / a personal example is / in other words / a striking example of
  this / a classic example is / a clear example of this can be seen /
  such as / illustrated by.
- **Stalling** (the most-tested category for fluency): let me think about
  that / that's a difficult/interesting question / I haven't thought of
  that before / well… / actually / basically.
- **Result**: as a result / because of this / therefore / consequently /
  so / then.
- **Generalising**: generally / broadly speaking / as a rule / on the
  whole / it is often said that / in most cases / the vast majority of / a
  small minority of.

IDP explicitly warns that **repetitive use detracts from naturalness** —
the marker must "genuinely express your intended function rather than [be]
used mechanically." IDP also distinguishes connectives (which join clauses
and signal relations like reason/result) from discourse markers proper
("actually", "in fact", "well", "probably", "unfortunately"), which keep
speech going and signal tone/attitude
(https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-ielts-speaking-fluency-and-coherence).

### Discourse markers and spoken English (UK reference grammars)

- Cambridge Grammar describes discourse markers such as "anyway", "right",
  "okay", "as I say" and "to begin with" as words or phrases used to
  connect, organise and manage speech/writing or express attitude. It
  gives functional examples: "so" begins a new part of conversation,
  "well" changes focus, "right" responds/agrees, and "anyway" shifts
  topic. Cambridge also explains that "you know" can mark old/shared/
  expected knowledge, while "you see" or "the thing is" can mark new
  information the listener may not know
  (https://dictionary.cambridge.org/grammar/british-grammar/discourse-).
- British Council TeachingEnglish defines discourse markers as words and
  phrases that signpost discourse by showing turns, joining ideas, showing
  attitude and controlling communication, and says skilful use often
  indicates higher fluency and authentic-language control
  (https://www.teachingenglish.org.uk/professional-development/teachers/knowing-subject/d-h/discourse-markers).

### British Council teacher guidance

- British Council teacher guidance describes F&C as the ability to speak at
  length, organise ideas logically, use appropriate signposting, and
  express/justify/discuss opinions without long pauses or repetition
  (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-fluency-coherence).
- British Council warns that memorising large chunks is not a good
  Speaking strategy and says examiners assess criteria, not appearance,
  body language or creativity of ideas
  (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-fluency-coherence).
- British Council suggests building Part 1 answers by adding details,
  using the Part 2 task-card bullets to structure the long turn, and in
  Part 3 developing reasons, effects, comparisons, examples and personal
  experience with clear signposting such as "In my view…", "I agree…" or
  "I think we'll probably see…"
  (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-fluency-coherence).
- British Council's Part 1 lesson plan aims to help students speak at
  length using connectives/discourse markers and identify phrases for
  opinions, reasons, results, facts and uncertainty
  (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/speaking_part_1_developing_fluency_and_coherence_.pdf).
- In the same British Council classroom activity, a practice round stops
  when a student hesitates too long, operationalised for teaching as **10
  seconds**, or when ideas are repeated; this is a training diagnostic,
  not an official IELTS band cutoff
  (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/speaking_part_1_developing_fluency_and_coherence_.pdf).

### IELTS research evidence

- The IELTS-funded Seedhouse, Harris, Naeb and Üstünel mixed-methods study
  used 60 IELTS Speaking test recordings (15 each at bands 5, 6, 7, 8) to
  study which speaking features distinguish bands
  (https://ielts.org/researchers/our-research/research-reports/the-relationship-between-speaking-features-and-band-descriptors-a-mixed-methods-study).
- That study operationalised fluency partly as **pause length per 100
  words** and found significant differences between bands 5 and 8 — pause
  burden is a usable fluency signal, but pause count alone is not a
  complete band scorer.
- The same study says qualitative analysis did not find one single
  speaking feature that separates all bands; instead a *cluster* of
  assessable features tends to lead toward a score, which supports
  combining pause, repetition, self-correction, topic development and
  marker use rather than using any feature in isolation.

## Country-specific prep material

### South Korea (한국)

- Skybel — 연결어 오답 유형 (connective error patterns) flags four
  high-frequency Korean-L1 errors that drag F&C scores: *because…so*
  redundancy ("Because I was tired, so I went to bed", the
  causal+result calque from 왜냐하면…그래서); *in addition to…also*
  doubling; *despite* used as a conjunction ("Despite I was tired"); and
  *although ↔ however* confusion (clausal vs sentential). The blog
  stresses that 연결어를 무작정 끼워 넣기보다는 (rather than blindly
  inserting connectives), accuracy and contextual fit matter more than
  inventory
  (https://blog.skybel.co.kr/blog/%EC%95%84%EC%9D%B4%EC%97%98%EC%B8%A0%20%EC%8A%A4%ED%94%BC%ED%82%B9/%EC%95%84%EC%9D%B4%EC%97%98%EC%B8%A0-%EC%8A%A4%ED%94%BC%ED%82%B9-%EA%B3%A0%EB%93%9D%EC%A0%90%EC%9D%84-%EB%A7%89%EB%8A%94-%EC%97%B0%EA%B2%B0%EC%96%B4-%EC%98%A4%EB%8B%B5-%EC%9C%A0%ED%98%95-%ED%97%B7%EA%B0%88%EB%A6%AC%EB%8A%94-%ED%91%9C%ED%98%84-%EC%A0%95%EB%A6%AC).
- Skybel's Linking Words 15선 recommends a tight Band-7 set: *however, in
  contrast, on the other hand, for instance, as a result, on top of that,
  to be honest, what I mean is*
  (https://blog.skybel.co.kr/blog/%EC%95%84%EC%9D%B4%EC%97%98%EC%B8%A0%20%EC%8A%A4%ED%94%BC%ED%82%B9/%EC%95%84%EC%9D%B4%EC%97%98%EC%B8%A0-%EC%8A%A4%ED%94%BC%ED%82%B9-%EC%8B%A4%EC%A0%84-%ED%91%9C%ED%98%84-%EA%B3%A0%EB%93%9D%EC%A0%90%EC%9D%84-%EB%B6%80%EB%A5%B4%EB%8A%94-Linking-Words-15%EA%B0%80%EC%A7%80-%EC%99%84%EB%B2%BD-%EC%A0%95%EB%A6%AC).
- Skybel's IELTS Speaking course page emphasises logical/consistent answer
  structure, spontaneous natural answers without scripts, and repeated
  practice with correction and feedback
  (https://www.skybel.co.kr/sub/edpro_02.php).
- Hackers — 6.0→7.0 jump tips
  (https://www.gohackers.com/?c=ielts/ielts_info2/ielts_method&type=url&uid=538610)
  and Score Factory Part 2 vocabulary
  (https://scorefactory.io/ielts_board_5/1497081) emphasise replacing
  음/어 (Korean filler equivalents of "um") with English stalling phrases
  ("That's a good point", "Let me think for a second"), since L1 fillers
  are immediately flagged.
- IDP IELTS Korea's country page says coherent speech with natural pauses
  and fillers makes ideas easier to communicate, while long pauses to
  decide what to say or access vocabulary/grammar make listeners lose
  track. It treats discourse markers as tools to organise speech, connect
  ideas, express attitude, and buy thinking time; a stalling phrase such
  as "Let me think about that for a minute…" is better than seconds of
  silence or intrusive "umm/err". It also warns that repetitive
  "firstly/secondly/thirdly…" overuse becomes noticeable and inappropriate
  (https://ieltskorea.org/korea/prepare/article-discourse-markers-ielts-speaking/en-gb).
- A Korean-language Practiceme guide frames "well", "you know", "I mean"
  and "actually" as short expressions that make spoken English sound less
  textbook-like, with roles such as buying time, softening statements and
  keeping the listener engaged; it warns these should be added naturally
  rather than in every sentence
  (https://practiceme.app/ko/blog/english-filler-words-conversation).

### China (中国)

- 新东方 — 雅思口语上7谈何容易 argues the Band-6→7 gap is mainly about
  *flexibility* of discourse markers, not vocabulary. Chinese 9-band
  tutors push students to drop *first / second / and then* triplets (the
  calque of 首先…然后…) and adopt varied phrasing ("to begin with", "on
  top of that", "what's more")
  (https://ielts.xdf.cn/202210/12339985.html).
- 知乎 — Connectives and Discourse markers splits markers into 逻辑关系
  连接词 (logical connectives: firstly, however, therefore) and 话语
  标记词 (pragmatic markers: well, you know, I mean, actually, the
  thing is). It warns that Chinese candidates over-rely on the first set
  and under-use the second — the inverse of native speakers
  (https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/121677680).
- 知乎 — 雅思口语评分标准详解 notes that "keep going" replaced "speak
  at length" in the 2023 descriptor refresh: examiners reward sustained
  delivery even with imperfect grammar, whereas stop-start speech that's
  grammatically clean still caps at Band 6
  (https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/595039373).
- IDP China hosts a Chinese public version of the IELTS Speaking band
  descriptors; its F&C translation mirrors the official boundary that
  Band 9 hesitation is content-based, Band 7 may include language-related
  hesitation but no coherence loss, Band 6 may lose coherence through
  hesitation/repetition/self-correction, and Band 5 overuses some
  connectives/discourse markers
  (https://www.idp.cn/special/subject/IELTS/pdf/kscj_f2.pdf).
- New Channel Wuhan explains the F&C dimension as natural speed plus
  logical expression, not "the faster the better"; it accepts brief
  content-related hesitation but flags long pauses, frequent
  self-correction, meaningless repetition and excessive fillers as risks.
  It uses a local prep rule that pauses over **3 seconds** are
  problematic — treat this as a coaching heuristic rather than an
  official cutoff
  (https://wh.xhd.cn/info/ieltsSpeak/1059815.html).
- Talk915's Chinese band-score guide reproduces the key F&C distinctions:
  Band 5 relies on repetition/self-correction/slow speech and overuses
  discourse markers; Band 6 uses a range but not always appropriately;
  Band 7 uses spoken markers/connectives/cohesive devices flexibly;
  Band 8 hesitation is usually content-related
  (https://www.talk915.com/web/article-details/328).
- Global Education Beijing argues that speed is not directly equivalent
  to fluency and that occasional topic-related pauses can be acceptable
  if the candidate is willing to speak at length; one-word or very short
  answers leave too little evidence for fluency assessment
  (https://m.beijing.gedu.org/contents/9158/333616.html).

### Vietnam (Việt Nam)

- Net English — Discourse Markers Band 7.0+ organises markers into three
  functional clusters: *connecting ideas* (sequencing, contrast,
  illustration, result), *managing conversation* (stalling, rephrasing,
  topic shifts), and *expressing attitude* (certainty/uncertainty/
  surprise/emphasis). Warns against direct Vietnamese-to-English calques
  ("after that then" from *sau đó thì*)
  (https://netenglish.edu.vn/discourse-markers-trong-ielts-speaking/).
- Zim.vn paraphrases the public descriptors for Vietnamese learners and
  stresses that at Band 6–7, hesitation/stuttering are similar in
  *amount* but differ in *cause* — the Band-7 speaker hesitates while
  choosing among options, the Band-6 speaker hesitates while retrieving
  any option (https://zim.vn/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors).
- Izone — Discourse markers là gì flags that overuse ("the more, the
  better") produces robotic speech that examiners immediately recognise
  (https://www.izone.edu.vn/luyen-thi-ielts/discourse-markers-la-gi/).
- IDP Vietnam explains discourse markers as phrases that link ideas and
  help transmit ideas fluently and coherently, and explicitly links them
  to F&C (25% of the Speaking score). It groups useful markers by
  function: opening/closing, topic shift ("Anyway", "By the way"),
  addition ("What's more", "Besides", "Additionally"), contrast ("On the
  one hand", "That being said"), certainty ("Definitely", "Without a
  doubt"), honesty/admission ("To be honest", "I must admit"), short
  responses ("I see", "Really", "Exactly") and hedging ("Apparently",
  "Sort of", "Perhaps"). It presents "Let me think about that for a
  minute…" as better than silence or continuous "umm… err…"
  (https://ielts.idp.com/vietnam/about/news-and-articles/article-discourse-markers-ielts-speaking).
- Vietnamese RES describes F&C as involving ability to speak longer
  answers, use discourse markers/conjunctions, maintain coherent
  sentences and stable speed, and limit self-correction; a three-word
  Part 1 answer such as "Yes, of course" is too short because the
  candidate should explain or give an example
  (https://res.edu.vn/unit-2-tieu-chi-cham-diem-ielts-speaking/).
- LangGo says discourse markers help open, lead and connect speech,
  organise/develop ideas, and express attitude; warns that markers used
  as fillers should not be abused and that lower-band candidates often
  overuse simple markers while Band 7+ candidates use varied markers
  reasonably and flexibly
  (https://langgo.edu.vn/discourse-markers-la-gi-cach-su-dung-hieu-qua-trong-ielts-speaking).
- Edmicro — IELTS Speaking Band 7 strategy
  (https://edmicro.edu.vn/ielts/ielts-speaking-band-7/) is the source
  used below for the ≥90 s Part-2 endurance threshold.

### India / Pakistan / Bangladesh

- British Council India notes most Indian candidates underperform on
  Speaking due to anxiety and L1-to-English translation latency, not
  lexis
  (https://www.britishcouncil.in/blog/top-tips-get-better-ielts-speaking-score).
- PND IELTS — Band 7 Speaking Strategy 2025
  (https://pndielts.in/blog/ielts-speaking-tips-for-band-7/) and Turbo

  side, what's more, to put it another way, that being said, by the same

- IDP India reproduces public Speaking band descriptors with the same
  wording as the British Council PDF
  (https://ieltsidpindia.com/information/ielts-band-scores/speaking).
- IDP's global preparation article warns that pausing and hesitation are
  natural but should not dominate, that speaking too slowly can reduce
  the amount of language produced, and that speaking fast is not the
  same as good fluency
  (https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-ielts-speaking-fluency-and-coherence).
- British Council Pakistan hosts a F&C IELTS video page consistent with

### Iran (ایران)

- IDP Iran's Speaking phrasebook emphasises stalling phrases as the
  cheapest way to gain content-related hesitation credit
  (https://ielts.idp.com/iran/prepare/article-ielts-speaking-best-phrases/fa-ir).
- Persian-language IELTS2 defines F&C as speaking without long pauses or
  excessive "um/uh" fillers and organising ideas logically, and
  recommends avoiding long pauses/unnecessary repetition, using linking
  expressions such as "for example" or "in contrast", and practising
  conversation/shadowing
  (https://ielts2.com/understanding-the-ielts-speaking-band-descriptors/).
- IELTS2's Persian guide on asking the examiner to repeat says a polite

### Conflicts and contested heuristics across regions

Local prep schools publish different numerical thresholds for the same
phenomena. None are official IELTS cutoffs. An LLM judge should treat
these as a soft range, not authoritative:

- **Maximum acceptable pause length.** New Channel Wuhan (China) calls
  pauses over **3 seconds** problematic
  (https://wh.xhd.cn/info/ieltsSpeak/1059815.html); Hafez Edu / Sara
  English School (Iran) define fluency as **no pause longer than ~5
  seconds**
  (https://www.hafezedu.com/ielts-speaking-tricks/;
  https://saraenglishschool.com/fluency-coherence-ielts/); the British
  Council Part 1 classroom activity uses **10 seconds** as a
  practice-game stop condition
  (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/speaking_part_1_developing_fluency_and_coherence_.pdf).
  The official descriptors give no numerical cutoff.

## Open-source tools and code

These tools are useful for building transcript/audio measurement
features, but none should be treated as an IELTS band scorer without
validation against the official descriptors and human-rated IELTS data;
IELTS research found that no single speaking feature separates all bands
(https://ielts.org/researchers/our-research/research-reports/the-relationship-between-speaking-features-and-band-descriptors-a-mixed-methods-study).

- **WhisperX** — word-level ASR timestamps and diarization/alignment
  hooks for estimating pauses, speech rate and examiner/candidate turns
  (https://github.com/m-bain/whisperX).
- **pyannote-audio** — speaker diarization for isolating candidate
  speech from examiner prompts
  (https://github.com/pyannote/pyannote-audio).
- **openSMILE** — acoustic/prosodic feature extraction useful for speech
  rate, pauses and connected-speech/prosody studies
  (https://github.com/audeering/opensmile).
- **Parselmouth** — Python Praat wrapper for duration, pitch and
  pause-related acoustic features
  (https://github.com/YannickJadoul/Parselmouth).
- **deep_disfluency (CLP Research)** — detection of repairs, restarts
  and filled pauses in spontaneous speech transcripts
  (https://github.com/clp-research/deep_disfluency).
- **jjordanoc/robust-english-speech-fluency-classification** — L2
  English fluency classifier using SVM/MLP on speech rate, filled-pause
  rate, phonation ratio, effective speech rate and MFCCs; useful
  prior-art for audio-side fluency features (not IELTS-trained)
  (https://github.com/jjordanoc/robust-english-speech-fluency-classification).
- **pariajm/joint-disfluency-detector-and-parser** — disfluency
  detection (false starts, repeats, repairs, filled pauses) trained on
  PTB-3 Switchboard; outputs reparandum / interregnum / repair tags that
  align with the descriptors' "repetition" and "self-correction"
  categories (https://github.com/pariajm/joint-disfluency-detector-and-parser).
- **pariajm/deep-disfluency-detector** — Auto-Correlational NN variant
  of the same disfluency detector
  (https://github.com/pariajm/deep-disfluency-detector).
- **SpeechAce API samples** and **SpeechSuper API samples** —
  commercial closed-weight APIs (open client samples) that surface
  words-correct-per-minute, pause count and IELTS/PTE estimated bands
  (https://github.com/speechace/speechace-api-samples;
  https://github.com/speechsuper/SpeechSuper-API-Samples).
- Academic baseline: *Phonetic and Prosody-aware Self-supervised
  Learning Approach for Non-native Fluency Scoring* — handcrafted vs
  SSL feature ablations on non-native fluency scoring
  (https://arxiv.org/pdf/2305.11438).
- Corpus-linguistic priors for "what looks native": a corpus-based study
  on discourse markers in native vs non-native spoken English
  (https://www.researchgate.net/publication/368973740_A_Corpus-Based_Study_on_Discourse_Markers_in_Native_and_Non-native_Spoken_English)
  and *Frontiers* on "you know" and "I mean" in mediatized English
  (https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/communication/articles/10.3389/fcomm.2024.1427062/full).
  Native speakers produce ~5× the rate of pragmatic markers per turn
  that L2 learners do, and the ratio shifts toward interpersonal/
  interactive use as L2 proficiency approaches C1.

## Practical signals for an LLM judge

Ranked by directness for scoring a transcript. Bands in brackets are
calibrated against the public descriptors; quantitative thresholds drawn
from prep schools are flagged as heuristics, not official cutoffs.

1. **Hesitation type triage.** Tag every hesitation as content-related
   ("hmm, let me think… I suppose…") or language-related ("the… er… the
   thing… that I…"). Band 8–9 hesitation is mostly content-related;
   Band 7 may show some language-related hesitation without coherence
   loss; Band 6 may lose coherence; Band 5 hesitates mid-sentence for
   basic lexis/grammar. Heuristic: content:language ratio ≥ 4:1 supports
   Band 8+; ≤ 1:1 supports Band 6 or below
   (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf;
   https://ted-ielts.com/hesitation/).
2. **Pause placement, not just count.** Clause-boundary pauses that mark
   stages in narration/argument can be coherent; mid-clause pauses
   while the candidate searches for a word are negative fluency
   evidence
   (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf).
3. **L1 filler check.** Any non-English filler (음, 那个, ano, эээ, چی
   بگم, anu, eee) is a hard penalty — caps F&C at Band 6 even when
   other features are stronger
   (https://ted-ielts.com/hesitation/;
   https://osee.co.id/ielts-speaking-tips-skor-tinggi/;
   https://www.hafezedu.com/ielts-speaking-tricks/).
4. **Self-correction quality.** Meaning corrections ("I went to the
   bank — well, I mean the river bank") read as fluency *strength*;
   grammar/word-search corrections ("I goed — went — I went") read as
   *weakness*. Heuristic from Liz/descriptors: three or more
   language-related corrections per Part-2 turn caps at Band 6;
   verbatim repetition under correction is not a fluency strength even
   when the second version is "right"
   (https://ieltsliz.com/ielts-speaking-self-correction/).
5. **Functionless repetition.** Word/phrase repeats with no
   communicative function ("the the the", "I think I think") are
   disfluency markers; count them separately from rhetorical repetition
   ("very, very important")
   (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf;
   https://github.com/pariajm/joint-disfluency-detector-and-parser).
6. **Discourse-marker inventory & spread.** Reward varied use across
   IDP's nine functional categories (sequencing, adding, contrast,
   example, result, opinion, comparing, stalling, generalising).
   Heuristic: Band 7 expects ≥ 6 distinct markers across ≥ 3
   categories; Band 6 typically shows 2–4 markers concentrated in 1–2
   categories
   (https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-discourse-markers-ielts-speaking).
7. **Marker over-repetition.** If a single marker (commonly *and*,
   *so*, *also*, *because*) accounts for > 40% of all discourse-marker
   tokens, penalise toward Band 5–6 ("may over-use certain connectives
   and discourse markers" — Band 5 verbatim). Repetitive
   "firstly/secondly/thirdly…" in every answer is the canonical example
   (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf;
   https://ieltskorea.org/korea/prepare/article-discourse-markers-ielts-speaking/en-gb).
8. **Marker–function fit.** Misused or mechanical markers ("on the
   other hand" introducing a similar idea; "for example" not followed
   by an example) signal Band 6 use, not Band 7
   (https://blog.skybel.co.kr/blog/%EC%95%84%EC%9D%B4%EC%97%98%EC%B8%A0%20%EC%8A%A4%ED%94%BC%ED%82%B9/%EC%95%84%EC%9D%B4%EC%97%98%EC%B8%A0-%EC%8A%A4%ED%94%BC%ED%82%B9-%EA%B3%A0%EB%93%9D%EC%A0%90%EC%9D%84-%EB%A7%89%EB%8A%94-%EC%97%B0%EA%B2%B0%EC%96%B4-%EC%98%A4%EB%8B%B5-%EC%9C%A0%ED%98%95-%ED%97%B7%EA%B0%88%EB%A6%AC%EB%8A%94-%ED%91%9C%ED%98%84-%EC%A0%95%EB%A6%AC).
9. **Pragmatic vs textual markers.** Presence of pragmatic markers at
   clause boundaries (*well, actually, I mean, you know, the thing is,
   to be honest*) — not only sequencing connectives — pushes toward
   Band 7+, because native corpora show ~5× higher pragmatic-marker
   rates than L2
   (https://www.researchgate.net/publication/368973740_A_Corpus-Based_Study_on_Discourse_Markers_in_Native_and_Non-native_Spoken_English;
   https://dictionary.cambridge.org/grammar/british-grammar/discourse-).
10. **Stalling quality.** "Let me think about that for a moment" or
    "That's an interesting question, I haven't thought about it before"
    earn content-related hesitation credit; bare *um/er/uh* sequences
    do not. Repeated use of the same stalling phrase across turns
    (mechanical) is itself a Band 6 signal
    (https://www.essentialielts.com/ielts-speaking-7-fluency-markers;
    https://ieltskorea.org/korea/prepare/article-discourse-markers-ielts-speaking/en-gb).
11. **Topic-shift cohesion.** A Band 7+ candidate uses an explicit
    transition ("Moving on to that…", "When it comes to X, I'd say…")
    when the examiner changes topic. Jumping straight into the new
    topic without transition is a Band 6 signal
    (https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-discourse-markers-ielts-speaking).
12. **Cohesion via reference, not just markers.** Band 8–9 speakers
    use pronouns and substitution ("it", "that one", "do so") to bind
    utterances. Over-reliance on explicit markers without
    pronoun/ellipsis cohesion reads as Band 6–7
    (https://assets.cambridgeenglish.org/webinars/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).
13. **Pause burden per 100 words.** IELTS research found significant
    band 5 vs band 8 differences in pause length per 100 words. Useful
    as a quantitative feature, but never as a sole band scorer — bands
    are distinguished by clusters
    (https://ielts.org/researchers/our-research/research-reports/the-relationship-between-speaking-features-and-band-descriptors-a-mixed-methods-study).
14. **Single mid-utterance pause length.** **Conflict across prep
    schools** — Chinese coaching flags pauses > 3 s, Iranian prep > 5 s,
    BC classroom drill > 10 s. None is official. Treat as a soft
    threshold and weight pauses without a content reason more heavily
    than reflective pauses
    (https://wh.xhd.cn/info/ieltsSpeak/1059815.html;
    https://www.hafezedu.com/ielts-speaking-tricks/;
    https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/speaking_part_1_developing_fluency_and_coherence_.pdf).
15. **Speech pace.** Heuristic from Indian prep: target 130–150 wpm
    (https://kadamboverseas.com/ielts-speaking-techniques-band-7-confidence/).
    Below 100 wpm with frequent gaps suggests Band 5–6; above 170 wpm
    often signals memorised script, which examiners discount. IDP and
    Chinese prep both warn that speaking fast is not the same as
    fluent
    (https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-ielts-speaking-fluency-and-coherence;
    https://wh.xhd.cn/info/ieltsSpeak/1059815.html).
16. **Part-2 long-turn endurance.** A Band-7 Part 2 turn fills ≥ 90 s
    of the 2-minute slot without examiner intervention. Stopping at
    30–60 s reads as "willingness to speak at length" (Band 6 wording)
    only
    (https://edmicro.edu.vn/ielts/ielts-speaking-band-7/).
17. **Develop, don't bare-answer.** A transcript with only short
    answers gives weak F&C evidence; develop with reasons, examples,
    comparisons, personal experience. A three-word Part 1 reply such
    as "Yes, of course" is too short
    (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-fluency-coherence;
    https://res.edu.vn/unit-2-tieu-chi-cham-diem-ielts-speaking/).
18. **Clarification requests.** One polite clarification request is
    interactional management; repeated requests that appear to buy time
    or replace answering contribute to F&C concerns
    (https://ielts2.com/can-i-ask-the-ielts-examiner-to-repeat-the-question/).
19. **Examiner-cut endings are not penalised.** Score continuity,
    coherence and development before the cut, not whether the examiner
    allowed a full conclusion
    (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-fluency-coherence).
20. **Don't penalise spoken ellipsis or verbless units.** IELTS
    explicitly recognises spoken sentences may be verbless or
    elliptical and still function as sentence-like units
    (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf).
