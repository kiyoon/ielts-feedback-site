# Band descriptors (0–9) for all four speaking criteria, plus Band 6 vs 7 deltas and examiner perspective

_Crawled by claude on 2026-05-19T00:00Z. Sources cited inline._

## Summary

The IELTS Speaking test is assessed against four publicly published criteria — Fluency & Coherence (FC), Lexical Resource (LR), Grammatical Range & Accuracy (GRA) and Pronunciation (P) — each worth exactly 25% of the speaking mark and each rated independently on the 0–9 band scale. The reported speaking score is the unrounded average of the four, then rounded to the nearest half band ([IDP](https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-understanding-the-ielts-speaking-band-descriptors); [IELTS Tutors](https://ieltstutors.org/speaking-band-descriptors/)). Examiners apply a "best-fit" approach: a candidate is placed at the band where the bulk of features in their performance sit, even if individual features dip up or down by a band ([IELTS Australia: Band 6 vs Band 7](https://ielts.com.au/australia/about/news-and-articles/article-speaking-band-6-vs-band-7)). The single most useful insight for an automated judge: the jump from Band 6 → 7 is fundamentally about **sustaining** features rather than acquiring new ones — Band 6 speakers show 7-level traits in flashes; Band 7 speakers hold them across the whole test.

## Authoritative material

### Public-version band descriptors (Bands 0–9, all four criteria)

Sourced from the official British Council PDF ([takeielts.britishcouncil.org/.../ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf)) and the IELTS.org-published version reproduced by the AGAPE IELTS Academy archive ([ieltsagape.wordpress.com/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors](https://ieltsagape.wordpress.com/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors/)). Wording is the official public-version text where in quotation marks; everything else is paraphrase.

**Band 9 — "Expert user"**
- FC: "Speaks fluently with only rare repetition or self-correction"; any hesitation is content-related (i.e. thinking about *what* to say, not *how* to say it). "Speaks coherently with fully appropriate cohesive features."
- LR: "Uses vocabulary with full flexibility and precision in all topics. Uses idiomatic language naturally and accurately."
- GRA: "Uses a full range of structures naturally and appropriately. Produces consistently accurate structures apart from 'slips' characteristic of native speaker speech."
- P: "Uses a full range of pronunciation features with precision and subtlety. Sustains flexible use of features throughout. Is effortless to understand."

**Band 8**
- FC: "Speaks fluently with only occasional repetition or self-correction; hesitation is usually content-related and only rarely to search for language. Develops topics coherently and appropriately."
- LR: "Uses a wide vocabulary resource readily and flexibly to convey precise meaning. Uses less common and idiomatic vocabulary skilfully, with occasional inaccuracies. Uses paraphrase effectively as required."
- GRA: "Uses a wide range of structures flexibly. Produces a majority of error-free sentences with only very occasional inappropriacies or basic/non-systematic errors."
- P: "Uses a wide range of pronunciation features. Sustains flexible use of features, with only occasional lapses. Is easy to understand throughout; L1 accent has minimal effect on intelligibility."

**Band 7**
- FC: "Speaks at length without noticeable effort or loss of coherence. May demonstrate language-related hesitation at times, or some repetition and/or self-correction. Uses a range of connectives and discourse markers with some flexibility."
- LR: "Uses vocabulary resource flexibly to discuss a variety of topics. Uses some less common and idiomatic vocabulary and shows some awareness of style and collocation, with some inappropriate choices. Uses paraphrase effectively."
- GRA: "Uses a range of complex structures with some flexibility. Frequently produces error-free sentences, though some grammatical mistakes persist."
- P: Shows "all the positive features of Band 6 and some, but not all, of the positive features of Band 8."

**Band 6**
- FC: "Is willing to speak at length, though may lose coherence at times due to occasional repetition, self-correction or hesitation. Uses a range of connectives and discourse markers but not always appropriately."
- LR: "Has a wide enough vocabulary to discuss topics at length and make meaning clear in spite of inappropriacies. Generally paraphrases successfully."
- GRA: "Uses a mix of simple and complex structures, but with limited flexibility. May make frequent mistakes with complex structures, though these rarely cause comprehension problems."
- P: "Uses a range of pronunciation features with mixed control. Shows some effective use of features but this is not sustained. Can generally be understood throughout, though mispronunciation of individual words or sounds reduces clarity at times."

**Band 5**
- FC: "Usually maintains flow of speech but uses repetition, self-correction and/or slow speech to keep going. May overuse certain connectives and discourse markers. Produces simple speech fluently, but more complex communication causes fluency problems."
- LR: "Manages to talk about familiar and unfamiliar topics but uses vocabulary with limited flexibility. Attempts to use paraphrase but with mixed success."
- GRA: "Produces basic sentence forms with reasonable accuracy. Uses a limited range of more complex structures, but these usually contain errors and may cause some comprehension problems."
- P: Shows "all the positive features of Band 4 and some, but not all, of the positive features of Band 6."

**Band 4**
- FC: "Cannot respond without noticeable pauses and may speak slowly, with frequent repetition and self-correction. Links basic sentences but with repetitious use of simple connectives and some breakdowns in coherence."
- LR: "Is able to talk about familiar topics but can only convey basic meaning on unfamiliar topics and makes frequent errors in word choice. Rarely attempts paraphrase."
- GRA: "Produces basic sentence forms and some correct simple sentences but subordinate structures are rare. Errors are frequent and may lead to misunderstanding."
- P: "Uses a limited range of pronunciation features. Attempts to control features but lapses are frequent. Mispronunciations are frequent and cause some difficulty for the listener."

**Band 3**
- FC: "Speaks with long pauses. Has limited ability to link simple sentences. Gives only simple responses and is frequently unable to convey basic message."
- LR: "Uses simple vocabulary to convey personal information. Has insufficient vocabulary for less familiar topics."
- GRA: "Attempts basic sentence forms but with limited success, or relies on apparently memorised utterances. Makes numerous errors except in memorised expressions."
- P: Shows "some of the features of Band 2 and some, but not all, of the positive features of Band 4."

**Band 2**
- FC: "Pauses lengthily before most words. Little communication possible."
- LR: "Only produces isolated words or memorised utterances."
- GRA: "Cannot produce basic sentence forms."
- P: "Speech is often unintelligible."

**Band 1**
- All criteria: "No communication possible. No rateable language."

**Band 0**
- "Does not attend."

### How examiners actually apply the descriptors

- All examiners are trained, certified and re-certified every two years against Cambridge ESOL standardisation samples; this is what keeps the four sub-scores comparable globally ([Cambridge "Assessing IELTS Speaking" PDF metadata](https://ielts.ch/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/assessing-IELTS-speaking.pdf)).
- The assessment is "best-fit": when a candidate's features straddle two adjacent bands, the examiner picks the band where the majority of features sit, then asks whether enough features of the higher band are present to bump it ([IELTS Australia](https://ielts.com.au/australia/about/news-and-articles/article-speaking-band-6-vs-band-7); [IELTS.org news article on Band 6 vs 7](https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/differences-between-ielts-6-and-7-for-productive-skills)).
- Self-correction is **not** automatically penalised — examiners explicitly treat occasional self-correction as evidence of language awareness; it only hurts when it becomes frequent enough to cost coherence ([My IELTS Classroom](https://blog.myieltsclassroom.com/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors/); [IELTS Australia](https://ielts.com.au/australia/about/news-and-articles/article-speaking-band-6-vs-band-7)).
- "Pausing to gather thoughts is acceptable; pausing to find words is not" — examiner shorthand for the FC distinction between content-related and language-related hesitation, repeated almost verbatim in Band 7/8 descriptors ([My IELTS Classroom](https://blog.myieltsclassroom.com/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors/)).
- Native British or American accent is *not* a scoring factor. The pronunciation criterion is about intelligibility and the use of suprasegmental features (stress, intonation, chunking, weak forms) — L1 accent only matters if it actually obscures meaning ([IDP](https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-understanding-the-ielts-speaking-band-descriptors); [My IELTS Classroom](https://blog.myieltsclassroom.com/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors/)).
- Per IELTS.org's own analysis of the 6 → 7 productive-skills gap, Band 7 requires the speaker to "express nuanced opinions, handle interruptions or follow-up questions and use a broader vocabulary," whereas a Band 6 speaker "can communicate adequately but may find longer academic discussions challenging" ([ielts.org/news-and-insights/differences-between-ielts-6-and-7](https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/differences-between-ielts-6-and-7-for-productive-skills)).
- Common candidate-side traps that cap GRA at Band 6: tense slippage between past/present/future within one response, and reverting to mostly simple sentences once Part 3 abstraction increases ([My IELTS Classroom](https://blog.myieltsclassroom.com/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors/)).

### The Band 6 → 7 delta in one table

| Criterion | Band 6 evidence | Band 7 evidence |
|---|---|---|
| FC | Willing to speak at length; **occasional** loss of coherence from repetition/self-correction/hesitation; connectives present but not always appropriate. | Speaks at length **without noticeable effort or loss of coherence**; only language-related hesitation; uses connectives and discourse markers flexibly. |
| LR | Wide enough vocab to cover topics; meaning clear despite inappropriacies; generally paraphrases successfully. | **Flexible** vocab across varied topics; some less-common and idiomatic items; awareness of style/collocation (still with some misfires). |
| GRA | Mix of simple + complex with **limited flexibility**; frequent errors in complex structures but mostly intelligible. | Range of complex structures with **some flexibility**; **frequently** produces error-free sentences. |
| P | Mixed control of pronunciation features; effective use is **not sustained**; mispronunciation reduces clarity at times. | Sustained control with several Band 8 features starting to appear; understandable throughout. |
| Overall | Right features **sometimes**. | Same features **sustained throughout**. |

Sources: [British Council PDF](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf), [IELTS Australia](https://ielts.com.au/australia/about/news-and-articles/article-speaking-band-6-vs-band-7), [IDP Difference Between Band 6 and 7](https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-difference-between-band-6-and-7-ielts).

## Country-specific prep material

### South Korea — Hackers (해커스), YBM, TestGlider
- Hackers Academy markets a dedicated "6.0 → 7.0 jump" pathway, and one of their published student case studies attributes the breakthrough specifically to integrating **idioms and chunked expressions** rather than to grammar work or accent training — they explicitly note that accent is not scored and that asking the examiner to repeat a question is *not* penalised ([gohackers.com 고득점멘토 case study](https://www.gohackers.com/?c=ielts/ielts_info2/ielts_method&type=url&uid=538610); [hackers.ac IELTS overall 7 landing page](https://www.hackers.ac/contents/?m=landing&v=ielts/overall7)).
- IDP Korea's own Korean-language descriptors page positions Band 6.5 as the practical floor for university admission and frames the 6 → 7 leap as "some inaccuracies or misunderstandings remain, but the candidate can adapt flexibly" ([ieltskorea.org band-score-calculation](https://ieltskorea.org/korea/results/band-score-calculation); [ieltskorea.org how-ielts-is-marked](https://ieltskorea.org/korea/results/how-ielts-is-marked)).
- TestGlider (Korean prep blog widely shared in 학원 circles) emphasises that **fluency is the fastest criterion to move in the short term**, and advises that getting stuck on one word should trigger paraphrase rather than silence — operationally consistent with the LR Band 6 descriptor ([testglider.com guide to Band 6.5](https://blog.testglider.com/%EC%95%84%EC%9D%B4%EC%97%98%EC%B8%A0-band-6-5%EB%AA%A9%ED%91%9C-%ED%9A%A8%EA%B3%BC%EC%A0%81%EC%9D%B8-%EC%98%81%EC%97%AD%EB%B3%84-%EA%B3%B5%EB%B6%80%EB%B2%95/)).

### China — Zhihu, 新通, 新航道, 老烤鸭, 学为贵
- Zhihu "口语5/6/7/8分的区别" thread (widely circulated in Chinese 雅思 prep): Band 7 candidates produce **noticeably less** language-related hesitation, self-correction and repetition than Band 6 candidates, and Band 7 grammar shows "more flexible deployment of complex structures with persistent but non-blocking errors" ([zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/34529961](https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/34529961)).
- 新通教育 frames the 6 → 7 LR shift as: Band 6 = "wide enough vocab to discuss broad topics, can use some uncommon items or idioms though not always accurately"; Band 7 = "rich vocabulary deployed naturally and idiomatically across all topics" ([igo.cn/kp/info/details/323203.shtml](https://www.igo.cn/kp/info/details/323203.shtml)).
- 学为贵 ("万年 5.5 中招原因" — "why people get stuck at 5.5 forever") attributes the cap to overuse of memorised templates and absence of complex structures in Part 3 — a useful diagnostic signal that maps onto Band 5 GRA wording ([ielts.guixue.com/article/2889.shtml](https://ielts.guixue.com/article/2889.shtml)).
- TestDaily 厚朴优学 highlights that of the four criteria, **fluency** is the most short-term-improvable and most often the lever that lifts a 6.5 to 7.0 ([testdaily.cn/ielts-speak-20201020](https://www.testdaily.cn/ielts-speak-20201020/)).

### Vietnam — IELTS Power-up, IDP Vietnam, Lister
- IELTS Power-up (Vietnamese cram resource) operationalises the LR Band 6 → 7 jump as "from clear meaning despite inappropriacies → flexible deployment of less common and idiomatic items with awareness of style," and gives concrete paraphrase drills as the bridging exercise ([ielts-powerup.com/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors](https://ielts-powerup.com/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors/)).
- IDP Vietnam stresses that the Band 6 → 7 P delta is about **sustaining** prosodic features (stress, intonation, chunking) rather than acquiring a new feature inventory ([ielts.idp.com/vietnam/prepare/article-understanding-the-ielts-speaking-band-descriptors](https://ielts.idp.com/vietnam/prepare/article-understanding-the-ielts-speaking-band-descriptors/en-gb)).

### Iran — Englisikadeh, Irsafam, Salamzaban, Afaq

- Afaq IELTS notes that the four criteria are weighted equally at 25% each — a fact that Iranian candidates often miss because grammar feels "harder" so they over-weight it in self-assessment ([afaqielts.com](https://afaqielts.com/%D9%86%D9%85%D8%B1%D9%87-%D8%AF%D9%87%DB%8C-%D8%A8%D8%AE%D8%B4-%D8%A7%D8%B3%D9%BE%DB%8C%DA%A9%DB%8C%D9%86%DA%AF-%D8%A2%DB%8C%D9%84%D8%AA%D8%B3/)).

### Indonesia — AUG Study, OSEE, Lister, Titiknol English
- Indonesian prep consensus: the Band 6 → 7 gap is **rarely grammar** and almost always about (a) developing answers spontaneously and (b) handling abstract Part 3 questions — concrete advice: record yourself and listen for unfinished sentences, flat intonation, and "umm/eee" fillers, all of which mark a Band 6 ceiling ([augstudy.com tips ielts speaking band 7](https://augstudy.com/rahasia-tips-speaking-ielts-band-7-agar-skor-naik-drastis/); [osee.co.id ielts-speaking-tips](https://osee.co.id/ielts-speaking-tips-skor-tinggi/); [lister.co.id ielts-speaking-band-descriptors](https://lister.co.id/blog/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors/)).
- Pronunciation framing: word stress (PHOtograph vs phoTOGraphy), sentence stress, and rising vs falling intonation are the three measurable features Indonesian schools drill — Indonesian accent itself is explicitly stated not to be penalised ([osee.co.id](https://osee.co.id/ielts-speaking-tips-skor-tinggi/)).

### Russia / CIS — Divelang, Tower, Stegmax, Itec
- Russian-language criteria summaries highlight that at Band 7 P "some words or phonemes may still be mispronounced, but it does not impair intelligibility; speech can be understood without effort" — phrasing that translates the Band 7 P descriptor precisely ([tower.ua/ru/ielts-test](https://www.tower.ua/ru/ielts-test/ocenka-ielts-speaking-and-writing/); [stegmax.com speaking criteria](https://ru.stegmax.com/exams/ielts/speaking-criteria-for-assessing-oral-speech/)).
- Divelang explicitly warns Russian candidates against monosyllabic "yes/no" answers — the FC descriptor implicitly requires extended turns, so single-word responses lock the score at the Band 4–5 ceiling ([divelang.ru](https://www.divelang.ru/blog/useful/kak-podgotovitsya-k-ielts-speaking-chtoby-sdat-na-otlichno/)).

### India / South Asia — Gurully, Leap, IELTS Material, GeeksforGeeks
- Practical operational rule from Indian academy material: 1–2 well-chosen idioms per Part 2 response is the deployment density associated with Band 7 LR; more than that risks sounding rehearsed and triggers the "memorised utterances" red flag in the Band 3 descriptor ([gurully.com tips band 7](https://www.gurully.com/blog/ielts-speaking-tips-band-7-or-higher/); [ielts-blog.com 10 expressions for band 7+](https://www.ielts-blog.com/ielts-preparation-tips/speaking-tips/10-expressions-to-help-you-get-band-7-in-ielts-lesson-4/)).
- IDP Nepal's vocabulary-for-Band-7+ list operationalises LR as "use the less common item *in collocation*" — single uncommon words without natural co-text often get flagged as "inappropriate choice" rather than as evidence of range ([ielts.idp.com/nepal/.../ielts-speaking-vocabulary](https://ielts.idp.com/nepal/about/news-and-articles/article-ielts-speaking-vocabulary)).

### UK / Australia / NZ native-perspective material
- British Council India's "Journey from Band 5 to Band 7" framing: discourse-marker range plus low hesitation density is the gating feature for Band 7 FC ([britishcouncil.in journey-ielts-band-5-band-7](https://www.britishcouncil.in/blog/journey-ielts-band-5-band-7-ielts-speaking-test)).

## Practical signals for an LLM judge

Operational rules for scoring a Part 1/2/3 transcript. Apply each per criterion; combine via best-fit.

1. **Sustained vs intermittent.** The single most reliable Band 6 ↔ Band 7 discriminator: do the desirable features (range, complexity, idiomaticity, prosody) appear *throughout* the transcript, or only in one part / one answer? Intermittent → Band 6; sustained → Band 7 ([British Council PDF](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf); [IELTS Australia](https://ielts.com.au/australia/about/news-and-articles/article-speaking-band-6-vs-band-7)).
2. **Hesitation type matters more than count.** Hesitations preceded or accompanied by content rephrasing ("…what I mean is…", "…actually…") are **content-related** and Band-7-compatible. Hesitations followed by silence, repeated function words, or a word search are **language-related** and pull toward Band 6 or below ([British Council PDF Band 7/8 FC](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf); [My IELTS Classroom](https://blog.myieltsclassroom.com/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors/)).
3. **Idiom density target (LR, Part 2).** Aim for **1–2 well-collocated idiomatic chunks per ~90-second Part 2 turn** as the Band 7 LR floor. Zero idiomatic items across the whole test caps LR at Band 6. **More than 4–5 forced idioms** in a single short response is a "memorised utterances" red flag and should pull LR *down*, not up ([gurully.com](https://www.gurully.com/blog/ielts-speaking-tips-band-7-or-higher/); [British Council PDF Band 3 GRA](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf)).
4. **Error-free sentence ratio (GRA).** Band 7 requires "frequently produces error-free sentences." A practical heuristic for a transcript: **at least ~50% of complex sentences** (those with subordination/coordination) free of grammatical error. Under 30% → Band 6 ([British Council PDF Band 7 GRA](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf)).
5. **Complex-structure inventory (GRA).** Tag the transcript for: conditionals (any type), passive voice, reported speech, perfect aspects, relative clauses, cleft sentences. **Band 7 needs at least 3 of these 6 categories present and mostly accurate**; a Band 6 transcript typically shows ≤ 2 categories or shows them with high error rate ([salamzaban.com](https://salamzaban.com/blog/ielts-speaking-7-8/); [britishcouncil.in](https://www.britishcouncil.in/blog/journey-ielts-band-5-band-7-ielts-speaking-test)).
6. **Discourse marker range (FC).** Count distinct discourse markers used appropriately ("however", "for example", "as a result", "on the other hand", "consequently", "well", "I mean", "you know"). **Band 7 ≥ 5 distinct markers used appropriately**; Band 6 typically shows 2–4 with at least one inappropriate use ([britishcouncil.in](https://www.britishcouncil.in/blog/journey-ielts-band-5-band-7-ielts-speaking-test); [My IELTS Classroom](https://blog.myieltsclassroom.com/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors/)).
7. **Self-correction is not free.** ≤ 2 self-corrections per Part 2 turn is healthy / Band 7 compatible. ≥ 4 in one turn becomes a coherence cost and pulls FC toward Band 6 or below ([IELTS Australia](https://ielts.com.au/australia/about/news-and-articles/article-speaking-band-6-vs-band-7); [My IELTS Classroom](https://blog.myieltsclassroom.com/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors/)).
8. **Part 3 abstraction test.** If the transcript shows a clear drop in sentence complexity, idea-development length, or vocabulary range when moving from Part 1 → Part 3, the candidate is failing the "discuss abstract topics" test and should be capped at Band 6 regardless of strong Part 1 performance ([augstudy.com](https://augstudy.com/rahasia-tips-speaking-ielts-band-7-agar-skor-naik-drastis/); [My IELTS Classroom](https://blog.myieltsclassroom.com/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors/)).
9. **Yes/no responses are a hard cap.** Multiple single-word or under-one-sentence answers anywhere in the transcript cap FC at Band 5 — the descriptor requires "willing to speak at length" from Band 6 upward ([divelang.ru](https://www.divelang.ru/blog/useful/kak-podgotovitsya-k-ielts-speaking-chtoby-sdat-na-otlichno/); [British Council PDF Band 3 FC](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf)).
10. **Pronunciation must be inferred from transcript artefacts.** Without audio, score P from: (a) examiner-flagged comprehension breakdowns ("Sorry?", "Could you repeat?"), (b) overt mispronunciation calls in the transcript, (c) homophone-induced word choice errors, (d) candidate's own pronunciation repairs. Absent any of these signals, P should be scored at the same level as LR/GRA per the "best fit" rule rather than artificially low ([British Council PDF Band 6/7 P](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf); [IDP](https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-understanding-the-ielts-speaking-band-descriptors)).
11. **Accent is never a deduction by itself.** A transcript that flags "speaks with [L1] accent" with no comprehension breakdown should not move P at all ([IDP](https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-understanding-the-ielts-speaking-band-descriptors); [osee.co.id](https://osee.co.id/ielts-speaking-tips-skor-tinggi/)).
12. **Paraphrase as a Band 6+ floor.** The Band 6 LR descriptor requires "generally paraphrases successfully." If the transcript shows the candidate stopping at a word search without producing a workable paraphrase, LR floors at Band 5 ([My IELTS Classroom](https://blog.myieltsclassroom.com/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors/); [British Council PDF Band 5/6 LR](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf)).
13. **Articulation-rate proxy.** If the transcript carries timestamps, use words-per-minute as a fluency proxy: Suzuki & Kormos find articulation rate (with mid-clause pause frequency and repetition frequency) predicts functional adequacy in IELTS Part 2 ([Tandfonline 10.1080/15434303.2023.2283839](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/15434303.2023.2283839)).
14. **Round-up rule.** The four criteria are averaged then rounded to the nearest half-band — a candidate with 7/7/6/7 averages 6.75 → reported as **7.0**, while 7/6/6/7 averages 6.5. The judge must report the four sub-scores separately *and* the rounded overall, not pre-round per criterion ([IDP](https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-understanding-the-ielts-speaking-band-descriptors); [IELTS Tutors](https://ieltstutors.org/speaking-band-descriptors/)).
15. **Best-fit, not penalty-counting.** Do not deduct from a Band 7 transcript every time a feature dips into Band 6; instead, ask whether the *bulk* of evidence in the transcript matches Band 6 or Band 7 wording for that criterion ([IELTS Australia](https://ielts.com.au/australia/about/news-and-articles/article-speaking-band-6-vs-band-7); Cambridge examiner standardisation framing in [Assessing IELTS Speaking PDF](https://ielts.ch/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/assessing-IELTS-speaking.pdf)).
