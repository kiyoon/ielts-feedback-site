# Band descriptors (0–9) for all four speaking criteria, plus Band 6 vs 7 deltas and examiner perspective

_Crawled by codex on 2026-05-19T07:33:17Z. Sources cited inline._

## Summary

This topic gives the scoring backbone for an IELTS Speaking judge: the public 0-9 descriptors for Fluency & Coherence, Lexical Resource, Grammatical Range & Accuracy, and Pronunciation, plus the construct definitions behind those descriptors (https://cdn.ielts.org/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf; https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf). The most important scoring boundary for many candidates is Band 6 vs Band 7: Band 6 is generally communicative but limited, inconsistent or error-prone, while Band 7 shows flexible use, frequent accuracy, and no major loss of coherence or intelligibility (https://cdn.ielts.org/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf). For automated judging, the descriptors should be applied as a cluster of evidence across the whole 11-14 minute test, not by one feature such as speed, one idiom, or one grammar error (https://ielts.org/researchers/our-research/research-reports/candidate-discourse-in-the-revised-ielts-speaking-test; https://ielts.org/researchers/our-research/research-reports/the-relationship-between-speaking-features-and-band-descriptors-a-mixed-methods-study).

## Authoritative material

### Official scoring frame

- IELTS Speaking is a face-to-face interview, it is recorded, and certificated IELTS examiners assess performance throughout the test using four criteria: Fluency and Coherence, Lexical Resource, Grammatical Range and Accuracy, and Pronunciation (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking).
- IELTS says each Speaking criterion has equal weighting and the average of the four criteria gives the IELTS Speaking score, so a judge should not let one strong criterion completely mask weak evidence in another criterion (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/your-results/ielts-scoring-in-detail).
- The public descriptor PDF says a candidate must "fully fit" the positive features of a descriptor at a band level and is rated on average performance across all parts of the test, which means a single good Part 2 answer cannot by itself justify a high band if Part 1 and Part 3 show weaker average performance (https://cdn.ielts.org/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).
- The Speaking test has three parts: Part 1 familiar topics, Part 2 a prepared long turn of up to two minutes after one minute of notes, and Part 3 a more abstract discussion linked to Part 2 (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking).
- Part 2 specifically tests the ability to speak at length, use appropriate language and organise ideas logically; Part 3 tests explaining opinions, analysing, discussing and speculating, so evidence of abstract language and complex grammar is especially likely to appear in Part 3 (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking).

### Official construct definitions

- Fluency and Coherence means speaking with normal continuity, rate and effort, and linking ideas and language into coherent connected speech; IELTS lists speech rate, speech continuity, logical sequencing, relevant spoken sentences, appropriate pausing, spoken discourse markers, fillers and cohesive devices as key indicators (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf).
- IELTS defines a "spoken sentence" as the closest speech equivalent of a written sentence, but it may include elliptical or verbless structures if they perform a sentence-like function, so a transcript judge should not penalise natural spoken ellipsis simply because it would be incomplete in writing (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf).
- Lexical Resource means the range of vocabulary available to the candidate, the precision with which meaning and attitude are conveyed, appropriacy for referential meaning/style/collocation, idiomatic expression, and the ability to paraphrase around a vocabulary gap (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf).
- Grammatical Range and Accuracy means accurate and appropriate use of syntactic forms plus enough grammatical range to express complex propositions; IELTS indicators include spoken sentence length, subordinate clauses, verb phrase complexity such as aspect/modality/passive voice, other phrase complexity such as modification, sentence-structure range for information focus, error density and the communicative effect of errors (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf).
- Pronunciation means accurate and sustained use of phonological features to convey meaning; IELTS indicators include meaningful chunking, rhythm, stress timing, linking/elision, emphatic or contrastive stress, intonation, word stress, vowel/consonant production, listener effort, and the effect of accent on intelligibility (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf).

### Band 6 vs Band 7 deltas by criterion

| Criterion | Band 6 evidence | Band 7 evidence | Operational scoring delta |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fluency & Coherence | Candidate keeps going and shows willingness for long turns, but coherence can be lost through hesitation/repetition/self-correction, and cohesive features are not always appropriate (https://cdn.ielts.org/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf). | Candidate readily produces long turns without noticeable effort; language-search hesitation may appear but does not harm coherence; discourse markers/connectives/cohesion are flexible (https://cdn.ielts.org/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf). | Move from 6 to 7 when hesitation and repairs remain local rather than derailing the answer, and when linking is flexible rather than mechanical or misused (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf). |
| Lexical Resource | Enough vocabulary to discuss topics at length; inappropriate choices occur but meaning remains clear; paraphrase is generally successful (https://cdn.ielts.org/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf). | Vocabulary is flexible across varied topics; less common/idiomatic items and style/collocation awareness appear, with some inappropriate choices; paraphrase is effective (https://cdn.ielts.org/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf). | Move from 6 to 7 when the candidate goes beyond sufficient topic vocabulary into flexible, precise, collocationally aware choices and recovers lexical gaps cleanly by paraphrase (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf). |
| Grammatical Range & Accuracy | Simple and complex forms are mixed, but flexibility is limited; complex structures often contain errors, rarely blocking communication (https://cdn.ielts.org/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf). | A range of structures is used flexibly; error-free sentences are frequent; simple and complex sentences are effective despite some errors and a few persistent basic errors (https://cdn.ielts.org/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf). | Move from 6 to 7 when complex structures are not just attempted but frequently controlled, and when error-free clauses/sentences appear often enough to show flexible command (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf). |
| Pronunciation | A range of phonological features is attempted but control is variable; rhythm/stress timing may be affected; intonation/stress use is not sustained; occasional word/phoneme errors reduce clarity, but the candidate is generally understood without much effort (https://cdn.ielts.org/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf). | Band 7 must include all Band 6 positives and some Band 8 positives, so it must show generally easy understanding plus some sustained control of rhythm, stress, intonation, connected speech or subtle meaning (https://cdn.ielts.org/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf). | Move from 6 to 7 when intelligibility is not merely adequate but consistently easy, and when at least some higher-band prosodic control is evident; do not require a native accent because IELTS assesses intelligibility and accent effect, not accent identity (https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-does-ielts-speaking-assess-your-accent; https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf). |

### Examiner perspective and scoring behaviour

- IDP says IELTS Writing and Speaking examiners must have a related undergraduate degree, a recognised English-language teaching qualification, and at least three years of English-language teaching experience mostly with adults (https://ielts.idp.com/canada/prepare/article-understanding-ielts-examiners-and-how-they-score-tests).
- IDP says examiner certification involves standardised training, practice marking Speaking and Writing tests, consistent and accurate marking, regular monitoring after certification, and re-certification every two years (https://ielts.idp.com/canada/prepare/article-understanding-ielts-examiners-and-how-they-score-tests).
- IDP's examiner-perspective article recommends candidates become familiar with the public band descriptors because they show what the examiner looks for when marking tests (https://ielts.idp.com/canada/prepare/article-understanding-ielts-examiners-and-how-they-score-tests).
- IELTS research by Brown says the revised Speaking test moved from a single global scale to four analytic scales, and that the descriptors/indicators were analysed across interviews from operational IELTS administrations to validate the analytic rating scales (https://ielts.org/researchers/our-research/research-reports/candidate-discourse-in-the-revised-ielts-speaking-test).
- Brown's IELTS research found most measures increased in the expected direction over levels, but also found high variation among candidates at the same level and overlap between levels, which supports using multiple features rather than a rigid one-metric rule (https://ielts.org/researchers/our-research/research-reports/candidate-discourse-in-the-revised-ielts-speaking-test).
- Seedhouse, Harris, Naeb and Ustunel studied 60 IELTS Speaking recordings, 15 each at Bands 5, 6, 7 and 8, and found that no single speaking feature distinguished all bands; a cluster of assessable features tends to lead toward a score (https://ielts.org/researchers/our-research/research-reports/the-relationship-between-speaking-features-and-band-descriptors-a-mixed-methods-study).
- The same mixed-methods IELTS study used pause length per 100 words as one fluency measure and found significant differences between Bands 5 and 8, but this does not justify a simple pause-count scorer because the qualitative analysis did not identify one universal band separator (https://ielts.org/researchers/our-research/research-reports/the-relationship-between-speaking-features-and-band-descriptors-a-mixed-methods-study).
- British Council teacher guidance says memorising large chunks before the Speaking test is not a good strategy and is unlikely to impress the examiner, so transcript evidence of memorised but poorly integrated answers should not be rewarded as flexible language control (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-fluency-coherence).
- British Council teacher guidance says candidates are assessed on the four language criteria, not appearance, body language, or creativity of ideas, so an LLM judge reading a transcript should ignore confidence, charisma, politeness and opinion quality unless these affect language evidence (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-fluency-coherence).
- IELTS Australia says candidates are not judged on whether their opinions or experiences are right or wrong; they are assessed on how they express themselves in English (https://ielts.com.au/australia/prepare/article-ielts-for-native-english-speakers).
- IDP says IELTS Speaking pronunciation does not require hiding or changing a natural accent, because the relevant issue is clarity/intelligibility through clear words/sounds, stress, rhythm and intonation (https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-does-ielts-speaking-assess-your-accent).

## Country-specific prep material

### Korea

- IDP IELTS Korea explains that Speaking is assessed by fluency/coherence, lexical resource, pronunciation, and grammatical range/accuracy, and directs candidates to the public Speaking band descriptors for details by band (https://ieltskorea.org/korea/results/band-score-calculation).
- Korean prep provider Skybel summarises the 6.0 to 7.0 gap as moving from basic continuity, repeated vocabulary and unstable complex sentences toward natural speed, coherent development, topic-group collocations, varied sentence structures, recording-based error review, and pronunciation clarity through stress/rhythm/intonation practice (https://blog.skybel.co.kr/blog/%EC%95%84%EC%9D%B4%EC%97%98%EC%B8%A0%20%EC%8A%A4%ED%94%BC%ED%82%B9/%EC%95%84%EC%9D%B4%EC%97%98%EC%B8%A0-%EC%8A%A4%ED%94%BC%ED%82%B9-%EC%A4%80%EB%B9%84-%EB%8F%85%ED%95%99-%EB%A3%A8%ED%8B%B4%EC%9C%BC%EB%A1%9C-60%EC%97%90%EC%84%9C-70-%EC%8B%A4%EC%A0%84-%EC%A0%90%EC%88%98-%ED%96%A5%EC%83%81-%EC%A0%84%EB%9E%B5-%EA%B3%B5%EA%B0%9C).
- Korean-language ELT Japan describes the same Band 6 to Band 7 boundary as less mechanical linking, more flexible connectors, natural idioms/collocations, more frequent error-free complex sentences, and intelligibility despite an L1 accent, which is useful as a Korean-language coaching interpretation rather than a separate official standard (https://www.eltschool.jp/ko/column/ielts-speaking-band-7-tips).

### China

- The IELTS Chinese official site says examiners award bands using the four Speaking criteria: fluency/coherence, lexical richness, grammatical range/accuracy, and pronunciation (https://www.chinaielts.org/prepare/understand-ielts-score/assessment-speaking).
- IDP China hosts a Chinese public-version Speaking descriptor PDF that mirrors the official Band 7 requirement for detailed, coherent expression with flexible connectives/discourse markers, flexible vocabulary with less common/idiomatic items, flexible complex grammar, and pronunciation showing all Band 6 positives plus some Band 8 positives (https://www.idp.cn/special/subject/IELTS/pdf/kscj_f2.pdf).
- New Channel Wuhan's Chinese prep page summarises Band 7+ as stable performance across all four criteria, fewer mistakes, natural expression and strong logic; it also treats "Part 2 can fill two minutes" as coaching evidence for FC, which is useful as a practice check but not an official IELTS cutoff (https://wh.xhd.cn/info/cjzx/1058456.html).
- New Oriental's Chinese guide warns that fluency is not simply non-stop fast speech, because excessive speed can cause more grammar errors and long hesitation can slow delivery; it also stresses that vocabulary must be usable in speech, not merely memorised in lists (https://ielts.xdf.cn/202004/11052541.html).
- Laokaoya's Chinese Band 7 guide emphasises flexibility: Band 7 adds flexible topic discussion, less common/idiomatic vocabulary with style/collocation awareness, flexible complex grammar, and pronunciation that preserves meaning even with Chinese-accented speech (https://www.laokaoya.com/17054.html).
- A Chinese Zhihu IELTS article frames the public descriptors as a guard against blind template memorisation and summarises Band 7 as flexible vocabulary, some less common/idiomatic items, complex grammar with some errors, and pronunciation judged by intelligibility rather than accent identity (https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/1945919027349266867).

### Vietnam

- IDP Vietnam explains discourse markers as phrases that connect ideas and support fluent, coherent transmission of ideas, explicitly linking them to the Fluency and Coherence criterion, which carries 25% of the Speaking score (https://ielts.idp.com/vietnam/about/news-and-articles/article-discourse-markers-ielts-speaking).
- IDP Vietnam says discourse markers can buy thinking time, and gives "Let me think about that for a minute" as preferable to silence or repeated "umm/err", which is useful for distinguishing functional fillers from disfluent searching (https://ielts.idp.com/vietnam/about/news-and-articles/article-discourse-markers-ielts-speaking).
- Vietnamese RES explains FC through longer answers, discourse markers/conjunctions, coherent sentence flow, stable speed and limited self-correction; it says a minimal Part 1 answer such as "Yes, of course" is too short because candidates should add explanation or an example (https://res.edu.vn/unit-2-tieu-chi-cham-diem-ielts-speaking/).
- IELTS LangGo says Band 7+ candidates use varied discourse markers reasonably and flexibly, while lower-band speakers often overuse simple markers; this aligns with the official distinction between Band 7 flexible cohesive use and Band 5 marker overuse (https://langgo.edu.vn/discourse-markers-la-gi-cach-su-dung-hieu-qua-trong-ielts-speaking; https://cdn.ielts.org/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).

### Iran / Persian-language prep

- Persian IELTS2 describes the band descriptors as the scoring framework examiners use across the four Speaking criteria, and frames Band 6 as communicative but still affected by grammatical/lexical errors and Band 7+ as requiring stronger control and flexibility (https://ielts2.com/understanding-the-ielts-speaking-band-descriptors/).
- Persian IELTS2 advises that long pauses and unnecessary repetition damage Fluency and Coherence, while linking expressions such as "for example" and "in contrast" help organise ideas when used naturally (https://ielts2.com/understanding-the-ielts-speaking-band-descriptors/).
- Persian-language IELTS International summarises IELTS Speaking scoring as four equal 25% criteria and describes a higher-band speaker as switching style appropriately, using flexible phrasing to buy time naturally, and showing clear pronunciation with natural stress and intonation (https://www.ielts.international/fa/ielts-speaking-scoring).

### India and Pakistan

- IDP India reproduces the public Speaking descriptors, including the Band 6 and Band 7 distinction that Band 6 may lose coherence at times while Band 7 speaks at length without noticeable effort or loss of coherence (https://ieltsidpindia.com/information/ielts-band-scores/speaking).
- IDP's global tips page says the examiner awards 0-9 criterion scores for Fluency and Coherence, Lexical Resource, Grammatical Range and Accuracy, and Pronunciation; it recommends studying the assessment criteria, expanding answers, using a mix of simple and complex sentences, speaking English often, and practising rephrasing ideas (https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-10-tips-improve-your-ielts-speaking-band-score).
- Indian prep provider PND IELTS frames the 6.5 to 7 jump as consistency, not just isolated vocabulary or speed, which is compatible with the official requirement that a candidate must fully fit a band's positive features and be rated on average performance (https://www.pndielts.in/blog/ielts-speaking-tips-for-band-7/; https://cdn.ielts.org/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).
- British Council Pakistan hosts a Fluency and Coherence IELTS video page, and its Pakistan-facing summary says the video explains FC aspects considered in IELTS Speaking and gives improvement advice (https://www.britishcouncil.pk/exam/ielts/prepare/videos/fluency-coherence).

### Indonesia

- IDP Indonesia says the Speaking test is assessed through fluency/coherence, lexical resource, grammatical range/accuracy and pronunciation, and explains fluency as speaking without excessive pauses/hesitation/repetition while coherence is logical sequencing and easy-to-follow ideas (https://ielts.idp.com/indonesia/prepare/article-ielts-speaking-tested-skills/en-gb).
- IDP Indonesia explains lexical resource as range and contextual use of vocabulary, including phrasal verbs, figurative language and paraphrasing, and explains grammatical range as accurate, complex and appropriate grammar rather than playing safe with only short simple sentences (https://ielts.idp.com/indonesia/prepare/article-ielts-speaking-tested-skills/en-gb).
- IDP Indonesia's band-score page says IELTS Speaking band descriptors help candidates understand how an examiner will assess them during the Speaking test, reinforcing that Indonesian candidates are judged by the same public descriptors rather than a local standard (https://ielts.idp.com/indonesia/results/how-ielts-is-marked/band-score-calculator/en-gb).

### Russia and Kazakhstan

- Russian-language IELTS Score 7 summarises the four criteria and explains Band 6 vs 7 in Russian: Band 6 can speak at length but may lose coherence, while Band 7 speaks at length without noticeable effort and uses linking flexibly; Band 6 grammar mixes simple/complex structures with errors, while Band 7 has varied complex structures and frequent error-free sentences (https://ieltsscore7.com/ru/guides/ielts-speaking-scoring-criteria/).
- IELTS.ru's Kazakhstan/Almaty page says every IELTS examiner is personally trained and certified to apply the assessment criteria correctly, and links to the public Speaking criteria (https://ielts.ru/alm/band_descriptors).
- IELTS Moscow hosts a public-version Speaking descriptor PDF mirroring the older public wording, including the Band 6 vs 7 distinction for long turns, flexible vocabulary, complex grammar and Pronunciation as Band 6 positives plus some Band 8 positives (https://www.ielts-moscow.ru/files/SpeakingFinal.pdf).

### UK, Australia and Canada native-perspective material

- British Council teacher guidance says candidates should speak at length, organise ideas logically, signpost appropriately, and avoid memorised large chunks because memorisation is unlikely to impress examiners (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-fluency-coherence).
- British Council lexical-resource guidance says memorising a long list of words is not enough; candidates must use vocabulary effectively, including collocation, connotation, less common expressions used correctly, and paraphrase around gaps (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-lexical-resource).
- British Council grammar guidance says a Band 7 score allows some grammatical mistakes when frequent error-free sentences are present, and that a communication-blocking error is more serious than an error that is easily passed over in conversation (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-grammar).
- British Council pronunciation guidance says pronunciation includes individual sounds, word stress, sentence stress and intonation, and highlights chunking as a useful technique; this supports scoring pronunciation beyond isolated phoneme accuracy (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-pronunciation).
- IELTS Australia compares Band 6 and Band 7 Speaking performances and reiterates that all four criteria each contribute 25% of the overall Speaking score (https://ielts.com.au/australia/about/news-and-articles/article-speaking-band-6-vs-band-7).
- IELTS Australia says native speakers still need to demonstrate structure, vocabulary range, grammatical control and clear pronunciation, because high scores are not just fluency and not based on opinion correctness (https://ielts.com.au/australia/prepare/article-ielts-for-native-english-speakers).
- IDP Canada explains examiner training, monitoring and re-certification, and says public band descriptors show what examiners look for when marking (https://ielts.idp.com/canada/prepare/article-understanding-ielts-examiners-and-how-they-score-tests).

## Open-source tools and code

No official open-source IELTS Speaking scorer was identified in the crawled official sources, and any automated system should be validated against human-rated IELTS-style data before using these descriptors operationally (https://ielts.org/researchers/our-research/research-reports/the-relationship-between-speaking-features-and-band-descriptors-a-mixed-methods-study). The projects below are relevant only as feature-extraction or preprocessing components for a diarized transcript/audio scoring pipeline, not as IELTS scoring authorities (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf).

- WhisperX can provide ASR, alignment and word-level timestamps that help estimate candidate speech rate, pause placement and turn timing when audio is available; GitHub URL: https://github.com/m-bain/whisperX; license and last-updated date were not verified in this crawl.
- pyannote-audio can support speaker diarization, which is necessary if the system must separate examiner and candidate speech before applying IELTS Speaking criteria; GitHub URL: https://github.com/pyannote/pyannote-audio; license and last-updated date were not verified in this crawl.
- openSMILE can extract acoustic and prosodic features that may support pronunciation, rhythm, pause and stress-timing analysis, but the IELTS score must still be tied to descriptor evidence rather than raw acoustic features alone; GitHub URL: https://github.com/audeering/opensmile; license and last-updated date were not verified in this crawl.
- Parselmouth exposes Praat functionality in Python and can support pitch, duration, intensity and pause measurements for pronunciation/fluency research features; GitHub URL: https://github.com/YannickJadoul/Parselmouth; license and last-updated date were not verified in this crawl.
- CLP Research's deep_disfluency project is relevant to detecting filled pauses, restarts and repairs in spontaneous speech transcripts, which map to IELTS fluency indicators such as false starts, backtracking, repetition and self-correction; GitHub URL: https://github.com/clp-research/deep_disfluency; license and last-updated date were not verified in this crawl; IELTS indicator source: https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf.

## Practical signals for an LLM judge

- First score only candidate speech, not examiner prompts; IELTS Speaking is an interview, but the score is awarded for the test taker's performance across the full test (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking; https://cdn.ielts.org/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).
- Treat the official descriptors as "average performance across all parts", so use evidence from Part 1, Part 2 and Part 3 rather than allowing one strong answer to dominate (https://cdn.ielts.org/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).
- For Fluency & Coherence, classify hesitation by function: content-planning hesitation is compatible with high bands, while mid-sentence searches for basic vocabulary/grammar are Band 5-6 evidence unless they are rare and do not affect coherence (https://cdn.ielts.org/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).
- For Fluency & Coherence, cap around Band 6 when long turns exist but answer coherence is lost through repeated repairs, self-corrections or inappropriate connectors; consider Band 7 only when long turns continue without noticeable effort or coherence loss (https://cdn.ielts.org/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).
- For Lexical Resource, do not reward rare words by themselves; reward precise, appropriate, collocationally natural choices and effective paraphrase, because IELTS defines lexical resource through appropriacy, style, collocation, attitude and paraphrase (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf).
- For Lexical Resource, distinguish Band 6 "meaning is clear despite inappropriate vocabulary" from Band 7 "flexible vocabulary with some less common/idiomatic items and style/collocation awareness" (https://cdn.ielts.org/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).
- For Grammar, count both range and accuracy: a candidate who attempts complex clauses but makes frequent errors in them is closer to Band 6, while a candidate who frequently produces error-free simple and complex sentences with flexible structure use is closer to Band 7 (https://cdn.ielts.org/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).
- For Grammar, treat communication-blocking errors as more serious than minor slips, because IELTS key criteria include the communicative effect of errors and British Council grammar guidance makes the same distinction (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf; https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-grammar).
- For Pronunciation, if no audio is available, mark confidence low and avoid inferring pronunciation from spelling-like transcript errors; IELTS pronunciation indicators are acoustic/prosodic features such as chunking, rhythm, stress, intonation, word stress and sound production (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf).
- For Pronunciation with audio or reliable transcript annotations, Band 6 allows generally understandable speech with variable phonological control and occasional clarity loss, while Band 7 requires all Band 6 positives plus some Band 8-like control such as easier understanding, sustained rhythm, or flexible stress/intonation (https://cdn.ielts.org/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).
- Do not penalise accent identity; penalise only the effect of accent or pronunciation on intelligibility and listener effort, because IELTS and IDP frame pronunciation around clarity, stress, rhythm, intonation and intelligibility rather than native-like accent (https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-does-ielts-speaking-assess-your-accent; https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf).
- Treat memorised chunks cautiously: polished memorised language without flexible adaptation should not be credited as broad fluency/lexical/grammar control, because British Council warns memorising large chunks is not a good Speaking strategy and is unlikely to impress the examiner (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-fluency-coherence).
- Ignore content truth, personal opinion quality, body language and charisma unless they affect language evidence; IELTS Australia and British Council state that the test judges English expression and the language criteria, not whether opinions are right or whether ideas are creative (https://ielts.com.au/australia/prepare/article-ielts-for-native-english-speakers; https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-fluency-coherence).
- Use cluster scoring: IELTS research found overlap between candidates at the same level and no single speaking feature that separates all bands, so combine pause burden, coherence, vocabulary flexibility, grammar range/accuracy and pronunciation evidence rather than using one hard rule (https://ielts.org/researchers/our-research/research-reports/candidate-discourse-in-the-revised-ielts-speaking-test; https://ielts.org/researchers/our-research/research-reports/the-relationship-between-speaking-features-and-band-descriptors-a-mixed-methods-study).
