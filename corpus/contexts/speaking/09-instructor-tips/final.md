# Cram-school and YouTube examiner tips across Korea, China, Vietnam, Iran, India, Pakistan, Indonesia, plus native-speaker test-prep channels

_Merged from codex + claude crawls on 2026-05-19T08:00:00Z._

## Summary

This topic collects the operational advice that the world's largest IELTS-prep
ecosystems give Speaking candidates, alongside the authoritative scoring
documents the advice is calibrated against. An LLM judge cares because
test-prep advice converges on a small set of measurable surface behaviours —
formulaic openers, specific discourse markers, fixed Part-2 templates,
"speak-non-stop" tactics, body-language coaching — which the judge can both
reward (when they actually hit Band-7 descriptor targets like coherent
extension and flexible paraphrase) and discount (when they expose
memorisation or merely scaffold mid-band output). The official Speaking
score still depends on the four equally-weighted criteria — Fluency &
Coherence, Lexical Resource, Grammatical Range & Accuracy, Pronunciation —
applied holistically across the whole 11–14-minute interview
([IELTS format](https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking),
[IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf),
[British Council assessment guide](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/test-information/assessment),
[IDP score explanation](https://ielts.idp.com/vietnam/results/scores/speaking/en-gb),
[IDP Speaking score page](https://ielts.idp.com/results/scores/speaking)).
The country-specific sections below preserve the L1-transfer issues each
local industry obsesses over and the strategy tactics they sell, so the
judge can read those patterns into a transcript instead of being surprised
by them.

## Authoritative material

### Test format and scoring

- The official IELTS Speaking test is a recorded face-to-face interview
  with three parts: familiar questions in Part 1 (4–5 min), a long-turn
  cue card in Part 2 (1-min prep, 1–2 min monologue), and a deeper two-way
  discussion in Part 3 (4–5 min) related to the Part-2 topic. Certificated
  examiners assess the whole performance, not a single answer in isolation
  ([IELTS format](https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking),
  [IDP Iran — Speaking Part 1](https://ielts.idp.com/iran/prepare/article-ielts-speaking-test-part-1),
  [IDP Indonesia Speaking preparation](https://ielts.idp.com/indonesia/prepare/speaking/en-gb)).
  For a diarized transcript, aggregate evidence across examiner–candidate
  turns while treating Part 2 as the clearest sample of independent sustained
  speech.
- The four Speaking criteria are equally weighted and the final band is
  effectively the average ([IDP Speaking score page](https://ielts.idp.com/results/scores/speaking),
  [IDP Speaking band descriptors PDF (BC mirror)](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf)).
  IELTS describes Fluency & Coherence as normal-speed speech with limited
  hesitation, logical sequencing, and appropriate cohesive devices; Lexical
  Resource as range, accuracy, and appropriacy of vocabulary; Grammatical
  Range & Accuracy as the range and accuracy of grammar used; Pronunciation
  as intelligibility plus phonological features such as connected speech,
  chunking, stress, rhythm, and intonation
  ([IELTS format](https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking),
  [IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf)).

### Band descriptor boundaries (Band 6 → 7 → 8)

- **Fluency & Coherence.** Band 6 can keep going and produce long turns
  but may lose coherence through hesitation, repetition, or self-correction;
  Band 7 speaks at length without noticeable effort or loss of coherence and
  may demonstrate language-related hesitation only at times; Band 8 is
  fluent with only occasional repetition or self-correction, with hesitation
  usually content-related rather than language-search related
  ([IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf),
  [IELTS Australia — Band 6 vs Band 7](https://ielts.com.au/australia/about/news-and-articles/article-speaking-band-6-vs-band-7),
  [BabyCode FC by band](https://blog.babycode.org/2025-08-19-ielts-speaking-band-descriptors-fluency-coherence-what-band-7-8-9-sound-like/)).
- **Lexical Resource.** Band 6 has "wide enough vocabulary" and generally
  successful paraphrase; Band 7 shows flexible vocabulary, some less-common
  or idiomatic items, awareness of style/collocation, and effective
  paraphrase (inappropriacies allowed); Band 8 requires wide vocabulary used
  readily and flexibly with skilful less-common or idiomatic language,
  despite occasional inaccuracies
  ([IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf),
  [ieltstutors descriptors](https://ieltstutors.org/speaking-band-descriptors/)).
- **Grammatical Range & Accuracy.** Band 6 mixes simple and complex forms
  with frequent complex-structure errors that rarely block communication;
  Band 7 uses a range of complex structures with some flexibility and
  frequent error-free sentences; Band 8 uses a wide range of structures
  flexibly with a majority of error-free sentences
  ([IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf)).
- **Pronunciation.** The current public descriptors cover intelligibility,
  phonological features, connected speech, chunking, stress-timing, rhythm,
  and L1 accent effect; Band 6 has variable control, Band 7 combines all
  positive Band 6 features with some Band 8 features, and Band 8 is easy
  to understand throughout with only occasional lapses
  ([IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf)).

### Examiner training, accent, and the "don't memorise" warning

- IELTS examiners are certified Band-9 themselves, retrained every two
  years, and assessed via standardised speaking samples; the rubric is
  applied holistically against a "best-fit" band rather than by counting
  errors ([British Council — become an examiner](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/become-ielts-examiner)).
- Any L1 accent is accepted provided it does not impair intelligibility,
  so the assessable pronunciation features are word/sentence stress,
  chunking, rhythm, and rising/falling intonation, not the "Englishness" of
  vowel quality ([British Council India — pronunciation](https://www.britishcouncil.in/blog/pronunciation-ielts-speaking-test),
  [IDP Pakistan — accent](https://ielts.idp.com/pakistan/prepare/article-accents-ielts-speaking-scores)).
- IELTS.org's own advice piece "Don't overdo it" tells candidates not to
  script answers — examiners are trained to spot the pattern, and over-
  rehearsed candidates trigger the memorised-content deduction; the same
  warning is replicated by every major regional academy below
  ([IELTS.org — don't overdo it](https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/dont-overdo-it-how-to-ace-your-ielts-speaking-test),
  [IELTS.org — demystifying Speaking](https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/demystifying-the-ielts-speaking-test)).

### British Council teaching materials

- British Council teacher materials reinforce that preparation should be
  criterion-based: their Speaking lesson plans include Part 1 test
  familiarisation, lexical resource, fluency/coherence, words and phrases
  for opinions/reasons/results/facts, extended answers, and Part 2
  description/definition language ([British Council Speaking lesson plans](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/lesson-plans-speaking)).
- British Council's PPF (Past-Present-Future) materials teach PPF as a
  Part-2 strategy to help students speak at length and practise a range of
  verb tenses, while stating that it is not essential to cover all three
  time frames in every answer
  ([PPF strategy PDF](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/speaking_part_2_-_ppf_strategy.pdf),
  [PPF classroom PDF](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/speaking_part_2_using_ppf_strategy_.pdf)).

## Country-specific prep material

### South Korea — 해커스 (Hackers), 파고다 (Pagoda), Lexis

Korean academies stratify Speaking classes by target band (5.5+ / 6.0+ /
6.5+ / 7.0+) rather than by general level — the implicit assumption is
that the bottleneck for Korean candidates is hitting a specific descriptor,
not raw English ability
([WhereWork — Korean IELTS academies](https://whereisyourwork.com/education-english-ielts-speaking-academy/),
[Lexis Korea](https://lexisenglish.co.kr/)).

- A SegyeBiz/Hackers report quotes Hackers IELTS Speaking instructor Yun
  Sung-won saying the test values communicative delivery over mere accuracy,
  with Part-2 advice to use eye contact and gestures with the examiner
  ([SegyeBiz/Hackers article](https://m.segyebiz.com/newsView/20200609512419)).
  Body language is not audible transcript evidence; the useful signal is
  whether the candidate maintains intelligible, interactive delivery without
  collapsing into scripted recitation.
- Hackers' 1:1 Speaking Interview class report frames Korean prep around
  individual interview practice, examiner-like feedback, and whole-course
  management for target-score students
  ([iNews24/Hackers report](https://www.inews24.com/view/1203337)). Hackers'
  own self-study guide pushes shadowing + recording as the primary daily
  loop, with weekly review of one's own audio for unfilled pauses >2 s
  ([Hackers independent-study guide](https://www.gohackers.com/?c=ielts/ielts_info2/ielts_method&type=url&uid=580926)).
- PAGODA's IELTS Speaking textbook markets a 5.5–6.5 target basic course
  with topic study, answer strategies, practice, part-by-part core topics,
  target-band model answers, scoring points, topic vocabulary, and
  expressions from PAGODA IELTS instructors
  ([YES24 PAGODA listing](https://www.yes24.com/product/goods/112186739)).
  PAGODA's online 1:1 coaching/feedback advertises Part-1/3 feedback on
  pronunciation, intonation, stress, relevance, completion, and overall
  performance, and Part-2 feedback on practice method plus pronunciation,
  intonation, and stress ([PAGODA Star premium coaching](https://www.pagodastar.com/course/premium)).
- Korean instructor diagnoses of typical L1 problems:
  - Korean students score lower in Speaking than Writing because they
    construct complex sentences in their head before speaking, producing
    long mid-utterance pauses that destroy FC
    ([IDP Canada — Korean tips](https://ielts.idp.com/canada/prepare/article-ielts-tips-korean-language)).
  - Korean schooling habituates students to "repeat the teacher / memorise
    the script" Speaking practice, which is exactly the behaviour the
    examiner penalises ([IDP Canada — Korean tips](https://ielts.idp.com/canada/prepare/article-ielts-tips-korean-language)).
  - Skybel and similar Korean prep blogs push a "Don't stop talking —
    rephrase" rule: when the target word does not surface, paraphrase
    immediately rather than freeze, because the FC drop from a 3-second
    silence beats the LR drop from a slightly less precise word
    ([Skybel high-band routines](https://blog.skybel.co.kr/blog/%EC%95%84%EC%9D%B4%EC%97%98%EC%B8%A0%20%EC%8A%A4%ED%94%BC%ED%82%B9/%EC%95%84%EC%9D%B4%EC%97%98%EC%B8%A0-%EC%8A%A4%ED%94%BC%ED%82%B9-%EA%B3%A0%EB%93%9D%EC%A0%90%EC%9D%84-%EC%9C%84%ED%95%9C-%EA%B8%B4%EC%9E%A5-%EA%B7%B9%EB%B3%B5%EB%B2%95-%EC%8B%AC%EB%A6%AC%EC%A0%81-%EC%95%88%EC%A0%95%EB%B6%80%ED%84%B0-%EB%8B%B5%EB%B3%80-%EB%A3%A8%ED%8B%B4%EA%B9%8C%EC%A7%80)).
  - Han Balance, much-referenced on Naver, frames fluency as "다양한
    형용사로 시간 벌기" — stock up on descriptor adjectives so a slow-moving
    thought still produces grammatically correct output
    ([Han Balance — fluency post](https://joynlink.today/entry/ielts-speaking-fluency)).

### Mainland China — 新东方 (XDF), 雅思哥, 小站, 新航道, 新通, 沪江, Zhihu high-band guides

- New Oriental Online's Chinese Part-2 guide warns that students struggle
  to organise language in the 1-min preparation period and may be cut off
  before reaching the main point; it advises strategic use of the 1-min prep
  and 1–2 min response ([新东方在线 Part 2 答题技巧](https://ielts.koolearn.com/20150422/792990.html)).
  Long preambles that burn time without topic development are a transcript-
  level red flag.
- XDF's flagship four-criteria summary notes that the FC band jump from
  6→7 is gated on willingness to "speak at length" with sentences longer
  than simple SVO — long-sentence production is more diagnostic than
  vocabulary at this band ([XDF — four-criteria scoring](https://ielts.xdf.cn/202004/11052541.html)).
- Xiaozhan's scoring-standard page explains the four criteria with practical
  examples: vocabulary enriched by topic, grammar range shown through
  clauses and emphatic structures, and pronunciation errors such as
  misplaced stress or difficult phonemes can hurt comprehensibility
  ([小站雅思评分标准](https://ielts.zhan.com/rumen119797.html)).
- Xintong Education's Chinese Part-2 article warns against over-long setup,
  overly simple content, identical training-school templates, unclear
  pronunciation, and irregular rhythm; it recommends BBC speech exposure for
  intonation, connected speech, stress, rhythm, and rising/falling tones
  ([新通教育 Part 2 得分技巧](https://www.igo.cn/kpnews/220153.shtml)).
- Chinese Band-7+ doctrine (especially via Zhihu) hinges on two LR levers:
  less-common expressions in short native register ("haggle", "pushy",
  "bossy", "commute", "splurge", "knack" — not GRE-style "big words") and
  idiomatic vocabulary, with *English Idioms in Use* / *English Phrasal
  Verbs in Use* as the canonical books
  ([Zhihu — lexical 7→9](https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/268284465),
  [新航道 Beijing — Band-7 vocabulary](https://bj.xhd.cn/ielts/ysky/803600.html)).
- The 雅思哥 app (de-facto Chinese question-bank recall) feeds Part-3
  material; XDF and TestDaily recommend drilling recalled questions to
  stockpile content for the discursive turn
  ([TestDaily Part 3 strategy](https://www.testdaily.cn/32707/),
  [XDF Part 3 deep dive](https://ielts.xdf.cn/202210/12339985.html),
  [XDF Speaking portal](https://ieltscat.xdf.cn/)).
- Discourse-marker lists are heavily standardised: 老烤鸭 and 新东方 both
  push a Part-3 connective bank of "Nevertheless / Furthermore / On the
  contrary / All things considered", with the warning that over-use triggers
  the memorised-connectives examiner flag
  ([XDF FC tips](https://ielts.xdf.cn/201711/10726583.html),
  [Zhihu — Part 1 fluency](https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/627339433)).
- Zhihu writer 雷哥雅思 characterises the 5/6/7 gap as: Band 5 = noticeable
  pauses + slow speech; Band 6 = some hesitation, willing to extend; Band 7
  = effort-free at length with a working range of connectives
  ([Zhihu 5/6/7/8 differences](https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/34529961)).
- The Chinese IDP descriptor PDF preserves the official descriptor content
  in Chinese — prefer it over commercial paraphrases when Chinese prep sites
  claim "band-7 phrases" or "high-score templates"
  ([IDP China descriptors](https://www.idp.cn/special/subject/IELTS/pdf/kscj_f2.pdf)).

### Vietnam — IELTS Fighter, DOL, Vietop, LangGo

- IELTS Fighter, Vietnam's largest chain (Hà Nội + HCMC + heavy YouTube),
  crystallises its method around the **A.R.E.A formula** — Answer the
  question, give a Reason, give an Example, then Add (counter or extension);
  this is the de-facto Part-1 and Part-3 template
  ([IELTS Fighter — Speaking method](https://ielts-fighter.com/tin-tuc/Phuong-phap-luyen-thi-IELTS-Speaking_c1448358933.html),
  [IELTS Fighter — 0 to 7+](https://ielts-fighter.com/tin-tuc/tu-hoc-ielts-speaking_mt1535062012.html)).
- IELTS Fighter's pronunciation focus is unusually narrow: word-stress and
  connected-speech drills, on the diagnosis that mis-stressed Vietnamese-
  accented English (over-stressed final consonants and missing linking) is
  the single biggest Vietnamese-candidate intelligibility killer. Like the
  Chinese chains, they push shadowing and warn against memorising full
  answers because examiners detect the recited cadence
  ([IELTS Fighter — self-study route](https://ielts-fighter.com/tin-tuc/tu-hoc-ielts-cho-nguoi-bat-dau-tu-0-len-7_mt1533596000.html)).
- IELTS Fighter's separate Vietnamese lesson on coherence/fluency lists
  Pronunciation among the IELTS Speaking criteria and teaches that
  fluency/coherence depends on the ability to produce longer sentences and
  use linking/filler language flexibly
  ([IELTS Fighter coherence-fluency lesson](https://ielts-fighter.com/speaking/Unit-2-Tieu-chi-cham-diem-cua-Ielts-speaking-What-is-coherence-fluency-_mt1453464253.html)).
- DOL's Vietnamese IELTS A-Z guide advises candidates to keep going in
  Part 2 until the examiner stops them and to avoid running out of words or
  sentences to explain ideas
  ([DOL IELTS A-Z PDF](https://cbq1uz9dtwobj.vcdn.cloud/PUBLIC/MEDIA/dol_vn_IELTS_A_Z_09ae637386.pdf)).
- Vietop hosts Simon-style IELTS Speaking materials with Part-2 tips and
  samples, reflecting a Vietnamese prep culture that uses model answers for
  topic coverage and language mining
  ([Vietop Simon Speaking PDF](https://vietop.edu.vn/wp-content/uploads/2019/12/IELTS-Speaking-Tips-and-samples-IELTS-Smion.pdf)).
- British Council Vietnam says Part 2 is difficult partly because real
  conversation rarely requires one person to speak for 1–2 minutes, and
  recommends home practice beyond two minutes so the test duration feels
  manageable ([British Council Vietnam Part 2 extension](https://www.britishcouncil.vn/en/adults/tips/extend-ielts-speaking-part-2-answer)).

### Iran — IDP Iran, Iran-Europe English Language Institute, Tehran academies

Iran's prep market is heavily IDP-oriented (no British Council operates
in-country), so the IDP Iran 10-tips list is the dominant syllabus baseline
([IDP Iran — 10 tips](https://ielts.idp.com/iran/prepare/article-10-tips-ielts-speaking),
[IDP Iran — Speaking Part 1](https://ielts.idp.com/iran/prepare/article-ielts-speaking-test-part-1)).

- Iran Cambridge's Persian Speaking tips state that the test has three parts
  and explicitly warn candidates not to present pre-memorised answers
  because professional examiners detect them
  ([Iran Cambridge IELTS Speaking tips](https://www.ircambridge.com/ielts-speaking-tips/)).
- Rad Time's Persian Part-3 article explains that Part 3 relates to the
  Part-2 topic, unlike the Part-2 monologue, and requires real dialogue
  with the examiner ([Rad Time Part 3 guide](https://radtime.org/part-three-speaking/)).
- Iran-Europe English Language Institute markets a dedicated "Tips and
  Techniques" track claiming a 0.5-band lift purely from test-strategy
  training — typical of the high-intensity Tehran market, which tends to
  treat Speaking as strategically gameable
  ([Iran-Europe — IELTS classes](https://iran-europe.net/en/ielts-classes/)).
- Persian-language teacher blogs (e.g. blogfa-hosted EEA Tehrani) emphasise
  listening to multiple native accents specifically to neutralise the
  Persian habit of staccato sentence rhythm — Farsi's syllable-timed prosody
  produces flat IELTS intonation under stress
  ([EEA Tehrani Persian blog](https://eea-tehrani.blogfa.com/post/327)).

### India — IDP India, British Council India, IELTSMaterial, ABNEducation

- British Council India's tips tell Indian candidates not to think first in
  Hindi and translate mentally into English, because this slows speech; the
  same article recommends lexical fields rather than random "difficult
  words", with food-topic examples such as taste adjectives and idioms used
  only when natural ([British Council India tips](https://www.britishcouncil.in/blog/ielts-speaking-test-preparation-tips),
  [British Council India — Band 5 to 7](https://www.britishcouncil.in/blog/journey-ielts-band-5-band-7-ielts-speaking-test)).
- IDP India's Band-9 Speaking guide takes a maximalist line: fully extend
  every answer, use a wide range of vocabulary and complex structures, vary
  intonation deliberately ([IDP India — Band-9](https://ieltsidpindia.com/blog/ielts-speaking-tips-and-tricks-for-band-9),
  [IDP India — Band 8 in each section](https://ieltsidpindia.com/blog/how-to-get-8-band-in-ielts)).
- Indian-specific pronunciation literature converges on **monotone /
  sing-song intonation** as the biggest L1-transfer problem: Indian English
  inherits a syllable-timed prosody where stress is roughly equally
  distributed, producing a flat IELTS line. Remedies pushed are deliberate
  falling intonation at statement-ends, rising at yes/no questions, and
  primary stress shifted onto content words rather than final words
  ([Magoosh — Indian intonation/pacing](https://magoosh.com/toefl/intonation-and-pacing-tips-for-indian-speakers-of-english/),
  [IDP India — accent](https://ieltsidpindia.com/blog/accent-in-ielts-speaking-test)).
  "Speak slightly slower" is repeated across Indian guides for a measurable
  reason — Indian-English informal speech rate regularly exceeds 160 wpm,
  above the 140-wpm conversational target examiners calibrate against.
- IELTSMaterial's cue-card guide gives the canonical Part-2 brainstorm-and-
  structure recipe (brainstorm, jot key points/vocabulary, follow bullets,
  avoid long pauses/fillers, vary vocabulary/grammar)
  ([IELTSMaterial cue-card guide](https://ieltsmaterial.com/what-is-cue-card-in-ielts-speaking/)),
  and the Part-2 criteria page restates the four criteria with continuous
  speech, appropriate pausing, linking words, accurate topical vocabulary,
  idiomatic language, clear articulation, stress/intonation, and varied
  grammar as targets ([IELTSMaterial Part 2 guide](https://ieltsmaterial.com/speaking/ielts-speaking-part-2/)).

### Pakistan — ABNEducation, IDP Pakistan, British Council Pakistan

- ABNEducation's "8+ from Pakistan" guide prescribes: never memorise; mind
  grammar but don't agonise over perfection; use the linker set "however /
  nevertheless / all in all / moreover" as the FC backbone; deliberately
  pick "less common" vocabulary in Part 3 to lift LR
  ([ABNEducation 8+ guide](https://www.abneducation.com/how-to-score-8-band-ielts/)).
- IDP Pakistan tells Pakistani candidates that the Urdu/Punjabi-accent worry
  is mis-placed — the real risk is mother-tongue interference at the
  segmental level (e.g. "v"/"w" merger, devoicing of final consonants); slow
  down to gain intelligibility ([IDP Pakistan — accent](https://ielts.idp.com/pakistan/prepare/article-accents-ielts-speaking-scores)).
- British Council Pakistan's preparation portal offers official mock
  speaking practice, lessons, seminars, workshops, and courses
  ([British Council Pakistan preparation](https://www.britishcouncil.pk/exam/ielts/prepare)).
  Its test-day page advises candidates to know the format, practise sample
  questions, check assessment criteria, record themselves and listen back
  for hesitation, repeated words, grammar errors, and overly fast speech,
  and learn synonyms for vocabulary variety
  ([British Council Pakistan test-day tips](https://www.britishcouncil.pk/prepare-your-test-day)).
- British Council Pakistan also notes that video-call Speaking uses the same
  test, examiners, content, scoring, timing, question format, and security
  as in-person Speaking — do not adjust scoring for delivery medium
  ([British Council Pakistan video-call Speaking](https://www.britishcouncil.pk/better-ielts-test-experience-video-call-speaking)).

### Indonesia — Kampung Inggris Pare, British Council Foundation Indonesia, Kobi Education, AUG, IDP Indonesia

Pare (Kediri, East Java) is Southeast Asia's largest English-immersion
ecosystem; IELTS programmes there are 1-month to 6-month residential with
24-hour English-only dorm rules
([Interpeace Pare](https://interpeacepare.com/jago-ielts/),
[Kampung Inggris Pare — IELTS 2026 guide](https://kampungpare.sch.id/panduan-ultimatif-kursus-ielts-kampung-inggris-2026/)).

- IDP Indonesia's Speaking preparation page gives the local official
  explanation: Part 1 familiar questions, Part 2 one-minute notes + two-
  minute speech, Part 3 discussion related to Part 2; recorded, conducted
  with a certified examiner
  ([IDP Indonesia Speaking preparation](https://ielts.idp.com/indonesia/prepare/speaking/en-gb),
  [IDP Indonesia Indonesian-URL Speaking](https://ielts.idp.com/indonesia/prepare/speaking)).
  Its Part-3 article says Part 3 requires expressing and justifying
  opinions, analysing, discussing, and speculating, with examiner questions
  exploring the topic in depth
  ([IDP Indonesia Part 3 tips](https://ielts.idp.com/indonesia/prepare/article-ielts-speaking-test-part-3/en-gb)).
- Part-1 timing: 4–5 minutes split across ~9–12 short questions; answers
  should be 2–4 complex sentences, not one-word
  ([Kampung Pare Part-1 prep](https://kampung-inggris.co.id/5-persiapan-menjawab-ielts-speaking-part-1/)).
- Vocabulary swaps as a fast-LR lever — e.g. "nuclear family" for "my
  parents and me", "commute" for "go to work" — exactly the "less common
  items" the descriptor names ([Kampung Pare Part-1 prep](https://kampung-inggris.co.id/5-persiapan-menjawab-ielts-speaking-part-1/)).
- Kobi Education's "12 tips" and AUG Study's Band-7 piece both hammer
  "ekspresi natural tanpa hafalan" (natural expression without memorisation),
  with AUG specifically warning that flat news-reader intonation is the
  single Indonesian-candidate behaviour most likely to cap pronunciation at
  Band 6 ([Kobi Education 12 tips](https://blog.kobieducation.com/tips-ielts-speaking/),
  [AUG Study Band-7](https://augstudy.com/zh/tips-ielts-speaking-band-7/),
  [Education Republic — Speaking strategi](https://www.myeducationrepublic.com/ielts/ielts-speaking-score-tips-strategi-efektif/)).
- British Council Foundation Indonesia tells candidates to budget the
  1-min Part-2 prep against four key bullets rather than attempt a full plan
  ([British Council Foundation Indonesia](https://www.britishcouncilfoundation.id/en/ielts-preparation-five-things-consider-your-speaking-test)).
- Indonesian guides commonly recommend 15–20 minute *daily* practice over
  2-hour weekly marathons ([Edupac — kriteria penilaian](https://www.edupac-pi.com/articles/kriteria-penilaian-speaking-ielts)).

### Russia / Kazakhstan

### Native-speaker and global test-prep channels — E2 IELTS, IELTS Liz, IELTS Advantage, IELTS Simon, Keith Speaking Academy, Oxford Online English, OpenIELTS

- **IELTS Liz** ([Liz hub](https://ieltsliz.com/ielts-speaking-free-lessons-essential-tips/),

## Open-source tools and code

- **pyannote.audio** — MIT-licensed Python toolkit for speaker diarization
  (GitHub release 4.0.4, 2026-02-07); produces reliable examiner/candidate
  speaker turns before transcript scoring. It does not assign IELTS bands
  ([pyannote.audio GitHub](https://github.com/pyannote/pyannote-audio)).
- **openSMILE** — modular feature extractor for speech/audio/video
  signal-processing research, source and binaries on GitHub from v3.0,
  research-oriented licensing; useful for acoustic correlates of rhythm,
  energy, and prosody for Pronunciation/Fluency research, but cannot replace
  descriptor judgment ([openSMILE docs](https://audeering.github.io/opensmile/index.html),
  [openSMILE licensing](https://audeering.github.io/opensmile/about.html),
  [openSMILE GitHub](https://github.com/audeering/opensmile)).
- **Praat** — phonetics tool ("doing phonetics by computer"), most source
  under GPL; useful for pitch, intensity, formants, stress/rhythm and
  pronunciation-feature analysis
  ([Praat GitHub](https://github.com/praat/praat)).
- These are evidence generators, not scoring authorities: IELTS sources
  define the criteria, while diarization/acoustic tools support consistency
  checks on turn timing, pause patterns, speech rate, stress/rhythm, and
  intelligibility proxies.

Closest adjacent corpus-style resources for calibration:

  ([BC Speaking practice tests](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/take-ielts/prepare/free-ielts-english-practice-tests/speaking)).

  list, mirrored by Chinese 雅思哥 and Vietnamese IELTS Fighter banks

## Practical signals for an LLM judge

Ranked roughly by how directly they apply when scoring a transcript:

1. **Anchor everything in the four official criteria.** Do not add separate
   marks for eye contact, gestures, confidence, accent prestige, factual
   originality, or cue-card bullet completion unless those behaviours affect
   FC, LR, GR, or Pronunciation in the transcript
   ([IELTS format](https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking),
   [IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf)).
2. **Country/L1 must not influence the band.** Accent is explicitly out of
   scope; the only pronunciation question is intelligibility (can the
   examiner reconstruct meaning without rewinding?)
   ([British Council India pronunciation](https://www.britishcouncil.in/blog/pronunciation-ielts-speaking-test),
   [IDP Pakistan accent](https://ielts.idp.com/pakistan/prepare/article-accents-ielts-speaking-scores)).
3. **Part-2 length target ≈ 90–120 seconds with all four bullets covered.**
   Under ~60 s gives weak evidence for Band-6+ willingness to produce long
   turns; >135 s usually means the examiner cut the candidate off, which is
   fine. Use bullets-covered, not raw minutes, as the primary FC signal;
   still aggregate with Parts 1 and 3
   ([IELTS International Russian guide](https://www.ielts.international/ru/ielts-preparation-tips/speaking-guide),
   [DOL IELTS A-Z PDF](https://cbq1uz9dtwobj.vcdn.cloud/PUBLIC/MEDIA/dol_vn_IELTS_A_Z_09ae637386.pdf),
   [Past Paper Hero Part 2](https://www.pastpaperhero.com/resources/ielts-part-2-long-turn-cue-card-organising-a-two-minute-talk),
   [IELTS Zone 2-min fluency](https://ieltszone.org/ielts-speaking-part-2-cue-card-fluently-2-minutes/)).
4. **Long unfilled pauses (>2 s) mid-clause cap FC at Band 6.** Korean prep
   diagnoses this specifically as a translation-from-L1 tell; multiple
   intra-sentence >2 s gaps cap FC regardless of vocabulary
   ([Skybel](https://blog.skybel.co.kr/blog/%EC%95%84%EC%9D%B4%EC%97%98%EC%B8%A0%20%EC%8A%A4%ED%94%BC%ED%82%B9/%EC%95%84%EC%9D%B4%EC%97%98%EC%B8%A0-%EC%8A%A4%ED%94%BC%ED%82%B9-%EA%B3%A0%EB%93%9D%EC%A0%90%EC%9D%84-%EC%9C%84%ED%95%9C-%EA%B8%B4%EC%9E%A5-%EA%B7%B9%EB%B3%B5%EB%B2%95-%EC%8B%AC%EB%A6%AC%EC%A0%81-%EC%95%88%EC%A0%95%EB%B6%80%ED%84%B0-%EB%8B%B5%EB%B3%80-%EB%A3%A8%ED%8B%B4%EA%B9%8C%EC%A7%80),
   [IDP Canada Korean tips](https://ielts.idp.com/canada/prepare/article-ielts-tips-korean-language)).
5. **Filler diagnosis is positional, not lexical.** Mid-utterance "um/uh/
   well" <0.5 s is native-like and does not drop FC; initial-position "um"
   before every new answer signals no pre-planned response and drops FC
   ([Liz fillers](https://ieltsliz.com/saying-umm-ahh-in-ielts-speaking/)).
6. **Distinguish content hesitation from language-search hesitation.**
   Band-8 hesitation is about ideas; Band-6 breakdown involves repeated
   repairs, word-searching, and lost coherence. Self-correction is fine at
   Band 7 only when seamless ("I went — sorry, I'd been going there for
   years"); stuttered restarts ("I — I — I think that — I mean — that the —
   uh — the people are…") cap at Band 6
   ([IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf),
   [IELTS Australia 6 vs 7](https://ielts.com.au/australia/about/news-and-articles/article-speaking-band-6-vs-band-7)).
7. **Speech-rate target ≈ 130–160 wpm.** >170 wpm clarity collapses
   (typical Indian-English failure mode); <110 wpm signals lexical-retrieval
   struggle; native conversational baseline ≈ 140 wpm
   ([VirtualSpeech](https://virtualspeech.com/blog/average-speaking-rate-words-per-minute),
   [Magoosh Indian pacing](https://magoosh.com/toefl/intonation-and-pacing-tips-for-indian-speakers-of-english/)).
8. **Discourse-marker count is non-linear with FC.** Practitioner heuristic:
   0 markers per Part-3 turn ≈ Band 5–6; 1–3 marker types used naturally ≈
   Band 7; >5 marker types crammed in ≈ Band 6 with memorisation flag
   ([IELTS Advantage](https://www.ieltsadvantage.com/2015/06/28/ielts-speaking-criteria/),
   [XDF FC](https://ielts.xdf.cn/201711/10726583.html),
   [IELTS Fighter coherence-fluency](https://ielts-fighter.com/speaking/Unit-2-Tieu-chi-cham-diem-cua-Ielts-speaking-What-is-coherence-fluency-_mt1453464253.html)).
   The judge should also check the markers actually link ideas.
9. **Reward paraphrase; discount echoing.** Band 6 generally paraphrases
   successfully; Band 7 effectively; Band 8 readily and flexibly. Repetition
   of the examiner's wording without expansion is weaker evidence
   ([IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf),
   [BC Pakistan test-day tips](https://www.britishcouncil.pk/prepare-your-test-day)).
10. **Less-common-item density is the LR Band-6→7 lever, not "big words."**
    Look for short, register-appropriate items ("haggle", "bossy", "pushy",
    "commute", "splurge", "knack"); GRE-register words (ubiquitous,
    paradigm) without surrounding ease are an over-prep tell, not LR
    evidence ([Zhihu 7→9](https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/268284465),
    [新航道 Beijing](https://bj.xhd.cn/ielts/ysky/803600.html),
    [BC India tips](https://www.britishcouncil.in/blog/ielts-speaking-test-preparation-tips)).
11. **Idiom rate has a practitioner target.** Chinese / Keith-Academy
    consensus is roughly **1 natural idiom per ~90-second Part-2 turn**
    for Band 7 (e.g. "to be honest", "to put it simply", "a piece of cake",
    "the icing on the cake"); zero idioms across the test caps LR at 6;
    >3 idioms in 90 s with unnatural fit is a memorisation tell
    ([Zhihu 7→9](https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/268284465),
    [Keith Speaking Academy](https://keithspeakingacademy.com/5-ways-get-band-8-ielts-speaking/)).
    **NOTE / conflict flag:** the codex crawl did not surface a specific
    idiom-rate number and the official descriptors do not name one; the
    Chinese practitioner range above is heuristic, not official, and the
    judge should use it as a soft prior rather than a hard threshold.
12. **Long-sentence production is the FC Band-6→7 marker (XDF).** A
    transcript dominated by simple SVO ("I like X. I do Y. I think Z.")
    caps at FC 6 regardless of vocabulary; subordination + coordination in
    most utterances signals 7+
    ([XDF four criteria](https://ielts.xdf.cn/202004/11052541.html)).
13. **Part-1 single-word answers are the Band-5 floor on FC.** "Yes" /
    "No" / "I like it" without expansion is the explicit example all chains
    warn against; even one such answer drops FC noticeably
    ([IELTS Advantage](https://www.ieltsadvantage.com/ielts-speaking/),
    [Kampung Pare Part 1](https://kampung-inggris.co.id/5-persiapan-menjawab-ielts-speaking-part-1/)).
14. **Use Part 3 as the main stress-test for Band 7+ abstraction.**
    Candidates should justify opinions, analyse, discuss, and speculate, not
    only give short personal anecdotes
    ([IDP Indonesia Part 3](https://ielts.idp.com/indonesia/prepare/article-ielts-speaking-test-part-3/en-gb),
    [Rad Time Part 3](https://radtime.org/part-three-speaking/)).
15. **Detect templates without auto-crediting them.** A.R.E.A, PPF, AAAE,
    IDEA, topic-details-feelings-close, idea-explain-example are Band-6/7
    scaffolds; credit them only when the resulting speech is responsive,
    coherent, and grammatically controlled. Band-8 candidates layer
    counter-arguments and concessions on top of the template
    ([IELTS Fighter A.R.E.A](https://ielts-fighter.com/tin-tuc/Phuong-phap-luyen-thi-IELTS-Speaking_c1448358933.html),
    [IELTS Simon Part 3](https://www.ielts-simon.com/ielts-help-and-english-pr/2014/02/ielts-speaking-part-3-add-more-detail.html),
    [BC PPF PDF](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/speaking_part_2_-_ppf_strategy.pdf),
    [IELTS Speaking Lab](https://www.ieltsspeakinglab.com/blog/ielts-speaking-part-2-strategies),
    [Learn English Weekly](https://learnenglishweekly.com/ielts/speaking/part-2-cue-cards)).
16. **Memorisation tells.** Cadence breaks when the candidate "drops" out
    of a recited block back into natural speech; unprompted use of very
    low-frequency formulaic openers ("In this fast-paced modern world…",
    "From my humble perspective…"); identical phrase reuse across distinct
    Part-1 answers; polished generic Part 2 followed by weak Part-3 follow-
    up. Any single instance drops the question's FC; clustered tells cap
    the whole Speaking test around Band 6
    ([IELTS.org don't overdo it](https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/dont-overdo-it-how-to-ace-your-ielts-speaking-test),
    [Iran Cambridge](https://www.ircambridge.com/ielts-speaking-tips/),
    [新通教育](https://www.igo.cn/kpnews/220153.shtml),
    [ielts-up](https://ielts-up.com/speaking/ielts-speaking-tips.html)).
17. **Monotone delivery caps Pronunciation at Band 6 regardless of
    segments.** Too-flat is as penalised as too-melodic. Minimum pattern:
    falling intonation on statements, rising on yes/no questions
    ([BC India pronunciation](https://www.britishcouncil.in/blog/pronunciation-ielts-speaking-test),
    [Magoosh](https://magoosh.com/toefl/intonation-and-pacing-tips-for-indian-speakers-of-english/),
    [AUG Study](https://augstudy.com/zh/tips-ielts-speaking-band-7/)).
18. **L1-segmental tells to log but not punish in isolation.** Mandarin:
    missing /sh/-/zh/ contrast, /θ/→/s/. Korean: /l/-/r/ merger, /f/→/p/.
    Indian/Pakistani: /v/-/w/ merger, devoiced finals. Persian: missing /w/,
    inserted vowel before initial /s/-clusters. These reduce intelligibility
    only when they recur; isolated instances are descriptor-irrelevant
    ([BC India pronunciation](https://www.britishcouncil.in/blog/pronunciation-ielts-speaking-test),
    [Grade-Online pronunciation guide](https://grade-online.com/ielts/blog/ielts-pronunciation-guide/),
    [小站雅思评分标准](https://ielts.zhan.com/rumen119797.html)).
19. **Notice translation-lag patterns from any L1.** Delayed starts, broken
    clauses, mid-sentence reformulations, unnatural word-for-word phrasing
    can indicate fluency, lexis, and grammar limits; occasional
    self-correction is normal at higher bands
    ([BC India tips](https://www.britishcouncil.in/blog/ielts-speaking-test-preparation-tips),
    [IDP Canada Korean tips](https://ielts.idp.com/canada/prepare/article-ielts-tips-korean-language)).
20. **Verify diarization before judging turns.** Confirm examiner prompts
    and candidate turns are separated correctly before judging turn length,
    interruptions, clarification requests, or overlap; diarization errors
    can fabricate fluency problems or hide examiner support
    ([pyannote.audio](https://github.com/pyannote/pyannote-audio)).
