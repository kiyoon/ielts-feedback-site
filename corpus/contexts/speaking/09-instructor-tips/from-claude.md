# Cram-school and YouTube examiner tips across Korea, China, Vietnam, Iran, India, Pakistan, Indonesia, plus native-speaker test-prep channels

_Crawled by claude on 2026-05-19T00:00:00Z. Sources cited inline._

## Summary

This file collects operational advice that the world's biggest IELTS-prep
ecosystems give candidates for the Speaking test. The reason an automated
judge cares about this material is that test-prep advice converges on a
small set of measurable behaviours — a candidate who follows their
instructor literally will produce predictable surface features (formulaic
openers, certain discourse markers, "extended" Part-3 answers built from
the same templates), which the judge can both reward (when they hit Band-7
descriptor targets) and discount (when they reveal memorisation). The
emphasis below is on country-specific tells, the L1-transfer issues each
local industry obsesses over, and the operational targets (timing, rate,
density) the judge can apply at scoring time.

## Authoritative material

### Official band descriptors (Cambridge / British Council / IDP)

The four equally-weighted criteria are Fluency & Coherence (FC), Lexical
Resource (LR), Grammatical Range & Accuracy (GR), Pronunciation (P); the
final band is the average ([IDP — Speaking band descriptors PDF](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf),
[IDP — speaking score page](https://ielts.idp.com/results/scores/speaking)).
The test is 11–14 minutes across Part 1 (4–5 min of familiar-topic
questions), Part 2 (1-min prep, then a 1–2 min long turn from a cue card),
and Part 3 (4–5 min of discussion of abstract issues linked to Part 2)
([IDP Iran](https://ielts.idp.com/iran/prepare/article-ielts-speaking-test-part-1)).

Band-7 vs Band-6 hinges on managed hesitation: a Band-6 candidate "may
self-correct and repeat, which sometimes disrupts flow"; a Band-7
candidate "speaks at length without noticeable effort or loss of coherence
[and] may demonstrate language-related hesitation at times" — the
hesitation exists but it doesn't break the thread ([IELTS Australia — Band 6 vs Band 7](https://ielts.com.au/australia/about/news-and-articles/article-speaking-band-6-vs-band-7)).
Lexical Resource band-7 demands "some ability to use less common and
idiomatic items" with awareness of style and collocation; inappropriacies
are allowed ([ieltstutors — band descriptors](https://ieltstutors.org/speaking-band-descriptors/)).

### Examiner training and what scoring is not

Examiners are certified IELTS Band-9 themselves, retrained every two
years, and assessed via standardised speaking samples; the rubric is
applied holistically against a "best-fit" band, not by counting errors
([British Council — become an examiner](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/become-ielts-examiner)).
Accent is explicitly out of scope: any L1 accent is accepted provided it
doesn't impair intelligibility, so the assessable features are word/
sentence stress, chunking, rhythm, and rising/falling intonation, not the
"Englishness" of vowel quality ([British Council India — pronunciation in Speaking](https://www.britishcouncil.in/blog/pronunciation-ielts-speaking-test),
[IDP — accent in Speaking](https://ielts.idp.com/pakistan/prepare/article-accents-ielts-speaking-scores)).

### Official "don't memorise" warning

IELTS.org's own advice piece "Don't overdo it" tells candidates not to
script answers — examiners are trained to spot the pattern, and over-
rehearsed candidates trigger the "memorised content" deduction; the same
warning is replicated by every major regional academy below
([IELTS.org — don't overdo it](https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/dont-overdo-it-how-to-ace-your-ielts-speaking-test)).

## Country-specific prep material

### South Korea — 해커스 (Hackers), 파고다 (Pagoda), Lexis

Both Hackers and Pagoda 학원 stratify their Speaking classes by target
band (5.5+ / 6.0+ / 6.5+ / 7.0+) rather than by general level — the
implicit assumption is that the bottleneck for Korean candidates is hitting
a specific descriptor, not raw English ability ([WhereWork — IELTS academies](https://whereisyourwork.com/education-english-ielts-speaking-academy/),
[Lexis Korea](https://lexisenglish.co.kr/)). Korean instructor diagnoses
of typical L1 problems:

- Korean students score lower in Speaking than Writing because they build
  complex sentences in their head before speaking, producing long mid-
  utterance pauses that destroy FC ([IDP Canada — Korean tips](https://ielts.idp.com/canada/prepare/article-ielts-tips-korean-language)).
- Korean schooling habituates students to "repeat the teacher / memorise
  the script" Speaking practice, which is exactly the behaviour the
  examiner penalises ([IDP Canada — Korean tips](https://ielts.idp.com/canada/prepare/article-ielts-tips-korean-language)).
- The Skybel and similar Korean prep blogs push a "Don't stop talking —
  rephrase" rule: if the target word doesn't surface, paraphrase
  immediately rather than freeze, because the FC drop from a 3-second
  silence beats the LR drop from a slightly less precise word
  ([Skybel — high-band Speaking routines](https://blog.skybel.co.kr/blog/%EC%95%84%EC%9D%B4%EC%97%98%EC%B8%A0%20%EC%8A%A4%ED%94%BC%ED%82%B9/%EC%95%84%EC%9D%B4%EC%97%98%EC%B8%A0-%EC%8A%A4%ED%94%BC%ED%82%B9-%EA%B3%A0%EB%93%9D%EC%A0%90%EC%9D%84-%EC%9C%84%ED%95%9C-%EA%B8%B4%EC%9E%A5-%EA%B7%B9%EB%B3%B5%EB%B2%95-%EC%8B%AC%EB%A6%AC%EC%A0%81-%EC%95%88%EC%A0%95%EB%B6%80%ED%84%B0-%EB%8B%B5%EB%B3%80-%EB%A3%A8%ED%8B%B4%EA%B9%8C%EC%A7%80)).
- Han Balance, a Korean-language blog much-referenced on Naver, frames
  fluency as "다양한 형용사로 시간 벌기" — stock up on descriptor adjectives
  so a slow-moving thought still produces grammatically correct output
  ([Han Balance — fluency post](https://joynlink.today/entry/ielts-speaking-fluency)).
- Hackers' own writer-blog "고우석사" course documents push "shadowing
  + recording" as the primary self-study loop, with weekly review of
  one's own audio looking for unfilled pauses >2s
  ([Hackers — independent study guide](https://www.gohackers.com/?c=ielts/ielts_info2/ielts_method&type=url&uid=580926)).

### Mainland China — 新东方 (XDF), 雅思哥, 沪江, 知乎 high-band guides

XDF's flagship summary of the four criteria notes that the FC band jump
from 6→7 is gated on willingness to "speak at length" with sentences
longer than simple SVO — long-sentence production is more diagnostic than
vocabulary at this band ([XDF — four-criteria scoring](https://ielts.xdf.cn/202004/11052541.html)).
Chinese prep doctrine, particularly via Zhihu high-band writers, hinges
on two LR levers above Band 6: less-common expressions ("haggle", "pushy",
"bossy" — short native register, not GRE-style "big words") and
idiomatic vocabulary, with English Idioms in Use / English Phrasal Verbs
in Use cited as the canonical books ([Zhihu — lexical 7→9](https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/268284465),
[新航道 Beijing — 7-band vocabulary](https://bj.xhd.cn/ielts/ysky/803600.html)).
The 雅思哥 app (effectively the de-facto Chinese question-bank
recall) feeds Part-3 prep material; XDF explicitly recommends drilling
recalled questions to stockpile content for the harder discursive turn
([TestDaily — Part 3 strategy](https://www.testdaily.cn/32707/),
[XDF — Part 3 cuoqu](https://ielts.xdf.cn/202210/12339985.html)).
Discourse-marker lists are heavily standardised: 老烤鸭 and 新东方 both
push a Part-3 connective bank of "Nevertheless / Furthermore / On the
contrary / All things considered" with the warning that overuse triggers
the "memorised connectives" examiner flag
([新东方 — fluency & coherence](https://ielts.xdf.cn/201711/10726583.html),
[Zhihu — Part 1 fluency](https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/627339433)).
Zhihu writer 雷哥雅思 explicitly characterises a 5-vs-6-vs-7 gap as:
Band 5 = noticeable pauses + slow speech; Band 6 = some hesitation,
willing to extend; Band 7 = effort-free at length, with a working range
of connectives ([Zhihu — 5/6/7/8 differences](https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/34529961)).

### Vietnam — IELTS Fighter

IELTS Fighter, Vietnam's largest chain (Hà Nội + HCMC + heavy YouTube),
crystallises its Speaking method around the **A.R.E.A formula** — Answer
the question, give a Reason, give an Example, then add (often a
counter/extension); this is their de-facto Part-1 and Part-3 template
([IELTS Fighter — Speaking method](https://ielts-fighter.com/tin-tuc/Phuong-phap-luyen-thi-IELTS-Speaking_c1448358933.html),
[IELTS Fighter — 0 to 7+](https://ielts-fighter.com/tin-tuc/tu-hoc-ielts-speaking_mt1535062012.html)).
Their pronunciation focus is unusually narrow: word-stress and
connected-speech drills, on the diagnosis that mis-stressed Vietnamese-
accented English (specifically over-stressed final consonants and missing
linking) is the single biggest Vietnamese-candidate intelligibility
killer. Like the Chinese chains, they push **shadowing** as the
day-to-day routine and tell candidates not to memorise full answers
because Vietnamese examiners are explicitly trained to detect the
"recited" cadence ([IELTS Fighter — self-study route](https://ielts-fighter.com/tin-tuc/tu-hoc-ielts-cho-nguoi-bat-dau-tu-0-len-7_mt1533596000.html)).

### Iran — IDP Iran, Iran-Europe English Language Institute, Tehran academies

Iran's prep market is heavily IDP-oriented (no British Council operates
in-country), so the official IDP Iran 10-tips list is the dominant
syllabus baseline ([IDP Iran — 10 tips](https://ielts.idp.com/iran/prepare/article-10-tips-ielts-speaking),
[IDP Iran — Speaking Part 1](https://ielts.idp.com/iran/prepare/article-ielts-speaking-test-part-1)).
Iran-Europe English Language Institute markets a dedicated "Tips and
Techniques" track that claims a 0.5-band lift purely from test-strategy
training (i.e. independent of language ability) — typical of the
high-intensity Tehran academy market, which tends to treat Speaking as
strategically gameable ([Iran-Europe — IELTS classes](https://iran-europe.net/en/ielts-classes/)).
Persian-language teacher blogs (e.g. blogfa-hosted EEA Tehrani) emphasise
listening to multiple native accents specifically to neutralise the
Persian habit of staccato sentence rhythm — Farsi's syllable-timed
prosody produces flat IELTS intonation under stress
([EEA Tehrani Persian blog](https://eea-tehrani.blogfa.com/post/327)).

### India — IDP India, ABNEducation-style cross-border guides, British Council India

IDP India's published Band-9 Speaking guide takes a maximalist line:
fully extend every answer, use a wide range of vocabulary and complex
structures, vary intonation deliberately ([IDP India — Band-9 tips](https://ieltsidpindia.com/blog/ielts-speaking-tips-and-tricks-for-band-9),
[IDP India — Band 8 in each section](https://ieltsidpindia.com/blog/how-to-get-8-band-in-ielts)).
The Indian-specific pronunciation literature converges on **monotone /
sing-song intonation** as the biggest L1-transfer problem: Indian English
inherits a syllable-timed prosody where stress is roughly equally
distributed, producing a flat IELTS line. The remedies pushed are
deliberate falling intonation at statement-ends, rising at yes/no
questions, and primary stress shifted onto content words rather than
final words ([Magoosh — Indian intonation/pacing](https://magoosh.com/toefl/intonation-and-pacing-tips-for-indian-speakers-of-english/),
[IDP India — accent](https://ieltsidpindia.com/blog/accent-in-ielts-speaking-test),
[British Council India — Band 5 to 7](https://www.britishcouncil.in/blog/journey-ielts-band-5-band-7-ielts-speaking-test)).
"Speak slightly slower" is repeated across Indian guides for a measurable
reason — Indian-English speech rate in informal registers regularly
exceeds 160 wpm, above the 140-wpm conversational target an examiner
naturally calibrates against.

### Pakistan — ABNEducation, IDP Pakistan

ABNEducation's "8+ from Pakistan" guide ([ABNEducation](https://www.abneducation.com/how-to-score-8-band-ielts/))
prescribes: never memorise; mind grammar but don't agonise over
perfection; use the linker set "however / nevertheless / all in all /
moreover" as the FC backbone; deliberately pick "less common" vocabulary
in Part 3 to lift LR. IDP Pakistan's article on accents bluntly tells
Pakistani candidates that the Urdu/Punjabi-accent worry is mis-placed —
the real risk is mother-tongue interference at the segmental level
(e.g. "v"/"w" merger, devoicing of final consonants) and slow it down
to gain intelligibility ([IDP Pakistan — accent](https://ielts.idp.com/pakistan/prepare/article-accents-ielts-speaking-scores)).

### Indonesia — Kampung Inggris Pare, British Council Foundation Indonesia, Kobi Education, AUG

Pare (Kediri, East Java) is Southeast Asia's largest English-immersion
ecosystem; the IELTS programmes there are 1-month to 6-month residential
with 24-hour English-only dorm rules ([Interpeace Pare](https://interpeacepare.com/jago-ielts/),
[Kampung Inggris Pare — IELTS guide 2026](https://kampungpare.sch.id/panduan-ultimatif-kursus-ielts-kampung-inggris-2026/)).
Indonesian prep specifically emphasises:

- Part 1 timing: 4–5 minutes is split across ~9–12 short questions;
  answers should be 2–4 complex sentences, not one-word ([Kampung Pare — Part 1 prep](https://kampung-inggris.co.id/5-persiapan-menjawab-ielts-speaking-part-1/)).
- Vocabulary swaps as a fast-LR lever — e.g. "nuclear family" instead of
  "my parents and me", "commute" instead of "go to work" — exactly the
  "less common items" the descriptor names ([Kampung Pare — Part 1 prep](https://kampung-inggris.co.id/5-persiapan-menjawab-ielts-speaking-part-1/)).
- The Kobi Education "12 tips" list and AUG Study's Band-7 piece both
  hammer the "ekspresi natural tanpa hafalan" (natural expression without
  memorisation) rule, with AUG specifically warning that flat "news-
  reader" intonation is the single Indonesian-candidate behaviour most
  likely to cap pronunciation at Band 6 ([Kobi Education — 12 tips](https://blog.kobieducation.com/tips-ielts-speaking/),
  [AUG Study — Band 7 natural speaking](https://augstudy.com/zh/tips-ielts-speaking-band-7/),
  [Education Republic — Speaking strategi](https://www.myeducationrepublic.com/ielts/ielts-speaking-score-tips-strategi-efektif/)).
- British Council Foundation Indonesia's pre-test piece tells candidates
  to budget the 1-minute Part-2 prep against four key bullets rather than
  attempt a full plan ([British Council Foundation Indonesia](https://www.britishcouncilfoundation.id/en/ielts-preparation-five-things-consider-your-speaking-test)).
- Indonesian guides commonly recommend 15–20 minute *daily* practice
  over 2-hour weekly marathons, citing the same research consensus
  ([Edupac — kriteria penilaian](https://www.edupac-pi.com/articles/kriteria-penilaian-speaking-ielts)).

### Native-speaker YouTube and online academies — E2 IELTS, IELTS Liz, IELTS Advantage, IELTS Simon, Keith Speaking Academy, Oxford Online English

- **IELTS Liz** ([ieltsliz.com](https://ieltsliz.com/ielts-speaking-free-lessons-essential-tips/))
  takes a moderate position on fillers: "um" / "uh" in mid-utterance is
  *not* penalised (it's native-like thinking time), but *initial-position*
  "um" signals "I don't have an answer" and should be replaced with
  proper stalling phrases ("Well, let me think about that...", "That's an
  interesting question, I suppose...") ([Liz — fillers post](https://ieltsliz.com/saying-umm-ahh-in-ielts-speaking/)).
- **IELTS Advantage** ([ieltsadvantage.com](https://www.ieltsadvantage.com/ielts-speaking/))
  hammers a single rule for Band 7+: discourse markers must be used
  *appropriately and only when necessary*, not sprinkled. The Band-6
  pattern is over-use ("firstly... secondly... thirdly..." every Part
  1 answer); the Band-7+ pattern is selective deployment that genuinely
  links ideas ([IELTS Advantage — Speaking criteria](https://www.ieltsadvantage.com/2015/06/28/ielts-speaking-criteria/)).
- **IELTS Simon** ([ielts-simon.com](https://www.ielts-simon.com/ielts-help-and-english-pr/2014/02/ielts-speaking-part-3-add-more-detail.html))
  reduces Part-3 extension to three drills: keep asking "why?", explain
  the alternative, give an example. The "idea — explain — example" pattern
  is the most-cited template across non-Chinese prep.
- **Keith Speaking Academy** ([keithspeakingacademy.com](https://keithspeakingacademy.com/5-ways-get-band-8-ielts-speaking/))
  defines the Band-8 leap as "developing the answer, not just answering"
  and pushes shadowing as the long-utterance training drill. Its case
  studies (e.g. Junjie 7.5→8) cite *idea-density* as the actual
  Band-8 lever, not vocabulary alone.
- **Oxford Online English** ([oxfordonlineenglish.com](https://www.oxfordonlineenglish.com/ielts-speaking-band-7))
  benchmarks a Band-7 performance video against the descriptors line by
  line — useful as a calibration reference for what "managed hesitation"
  actually sounds like.
- **E2 IELTS** and **British Council Tutorials** ([British Council Taiwan — fluency video](https://www.britishcouncil.org.tw/exam/ielts/courses-resources/videos/fluency-coherence))
  emphasise sentence-stress and chunking drills as the Pronunciation
  Band-6→7 lever.

## Practical signals for an LLM judge

These are the most operationalisable patterns the cram-school literature
suggests a judge can apply when reading a diarized transcript:

1. **Filler diagnosis is positional, not lexical.** Mid-utterance
   "um/uh/well" of <0.5s does *not* drop FC and is native-like; initial-
   position "um" before every new answer drops FC because it signals no
   pre-planned response. Penalise the latter, not the former
   ([Liz — fillers](https://ieltsliz.com/saying-umm-ahh-in-ielts-speaking/)).
2. **Long unfilled pauses (>2s) mid-clause are the Band-6 ceiling.**
   Korean-prep diagnoses this specifically as a translation-from-L1 tell;
   any transcript with multiple >2s gaps inside a single sentence is at
   most Band 6 on FC regardless of vocabulary ([Skybel](https://blog.skybel.co.kr/blog/%EC%95%84%EC%9D%B4%EC%97%98%EC%B8%A0%20%EC%8A%A4%ED%94%BC%ED%82%B9/%EC%95%84%EC%9D%B4%EC%97%98%EC%B8%A0-%EC%8A%A4%ED%94%BC%ED%82%B9-%EA%B3%A0%EB%93%9D%EC%A0%90%EC%9D%84-%EC%9C%84%ED%95%9C-%EA%B8%B4%EC%9E%A5-%EA%B7%B9%EB%B3%B5%EB%B2%95-%EC%8B%AC%EB%A6%AC%EC%A0%81-%EC%95%88%EC%A0%95%EB%B6%80%ED%84%B0-%EB%8B%B5%EB%B3%80-%EB%A3%A8%ED%8B%B4%EA%B9%8C%EC%A7%80),
   [IDP Canada — Korean tips](https://ielts.idp.com/canada/prepare/article-ielts-tips-korean-language)).
3. **Speech-rate target ≈ 130–160 wpm for native-like FC.** Above ~170
   wpm clarity collapses (typical Indian-English failure mode); below
   ~110 wpm signals lexical retrieval struggle. Native English
   conversation baseline is ~140 wpm ([VirtualSpeech](https://virtualspeech.com/blog/average-speaking-rate-words-per-minute),
   [Magoosh — Indian pacing](https://magoosh.com/toefl/intonation-and-pacing-tips-for-indian-speakers-of-english/)).
4. **Part-2 length target is 90–120 seconds with all four bullets
   covered.** Under 60s means the candidate either ran out of ideas or
   misjudged time; >135s usually indicates the examiner cut them off,
   which is fine. Use bullets-covered, not minutes, as the primary FC
   signal ([Past Paper Hero — Part-2 organisation](https://www.pastpaperhero.com/resources/ielts-part-2-long-turn-cue-card-organising-a-two-minute-talk),
   [IELTSZone — Part-2 fluency](https://ieltszone.org/ielts-speaking-part-2-cue-card-fluently-2-minutes/)).
5. **Discourse-marker count is non-linear with FC.** 0 markers per Part-3
   turn = Band 5–6; 1–3 marker types used naturally = Band 7; >5 marker
   types crammed in = Band 6 with memorisation flag (IELTS Advantage's
   "selective deployment" rule). Frequency alone misleads; the judge
   should also check the markers actually link ideas ([IELTS Advantage](https://www.ieltsadvantage.com/2015/06/28/ielts-speaking-criteria/),
   [新东方 — fluency & coherence](https://ielts.xdf.cn/201711/10726583.html)).
6. **Less-common-item density is the LR Band-6→7 lever, not "big
   words."** Look for short, register-appropriate items the Chinese prep
   canon names (haggle, bossy, pushy, commute, splurge, knack); GRE-
   register words (ubiquitous, paradigm) without surrounding ease are an
   over-prep tell, not LR evidence ([Zhihu 7→9](https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/268284465),
   [新航道 — vocab for Band 7](https://bj.xhd.cn/ielts/ysky/803600.html)).
7. **Idiom rate target.** Practitioner consensus is roughly 1 idiom per
   90-second Part-2 turn for Band 7 (e.g. "to be honest", "to put it
   simply", "a piece of cake", "the icing on the cake"); zero idioms
   caps LR at 6. >3 idioms in 90s with unnatural fit is a memorisation
   tell ([Zhihu 7→9](https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/268284465),
   [Keith Speaking Academy](https://keithspeakingacademy.com/5-ways-get-band-8-ielts-speaking/)).
8. **Self-correction is allowed at Band 7 only when seamless.** "I went
   — sorry, I'd been going there for years" is fine; "I — I — I think
   that — I mean — that the — uh — the people are…" caps at Band 6
   ([IELTS Australia — 6 vs 7](https://ielts.com.au/australia/about/news-and-articles/article-speaking-band-6-vs-band-7)).
9. **A.R.E.A / idea-explain-example template is Band 6–7 by itself.**
   Detecting the formula (Answer + Reason + Example + Alternative, in
   that order, for every Part-3 question) is consistent with mid-band
   schooled output; Band-8 candidates layer counter-arguments and
   concessions on top ([IELTS Fighter — A.R.E.A](https://ielts-fighter.com/tin-tuc/Phuong-phap-luyen-thi-IELTS-Speaking_c1448358933.html),
   [IELTS Simon — Part-3 detail](https://www.ielts-simon.com/ielts-help-and-english-pr/2014/02/ielts-speaking-part-3-add-more-detail.html)).
10. **Monotone delivery caps Pronunciation at Band 6 regardless of
    segments.** This is the Indian/Pakistani sing-song problem inverted
    — too-flat is just as penalised as too-melodic. Falling intonation
    on statements and rising on yes/no questions is the minimum required
    pattern ([British Council India](https://www.britishcouncil.in/blog/pronunciation-ielts-speaking-test),
    [Magoosh](https://magoosh.com/toefl/intonation-and-pacing-tips-for-indian-speakers-of-english/),
    [AUG Study](https://augstudy.com/zh/tips-ielts-speaking-band-7/)).
11. **L1-segmental tells to log but not punish.** Mandarin: missing /sh/
    /zh/ contrast, /θ/→/s/. Korean: /l/-/r/ merger, /f/→/p/. Indian/
    Pakistani: /v/-/w/ merger, devoiced finals. Persian: missing /w/,
    inserted vowel before initial /s/-clusters. These reduce
    intelligibility *only* when they recur; isolated instances are
    descriptor-irrelevant ([British Council India](https://www.britishcouncil.in/blog/pronunciation-ielts-speaking-test),
    [Grade-Online pronunciation guide](https://grade-online.com/ielts/blog/ielts-pronunciation-guide/)).
12. **Memorisation tells.** Cadence breaks when the candidate "drops"
    out of a recited block back into natural speech; unprompted use of
    very low-frequency formulaic openers ("In this fast-paced modern
    world…", "From my humble perspective…"); repetition of an exact
    phrase across two distinct Part-1 answers. Any one of these
    individually drops the question's FC score; clustered they cap the
    whole Speaking test at ~Band 6 ([IELTS.org — don't overdo it](https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/dont-overdo-it-how-to-ace-your-ielts-speaking-test),
    [ielts-up — 10 tips](https://ielts-up.com/speaking/ielts-speaking-tips.html)).
13. **Part-1 single-word answers are the Band-5 floor on FC.** "Yes" /
    "No" / "I like it" without expansion is the explicit example all the
    chains warn against; even one such answer drops FC noticeably
    ([IELTS Advantage](https://www.ieltsadvantage.com/ielts-speaking/),
    [Kampung Pare — Part 1](https://kampung-inggris.co.id/5-persiapan-menjawab-ielts-speaking-part-1/)).
14. **Long-sentence production is the FC Band-6→7 marker (XDF).**
    A transcript dominated by simple SVO ("I like X. I do Y. I think Z.")
    caps at FC 6 regardless of vocabulary; subordination + coordination
    in most utterances signals 7+ ([XDF — four criteria](https://ielts.xdf.cn/202004/11052541.html)).
15. **Country/L1 should NOT influence the band.** The judge should
    suppress any tendency to map accent to band; intelligibility (can
    the examiner reconstruct meaning without rewinding?) is the only
    pronunciation question ([British Council India](https://www.britishcouncil.in/blog/pronunciation-ielts-speaking-test),
    [IDP — accent](https://ielts.idp.com/pakistan/prepare/article-accents-ielts-speaking-scores)).
