# Cram-school and YouTube examiner tips across Korea, China, Vietnam, Iran, India, Pakistan, Indonesia, plus native-speaker test-prep channels

_Crawled by codex on 2026-05-19T07:32:45Z. Sources cited inline._

## Summary

This topic collects official IELTS Speaking scoring guidance and high-volume instructor advice from cram schools, national IELTS portals, and YouTube-oriented prep brands so the judge can distinguish real language evidence from coached test behaviour ([IELTS format](https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking), [IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf)). It is useful at scoring time because many candidates are trained to use cue-card templates, memorised topic banks, discourse markers, gestures, and "speak for two minutes" tactics, but the official score still depends on Fluency & Coherence, Lexical Resource, Grammatical Range & Accuracy, and Pronunciation across the whole interview ([British Council assessment guide](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/test-information/assessment), [IDP score explanation](https://ielts.idp.com/vietnam/results/scores/speaking/en-gb)).

## Authoritative material

- The official IELTS Speaking test is a recorded face-to-face interview with three parts: familiar questions in Part 1, a long-turn cue card in Part 2, and a deeper two-way discussion in Part 3; certificated IELTS examiners assess the whole performance, not a single answer in isolation ([IELTS format](https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking)). For a diarized transcript, the judge should therefore aggregate evidence across examiner-candidate turns, while treating Part 2 as the clearest sample of independent sustained speech ([IELTS format](https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking)).
- IELTS names four Speaking criteria: Fluency & Coherence, Lexical Resource, Grammatical Range & Accuracy, and Pronunciation; IELTS describes Fluency & Coherence as normal-speed speech with limited hesitation, logical sequencing, and appropriate cohesive devices ([IELTS format](https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking)). IELTS describes Lexical Resource as range, accuracy, and appropriacy of vocabulary for meaning, and Grammatical Range & Accuracy as the range and accuracy of grammar used ([IELTS format](https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking)).
- The public band descriptors operationalise the main 6-8 boundary: Band 6 can keep going and produce long turns but may lose coherence through hesitation, repetition, or self-correction; Band 7 speaks at length without noticeable effort or loss of coherence; Band 8 is fluent with only occasional repetition or self-correction, with hesitation usually content-related rather than language-search related ([IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf)).
- For Lexical Resource, the public descriptors separate Band 6 "wide enough vocabulary" and generally successful paraphrase from Band 7 flexible vocabulary, some less common or idiomatic vocabulary, awareness of style/collocation, and effective paraphrase; Band 8 requires wide vocabulary used readily and flexibly with skilful less-common or idiomatic language, despite occasional inaccuracies ([IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf)).
- For Grammar, the public descriptors separate Band 6 mixed simple/complex sentence forms with frequent complex-structure errors that rarely block communication, Band 7 a range of complex structures with some flexibility and frequent error-free sentences, and Band 8 a wide range of structures used flexibly with a majority of error-free sentences ([IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf)).
- For Pronunciation, the current public descriptors include intelligibility, phonological features, connected speech, chunking, stress-timing, rhythm, and L1 accent effect; Band 6 has variable control, Band 7 combines all positive Band 6 features with some Band 8 features, and Band 8 is easy to understand throughout with only occasional lapses ([IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf)).
- British Council teacher materials reinforce that preparation should be criterion-based: their Speaking lesson plans include Part 1 test familiarisation, lexical resource, fluency/coherence, words and phrases for opinions/reasons/results/facts, extended answers, and Part 2 description/definition language ([British Council Speaking lesson plans](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/lesson-plans-speaking)).
- British Council's PPF materials teach Past-Present-Future as a Part 2 strategy to help students speak at length and practise a range of verb tenses, while also stating that it is not essential to cover all three time frames in every Part 2 answer ([British Council PPF strategy PDF](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/speaking_part_2_-_ppf_strategy.pdf), [British Council PPF classroom PDF](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/speaking_part_2_using_ppf_strategy_.pdf)).
- IDP Indonesia states that the Speaking test is the same for Academic and General Training, takes 11-14 minutes, is conducted with a certified examiner, and gives one minute of notes before a two-minute Part 2 response; the same page says Part 3 is a two-way discussion related to the Part 2 topic ([IDP Indonesia Speaking preparation](https://ielts.idp.com/indonesia/prepare/speaking/en-gb)). IDP Indonesia's Part 3 article says Part 3 tests expression/justification of opinions and ability to analyse, discuss, and speculate about issues related to the Part 2 topic ([IDP Indonesia Part 3 tips](https://ielts.idp.com/indonesia/prepare/article-ielts-speaking-test-part-3/en-gb)).

## Country-specific prep material

### Korea: 해커스, 파고다, and Korean academy patterns

- A Korean report on Hackers' IELTS speaking/writing high-score services quotes Hackers IELTS Speaking instructor Yun Sung-won saying the IELTS Speaking test values communicative delivery over mere accuracy, and it reports her Part 2 advice to use eye contact and gestures with the examiner ([SegyeBiz/Hackers article](https://m.segyebiz.com/newsView/20200609512419)). For scoring, eye contact and gesture are not audible transcript evidence; the useful signal is whether the candidate maintains intelligible, interactive delivery without collapsing into scripted recitation, because the official criteria are language and pronunciation criteria rather than body-language criteria ([IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf)).
- Another Korean report on Hackers' 1:1 Speaking Interview class frames Korean IELTS Speaking preparation around individual interview practice, examiner-like feedback, and whole-course management for target-score students ([iNews24/Hackers interview-class report](https://www.inews24.com/view/1203337)). This supports a scoring expectation that Korean academy-trained candidates may produce interview-formatted answers and polished Part 1/Part 2 routines, but the judge should still reward flexible paraphrase, accurate collocation, and non-mechanical cohesion only when visible in the transcript ([IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf)).
- PAGODA's IELTS Speaking textbook listing markets the book as a 5.5-6.5 target basic course with topic study, answer strategies, practice, part-by-part core topics, target-band model answers, scoring points, topic vocabulary, and expressions from PAGODA IELTS instructors ([YES24 PAGODA IELTS Speaking listing](https://www.yes24.com/product/goods/112186739)). This is a classic cram-school template model: the judge should not automatically credit band-level vocabulary simply because a candidate uses stock phrases; the transcript must show topic-appropriate lexical control and flexible grammatical range across follow-up questions ([IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf)).
- PAGODA's online 1:1 coaching/feedback page advertises speaking feedback for Part 1/3 on pronunciation, intonation, stress, relevance, completion, and overall performance, and Part 2 feedback on practice method plus pronunciation, intonation, and stress ([PAGODA Star premium coaching](https://www.pagodastar.com/course/premium)). This maps well to transcript review: relevance/completion is not a separate IELTS criterion, but off-topic or incomplete answers can reduce evidence for coherence, topic development, and lexical/grammatical range ([IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf)).

### China: 新东方, 小站雅思, 新航道/新通, 雅思哥-style cue-card culture

- New Oriental Online's Chinese Part 2 guide presents the familiar Chinese test-prep problem: students struggle to organise language in the one-minute preparation period and may be interrupted before reaching the main point; it advises candidates to handle the one-minute prep and 1-2 minute response strategically ([新东方在线 Part 2 答题技巧](https://ielts.koolearn.com/20150422/792990.html)). A judge should watch for long preambles that burn time without topic development, because the official descriptors reward coherent and appropriately extended topic development rather than memorised setup language ([IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf)).
- Xiaozhan's Chinese scoring-standard page explains the four criteria and gives practical examples: vocabulary should be enriched by topic, grammar range can be shown through clauses and emphatic structures, and pronunciation errors such as misplaced stress or difficult English phonemes can affect comprehensibility ([小站雅思评分标准](https://ielts.zhan.com/rumen119797.html)). For a judge, this means a Chinese L1 transcript should be evaluated for actual intelligibility and stress/chunking impact, not for accent identity itself, because the official descriptors focus on intelligibility and accent effect ([IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf)).
- Xintong Education's Chinese Part 2 article warns against over-long setup, overly simple content, identical training-school templates, unclear pronunciation, and irregular rhythm; it also recommends exposure to BBC speech for intonation, connected speech, stress, rhythm, and rising/falling tones ([新通教育 Part 2 得分技巧](https://www.igo.cn/kpnews/220153.shtml)). The transcript-level signal is template detection: repeated, generic structures may show coherence, but they should not be counted as flexible lexical or grammatical control unless the candidate adapts them accurately to the examiner's actual question ([IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf)).
- The Chinese IDP public descriptor PDF provides the official band descriptor content in Chinese, preserving the same four criteria and band-level contrasts used by IELTS examiners ([IDP China public speaking descriptors](https://www.idp.cn/special/subject/IELTS/pdf/kscj_f2.pdf)). When Chinese prep sites paraphrase the descriptors, the judge should prefer this official descriptor source over commercial claims about "band 7 phrases" or "high-score templates" ([IDP China public speaking descriptors](https://www.idp.cn/special/subject/IELTS/pdf/kscj_f2.pdf)).

### Vietnam: IELTS Fighter, DOL, Vietop, LangGo-style Part 2 training

- IELTS Fighter's Vietnamese lesson on coherence/fluency lists Pronunciation among the IELTS Speaking criteria and teaches that fluency/coherence depends on the ability to produce longer sentences and use linking/filler language flexibly ([IELTS Fighter coherence-fluency lesson](https://ielts-fighter.com/speaking/Unit-2-Tieu-chi-cham-diem-cua-Ielts-speaking-What-is-coherence-fluency-_mt1453464253.html)). For scoring, fillers and linkers should be treated as helpful only when they support logical flow; over-use or mechanical use aligns more with lower descriptor language about imperfect or inappropriate cohesive devices ([IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf)).
- DOL's Vietnamese IELTS A-Z guide includes a Speaking Part 2 note advising candidates to continue until the examiner stops them and to avoid running out of words or sentences to explain ideas ([DOL IELTS A-Z PDF](https://cbq1uz9dtwobj.vcdn.cloud/PUBLIC/MEDIA/dol_vn_IELTS_A_Z_09ae637386.pdf)). A judge should interpret a very short Part 2 turn as weak evidence for "willingness to produce long turns" unless the candidate shows strong sustained speech elsewhere, because IELTS Band 6+ fluency descriptors explicitly value ability to keep going and speak at length ([IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf)).
- Vietop hosts Simon-style IELTS Speaking materials with Part 2 tips and samples, reflecting a Vietnamese prep culture that uses model answers for topic coverage and language mining ([Vietop Simon Speaking PDF](https://vietop.edu.vn/wp-content/uploads/2019/12/IELTS-Speaking-Tips-and-samples-IELTS-Smion.pdf)). Model-answer exposure can improve lexical range, but the judge should penalise memorised, mismatched, or unnatural chunks when they produce grammar/collocation errors or fail to answer the examiner's question ([IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf)).
- British Council Vietnam's Part 2 extension article says Part 2 is difficult partly because real conversation rarely requires one person to speak for one to two minutes, and it recommends home practice beyond two minutes so the test duration feels manageable ([British Council Vietnam Part 2 extension tips](https://www.britishcouncil.vn/en/adults/tips/extend-ielts-speaking-part-2-answer)). This supports using Part 2 length and idea development as a Fluency & Coherence diagnostic, while still avoiding a pure word-count score because all four official criteria must be assessed ([IELTS format](https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking)).

### Iran: Persian academy advice

- Iran Cambridge's Persian IELTS Speaking tips state that the test has three parts and explicitly warns candidates not to present pre-memorised answers because professional examiners can detect them ([Iran Cambridge IELTS Speaking tips](https://www.ircambridge.com/ielts-speaking-tips/)). For a judge, a memorised-answer suspicion should be based on transcript evidence: unusually polished generic material followed by breakdown on follow-up, off-question content, or formulaic idioms that do not fit the examiner's prompt ([IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf)).
- Rad Time's Persian Part 3 article explains that Part 3 questions relate to the Part 2 topic, unlike the monologue in Part 2, and that Part 3 requires a dialogue with the examiner ([Rad Time Part 3 guide](https://radtime.org/part-three-speaking/)). The judge should therefore expect Part 3 to show higher-level opinion, justification, analysis, and speculation, matching IDP's description of Part 3 as deeper discussion of issues linked to Part 2 ([IDP Indonesia Part 3 tips](https://ielts.idp.com/indonesia/prepare/article-ielts-speaking-test-part-3/en-gb)).
- Persian prep pages commonly stress avoiding memorisation and preparing for the social interview format rather than only learning topic lists; this matches official scoring because candidates are assessed on spoken English-language performance against defined criteria, not on factual content knowledge ([Iran Cambridge IELTS Speaking tips](https://www.ircambridge.com/ielts-speaking-tips/), [IDP score explanation](https://ielts.idp.com/vietnam/results/scores/speaking/en-gb)).

### India

- British Council India's Speaking tips tell Indian candidates not to think first in Hindi and translate mentally into English, because this slows speech; the same article recommends lexical fields rather than random "difficult words", using food-topic examples such as taste adjectives and idiomatic expressions only when natural and meaningful ([British Council India speaking tips](https://www.britishcouncil.in/blog/ielts-speaking-test-preparation-tips)). The judge should treat delayed starts, mid-sentence translation pauses, and unnatural high-register words as possible Fluency & Coherence and Lexical Resource weaknesses, not as proof of low ability by themselves ([IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf)).
- IELTSMaterial's cue-card guide says Part 2 gives one minute to prepare and up to two minutes to speak, and advises candidates to brainstorm, jot key points/vocabulary, structure the response, follow bullet points, avoid long pauses/fillers, and use varied vocabulary/grammar ([IELTSMaterial cue-card guide](https://ieltsmaterial.com/what-is-cue-card-in-ielts-speaking/)). This gives the judge a transcript pattern to look for: successful candidates often organise Part 2 with opening, details, feeling/evaluation, and closing, but the score should depend on whether this organisation is linguistically controlled rather than merely present ([IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf)).
- IELTSMaterial's Part 2 criteria page restates the four assessment criteria and recommends continuous speech, appropriate pausing, linking words, accurate topical vocabulary, idiomatic language, clear articulation, stress/intonation, and varied grammar ([IELTSMaterial Part 2 guide](https://ieltsmaterial.com/speaking/ielts-speaking-part-2/)). For scoring, idioms and linking words must be accurate and appropriate; forced idioms, repetitive "moreover/however" chains, and memorised model-answer phrases should not raise Lexical Resource or Coherence scores ([IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf)).

### Pakistan

- British Council Pakistan's IELTS preparation portal offers preparation resources, online lessons, face-to-face courses, seminars, workshops, and mock tests for IELTS candidates in Pakistan ([British Council Pakistan preparation](https://www.britishcouncil.pk/exam/ielts/prepare)). This shows the Pakistan prep ecosystem includes official-provider mock speaking practice, so the judge should expect some candidates to know the test format and still judge only the realised language in the transcript ([IELTS format](https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking)).
- British Council Pakistan's test-day preparation page advises candidates to know the test format, practise sample questions, check assessment criteria, record themselves doing practice IELTS speaking tests, and listen back for hesitation, repeated words, grammar errors, and overly fast speech; it also recommends learning synonyms because they help show vocabulary variety in Speaking and Writing ([British Council Pakistan test-day tips](https://www.britishcouncil.pk/prepare-your-test-day)). These are directly operational for a judge: repeated words, self-correction, grammatical error density, and speech-rate problems map onto official Fluency, Grammar, Lexis, and Pronunciation descriptors ([IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf)).
- British Council Pakistan's video-call Speaking page says the video-call format uses the same test, examiners, content, scoring, timing, question format, and security as in-person Speaking ([British Council Pakistan video-call Speaking](https://www.britishcouncil.pk/better-ielts-test-experience-video-call-speaking)). If a transcript comes from a video-call test, the judge should not adjust scoring merely because delivery medium differs; score the same four criteria ([British Council Pakistan video-call Speaking](https://www.britishcouncil.pk/better-ielts-test-experience-video-call-speaking)).

### Indonesia

- IDP Indonesia's Speaking preparation page gives the official local explanation: Part 1 familiar questions, Part 2 one-minute notes and two-minute speech, and Part 3 a discussion related to Part 2; it also says the Speaking test is recorded and conducted with a certified IELTS examiner ([IDP Indonesia Speaking preparation](https://ielts.idp.com/indonesia/prepare/speaking/en-gb)). The judge should use the part labels in a diarized transcript because each part elicits different evidence: short interactive responses, independent long turn, and abstract discussion ([IDP Indonesia Speaking preparation](https://ielts.idp.com/indonesia/prepare/speaking/en-gb)).
- IDP Indonesia's Part 3 article says Part 3 requires expressing and justifying opinions, analysing, discussing, and speculating, with examiner questions exploring the topic in depth ([IDP Indonesia Part 3 tips](https://ielts.idp.com/indonesia/prepare/article-ielts-speaking-test-part-3/en-gb)). Therefore, a candidate who performs well in Part 1/2 but gives very short unsupported answers in Part 3 may have weaker evidence for Band 7+ Fluency/Coherence, Lexis, and Grammar in abstract discussion ([IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf)).
- Indonesian-language IDP pages mirror this same format information for local candidates, so country-specific transcript differences should not be treated as different scoring rules ([IDP Indonesia Speaking preparation Indonesian URL](https://ielts.idp.com/indonesia/prepare/speaking)).

### Russian/Kazakh region

- IELTS International's Russian Speaking guide presents ten strategies for Band 7+ and explains Part 2 as a card with three or four bullet points, one minute of preparation, and one to two minutes of uninterrupted speech; it warns that running out after about 45 seconds seriously harms Fluency & Coherence ([IELTS International Russian Speaking guide](https://www.ielts.international/ru/ielts-preparation-tips/speaking-guide)). The judge should treat sub-minute Part 2 turns as a strong warning sign, but not an automatic score cap, because the final official score is based on all evidence across the test ([IELTS format](https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking)).
- The same Russian guide advises short keyword notes rather than full sentences during the one-minute preparation and recommends recording and reading one's own transcript to reveal vocabulary gaps and grammar patterns ([IELTS International Russian Speaking guide](https://www.ielts.international/ru/ielts-preparation-tips/speaking-guide)). For an LLM judge, this supports transcript-based evaluation of repeated lexical gaps, repair sequences, and grammar pattern errors, rather than relying on subjective impressions of confidence ([IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf)).
- An IELTS.ru PDF hosts IDP examiner-approved Speaking tips and says Part 2 gives a task card; this shows Russian-language candidates also receive official-provider tactic sheets rather than only local commercial advice ([IELTS.ru IDP Speaking tips PDF](https://ielts.ru/images/practice/TipsForSpeaking.pdf)). A Kazakh education PDF includes "IELTS Speaking Part 2: Useful Language", showing regional classroom use of Part 2 phrase banks and useful-language lists ([Kazakh education PDF](https://rmebrk.kz/journals/3580/58646.pdf)). The judge should regard useful-language chunks as neutral evidence until accuracy, appropriacy, and flexibility are visible in context ([IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf)).

- IELTS Liz's Part 2 topic page provides cue-card topics and model-answer style practice for candidates preparing idea banks ([IELTS Liz Part 2 topics](https://ieltsliz.com/ielts-speaking-part-2-topics/)). The judge should separate idea richness from language score: content originality is not directly scored, but richer personal examples can give more opportunities to demonstrate vocabulary, grammar, and coherent development ([IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf)).
- Keith Speaking Academy markets Part 2 support around finding ideas quickly and speaking non-stop for two minutes on new topics, with useful phrases for flexible sentence creation ([Keith Speaking Academy Part 2 course](https://courses.keithspeakingacademy.com/p/crack-ielts-speaking-part-2)). For scoring, useful phrases are positive only if they are adapted flexibly; phrase-list recital without responsive content should not be credited as Band 7+ lexical control ([IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf)).
- An E2Language IELTS Speaking tips article mirrored by EVBN describes the 11-14 minute Speaking test, the three sections, one-minute preparation and 1-2 minute Part 2 speech, and the assessment of oral fluency, coherence, vocabulary, and grammar ([EVBN mirror of E2Language blog](https://evbn.org/ielts-speaking-all-the-tips-that-you-need-the-e2language-blog-1680224300/)). - OpenIELTS's former-examiner Part 2 guide says examiners listen for smoothness, logical connections, vocabulary range/accuracy, grammar range, and intelligibility, and it warns that fluency is about how speech is produced, not whether the story is impressive ([OpenIELTS Part 2 guide](https://www.openielts.org/blog/ielts-speaking-part-2-cue-card)). This aligns with the official descriptors and helps the judge avoid over-scoring dramatic stories with weak grammar or unintelligible pronunciation ([IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf)).
- Learn English Weekly's Part 2 guide says cue-card prompts are a framework rather than a strict checklist, recommends keyword notes rather than full sentences, and says strong candidates use a simple topic-details-importance/feelings-close structure ([Learn English Weekly Part 2 cue cards](https://learnenglishweekly.com/ielts/speaking/part-2-cue-cards)). The judge should treat cue-card bullet coverage as a coherence and topic-development aid, not as a separate task-achievement rubric, because IELTS Speaking does not have a Writing-style Task Response criterion ([IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf)).

## Open-source tools and code

- `pyannote.audio` is an MIT-licensed open-source Python toolkit for speaker diarization with a latest GitHub release visible as 4.0.4 on February 7, 2026; it is relevant for producing reliable examiner/candidate speaker turns before transcript scoring, not for assigning IELTS bands by itself ([pyannote.audio GitHub](https://github.com/pyannote/pyannote-audio)).
- openSMILE is a modular feature extractor for speech/audio/video signal-processing research, with documentation stating that its source and binaries are hosted on GitHub from version 3.0 and that licensing is research-oriented; it can help extract acoustic correlates such as rhythm, energy, and prosodic features for Pronunciation/Fluency research, but it should not replace human-like descriptor judgment ([openSMILE docs](https://audeering.github.io/opensmile/index.html), [openSMILE licensing/about](https://audeering.github.io/opensmile/about.html), [openSMILE GitHub](https://github.com/audeering/opensmile)).
- Praat is a phonetics tool whose GitHub page describes it as software for "doing phonetics by computer" and says most source code is distributed under GPL versions; it is useful for pitch, intensity, formants, stress/rhythm, and pronunciation-feature analysis, while IELTS scoring still depends on intelligibility and communicative effect in the descriptors ([Praat GitHub](https://github.com/praat/praat)).
- These tools are evidence generators, not scoring authorities: official IELTS sources define the score criteria, while diarization/acoustic tools can support consistency checks on turn timing, pause patterns, speech rate, stress/rhythm, and intelligibility proxies ([IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf), [pyannote.audio GitHub](https://github.com/pyannote/pyannote-audio), [openSMILE docs](https://audeering.github.io/opensmile/index.html), [Praat GitHub](https://github.com/praat/praat)).

## Practical signals for an LLM judge

- Anchor all scoring in the four official criteria; do not add separate marks for eye contact, gestures, confidence, accent prestige, factual originality, or cue-card bullet completion unless those behaviours affect Fluency/Coherence, Lexis, Grammar, or Pronunciation in the transcript ([IELTS format](https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking), [IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf)).
- Treat Part 2 duration as a fluency diagnostic: a response below roughly one minute gives weak evidence for Band 6+ willingness to produce long turns, while a 90-120 second response with coherent development gives stronger evidence; still aggregate with Parts 1 and 3 because IELTS examiners assess the whole test ([IELTS format](https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking), [IELTS International Russian Speaking guide](https://www.ielts.international/ru/ielts-preparation-tips/speaking-guide), [DOL IELTS A-Z PDF](https://cbq1uz9dtwobj.vcdn.cloud/PUBLIC/MEDIA/dol_vn_IELTS_A_Z_09ae637386.pdf)).
- Distinguish content hesitation from language-search hesitation: Band 8-style hesitation is usually about ideas, while Band 6-style breakdown often involves repeated repairs, word-searching, and lost coherence ([IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf)).
- Count discourse markers only when they create logical relations; repeated "and then", "so", "moreover", or filler chains without clear relation should not raise Coherence and may resemble descriptor language about inappropriate or over-used cohesive devices ([IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf), [IELTS Fighter coherence-fluency lesson](https://ielts-fighter.com/speaking/Unit-2-Tieu-chi-cham-diem-cua-Ielts-speaking-What-is-coherence-fluency-_mt1453464253.html)).
- Flag memorisation patterns: polished generic Part 2 material followed by weak Part 3 interaction, off-prompt content, unnatural idiom stacking, or identical formulaic openings should reduce confidence in high Lexical/Grammar/Coherence scores unless the candidate adapts accurately to follow-up questions ([Iran Cambridge IELTS Speaking tips](https://www.ircambridge.com/ielts-speaking-tips/), [新通教育 Part 2 得分技巧](https://www.igo.cn/kpnews/220153.shtml), [IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf)).
- Reward paraphrase when it solves a communicative problem: Band 6 generally paraphrases successfully, Band 7 paraphrases effectively, and Band 8 paraphrases readily and flexibly; repetition of the examiner's words without expansion is weaker evidence ([IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf), [British Council Pakistan test-day tips](https://www.britishcouncil.pk/prepare-your-test-day)).
- Evaluate idioms and less-common words by appropriacy and collocation, not rarity: random "difficult words" or memorised idioms should not improve Lexical Resource if they are semantically wrong, stylistically odd, or unconnected to the topic ([British Council India speaking tips](https://www.britishcouncil.in/blog/ielts-speaking-test-preparation-tips), [IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf)).
- Use Part 3 as the main stress test for Band 7+ abstraction: candidates should justify opinions, analyse, discuss, and speculate, not only give short personal anecdotes ([IDP Indonesia Part 3 tips](https://ielts.idp.com/indonesia/prepare/article-ielts-speaking-test-part-3/en-gb), [Rad Time Part 3 guide](https://radtime.org/part-three-speaking/)).
- Do not penalise L1 accent itself; penalise only intelligibility, misplaced stress, rhythm/chunking problems, connected-speech control, or mispronunciations that make meaning harder to recover ([IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf), [小站雅思评分标准](https://ielts.zhan.com/rumen119797.html)).
- Notice translation-lag patterns in candidates from any L1 background: delayed starts, broken clauses, mid-sentence reformulations, and unnatural word-for-word phrasing can indicate fluency, lexical, and grammatical limits, while occasional self-correction can be normal at higher bands ([British Council India speaking tips](https://www.britishcouncil.in/blog/ielts-speaking-test-preparation-tips), [IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf)).
- Treat cue-card structures such as PPF, AAAE, IDEA, and topic-details-feelings-close as scaffolds; credit them only when they produce coherent, responsive, grammatically controlled speech ([British Council PPF strategy PDF](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/speaking_part_2_-_ppf_strategy.pdf), [IELTSSpeakingLab Part 2 strategies](https://www.ieltsspeakinglab.com/blog/ielts-speaking-part-2-strategies), [Learn English Weekly Part 2 cue cards](https://learnenglishweekly.com/ielts/speaking/part-2-cue-cards)).
- When diarization is available, verify that examiner prompts and candidate turns are separated correctly before judging turn length, interruptions, clarification requests, or overlap; diarization errors can falsely create fluency problems or hide examiner support ([pyannote.audio GitHub](https://github.com/pyannote/pyannote-audio), [IELTS format](https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking)).
