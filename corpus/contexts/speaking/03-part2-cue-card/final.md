# Part 2 (long turn) — cue card format, prep strategies, recent cue card banks

_Merged from codex + claude crawls on 2026-05-19T08:00:00Z. Sources cited inline._

## Summary

IELTS Speaking Part 2 is the "individual long turn": the examiner hands the candidate a printed task card with a topic and three to four sub-points, the candidate gets exactly one minute to prepare with pencil and paper, then must speak for one to two minutes uninterrupted before the examiner stops them and asks one or two short rounding-off questions on the same topic (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking; https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/take-ielts/prepare/free-ielts-english-practice-tests/speaking/part-2). Total Part 2 duration is three to four minutes including preparation. For scoring, Part 2 is the single richest transcript window — long enough to expose sustained Fluency & Coherence, Lexical Resource, Grammatical Range and Pronunciation under load — and is therefore the highest-leverage section for an LLM judge, even though the four criteria are equally weighted (25% each) and averaged across the whole Speaking test (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf; https://ielts.org/take-a-test/your-results/ielts-scoring-in-detail). The IELTS Speaking pool refreshes on a four-month cycle (Jan–Apr, May–Aug, Sep–Dec) with roughly half the cards rotating and the rest retained; non-native test-takers in Asia (China, India/Pakistan, Korea, Vietnam, Iran, Indonesia, Russia) routinely walk in having drilled the current cue-card bank, so judges should expect rehearsed openings on familiar topics and treat the rounding-off question / Part 3 transition as the candour test (https://next.ieltsbro.com/oral_season/; https://www.scribd.com/document/981773823/Makkar-All-Categories-Cue-Cards-Jan-Apr-2026; https://www.shiksha.com/studyabroad/exams/ielts/cue-cards-ecp). Cue-card banks are useful for prep and corpus topic coverage but should not be treated as official disclosed test inventories unless published by IELTS owners (https://ieltsliz.com/ielts-speaking-part-2-topics/).

## Authoritative material

### Official format and task mechanics

- IELTS.org describes Speaking Part 2 as the "individual long turn": the examiner gives a task card with a topic, points to include, and an instruction to explain one aspect of the topic; the candidate has one minute to prepare and receives pencil and paper for notes; the examiner stops the candidate after two minutes and may ask one or two questions on the same topic (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking).
- Part 2 lasts three to four minutes including the preparation time; identical format for Academic and General Training (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking; https://www.ielts.org/how-to-use-ielts-results/four-skills/speaking/format).
- British Council's official practice page confirms the same sequence and shows the canonical cue-card shape: a "Describe..." main prompt, three factual bullets, an "explain why..." bullet, the one-minute prep instruction, and the one-to-two-minute speaking instruction; the public sample is "Describe something you own which is very important to you" with two rounding-off questions about value and replacement (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/take-ielts/prepare/free-ielts-english-practice-tests/speaking/part-2).
- IDP's official speaking-practice article states the topic is handed on a card, the candidate receives paper and pen, the examiner uses a timer, the candidate has exactly one minute for notes, the examiner stops the talk after two minutes, and candidates should aim to keep talking for the full two minutes (https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-free-speaking-practice-questions).
- IELTS research by Elder and Wigglesworth includes the interviewer administration wording for one-minute planning: candidates are told they will have one minute to think, may make notes, receive paper and pencil, and are reminded they have one to two minutes and should not worry about being stopped (https://cdn.ielts.org/Research/investigation-of-effectiveness-and-validity-of-planning-time-in-part-2-of-ielts-speaking-test-elder-et-al-2006.pdf).
- Seedhouse et al. (2011) "Topic development in the IELTS Speaking Test" is one of the few peer-reviewed sources directly on Part 2 dynamics and examines how candidates at different bands manage the long turn (https://ielts.org/cdn/Research/topic-development-in-ielts-speaking-test-seedhouse-et-al-2011.pdf).

### What Part 2 tests and how it is scored

- IELTS.org says Part 2 tests the ability to "speak at length on a given topic, use appropriate language, organise ideas logically, and draw on personal experiences to complete the long turn" (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking).
- The four Speaking criteria — Fluency & Coherence, Lexical Resource, Grammatical Range & Accuracy, Pronunciation — are equally weighted (25% each) and averaged across Parts 1+2+3. Part 2 is not separately scored, but is widely regarded as the highest-leverage section because it is the only sustained sample (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/your-results/ielts-scoring-in-detail; https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf).
- Part 2 is particularly informative for Fluency and Coherence because Band 7 requires candidates to "readily produce long turns" without noticeable effort, Band 6 requires willingness to produce long turns though coherence may be lost, and Band 5 often relies on repetition/self-correction/slow speech to keep going (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).
- For Lexical Resource, Band 7+ expects flexible topic vocabulary, some less common/idiomatic items and effective paraphrase, while Band 5 has limited flexibility and unsuccessful paraphrase attempts; the long turn gives enough speech to see whether vocabulary range is sustained beyond short answers (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).
- For Grammatical Range and Accuracy, a long turn reveals whether the candidate uses a mix of simple and complex structures, past/present/future references, subordination and error control under sustained production (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).
- For Pronunciation, Part 2 should be judged by intelligibility, chunking, rhythm, stress, intonation and connected speech across an extended turn; Band 8 sustains rhythm and flexible stress/intonation across long utterances, while Band 6 has variable phonological control and rhythm may be affected by stress timing or rapid speech rate (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).
- **No "task achievement" criterion in Speaking.** IELTSetc (teacher/examiner-perspective) notes that there is no marking criterion for "answering the question perfectly" in the Speaking Test; the examiner scores how the candidate communicates, not whether the story is true or whether every bullet is hit. Skipping a bullet typically shows up indirectly as thin coherence or a short turn (https://ieltsetc.com/ielts-speaking-part-2-long-turn/).

### Official preparation-time guidance

- IELTS.org's official prep-time guide says: first check the task-card topic and immediately ask the examiner if a word on the card is not understood (waiting wastes planning time); choose the first example you can describe in English; slightly adapt the topic if necessary while making the adaptation clear at the start; prefer real examples (invented stories run out of content); take notes in English, in phrases not full sentences, in intended speaking order, with clear handwriting; add one or two useful phrases if time remains (https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/how-to-use-the-preparation-time-in-ielts-speaking-part-2).
- IDP Canada recommends three preparation steps: understand the topic and keywords; brainstorm at least two key ideas for each task-card point; note relevant vocabulary/expressions because examiners assess range and precision (https://ielts.idp.com/canada/prepare/article-did-you-say-2-minutes-speaking-test).
- IDP's long-turn strategy article recommends drawing a cross or dividing the note paper into four columns for the three bullets plus final explanation, focusing on question words (where/how long/what/why), and continuing to write notes until the examiner asks the candidate to begin (https://ielts.idp.com/turkey/prepare/article-ielts-speaking-test-long-turn-part-2/en-gb/).
- IDP warns against speaking too fast through nerves, recommends a measured pace and breathing between sentences, and suggests hesitation devices ("well, let me think", "another thing I remember…") to keep the long turn moving (https://ielts.idp.com/argentina/prepare/article-ielts-speaking-test-long-turn-part-2/).
- IDP Vietnam lists seven specific stalling techniques when running out of ideas: re-read the card aloud and rephrase; "let me think about that for a minute"; always say "yes" to "is there anything else?"; never produce filler `eh / uh / um`. Examiner cuts off at 2 minutes regardless of completeness (https://ielts.idp.com/vietnam/about/news-and-articles/article-ielts-speaking-test-long-turn-part-2).
- British Council's PPF Strategy lesson plan presents Past-Present-Future as a Part 2 framework to help candidates speak at length and demonstrate more complex grammar structures (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/speaking_part_2_-_ppf_strategy.pdf).
- Cambridge English's classroom activity has students practise a short talk without planning, then with one minute planning, then extended to two minutes; teacher notes explicitly ask students to use prep time for a real personal experience and to write key ideas/vocabulary (https://www.cambridgeenglish.org/images/168248-ielts-speaking-part-2.pdf).

### Recent and historical cue-card banks (overview)

- Official sample cards are sparse and for practice format, not a live question bank; British Council's public sample is "Describe something you own which is very important to you" (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/take-ielts/prepare/free-ielts-english-practice-tests/speaking/part-2).
- IDP's sample-task page provides official-style Part 2 prompts and states task-card points help candidates think of what to say and keep speaking for the full two minutes (https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-free-speaking-practice-questions).
- IELTS Liz (UK-examiner teacher resource) maintains a long-running Part 2 topic list grouped by objects, people, places, events/occasions and activities, warns not to memorise full cue-card answers, and notes that common topics often repeat year to year (https://ieltsliz.com/ielts-speaking-part-2-topics/).
- IELTS Buddy's historical Part 2 collection presents example cards and reminds learners that real topics may differ (https://www.ieltsbuddy.com/ielts-speaking-topics.html).
- Cathoven publishes seasonal cue-card banks: "May–August 2026" (released 2026-03-16, last updated 2026-04-29) and a Jan–Apr 2026 bank with 55 Part 2 topics and Part 3 follow-up discussion questions (https://resources.cathoven.com/ielts-speaking/part-2-cue-cards; https://resources.cathoven.com/ielts-speaking/cue-card-bank).
- IELTS International's 2026 multilingual bank lists 100+ practice Part 2 topics grouped by people/places/objects/events/activities, in English, Korean, Chinese and Russian versions (https://www.ielts.international/ielts-speaking-part-2-cue-cards; https://www.ielts.international/ko/ielts-speaking-part-2-cue-cards; https://www.ielts.international/zh/ielts-speaking-part-2-cue-cards; https://www.ielts.international/ru/ielts-speaking-part-2-cue-cards).
- IELTSKaro's 2026 page frames Part 2 as the most controllable section because the format is predictable and provides sample one-minute notes alongside cue-card topics and model answers (https://ieltskaro.com/ielts-speaking-part-2-cue-cards-topics/).
- AllThingsIELTS publishes 2026 cue-card topics with model answers and an introduction-main-points-conclusion structure (https://allthingsielts.com/ielts-speaking/part-2-cue-cards.php; https://allthingsielts.com/ielts-speaking/cue-cards-2026).
- Historical Makkar-style cue-card PDFs (e.g. May–Aug 2020) show how prep markets circulate seasonal cue-card lists by window; useful for historical topic taxonomy, not official disclosure (https://magicaloverseas.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/06/Makkar-IELTS-Speaking-May-to-Aug-2020.pdf).
- **Cycle mechanics.** Pools refresh ~Jan, May, Sept; roughly ~50% of cards turn over each cycle, with the rest "retained" from the prior cycle. Recognising a card-stem the candidate has clearly memorised is itself a signal — overly polished openings on retained-bank topics, paired with brittleness on the rounding-off question, often indicate rehearsal rather than competence (https://www.shiksha.com/studyabroad/exams/ielts/cue-cards-ecp; https://www.kanan.co/ielts/speaking/cue-card/).

### Concrete topic content (recent cycles)

- **May–Aug 2026 cycle (current at crawl time).** High-frequency reported cards: *Describe a picture/photograph of you that you like; Describe something you did with a group of people; Describe a famous person who is a role model for young people; Describe a subject you would like to learn in the future; Describe a natural place (park, mountain, garden)* (https://leapscholar.com/blog/ielts-speaking-cue-cards-topics-sample-answers/; https://www.ieltsworldly.com/makkar-ielts-speaking-pdf-2024-may-to-august-cue-cards/).
- **Jan–Apr 2026 cycle (just rolled off).** *Describe a person who inspires you / a famous person in your country / someone who often helps others / a sportsperson you admire / an intelligent person you know; Describe a park or garden / an area in your home where you feel relaxed / a beautiful city / your hometown; Describe an invention useful in daily life / an object you think is beautiful / a useful object in your home / your first mobile phone; Describe a party you enjoyed / a traditional festival / a rule important at school or work / an important decision; Describe a long car journey / a difficult thing you did and succeeded / a time you were very busy / a long walk / a speech you gave* (https://www.gradding.com/blog/ielts/speaking-cue-cards; https://www.ieltsbykamal.com/p/ielts-speaking-cue-cards-january-to.html?m=1).
- **Sep–Dec 2025 cycle (commonly carried over).** *Describe an interesting traditional story; a successful sportsperson you admire; a talk you gave to a group; a good habit your friend has that you want to develop; something interesting on social media; a time you told your friend an important truth; the first time you spoke a foreign language; a useful book you read; a time someone apologized to you; an occasion when you lost your way; an outdoor activity at a place you visited; a singer whose music you like* (https://ieltsfever.org/september-2025-to-december-2025-cue-cards-with-answers-updating-weekly/; https://englishwithroop.com/september-to-december-2025-ielts-speaking-cue-cards/).

## Country-specific prep material

### Korea (한국)

- **TestGlider B-E-F-L 구조** (Background → Experience → Feeling → Lesson/Link). Claims this four-block framework naturally produces 1m 50s – 2m 10s of speech, with per-block budgets: B 20–30s, E 30–40s, F 20–30s, L 20–30s. Prep-minute timing: 0–15s underline the four bullets and choose the topic, 15–40s write keywords only under each B/E/F/L slot, 40–60s mentally rehearse the opening sentence. Warns that very short answers reduce evidence for Fluency, Lexical Resource and Grammatical Range (https://blog.testglider.com/%EC%95%84%EC%9D%B4%EC%97%98%EC%B8%A0-speaking-part2-%ED%81%90%EC%B9%B4%EB%93%9C-b-e-f-l-%EA%B5%AC%EC%A1%B0/).
- **Skybel cram school** pushes a `when → where → who → what → why → feeling` flow with single-word notes per slot (*Topic: a trip / When: last summer / Where: Jeju / Who: friend / Why: relax / Feeling: happy*). Explicit warning that writing full sentences during the prep minute triggers a memorisation reflex that "kills naturalness and speed" once speaking begins (https://blog.skybel.co.kr/blog/%EC%95%84%EC%9D%B4%EC%97%98%EC%B8%A0%20%EC%8A%A4%ED%94%BC%ED%82%B9/%EC%95%84%EC%9D%B4%EC%97%98%EC%B8%A0-%EC%8A%A4%ED%94%BC%ED%82%B9-Part-2-1%EB%B6%84%EC%9D%98-%EA%B8%B0%EC%A0%81-%EB%81%8A%EA%B8%B0%EC%A7%80-%EC%95%8A%EB%8A%94-%EB%8B%B5%EB%B3%80%EC%9D%84-%EB%A7%8C%EB%93%9C%EB%8A%94-%ED%95%B5%EC%8B%AC-%EC%A0%84%EB%9E%B5).
- **IELTS International (KR)** Korean-language version of the 2026 cue-card bank: summarises Part 2 as a card with topic and four prompts, one minute prep with pencil/paper, one to two minutes uninterrupted speech, with one or two follow-up questions; advises recording practice and aiming for at least 1:30 per card (https://www.ielts.international/ko/ielts-speaking-part-2-cue-cards).
- **IDP Korea** Korean-language brochure and prep page describe Part 2 as a card with the topic and key points, memo paper and pencil, one minute to prepare, two-minute long-turn task assessed by trained examiners on the four criteria, with Academic/GT differentiation (https://assets.ctfassets.net/unrdeg6se4ke/1F86pv4cnHXZJK53b7yoTx/977a389df8c95f4cc72e5aaced3355d5/IDP_IELTS______________190902___________.pdf; https://ieltskorea.org/korea/prepare/speaking).

### China (中国) / Chinese-language prep and banks

- **IELTS Bro / 雅思哥** is the canonical mainland Chinese cue-card bank, updated every 4 months at `next.ieltsbro.com/oral_season/`. Topics are classified as 必考 (must-prep), 新题 (new this cycle) and 保留 (retained). Mainland prep priority taught on Zhihu: prioritise 人物 / 物品 / 经历 (people / objects / experiences) as the three highest-frequency categories (https://next.ieltsbro.com/oral_season/; https://www.ieltsbro.com/question-bank/; https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/1996750320500496235; https://www.sohu.com/a/968538819_121124350).
- **Gaodun** describes Part 2 success as "clear structure + rich details", advises covering all prompt points with concrete examples, and recommends daily recording practice of one Part 2 topic to check length and content (https://www.gaodun.com/ielts/1629728.html).
- **Lyle IELTS** focuses on the one-minute prep window: identify the topic type, write one or two words per point (time/place/person/feeling/turning point), and use the minute to prevent blanking during the two-minute monologue (https://www.lyle100.com/sys-nd/19924.html).
- **New Channel** says Part 2 is normally three to four minutes including one minute prep and roughly 1:30–2:00 of speech; recommends repeated recording: first for content, second under strict timing, third in exam-like conditions (https://m.xhd.cn/news/05002728.html).
- **Seasonal Chinese-market banks.** Shanghai New Channel's May–August 2026 page reports a seasonal Speaking bank and distinguishes retained Part 2/3 topics; New Oriental's 2026 May–August article advertises Part 2 topics with answers, illustrating the Jan/May/Sept refresh cycle used in Chinese prep discourse (https://sh.xhd.cn/ielts/ysrrbwz/966602.html; https://mtoutiao.xdf.cn/ielts/202605/15188096.html).
- **CSDN** 2026年1–3月 topic list with prep tactics: if a word on the card is unknown, ask the examiner immediately at the start of the prep minute, not after; don't write sentences, write a keyword chain you can walk down while speaking (https://blog.csdn.net/weixin_44638453/article/details/157620670).

### Vietnam

- **ZIM** Vietnamese article on cue-card use says Part 2 is about three minutes overall, includes a cue card with "You should say" prompts, one minute prep, up to two minutes to present; cue-card definition article notes 3–4 main prompts beginning with 5W1H words, pencil for outlining, write keywords, answer the main question directly, and don't memorise content. Pushes "detail explosion" — every bullet elaborated with at least one sensory detail or specific year/place/name (https://zim.vn/3-quy-tac-toi-uu-hoa-phieu-cau-hoi-va-1-phut-chuan-bi-trong-bai-thi-ielts-speaking-part-2; https://zim.vn/cue-card-la-gi; https://zim.vn/ielts-speaking-part-2).
- **DOL English** type-by-type framework using a *what / when / who / how / feeling / conclusion* expansion (https://www.dolenglish.vn/blog/cach-tra-loi-ielts-speaking-part-2).
- **VUS** Vietnamese model answers and strategies; common cross-VN advice: speak the full 2 minutes; stopping early auto-caps Fluency near Band 6 (https://vus.edu.vn/blog-vus/ielts-speaking-part-2).
- **IELTS Fighter** lists Part 2 format (monologue, topic card, 1 min prep, 1–2 min speech) and gives topic-specific structures for "Describe a person" cards; "common mistakes" unit warns candidates often focus on "what to say" while neglecting "how to say" under the scoring criteria; one-minute-prep unit argues that simple notes like "old building/library/books" produce a weak generic answer and proposes more deliberate selection (https://ielts-fighter.com/speaking/ielts-speaking-topic-people_mt1629951974.html; https://ielts-fighter.com/speaking/Unit-7-Loi-hay-gap-khi-luyen-IELTS-Speaking-Part-2_mt1460540845.html; https://ielts-fighter.com/speaking/Unit-8-Nhung-viec-can-phai-lam-trong-1-phut-chuan-bi-o-IELTS-Speaking-Part-2_mt1460541053.html).
- **IDP Vietnam** strategy page reinforces address-each-bullet-in-order with linking words (and/so/but/or) and the seven stalling techniques above (https://www.idp.com/vietnam/blog/ielts-speaking-part-2-strategy/).

### Iran (فارسی)

- **IELTS2** Persian Part 2 phrases guide frames pre-learned functional phrases as support for starting and organising the talk rather than full memorised answers; Part 2 topics page is a Persian-market topic repository (https://ielts2.com/phrases-for-ielts-speaking-part-2/; https://ielts2.com/ielts-speaking-part-2-topics-questions/; https://ielts2.com/ielts-speaking-test-information/).
- **Syntus** describes Part 2 as about three to four minutes with one minute prep, one to two minutes of speech, and short follow-ups; warns that failing to use prep time and not writing keywords hurts coherence (https://syntus.org/ielts-speaking-timing/).
- **LMAP, Deed Language, IELTS Teachers Iran** emphasise a mind-map approach in the 1-minute prep: write the topic in the centre, branch into *who / where / when / why*. Single most-repeated rule: "بهترین روش...تنها کلمات کلیدی...یادداشت کنید" (best method = keywords only; writing sentences takes more than a minute). For Band 7+, recommend memorising 4–5 "advanced anchor phrases" per topic family (e.g. for a *place* card: *off the beaten track, hidden gem, stumble upon*) (https://lmapgroup.com/blog/Cue_Card_%D8%AF%D8%B1_%D8%A2%D8%B2%D9%85%D9%88%D9%86_%D8%A2%DB%8C%D9%84%D8%AA%D8%B3; https://deedlanguage.ir/article-page/%D9%86%DA%A9%D8%A7%D8%AA-%D8%A2%DB%8C%D9%84%D8%AA%D8%B3-%7C-%D9%86%DA%A9%D8%A7%D8%AA-%D9%88-%D8%AA%DA%A9%D9%86%DB%8C%DA%A9-%D9%87%D8%A7%DB%8C-%D9%BE%D8%A7%D8%B1%D8%AA-%D8%AF%D9%88%D9%85-%D8%A7%D8%B3%D9%BE%DB%8C%DA%A9%DB%8C%D9%86%DA%AF-%D8%A2%DB%8C%D9%84%D8%AA%D8%B3; https://ieltsteachers.org/%D9%BE%D8%A7%D8%B1%D8%AA-%D8%AF%D9%88%D9%85-%D8%A2%D8%B2%D9%85%D9%88%D9%86-%D8%A7%D8%B3%D9%BE%DB%8C%DA%A9%DB%8C%D9%86%DA%AF/).

## Practical signals for an LLM judge

Numeric thresholds are pragmatic heuristics derived from cram-school + examiner sources, not the official descriptors (which are qualitative). Treat as priors, not hard cutoffs.

1. **Segment detection.** Identify Part 2 by examiner script patterns: card/topic handover, one-minute preparation, "start speaking now", a candidate monologue, then "thank you" and one or two short rounding-off questions before Part 3 begins (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/take-ielts/prepare/free-ielts-english-practice-tests/speaking/part-2; https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-free-speaking-practice-questions).

2. **Duration as a hard prior on Fluency & Coherence.** <60s of candidate speech is strong negative evidence (≈ Band ≤5); 60–90s is acceptable but often thin (≈ Band 6 with prompting); sustained 90–120s with no examiner re-prompt is a Band 7+ precondition. The diagnostic Band 4.5 example in published guides is the candidate stopping at ~30–40s and the examiner saying "anything else?" Do **not** penalise the candidate merely for being stopped at two minutes — being stopped at the limit is normal and is positive evidence of sustained production (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking; https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-free-speaking-practice-questions; https://ielts.idp.com/indonesia/prepare/ielts-academic-preparation/ielts-academic-speaking/en-gb; https://learnenglishweekly.com/ielts/speaking/part-2-cue-cards). *(Note: the 90s "long-turn" target is a coaching heuristic from prep sources, not an official band cutoff.)*

3. **Bullet coverage as a coherence proxy, not a checklist.** A response that skips 2+ bullets typically reads as poorly organised even when fluent. Count bullet hits and weight Coherence accordingly; do *not* directly deduct for "missing content" — Speaking has no "task achievement" criterion (https://ieltsetc.com/ielts-speaking-part-2-long-turn/; https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).

4. **The rounding-off question is the candour test.** Compare fluency between the main turn and the 1–2 short follow-ups: a large drop suggests the long turn was rehearsed, and the speaking score should anchor closer to the round-off / Part 3 performance. Overly polished openings on retained-bank topics, paired with brittleness on the follow-up, indicate memorisation (https://www.shiksha.com/studyabroad/exams/ielts/cue-cards-ecp; https://ieltsetc.com/ielts-speaking-part-2-long-turn/).

5. **Rehearsed-opening detection.** Sentences like "The person I am going to talk about today is..." or any opener exactly mirroring the cue-card stem in formal register suggest memorisation. The Fluency signal is the *delta* — a drop in fluency or grammatical accuracy in the second half of the turn as the script runs out. High-band openers are short and specific ("Today I'd like to tell you about my grandfather") rather than over-formal templates (https://ieltsliz.com/ielts-speaking-part-2-topics/; https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/how-to-use-the-preparation-time-in-ielts-speaking-part-2; https://www.ieltsadvantage.com/ielts-speaking-part-2/).

6. **Tense control across the talk.** Person/place/event cards typically need past-tense narrative + present-tense reflection ("…and to this day I still…") + a hypothetical/future closing ("…I'd love to do it again"). A turn locked in a single tense throughout caps Grammatical Range below Band 7. Matches both British Council's PPF strategy and Keith Speaking Academy's "match the bullet's tense" rule (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/speaking_part_2_-_ppf_strategy.pdf; https://keithspeakingacademy.com/ielts-speaking-part2-cue-card-topics-tips/; https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).

7. **Repetition / running out of content.** Penalise repetition and self-correction loops more than ordinary brief pauses: Band 6 keeps going but may lose coherence through hesitation/repetition; Band 5 relies on repetition/self-correction/slow speech to continue. A single fluent self-correction ("…I went to… I mean, I had been to…") is band-positive; repeated mid-clause restarts (>3 per minute) drop Fluency (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).

8. **Discourse markers per minute (heuristic).** Band 7+ candidates typically deploy ≥4 cohesive devices per 90s (e.g. *what's more, on top of that, having said that, I suppose, the thing is*); sub-2 per 90s with over-reliance on `and / so / but` is a Band 6 signal (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf; https://www.ieltspodcast.com/ielts-speaking/making-notes-on-cue-cards/).

9. **Idiomatic chunks per turn (heuristic).** Band 7+ candidates typically produce ≥2 idiomatic expressions per 2-minute turn (*a hidden gem, off the beaten track, broaden my horizons, out of the blue*). Zero idioms + fully literal phrasing usually maps to Band 6 Lexical Resource. *(Heuristic; not an official cutoff.)* (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).

10. **Stalling phrases ≠ filler sounds.** "Let me think about that for a moment" is band-neutral or band-positive; sequences of `um / er / uh` (3+ in a 30-second window) are a clear Fluency penalty (https://ielts.idp.com/vietnam/about/news-and-articles/article-ielts-speaking-test-long-turn-part-2; https://ieltsetc.com/ielts-speaking-part-2-long-turn/).

11. **Topic-family vocabulary priors.** Calibrate Lexical Resource by topic family: a "describe a place" card should surface place-specific lexis (*bustling, scenic, off the beaten track*); a "describe a person" card character lexis (*down-to-earth, charismatic, role model*); objects → function/value; events → sequence/emotion; skills → ability/process. Generic adjectives (`nice, good, big`) across *any* topic family ≈ Lexical Resource ≤ 6 (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf; https://www.ieltsbuddy.com/ielts-speaking-topics.html).

12. **Speech rate.** Native-norm range cited by examiner blogs is ~130–160 wpm; >180 wpm typically loses pronunciation marks (slurred word boundaries); <90 wpm typically loses fluency marks (excessive pausing).

13. **Pronunciation across the long turn.** Use the sustained monologue to judge phrase chunking, rhythm, stress and intonation. Do not penalise accent identity if intelligibility is maintained — IDP Indonesia explicitly says accent is only a problem when it prevents understanding, and the official descriptors focus on intelligibility and phonological control (https://ielts.idp.com/indonesia/prepare/ielts-academic-preparation/ielts-academic-speaking/en-gb; https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).

14. **"Notes read aloud" signal.** Robotic delivery that mirrors note bullets exactly ("when… last summer… where… Jeju… who… my friend…") signals note-dependence; Fluency rarely scores above 6. Unnaturally essay-like phrasing, abrupt topic mismatch, or a polished block followed by weak follow-up answers are related signals (https://blog.skybel.co.kr/...; https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/how-to-use-the-preparation-time-in-ielts-speaking-part-2).

15. **Reward specific personal detail.** Concrete sensory description, specific names/dates/places, emotions and reflection are positive Fluency/Coherence and Lexical Resource evidence — IELTS.org recommends real examples, IDP Canada recommends brainstorming key ideas per bullet, and Indonesian/Vietnamese cram schools push "detail explosion" (https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/how-to-use-the-preparation-time-in-ielts-speaking-part-2; https://ielts.idp.com/canada/prepare/article-did-you-say-2-minutes-speaking-test; https://elevau.id/tips-menjawab-ielts-speaking/; https://zim.vn/ielts-speaking-part-2).

16. **"Talk around it" / hypothetical substitution is fine.** If the candidate openly admits "I've never actually done X, but I imagine…" and proceeds to a coherent hypothetical narrative, do *not* penalise — Keith Speaking Academy and IDP endorse this; IELTS.org recommends slight topic adaptation made clear at the start. Penalise only if the substitution is off-topic (https://keithspeakingacademy.com/ielts-speaking-part2-cue-card-topics-tips/; https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/how-to-use-the-preparation-time-in-ielts-speaking-part-2).

17. **No penalty for clarifying a card word.** If the candidate asks about a word on the cue card at the start of preparation, that is officially recommended and not a score penalty (https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/how-to-use-the-preparation-time-in-ielts-speaking-part-2; https://blog.csdn.net/weixin_44638453/article/details/157620670).

18. **Don't reward a formal closing.** "So that is the person I admire most" is not required and often signals a memorised template. Lack of an explicit conclusion is not incoherence; examiners cut at 2 min regardless.

19. **Use bank topics for coverage, not as official answers.** Recent cue-card banks are useful only to ensure evaluation data covers common topic families and seasonal distributions across people/places/objects/events/activities/abstract follow-ups — not as official expected answers (https://ieltsliz.com/ielts-speaking-part-2-topics/; https://resources.cathoven.com/ielts-speaking/part-2-cue-cards; https://www.ielts.international/ielts-speaking-part-2-cue-cards).
