# IELTS Writing Task 2 — Question Types Overview

IELTS Writing Task 2 prompts fall into a small number of distinct question types. Misidentifying the question type is one of the most common reasons a candidate is downgraded on Task Response — even strong English produces low TR scores when the structure does not match the question. This file lists the six core question types with one canonical example each and a discussion of how the structure differs.

A high-level rule of thumb:

> The question's wording dictates how many "things" the essay must contain. **Opinion** = 1 stance + 2 reasons. **Discuss both views + opinion** = 2 views + 1 stance. **Two-part** = 2 separate questions, each needing a body paragraph. **Adv/disadv** = 2 sides; if asked "outweigh", a stance is also required.

---

## 1. Opinion Essay ("Agree/Disagree", "To what extent…")

### Example Prompt
> *Some people believe that all university students should be required to study a foreign language. To what extent do you agree or disagree?*

Other phrasings: "Do you agree or disagree?" / "To what extent do you agree or disagree?" / "Is this a positive or negative development?" (note: this is an opinion in disguise — not adv/disadv).

### What the Question Wants
A clear, sustained position. The position can be (a) total agreement, (b) total disagreement, or (c) partial agreement — all are valid as long as the position is stated up front, supported throughout, and reaffirmed in the conclusion.

### Recommended Structure
- **Intro:** Paraphrase the prompt + state your position clearly.
- **Body 1:** Reason 1 supporting your position + example.
- **Body 2:** Reason 2 supporting your position + example.
  *(Optional: counter-paragraph acknowledging the other side, then refuting it.)*
- **Conclusion:** Restate position + summarise the two reasons.

### Pitfalls
- Sitting on the fence ("there are two sides…") without committing to a position — capped at TR 6 or below.
- Giving equal weight to both sides — that is *discuss both views*, not opinion.
- Forgetting to state the position in the introduction; examiners look for "presents a clear position throughout the response" at Band 7+.

## 2. Discuss Both Views and Give Your Own Opinion

### Example Prompt
> *Some people think that universities should accept equal numbers of male and female students in every subject. Others believe that universities should accept students based on their interest and skills, regardless of gender. Discuss both views and give your own opinion.*

### What the Question Wants
**Three** components: view A, view B, and the writer's opinion. Most Band 7+ essays develop view A and view B in their own paragraph, then give the opinion either as a separate paragraph or integrated explicitly into the conclusion.

### Recommended Structure
- **Intro:** Paraphrase the prompt + state your opinion (which view you lean toward, or that you accept both).
- **Body 1:** View A — reasoning + example.
- **Body 2:** View B — reasoning + example.
- **Body 3 (or extended conclusion):** Your opinion explained, with at least one reason. (Don't just repeat view A or B.)
- **Conclusion:** Restate opinion + brief synthesis.

### Pitfalls
- Failing to give the writer's own opinion (some candidates only "discuss both views"). This is the single most common reason DBV essays score TR 5 or 6.
- Giving the opinion only in the conclusion as a single sentence — examiners want it stated up front and supported.
- Treating the opinion as a tepid "balance both" without committing — this can work at high bands if the synthesis is genuine, but lower-band candidates default to it because they have nothing original to say.

## 3. Advantages and Disadvantages

There are **two sub-types** that look similar but require different structures:

### Sub-type 3a: Pure Adv/Disadv
> *What are the advantages and disadvantages of working for a large company?*

Wants both sides, no opinion required.

### Sub-type 3b: Adv/Disadv + "Outweigh" (Opinion)
> *Young people are leaving rural areas to work or study in cities. Do the advantages of this development outweigh the drawbacks?*

Wants both sides **and** a stance on which is bigger.

### Recommended Structure (Sub-type 3a)
- **Intro:** Paraphrase + brief preview of one adv and one disadv.
- **Body 1:** Advantages (1–2 points + examples).
- **Body 2:** Disadvantages (1–2 points + examples).
- **Conclusion:** Summarise both sides.

### Recommended Structure (Sub-type 3b)
- Same as 3a but the **introduction must state which side outweighs**, and the **conclusion must restate the stance**. Many candidates lose TR 1–2 bands here by omitting the verdict.

### Pitfalls
- Treating "advantages outweigh disadvantages" as adv/disadv without taking a stance — TR drops to 5–6.
- Listing more advantages than disadvantages without explaining the asymmetry — examiners want a *reason* for the verdict.

## 4. Problem and Solution (Cause and Solution)

### Example Prompt
> *Many people in cities are becoming increasingly stressed. What are the causes of this problem and what solutions can be suggested?*

Other phrasings: "What are the reasons and what can be done?" / "Why is this happening and how can it be addressed?"

### What the Question Wants
**Two** halves: a "why" half and a "what to do" half. Both halves must be developed equally.

### Recommended Structure
- **Intro:** Paraphrase + preview ("This essay will examine the causes … and propose solutions").
- **Body 1:** Two causes, each with an example.
- **Body 2:** Two solutions, each linked to a cause and with an example. (Best practice: solution 1 addresses cause 1, solution 2 addresses cause 2.)
- **Conclusion:** Summarise causes and solutions.

### Pitfalls
- Listing four problems but only one solution (or vice versa) — TR drops to 6.
- Solutions that are vague ("the government should do something") with no mechanism. Specific, named solutions ("levy a congestion charge as in London") score better.
- Confusing this with adv/disadv — "problems" are negatives that need fixing, not "disadvantages".

## 5. Two-Part Question (Direct Question / Multi-part)

### Example Prompt
> *Many young people leave home to find work in cities. What are the reasons for this? Do you think this is a positive or negative development?*

The two parts can be (cause + opinion), (effect + solution), (reason + evaluation), etc.

### What the Question Wants
**Each direct question must have its own body paragraph.** This is the question type most often confused with "discuss both views" — there is no "view A" and "view B" to discuss; there are two distinct sub-questions.

### Recommended Structure
- **Intro:** Paraphrase + brief answer to *both* sub-questions.
- **Body 1:** Answer to question 1, fully developed.
- **Body 2:** Answer to question 2, fully developed.
- **Conclusion:** Summarise both answers.

### Pitfalls
- Answering only one of the two questions — TR capped at 5.
- Skewing the essay 80/20 toward one of the two — TR around 6.
- Treating "Is this a positive or negative development?" as adv/disadv — it is an opinion question.

---

## 6. Positive or Negative Development / Trend Evaluation

### Example Prompt
> *In many countries today, more and more people choose to live alone rather than with family or friends. Is this a positive or negative development?*

This is a sub-type of the opinion essay specifically asking the writer to evaluate a trend. Some prompts add a second part: "What are the reasons?" or "How does it affect society?" — those become two-part questions.

### What the Question Wants
A single, clear evaluation of the development. Either side (positive, negative, or balanced) is acceptable if argued well.

### Recommended Structure
- **Intro:** Acknowledge the trend + state your evaluation.
- **Body 1:** Reason 1 supporting your evaluation + concrete example.
- **Body 2:** Reason 2 supporting your evaluation + concrete example.
  *(Optional: a short concession recognising the opposite side.)*
- **Conclusion:** Restate the evaluation.

### Pitfalls
- Listing both positives and negatives without coming to a verdict — that's adv/disadv masquerading as positive/negative.
- Conflating "positive/negative" with "advantages/disadvantages" — the evaluator-question wants a stance.
- "The development has positive and negative aspects" as a thesis — too weak; you need a clear verdict (which side is *more* significant, and why).

## Side-by-Side Comparison

| # | Type | "Things" Required | Opinion Needed? | Typical Body Structure |
|---|------|-------------------|-----------------|------------------------|
| 1 | Opinion (Agree/Disagree, ToWE) | 1 stance + 2 reasons | Yes | 2 reasons supporting one stance |
| 2 | Discuss both views + opinion | 2 views + 1 opinion | Yes (often a 3rd para) | View A / View B / Your opinion |
| 3a | Pure Adv/Disadv | Adv + Disadv | No | Adv para / Disadv para |
| 3b | Adv/Disadv "outweigh" | Adv + Disadv + stance | Yes | Adv para / Disadv para + verdict |
| 4 | Problem/Solution | Causes + Solutions | No | Causes para / Solutions para |
| 5 | Two-part question | Answer to Q1 + Answer to Q2 | Sometimes | Q1 para / Q2 para |
| 6 | Positive/Negative development | 1 evaluation + 2 reasons | Yes | 2 reasons supporting evaluation |

---

## Identifying the Question Type — A 3-Step Check

1. **Read the final sentence.** This is almost always the instruction. Look for: "Do you agree or disagree?" / "Discuss both views and give your opinion." / "What are the advantages…?" / "What are the causes and solutions?" / "Is this positive or negative?".
2. **Count the question marks or imperatives.** Two questions = two-part question type. One question with "discuss both" = DBV. One question with "to what extent" = opinion.
3. **Check for "outweigh".** Adv/disadv prompts that include "do the advantages outweigh the disadvantages" need a stance, not just a list.

---

## Why Question Type Matters for the Band Score

Examiners assess Task Response on:
- **Address of the prompt** (have all parts been answered?).
- **Position presented** (where required).
- **Main ideas extended and supported**.

Mis-identifying the question type leads to:
- *Missing a required part* — drops TR to 5 even with otherwise excellent writing.
- *Adding a stance where none is asked* — wastes word count, can muddle TR.
- *Mismatched structure* (e.g., one big paragraph for a two-part prompt) — drops CC by one band as well.

A well-structured response to the *correct* question type, even with modest English, usually scores TR 6.5 or higher. A virtuosic response to the *wrong* question type can be capped at TR 5 regardless of language.
