# Paragraph Structure for Band 7

> A Band 7 essay is, in effect, four well-built paragraphs (intro, two bodies, conclusion) — sometimes five. Most Band 6 problems collapse if every paragraph is engineered to a clear pattern. This file gives those patterns explicitly.

---

## 1. The whole-essay skeleton

| Paragraph | Word count | Function |
|---|---|---|
| Introduction | 40–60 | Paraphrase prompt + thesis |
| Body 1 | 80–110 | First main idea, fully developed |
| Body 2 | 80–110 | Second main idea, fully developed |
| (Optional Body 3) | 60–80 | Third idea or counter-argument |
| Conclusion | 40–60 | Synthesis + restated position |
| **Total** | **270–290** | |

Below 250 words → automatic Task Response penalty.
Above 320 words → diminishing returns, usually weaker grammar from rushing.

---

## 2. Introduction structure

### Two-part introduction (recommended)

| Sentence | Function |
|---|---|
| 1 — Background / paraphrase | Re-state the prompt topic without copying |
| 2 — Thesis | Your position + brief signpost of reasoning |

### Three-part introduction (also acceptable)

| Sentence | Function |
|---|---|
| 1 — Background | General context |
| 2 — Paraphrase | Specific re-statement of prompt |
| 3 — Thesis | Position + signpost |

### Worked example

**Prompt:** *Some people argue that schools should focus more on creative subjects, while others believe academic subjects are more important. Discuss both views and give your opinion.*

**Band 7 introduction:**
> *Educators and policymakers continue to disagree over the relative weight that should be given to creative versus academic subjects in schools.* (paraphrase) *While each camp has reasonable grounds, I would argue that a balanced curriculum is preferable, since the two domains develop complementary rather than competing skills.* (thesis)

### Common Band 6 introduction errors

- Copying the prompt word-for-word
- Missing thesis: "This essay will discuss both views" (no position)
- Adding a clichéd opener: *"In today's modern era of globalisation…"*
- Stating an opinion without context
- Going beyond 4 sentences

## 3. Body paragraph structure (PEEL)

### The four moves

| Move | Function | Sentences |
|---|---|---|
| **P** Point | Topic sentence — the paragraph's claim | 1 |
| **E** Explain | Why is the claim true? Mechanism, reasoning | 1–2 |
| **E** Example | Concrete illustration | 1–2 |
| **L** Link | Tie back to question / position; transition | 1 |

### A worked Band 7 PEEL paragraph

**Topic:** Why creative subjects matter

> *Creative subjects play a far more significant role in cognitive development than is often acknowledged.* (P)
> *Disciplines such as music, drama, and visual art demand abstract thinking, sustained problem-solving, and an ability to handle ambiguity — skills that, while harder to measure on a standardised test, are increasingly valued in the modern workplace.* (E — explanation)
> *A 2020 OECD report on twenty-first-century skills, for example, listed creativity and critical thinking among the top five competencies employers cited as scarce.* (E — example)
> *Cutting these subjects, then, risks producing graduates who are technically competent but ill-equipped for the kinds of open-ended problems they will actually face at work.* (L)

Notice: 4 sentences, ~95 words, every sentence advances the argument.

### Topic sentences — strong vs weak

| Weak (Band 6) | Strong (Band 7) |
|---|---|
| *There are many reasons.* | *The most pressing reason is the long-term economic cost.* |
| *Firstly, technology is good.* | *Modern technology has, above all, transformed the way we communicate.* |
| *In addition, education is important.* | *A second, less obvious benefit lies in the social cohesion that universal education fosters.* |
| *I will discuss the disadvantages.* | *Despite these advantages, the approach carries one substantial drawback.* |

### Length per body paragraph

- **Minimum**: 4 substantive sentences (~70 words)
- **Target**: 5–7 sentences (~80–110 words)
- **Maximum**: 8 sentences (~120 words) — beyond this, focus weakens

## 4. Variants of PEEL for different prompt types

### 4.1 PEEL for an opinion essay (one-sided)

> P — main reason → E — why → E — concrete example → L — tie to position

### 4.2 PEEL for a discussion essay (one view)

> P — view stated objectively → E — proponents' reasoning → E — example or scenario → L — your assessment of this view

This is important: in a "discuss both views" essay, you should explain each view fairly even if you disagree with it. Personal commentary belongs in the **L** sentence.

### 4.3 PEEL for advantages / disadvantages

> P — name the advantage / disadvantage → E — mechanism → E — example → L — significance

### 4.4 PEEL for problem / solution

> P — problem stated → E — why it matters / who it affects → E — concrete instance → L — bridges to the solution paragraph

> P — proposed solution → E — how it works → E — example or precedent → L — expected impact

### 4.5 PEEL for a two-part question

> Each body paragraph answers one of the two sub-questions, fully PEEL'd.

## 5. Conclusion structure

### Two-sentence Band 7 conclusion

| Sentence | Function |
|---|---|
| 1 — Synthesis | Combine the two body arguments |
| 2 — Position re-statement (new wording) | Restate stance + optional implication |

### Three-sentence Band 7 conclusion

| Sentence | Function |
|---|---|
| 1 — Signal + synthesis | "On balance, …" |
| 2 — Position | Restate clearly |
| 3 — Implication / recommendation | "Policymakers should therefore…" |

### Worked Band 7 conclusion

> *On balance, both creative and academic disciplines clearly contribute to a well-rounded education, but each does so in distinct and irreplaceable ways.* (synthesis)
> *Rather than privileging one over the other, schools should aim for a curriculum in which the two are deliberately interwoven, so that students leave equipped with both depth of knowledge and flexibility of thought.* (position + implication)

### Conclusion don'ts

- Don't introduce a new argument
- Don't add a new example
- Don't simply paste the introduction with synonyms
- Don't change your position

## 6. Sentence-level templates by paragraph slot

### Topic-sentence templates

- *The most compelling argument for [X] is that [Y].*
- *A central reason why [X] is that [Y].*
- *One frequently overlooked aspect of [X] is that [Y].*
- *Perhaps the strongest case against [X] is [Y].*
- *The principal advantage of [X] lies in [Y].*

### Explanation templates

- *This is largely because [Y].*
- *The mechanism behind this is straightforward: [Y].*
- *In other words, [Y].*
- *What this means in practice is that [Y].*

### Example templates

- *A clear illustration of this is [example].*
- *Take, for instance, [example].*
- *In [country], for example, [example].*
- *Recent evidence from [source] supports this: [example].*

### Link / transition templates

- *For these reasons, [restate claim].*
- *This, in turn, suggests that [Y].*
- *Such considerations make a strong case for [Y].*
- *While these factors are significant, an arguably more fundamental issue is [next paragraph topic].*

## 7. Visualising the whole thing — full Band 7 essay map

**Prompt:** *Some believe governments should provide free university education, while others argue students should pay. Discuss both views and give your opinion.*

| Paragraph | Sentence-by-sentence sketch |
|---|---|
| **Intro (2 sentences)** | (1) Paraphrase: ongoing debate over funding higher education. (2) Thesis: I believe a hybrid model — means-tested support — best balances access and accountability. |
| **Body 1 — view A (5 sentences)** | (1) Topic: proponents of free university argue it removes financial barriers. (2) Explain: equity, social mobility. (3) Example: Germany's tuition-free model. (4) Strength: outcomes — broad access. (5) Link/limitation: but funded entirely by taxpayer. |
| **Body 2 — view B (5 sentences)** | (1) Topic: those who favour fees argue students should invest in their own future. (2) Explain: graduates earn more, so should contribute. (3) Example: UK tuition-fee system. (4) Limitation: deters poorer students. (5) Link: tension between access and accountability. |
| **Body 3 — own opinion (4 sentences)** | (1) Position: a means-tested model resolves both. (2) Explain: rich pay, poor receive support. (3) Example: Australia's HECS-style income-contingent loan. (4) Why this is better: access AND accountability. |
| **Conclusion (2 sentences)** | (1) Synthesis: each side captures part of the truth. (2) Position re-stated: a means-tested approach is the most defensible compromise. |

Total: ~290 words, 5 paragraphs, every paragraph fully developed.

## 8. Quick paragraph-engineering checklist

For every body paragraph before moving on, confirm:

- [ ] Topic sentence makes a claim (not a question, not a vague generality)
- [ ] At least one full sentence explains *why* the claim is true
- [ ] At least one concrete example (country, situation, scenario, study)
- [ ] Closing sentence ties back to the position or transitions
- [ ] No more than ONE sequence-marker (Firstly / Secondly etc.) in the whole essay
- [ ] Paragraph is between 4 and 8 sentences
- [ ] Topic sentence does not start with "Firstly"
