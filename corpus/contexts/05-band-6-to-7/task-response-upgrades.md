# Task Response Upgrades — Band 6 → Band 7

> Task Response is the criterion most students under-prepare for, yet it is the easiest to lose. The Band 6 ceiling exists because of three habits: **partial answer, drifting position, underdeveloped ideas**. Fix all three and Task Response moves to 7.

---

## 1. The Band 7 Task Response definition (decoded)

Official descriptor: *"addresses **all parts** of the task; presents a **clear position throughout** the response; presents, **extends and supports** main ideas, but there may be a tendency to over-generalise and/or supporting ideas may lack focus."*

Decoded into checkable behaviours:

| Behaviour | Test |
|---|---|
| Addresses all parts | Every sub-question in the prompt is answered explicitly |
| Clear position throughout | The same stance is identifiable in introduction, body, and conclusion |
| Extends ideas | Each main idea is **explained** (not just stated) |
| Supports ideas | Each main idea has at least one **concrete example** or evidence |
| Roughly equal coverage | Each part of the prompt receives comparable depth/length |

---

## 2. The five common Task 2 prompt types and what "addressing all parts" means

| Prompt type | Sub-questions to answer | Body-paragraph plan |
|---|---|---|
| **Opinion** ("To what extent do you agree…") | Your view + reasoning | One stance, two reasons supporting it |
| **Discussion** ("Discuss both views and give your own opinion") | View 1 + View 2 + your own view | One paragraph for each view + your stance integrated |
| **Advantages/Disadvantages** ("Do the advantages outweigh…") | Advantages + Disadvantages + Which outweighs | One paragraph each, plus weighing in conclusion |
| **Problem/Solution** ("What problems… What solutions…") | Problems + Solutions | One paragraph each, ideally linked |
| **Two-part / Direct question** ("Why is this happening? What can be done?") | Question 1 + Question 2 | One paragraph for each question |

If the prompt has 2 sub-questions and only 1 is answered, the maximum TR score is **5**.

---

## 3. Reading the prompt accurately (the first 2 minutes)

A Band 7 candidate spends the first 2 minutes underlining and decoding the prompt:

1. **Topic** — what is the subject area? (e.g. *online learning for young children*)
2. **Sub-topics / scope** — *young children* (not adults), *online* (not all education)
3. **Task verb** — *discuss / agree / suggest / explain / outline*
4. **Sub-questions** — usually 2; circle them
5. **Position required?** — yes if the prompt asks *to what extent*, *do you agree*, *which is better*, *give your opinion*

Example prompt:
> *Some people think that young children should be taught entirely online. To what extent do you agree or disagree?*

Decoded:
- Topic: education
- Scope: **young children**, **entirely online**
- Sub-question: do you agree?
- Position: required (must be clear)

If the essay drifts to "online learning at university" or "online learning in general," it is **off-topic** and TR is capped.

---

## 4. The clear-position rule

A clear position is one a stranger could state in one sentence after reading your essay.

### Test

Ask: *Which option does the writer support, and how strongly?*
- *Agree*
- *Disagree*
- *Partly agree* (which side leans heavier?)

### Band 6 — drifting position

> ***Intro:*** *I agree that working from home is beneficial.*
> ***Body 1:*** *Working from home saves time…*
> ***Body 2:*** *On the other hand, working from home can be lonely…*
> ***Conclusion:*** *In conclusion, both have advantages and disadvantages.*

The writer drifts from agreement → balanced → no position. TR capped at 6.

### Band 7 — clear position throughout

> ***Intro:*** *Although working from home does present some social challenges, on balance I firmly believe its advantages — particularly in productivity and work–life balance — outweigh these drawbacks.*
> ***Body 1:*** *(advantages, fully developed)*
> ***Body 2:*** *(acknowledges challenges but reframes them as manageable)*
> ***Conclusion:*** *Considering both sides, the productivity gains and improved life-balance afforded by remote work make a compelling case for it as the future norm.*

The position is identifiable from the introduction, sustained in the body, restated in conclusion.

## 5. Idea development: the four-move pattern (PEEL)

Every body paragraph needs at minimum **5 sentences** following Point–Explain–Example–Link.

| Move | Function | Length |
|---|---|---|
| **P** Point | Topic sentence — the paragraph's claim | 1 sentence |
| **E** Explain | Why is this the case? Mechanism / reasoning | 1–2 sentences |
| **E** Example | A concrete illustration (real or hypothetical) | 1–2 sentences |
| **L** Link | Tie back to the question or stance / signpost forward | 1 sentence |

### Band 6 paragraph (P only — no development)

> *Online learning is bad for young children. They cannot focus. They use their phones too much. So, online learning is bad.*

### Band 7 paragraph (PEEL)

> *Online learning is poorly suited to children of primary-school age.* (P) *Young learners depend heavily on direct, real-time interaction with a teacher who can spot confusion and respond to it immediately, something that a screen simply cannot replicate; without this scaffolding, weaker pupils tend to drift into passive non-engagement.* (E — explanation) *During the COVID-19 school closures, for example, several large-scale studies in both the UK and South Korea found that primary-age children fell measurably behind in literacy and numeracy compared with classroom-taught peers.* (E — example) *For these reasons, fully online instruction at this stage of education risks widening rather than closing the achievement gap.* (L)

Note: 4 sentences, ~80 words, fully developed.

## 6. Examples — making them concrete

| Vague example (Band 6) | Concrete example (Band 7) |
|---|---|
| *In many countries, this is a problem.* | *In Seoul, for instance, average housing prices have tripled since 2010, pricing young couples out of the market.* |
| *Some people think so.* | *Older generations, in particular, often hold this view, having lived through periods when employment was guaranteed.* |
| *Studies show that…* | *Research by the OECD has consistently shown that…* (only if true; otherwise: *evidence increasingly suggests that…*) |
| *Many students cheat.* | *In a 2022 university-conducted survey across the UK, almost a third of undergraduates admitted to having used AI tools to write coursework.* |

If you can't recall a real statistic, fall back on:
- A country you know well (your own, ideally)
- A clearly hypothetical case ("Consider a parent who…")
- A historical pattern ("Throughout the twentieth century, …")

**Avoid** invented numbers like *"Studies show that 78% of people…"* — examiners are trained to spot fabricated stats.

## 7. Introduction template (Band 7)

A Band 7 introduction has **two parts** (in 2–3 sentences):

1. **Background sentence** — paraphrases the prompt
2. **Thesis statement** — your position + a hint at your reasoning

| Element | Example |
|---|---|
| Background (paraphrase) | *In recent years, a growing number of educators have argued that primary-school instruction should move entirely online.* |
| Thesis | *Although this approach offers certain logistical advantages, I would argue that it is fundamentally unsuitable for children of this age, primarily because of the loss of direct teacher–student interaction.* |

**Templates that work:**
- *While X, this essay will argue that Y, primarily because Z.*
- *Although it is often claimed that X, I would contend that Y.*
- *Opinions on X are divided. While some argue Y, others maintain Z. In my view, ...*

**Templates to avoid (memorised, penalised):**
- *Education is a controversial topic in modern society…*
- *In today's modern world of globalisation…*
- *This essay will discuss both views in detail…*

---

## 8. Body paragraph structures by prompt type

### 8.1 Opinion essay (one-sided position)

| Para | Content |
|---|---|
| Body 1 | Reason 1 + explanation + example |
| Body 2 | Reason 2 + explanation + example |
| (Optional Body 3) | Brief acknowledgement of counter-view + rebuttal |

### 8.2 Discussion essay (both views + opinion)

| Para | Content |
|---|---|
| Body 1 | View A — explained fairly with example |
| Body 2 | View B — explained fairly with example |
| Body 3 (or integrated) | Your stance with reasoning |

**Common Band 6 trap:** writing about both views but never giving your own. The prompt asks for opinion → you must state one.

### 8.3 Advantages / disadvantages essay

| Para | Content |
|---|---|
| Body 1 | Main advantages (explained + example) |
| Body 2 | Main disadvantages (explained + example) |
| Conclusion | Which outweighs (if asked) — must be explicit |

### 8.4 Problem / solution essay

| Para | Content |
|---|---|
| Body 1 | Problem(s) explained, with consequences and example |
| Body 2 | Solution(s) explained, with mechanism and example |

**Tip:** make sure each solution maps onto a problem you raised.

### 8.5 Two-part / Direct question

| Para | Content |
|---|---|
| Body 1 | Answer to question 1 (explained + example) |
| Body 2 | Answer to question 2 (explained + example) |

---

## 9. Conclusion: synthesis, not repetition

A Band 6 conclusion just paraphrases the introduction. A Band 7 conclusion **synthesises** — it shows how the body's arguments fit together and re-states the position once.

### Band 6 conclusion

> *In conclusion, online learning has both advantages and disadvantages, and people have different opinions about it.*

### Band 7 conclusion

> *On balance, although fully online instruction may suit older, self-directed learners, the developmental needs of young children — for direct interaction, immediate feedback, and structured social environments — make a classroom setting clearly preferable. The most defensible approach for primary education is therefore a predominantly in-person model, complemented, but not replaced, by digital tools.*

### Conclusion ingredients (Band 7)

| Ingredient | Example |
|---|---|
| Position re-stated (new wording) | *I would maintain that…* |
| Synthesis of body arguments | *Taken together, these factors suggest…* |
| Optional implication / recommendation | *Policymakers should therefore…* |

### Conclusion templates

- *In light of the arguments above, it seems clear that…*
- *Weighing both perspectives, my view is that…*
- *On balance, while X is undeniable, the case for Y appears stronger because…*
- *To sum up, although there is some merit in X, the evidence on the whole points to Y.*

## 10. Common Task Response leaks (final checklist)

| Leak | Symptom | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Off-topic | Writing about a related but different topic | Underline scope words in prompt, refer back every paragraph |
| Partial answer | Only half the prompt addressed | Identify all sub-questions before writing |
| Position drift | Stance changes through essay | Write your thesis as one sentence on the question paper, refer to it for every paragraph |
| Underdeveloped ideas | Body paragraph < 4 sentences | Apply PEEL — at least 5 sentences |
| No examples / fake stats | "Studies show 78%..." | Use country-specific or historical examples instead |
| Memorised templates | "Two-edged sword", "In today's world…" | Use original phrasing |
| Unbalanced coverage | One side gets 200 words, the other 50 | Plan word counts in advance |
| Conclusion just repeats | "In conclusion, both have pros and cons" | Synthesise + restate position with new wording |
| Under length | < 250 words | Aim for 270–290 |
| Includes irrelevant supporting evidence | Example doesn't link to claim | Cut, or link explicitly back |

## 11. Side-by-side: a full prompt walked through

**Prompt:** *Some people believe that the best way to reduce crime is to give longer prison sentences. Others, however, believe there are more effective alternatives. Discuss both views and give your own opinion.*

### Band 6 response (sketch)
- *Intro:* "Crime is a big problem in modern society. Some people think prisons are good. Others don't. This essay will discuss both."
- *Body 1:* "Long prisons are good. They scare people. Criminals don't commit crime."
- *Body 2:* "On the other hand, education is also good. People can change."
- *Conclusion:* "In conclusion, both views have pros and cons."

Issues: no clear position, no examples, mechanical structure, generic vocabulary, paragraphs underdeveloped.

### Band 7 response (sketch)
- *Intro:* "There is an ongoing debate over how best to reduce crime, with one camp championing longer custodial sentences and another favouring more rehabilitative measures. While lengthy imprisonment may have some deterrent value, I would argue that alternatives such as education, employment programmes, and restorative justice are more effective in the long term."
- *Body 1 (longer sentences):* "Proponents of longer sentences argue that the threat of extended incarceration deters potential offenders, while also keeping habitual criminals off the streets. In countries such as the United States, however, decades of harsh sentencing have failed to produce a corresponding fall in serious crime, suggesting that this deterrent effect is weaker than supporters claim."
- *Body 2 (alternatives):* "By contrast, addressing the underlying drivers of crime — poverty, lack of education, and limited employment — has produced more durable results. Norway's prison system, which prioritises rehabilitation and education over punishment, reports recidivism rates roughly half those of comparable Anglo-American systems."
- *Body 3 / integrated opinion + conclusion:* "Taking these arguments together, although a justice system clearly needs a credible custodial component for the most serious offenders, the bulk of public investment is better directed toward prevention and rehabilitation. Lengthy prison terms, in short, treat the symptoms; education, employment, and restorative justice address the disease."
