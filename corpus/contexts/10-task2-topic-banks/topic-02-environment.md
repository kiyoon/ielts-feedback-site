# Topic: Environment

## Sub-topics commonly seen on the IELTS exam

- Climate change and global warming: causes, consequences, mitigation.
- Air, water and plastic pollution; specific industrial vs vehicle sources.
- Conservation of biodiversity, endangered species, deforestation, marine life.
- Recycling, waste reduction, circular-economy practices.
- Renewable vs fossil-fuel energy.
- Individual vs governmental responsibility for environmental action.
- The role of international cooperation in addressing transboundary issues.
- Environmental education in schools.
- Whether economic growth and environmental protection are compatible.
- The role of business / corporations in environmental degradation.

## Sample real exam prompts

1. *Climate change is one of the greatest challenges facing humanity today. Some argue that it is the responsibility of individuals to address this issue, while others believe that governments and corporations should take the lead. Discuss both views and give your own opinion.*
2. *Many people believe that protecting the environment is the responsibility of governments rather than individuals. To what extent do you agree?*
3. *Some people think that the best way to solve environmental problems is to increase the price of fuel. To what extent do you agree or disagree?*
4. *Many countries are facing serious environmental issues. What are the main causes of these problems, and what measures can be taken to solve them?*
5. *Plastic waste is becoming a major global problem. What are the causes, and what solutions can be proposed?*
6. *Some argue that economic growth is the only way to end global poverty, while others claim that it is damaging the environment and should therefore be stopped. Discuss both views.*
7. *In some countries, the government has tried to reduce traffic by, for instance, encouraging carpooling, car-sharing schemes and improving public transport. Are these methods effective?*
8. *Wild animals have no place in the 21st century, so protecting them is a waste of resources. To what extent do you agree?*

## Core arguments FOR (e.g. urgent action, government regulation, individual responsibility, renewables)

| # | Argument | Supporting reason | Concrete example | Useful phrase |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Individual action accumulates and signals demand to producers | Millions of small choices reshape markets faster than slow regulation | The rapid mainstreaming of plant-based meat and EVs followed consumer demand | "consumer choice exerts a cumulative pressure on industry" |
| 2 | Carbon pricing internalises the true cost of emissions | Fossil fuels are cheap only because their environmental damage is unpriced | The EU Emissions Trading Scheme has measurably reduced industrial CO₂ | "internalise the external costs of pollution" |
| 3 | Renewables now outcompete fossil fuels on price, not just morality | Solar and wind have fallen sharply in cost over the past decade | IEA reports show new solar generation is now the cheapest electricity in many regions | "switch to economically competitive clean energy" |
| 4 | Biodiversity loss undermines food security and medicine | Ecosystems supply pollination, fresh water and pharmaceutical compounds | Roughly three quarters of food crops depend at least partly on pollinators | "preserve the ecosystem services on which agriculture depends" |
| 5 | Government regulation is necessary because pollution is a market failure | Individual firms cannot self-regulate when competitors externalise costs | The phasing-out of leaded petrol succeeded only after global regulation | "address the market failure inherent in environmental externalities" |
| 6 | Environmental education shifts long-term cultural norms | Children who learn about waste become parents and voters who demand reform | Several Scandinavian curricula place sustainability at the centre of primary teaching | "embed environmental literacy in the next generation" |
| 7 | Investing in green infrastructure creates jobs | The transition is itself an economic stimulus | Renewable-energy installation has become a major employer in countries such as Germany and China | "drive employment in the green economy" |
| 8 | International cooperation is essential because pollution crosses borders | Air, water and climate are intrinsically transboundary | The Montreal Protocol on ozone-depleting substances is the standout success story | "tackle transboundary environmental challenges" |
| 9 | Recycling and circular-economy design reduce raw-material extraction | Reusing materials cuts both waste and mining pressure | The European "right-to-repair" rules push manufacturers to design for longevity | "transition towards a circular economy" |
| 10 | Fuel-tax and congestion charges effectively reduce private-car use | Behaviour responds to price signals more reliably than to appeals | London's congestion charge reportedly cut central-area traffic significantly when introduced | "leverage price signals to alter behaviour" |

## Core arguments AGAINST / counter (e.g. against blaming individuals, against rapid transition, against costly green policy)

| # | Argument | Supporting reason | Concrete example | Useful phrase |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Pinning the burden on individuals lets corporate polluters off the hook | A small number of large firms account for most industrial emissions | Carbon-Disclosure-Project reports identify a handful of producers as major emitters | "shifts the moral burden onto individuals while exempting structural polluters" |
| 2 | Fuel-tax rises disproportionately hurt the rural and the poor | Lower-income households spend a higher share of income on basic transport | The "yellow vests" protests in France were triggered by exactly this issue | "regressive in its incidence on lower-income households" |
| 3 | Premature shutdown of fossil-fuel industries threatens employment in transition regions | Coal communities in particular lack alternative work | Appalachia and parts of Poland illustrate the dilemma | "exposes transition communities to economic dislocation" |
| 4 | Renewables remain intermittent and need expensive grid upgrades | Sunshine and wind are not dispatchable | The 2021 Texas grid failure highlighted the vulnerabilities of mismatched supply | "raise concerns about grid reliability and storage capacity" |
| 5 | Strict environmental rules may push polluting industry to weaker jurisdictions | This "carbon leakage" simply shifts emissions abroad | Several heavy industries have relocated from Europe to less-regulated markets | "may merely displace, rather than reduce, emissions" |
| 6 | Recycling is often less effective than the public assumes | Much exported plastic ends up in landfill anyway | Investigations by media outlets have repeatedly traced "recycled" waste to dump sites in poorer countries | "promotes a misleading appearance of sustainability" |
| 7 | Asking developing countries to cut emissions can entrench global inequality | Industrialised nations created most of the existing CO₂ | The "common but differentiated responsibilities" principle in UN climate talks acknowledges this | "imposes the cost of mitigation on those least responsible" |
| 8 | Top-down government action without public buy-in tends to fail | Compliance collapses if citizens see policy as arbitrary | Several attempted plastic-bag bans were reversed after public pushback | "lacks the social legitimacy required for sustained compliance" |
| 9 | Eco-labelling and "green" branding are vulnerable to greenwashing | Consumers cannot easily verify claims | Multiple regulators have fined firms for misleading sustainability claims | "facilitates greenwashing rather than genuine reform" |
| 10 | Conservation of charismatic species sometimes neglects ecosystems as a whole | Funding follows pandas, not soil microbes | Habitat-level conservation arguably yields better returns | "fixates on iconic species at the expense of habitat integrity" |

## Concession patterns (band-7 nuance)

1. *While individual choices alone cannot reverse climate change, dismissing them as irrelevant overlooks the cumulative and signalling effects of consumer behaviour.*
2. *Granted that renewables are intermittent, technological progress in storage is rapidly closing this gap.*
3. *It is true that environmental regulation imposes costs on industry; equally, the cost of inaction is now widely recognised to be far greater.*
4. *Critics rightly note that fuel taxes can be regressive, but this is a design problem rather than a reason to abandon carbon pricing altogether.*
5. *Even sceptics of climate policy concede that air-quality and biodiversity benefits stand on their own, irrespective of CO₂.*
6. *Although developing nations have a legitimate claim to industrial development, this need not be premised on the most polluting technologies.*
7. *Recycling is, admittedly, no substitute for waste reduction at source; it is, however, a useful component of a broader circular-economy strategy.*

## Ready-to-adapt thesis statements

1. **(Opinion)** *I largely agree that responsibility for environmental protection rests primarily with governments, since only states can co-ordinate the regulation, taxation and infrastructure that meaningful action requires.*
2. **(Discuss both views)** *Although both individuals and governments have a role to play, in my view the burden ultimately falls on governments, whose regulatory and fiscal levers far outstrip what individuals can achieve in isolation.*
3. **(Advantages / disadvantages)** *While carbon taxation carries undeniable economic costs, particularly for lower-income households, its environmental and behavioural benefits, on balance, justify its introduction with appropriate safeguards.*
4. **(Problem / solution)** *The current environmental crisis is driven by a confluence of unchecked emissions, deforestation and unsustainable consumption; addressing it will require a coordinated package of regulatory, technological and educational measures.*
5. **(Two-part question)** *This essay will first examine the principal causes of plastic pollution before evaluating which interventions are most likely to deliver lasting reductions.*

## Sample fully-developed body paragraph #1 (band 7+)

> *(Topic sentence)* The most effective lever available to governments is fiscal: pricing carbon so that polluters bear at least some of the cost they impose on the wider population. *(Explanation)* In an unregulated market, fossil-fuel users pay only for the fuel itself, not for the air-quality damage, climate consequences or eventual clean-up that their consumption entails. A carbon tax or emissions-trading scheme makes those externalities visible in the price tag, which in turn shifts both consumer and corporate behaviour towards lower-emission alternatives. *(Concrete example)* The European Union's Emissions Trading Scheme, in operation for nearly two decades, has been associated with measurable reductions in industrial emissions even as the EU economy has continued to grow. *(Mini-conclusion)* Pricing carbon is therefore not a punitive measure but a practical correction of a market failure that voluntary action alone cannot remedy.

## Sample fully-developed body paragraph #2

> *(Topic sentence)* Critics nevertheless point out that environmental policies, however well-intentioned, can fall hardest on those who can least absorb their costs. *(Explanation)* Higher fuel duties, congestion charges and meat taxes all raise everyday prices, but the proportional impact is greatest on lower-income households, particularly in rural areas where private cars and basic groceries are not optional. *(Concrete example)* The 2018 *gilets jaunes* movement in France crystallised this concern: a fuel-tax rise framed as climate policy was widely perceived as an additional burden on already-stretched commuters. *(Mini-conclusion)* This does not invalidate carbon pricing, but it does mean that policymakers must pair such measures with rebates, subsidies for clean alternatives and investment in public transport — otherwise the green transition risks losing the social legitimacy on which its success depends.

## Common pitfalls / clichés to avoid

- "Mother Earth is crying." — Sentimental, low band.
- "We must save the planet for our children." — Tired and not specific.
- "Every coin has two sides." — Cliché transition; replace.
- Using "global warming" and "climate change" interchangeably without thought — they overlap but aren't identical.
- Claiming "individuals can solve climate change by recycling" — overstated.
- Citing fabricated percentages of pollution by country.
- "Pollution is the biggest problem in the world." — Vague, weak.
- Endless lists of pollution types (air, water, soil, noise, light) without development.
- "Trees are the lungs of the earth." — Overused metaphor.
- Confusing *effect* (noun) and *affect* (verb).
