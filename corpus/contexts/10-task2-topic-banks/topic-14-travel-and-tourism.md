# Topic: Travel and Tourism

## Sub-topics commonly seen on the IELTS exam

- Mass tourism and its effects on host communities.
- Over-tourism in popular destinations and how to manage it.
- Eco-tourism and its trade-offs.
- The cultural impact of tourism on local traditions.
- Whether international travel promotes mutual understanding.
- Gap years and long-term travel.
- The environmental footprint of aviation.
- Virtual tourism and its potential to reduce travel.
- Tourism as a tool of economic development.
- Local people's quality of life in heavily visited cities.

## Sample real exam prompts

1. *Tourism causes serious damage to local cultures and the environment. To what extent do you agree?*
2. *Some people argue that international tourism is a positive development that brings benefits to host countries, while others believe it has negative effects. Discuss both views and give your own opinion.*
3. *Eco-tourism is becoming more popular. What are the advantages and disadvantages of this development?*
4. *Some popular tourist destinations have begun to limit the number of tourists they admit each year. Is this a positive or negative development?*
5. *Some people argue that travelling abroad is the best way to learn about other cultures, while others think that this can also be done through books, films and the internet. Discuss both views.*
6. *Many young people now take a "gap year" between school and university to travel or work abroad. To what extent do you think this is beneficial?*
7. *In some countries, tourism has become the main source of income but has also damaged local life. What are the problems, and how can they be addressed?*
8. *Some people argue that air travel should be heavily taxed to reduce its environmental impact. To what extent do you agree?*

## Core arguments FOR (e.g. tourism is positive, eco-tourism, cultural exchange, taxing aviation)

| # | Argument | Supporting reason | Concrete example | Useful phrase |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Tourism provides essential income for many developing economies | Foreign-currency earnings can outweigh other sectors | Several Caribbean and South-East Asian economies are heavily tourism-dependent | "underpin the foreign-currency earnings of many developing economies" |
| 2 | Travel abroad fosters cross-cultural understanding | Direct contact reduces stereotypes | Erasmus and similar exchange programmes are routinely cited | "facilitate first-hand cross-cultural exchange" |
| 3 | Eco-tourism finances conservation that pure regulation cannot | Visitor fees fund park rangers and habitat protection | Costa Rica's national-park system is a leading example | "channel visitor revenue into conservation" |
| 4 | Cultural tourism preserves traditions by giving them economic value | Crafts and festivals revive when valued by visitors | Many regional cuisines and crafts have rebounded with tourism | "create economic incentives for cultural preservation" |
| 5 | Heavily taxing aviation prices in environmental costs | Flying is currently subsidised by untaxed fuel | Several countries are considering carbon-aviation levies | "internalise the environmental cost of aviation" |
| 6 | Virtual tourism offers access to those who cannot travel | The disabled, elderly and poor benefit | Major museums increasingly offer virtual tours | "extend cultural access beyond the wealthy and able-bodied" |
| 7 | Limits on visitor numbers protect both heritage and visitor experience | Crowded sites are unpleasant and degrade fast | Venice, Machu Picchu and others have introduced caps | "protect both site integrity and visitor experience" |
| 8 | A gap year develops independence and cultural fluency | Independent travel matures young people | Many universities now formally welcome gap-year students | "foster independence and intercultural fluency" |
| 9 | Tourism creates employment across skill levels | Hotels, transport, hospitality and crafts all draw on tourism | Tourism is a top employer in several countries | "generate employment across the skill spectrum" |
| 10 | Well-planned tourism distributes economic activity to peripheral regions | Visitors go to places domestic capital ignores | Coastal and rural regions in many countries depend on this | "spread economic activity to peripheral regions" |

## Core arguments AGAINST / counter (e.g. over-tourism harms, environmental costs, cultural commodification)

| # | Argument | Supporting reason | Concrete example | Useful phrase |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Over-tourism degrades the quality of life of local residents | Crowds, noise and rising rents push out locals | Barcelona and Venice have produced large-scale local protests | "displace residents from their own neighbourhoods" |
| 2 | Tourism can commodify culture into performance | Authentic ritual gives way to staged versions for visitors | Many indigenous communities have raised this concern | "commodify cultural traditions for visitor consumption" |
| 3 | Aviation has a disproportionate environmental footprint | A single long-haul flight outweighs years of household savings | IPCC reports consistently flag aviation as hard to decarbonise | "carry an environmental footprint disproportionate to its share of travellers" |
| 4 | Tourism revenue often leaks out via foreign-owned chains | Hotels and tour operators repatriate profits | Many small-island economies see most spending leave | "leak through foreign-owned chains rather than recirculating locally" |
| 5 | Eco-tourism can disturb the ecosystems it claims to protect | Even careful visits stress wildlife | Several wildlife-tourism sites have closed seasonally | "disturb fragile ecosystems despite protective intent" |
| 6 | Tourism-dependent economies are vulnerable to shocks | Pandemics, terrorism and recessions hit fast | COVID-19 devastated tourism-dependent economies | "expose dependent economies to systemic shocks" |
| 7 | Cultural tourism encourages communities to fossilise rather than evolve | The "traditional" version is preserved for visitors, not for local life | Cultural critics raise this concern recurrently | "freeze cultures into a curated past" |
| 8 | Gap years are skewed by income | Only middle-class students can afford to travel for a year | Equity-focused commentators raise this clearly | "embed economic privilege in supposed personal-development experiences" |
| 9 | Mass tourism contributes to housing crises | Short-term rentals reduce long-term housing supply | Several capitals have legislated against this | "deplete long-term housing stock through short-term rentals" |
| 10 | Virtual tourism can never substitute for embodied experience | Smell, sound, weather and chance encounters are irreproducible | Travel writers routinely make this point | "fail to replicate the embodied texture of place" |

## Concession patterns (band-7 nuance)

1. *While tourism brings undeniable economic benefits, treating it as an unqualified good has produced exactly the over-tourism many destinations now regret.*
2. *Granted that travel broadens the mind, the same effect is increasingly available — though not perfectly — through high-quality virtual experiences.*
3. *It is true that eco-tourism finances conservation; this should not obscure the fact that even the lightest visit places stress on fragile sites.*
4. *Critics rightly note that aviation is environmentally costly, but pricing it more accurately is a more constructive response than blaming individual travellers.*
5. *Even those who defend gap-year travel tend to concede that it is, in practice, an experience disproportionately available to the well-off.*
6. *To be sure, foreign-owned hotel chains repatriate profits, but their tax contribution and local employment remain substantial.*
7. *Although cultural tourism can commodify tradition, banning it altogether would also withdraw the economic incentive that has revived many crafts.*

## Ready-to-adapt thesis statements

1. **(Opinion)** *I largely agree that tourism, as currently practised in many destinations, causes more cultural and environmental damage than benefit, although a better-managed model could reverse this balance.*
2. **(Discuss both views)** *Although both views have merit, in my view well-regulated tourism remains a substantial net benefit for host countries, provided that residents and ecosystems are adequately protected.*
3. **(Advantages / disadvantages)** *While the rise of mass international tourism brings genuine economic benefits, the disadvantages — particularly in over-visited cities — on balance call for more deliberate management.*
4. **(Problem / solution)** *The harms attributed to over-tourism stem from a confluence of low marginal cost, weak regulation and concentrated promotion; addressing them requires action on visitor caps, taxation and dispersal alike.*
5. **(Two-part question)** *This essay will first examine the chief problems caused by over-tourism in popular destinations before considering which interventions are most likely to deliver lasting relief.*

## Sample fully-developed body paragraph #1 (band 7+)

> *(Topic sentence)* The most visible cost of mass tourism, particularly in historic European cities, has been the steady displacement of long-term residents from the very neighbourhoods that draw the visitors. *(Explanation)* When apartments can earn more in a week of short-term rentals than in a month of standard tenancies, landlords convert them, the local population shrinks, and the bakeries, cobblers and primary schools that defined the area give way to souvenir shops and lockboxes. The neighbourhood is then "preserved" only in appearance; the social life that gave it character has quietly relocated to the outskirts. *(Concrete example)* Cities such as Venice, Barcelona and Lisbon have all seen their central populations fall dramatically over the past two decades, with successive municipal administrations now legislating against further short-term-rental conversions. *(Mini-conclusion)* If tourism is to remain a viable industry rather than an extractive one, host cities will need to recognise that protecting local residents is not hostility to visitors but a precondition for the very experience visitors come to seek.

## Sample fully-developed body paragraph #2

> *(Topic sentence)* That said, dismissing tourism as inherently destructive overlooks the role it plays in financing the preservation of the things travellers come to see. *(Explanation)* Conservation of historic buildings, archaeological sites and protected ecosystems is rarely cheap, and in many countries the entrance fees, levies and downstream tax revenue from visitors fund precisely the maintenance that would otherwise rely on stretched public budgets. The choice, in many destinations, is therefore not between tourism and unspoiled heritage, but between regulated tourism and the slow decay that an unfunded site eventually succumbs to. *(Concrete example)* Costa Rica's national-park system, often cited as an eco-tourism flagship, channels admission fees directly into ranger salaries, habitat restoration and surrounding community programmes. *(Mini-conclusion)* The honest conclusion, then, is neither uncritical celebration nor blanket rejection but a serious commitment to designing tourism so that the value created flows visibly to the residents and ecosystems on whose hospitality the industry rests.

## Common pitfalls / clichés to avoid

- "Travel broadens the mind." — Cliché; develop the claim specifically.
- "Travelling is the best teacher." — Tired.
- "Tourists are ruining everything." — Sweeping.
- Confusing *travel* (uncountable, the activity) with *journey / trip* (countable).
- "Every coin has two sides." — Cliché.
- "Take only memories, leave only footprints." — Cliché.
- Treating *tourist* and *traveller* as if they had clear moral hierarchy.
- Citing fabricated visitor statistics.
- Romanticising "authentic" cultures as static.
- Confusing *site* (location) with *sight* (something seen).
