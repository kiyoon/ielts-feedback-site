# Topic: Technology

## Sub-topics commonly seen on the IELTS exam

- The internet and how it has transformed daily life, work and study.
- Smartphones, screen time and digital addiction, especially among children.
- Social media and its effects on relationships, mental health and politics.
- Artificial intelligence and automation, particularly job displacement.
- Online communication vs face-to-face interaction.
- Technology in education (e-learning, online universities, edtech tools).
- Digital privacy, surveillance and data protection.
- The digital divide between richer and poorer populations.
- Whether technology has made people lonelier, busier or less patient.
- Children's exposure to screens and online content.

## Sample real exam prompts

1. *Some people believe that the internet has brought people closer together, while others think it has made people more isolated. Discuss both views and give your own opinion.*
2. *Many people argue that smartphones have negatively affected human relationships. To what extent do you agree or disagree?*
3. *Increasing use of technology means people no longer need to memorise information. Is this a positive or negative development?*
4. *Some people think that artificial intelligence and automation will create more problems than they solve. To what extent do you agree?*
5. *Children are spending more and more time online, often unsupervised. What are the problems caused by this, and what can be done to address them?*
6. *Some people argue that social media has had a positive impact on society, while others believe it has done more harm than good. Discuss both views.*
7. *In the modern world, it is no longer necessary to use animals for food or clothing or for other purposes such as scientific research. Do you agree?*
8. *In some countries, more people are deciding to have children later in life because of advances in medicine. What are the advantages and disadvantages of this?*

## Core arguments FOR (e.g. technology is broadly positive, or its benefits outweigh costs)

| # | Argument | Supporting reason | Concrete example | Useful phrase |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Digital tools democratise access to information | A learner with internet has the same library as the wealthy | Wikipedia and Khan Academy serve free educational content to hundreds of millions | "democratise access to knowledge" |
| 2 | Communications technology bridges geographical separation | Migrants, students and remote workers can sustain relationships | Free video-call platforms now span any continent | "transcend geographical barriers" |
| 3 | Automation removes humans from dangerous and tedious tasks | Robots take on jobs that injure or bore | Industrial robots in car plants have reduced occupational injury significantly | "automate routine and hazardous work" |
| 4 | AI accelerates medical and scientific research | Pattern-finding at scales beyond any human team | AI-assisted protein-structure prediction has reportedly shortened drug-discovery timelines | "accelerate scientific discovery" |
| 5 | Online platforms enable small businesses to reach global markets | E-commerce removes the historic advantage of physical location | Small artisans now sell worldwide via Etsy and similar marketplaces | "level the playing field for small enterprises" |
| 6 | Smart-city technology improves urban services | Sensors optimise traffic, water, electricity in real time | Singapore is widely cited as a smart-city pioneer | "harness data for more efficient public services" |
| 7 | Digital literacy is now itself a transferable skill | Comfortable users participate fully in modern economies | Many entry-level jobs presuppose basic digital fluency | "embed digital literacy in the labour market" |
| 8 | Telemedicine extends specialist care to remote areas | Patients consult specialists without long journeys | Rural Australia has used telemedicine for cardiology consultations for years | "extend specialist services beyond major cities" |
| 9 | Online learning supports lifelong upskilling | Workers can retrain without leaving their jobs | LinkedIn Learning and Coursera now serve corporate-training needs at scale | "facilitate continuous professional development" |
| 10 | Big data improves disaster response and public health surveillance | Real-time signals catch outbreaks earlier | COVID-era genomic sharing illustrates the principle | "enhance early-warning systems" |

## Core arguments AGAINST / counter (e.g. screens, isolation, privacy, automation losses)

| # | Argument | Supporting reason | Concrete example | Useful phrase |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Heavy social-media use is correlated with adolescent mental-health decline | Comparison and curated lives undermine self-esteem | Studies suggest a sharp rise in teenage anxiety since smartphone saturation around 2012 | "linked to a marked deterioration in adolescent wellbeing" |
| 2 | Digital communication erodes the depth of face-to-face relationships | Texts compress nuance and discourage sustained conversation | Many adults now report dining with phones rather than companions | "displaces sustained, in-person interaction" |
| 3 | Automation threatens routine clerical and manual jobs | Predictable tasks are exactly what algorithms do best | Self-checkouts, automated trucks and chatbot agents are already cutting headcount in their sectors | "displaces workers performing predictable tasks" |
| 4 | Constant connectivity blurs work-life boundaries | Always-on email destroys evenings and weekends | Several European jurisdictions have legislated a "right to disconnect" | "erodes the boundary between professional and private life" |
| 5 | Online platforms harvest personal data with limited consent | Users rarely read terms; surveillance is built in | Multiple major regulators have fined large tech firms for data-protection breaches | "raise serious concerns over informational privacy" |
| 6 | Misinformation spreads faster than corrections | Algorithms amplify outrage because outrage drives engagement | Researchers at major universities have repeatedly shown false stories travelling further than true ones | "amplify misinformation at the expense of nuance" |
| 7 | Excessive screen time damages children's attention and development | Pre-school exposure correlates with reduced linguistic development | Several pediatric bodies advise no screens before age two | "interferes with cognitive and social development" |
| 8 | The digital divide entrenches inequality | Poor families may lack devices, broadband, or quiet study space | School closures during COVID exposed this gap dramatically | "widens, rather than narrows, social inequalities" |
| 9 | AI decisions can encode historical bias | Training data reflect existing prejudice | Hiring and predictive-policing algorithms have repeatedly been found to discriminate | "reproduce systemic bias in algorithmic form" |
| 10 | Over-reliance on technology atrophies basic skills | Calculators erode arithmetic; satnav erodes spatial memory | Most adults can no longer remember the phone numbers of close family | "fosters dependence at the expense of human capability" |

## Concession patterns (band-7 nuance)

1. *While social media has undeniably enriched some forms of communication, it has equally been implicated in a measurable rise in adolescent anxiety.*
2. *Granted that automation will displace certain occupations, history suggests that new categories of work tend to emerge in their place.*
3. *It is true that smartphones have transformed daily convenience; whether they have also transformed daily wellbeing is a more contested question.*
4. *Critics rightly highlight the privacy costs of digital platforms, but these are arguably tractable through regulation rather than abandonment.*
5. *Online learning offers genuine pedagogical advantages, yet it cannot fully substitute for the social fabric of a physical classroom.*
6. *Although AI systems can outperform humans on narrow tasks, this should not be confused with general intelligence or judgement.*
7. *To be sure, technology has accelerated the spread of misinformation, but it has also, paradoxically, equipped journalists with unprecedented tools for verification.*

## Ready-to-adapt thesis statements

1. **(Opinion)** *I largely agree that excessive smartphone use has weakened the quality of personal relationships, although the device itself is less to blame than the way in which it is used.*
2. **(Discuss both views)** *Although both views have merit, I would argue that, on balance, the internet has connected people in ways that more than compensate for the new forms of isolation it has produced.*
3. **(Advantages / disadvantages)** *While the rise of artificial intelligence carries serious risks of job displacement, its advantages in productivity and scientific advancement, in my view, outweigh these drawbacks if managed responsibly.*
4. **(Problem / solution)** *The principal harms associated with children's online activity stem from inadequate supervision and platform design; addressing them therefore requires action at the household, regulatory and corporate level.*
5. **(Two-part question)** *This essay will first identify the main reasons behind the explosion in screen time and then consider which mitigation strategies are most likely to be effective.*

## Sample fully-developed body paragraph #1 (band 7+)

> *(Topic sentence)* The most consequential effect of widespread digital connectivity has been the dismantling of geographical barriers to ordinary communication. *(Explanation)* Until comparatively recently, sustained contact with a friend or relative on a different continent required either expensive telephone calls or letters that travelled for weeks; today, free video calls and messaging apps make a daily exchange not only possible but routine. *(Concrete example)* This shift is particularly visible among economic migrants who, unlike their predecessors, can be present at family meals, birthdays and even bedtime stories despite being thousands of kilometres away. *(Mini-conclusion)* The technology has not, of course, replaced face-to-face contact, but it has substantially altered what separation actually means in practice — a change whose social significance has perhaps been underestimated.

## Sample fully-developed body paragraph #2

> *(Topic sentence)* That said, the same connectivity that sustains long-distance relationships has been linked to a measurable deterioration in adolescent wellbeing. *(Explanation)* Continuous exposure to curated, idealised images of other people's lives invites unfavourable self-comparison, while platform algorithms are designed to maximise the time spent on the device rather than the quality of that time. *(Concrete example)* A growing body of research has tracked rising rates of anxiety and disrupted sleep among teenagers since smartphones became near-universal in the early 2010s, with several national health services issuing formal advisories. *(Mini-conclusion)* While digital communication clearly offers benefits, the case for treating heavy social-media use as a public-health concern, particularly for the young, is now too strong to be dismissed.

## Common pitfalls / clichés to avoid

- "Technology is a double-edged sword." — Cliché; replace with a specific trade-off.
- "We are living in the digital era." — Empty opener.
- "Children are addicted to their phones." — Overstated without hedging.
- Treating "the internet" as a single thing — distinguish platforms, services, hardware.
- Claiming statistics ("90 % of teenagers") without source.
- "Robots will take all our jobs." — Overstated.
- "Technology has changed our lives a lot." — Vague.
- Using *technology* as a noun to mean any device or service indiscriminately.
- "Phones make us lazy." — Reductive.
- Confusing *affect* / *effect*; *practice* (n) / *practise* (v) in British English.
