# IELTS Writing — The Examiner's Perspective

This document distils what trained IELTS Writing examiners actually do when they mark a script. It synthesises the official IELTS Key Assessment Criteria, the Band Descriptors, IELTS-published research (Cotton et al. 2011 on coherence-and-cohesion rating), and widely cited examiner commentary. The aim is to help an LLM marker produce assessments that align with how human examiners interpret the descriptors.

---

## 1. Examiners are trained to apply descriptors — not their own taste

IELTS Writing examiners are recruited from teachers with relevant qualifications and experience, complete intensive training, and undergo regular standardisation sessions where they re-mark benchmark scripts. They are not free to invent their own criteria.

Practical consequence: a strong but unconventional script does **not** beat a conventional script that fits the descriptors. A 400-word essay with brilliant vocabulary but only one body paragraph can score worse than a textbook 270-word essay with two clear body paragraphs.

---

## 2. The four criteria are independent — there is no compensation

Each of the four criteria is rated separately. A weak Task Response cannot be saved by elegant grammar; great vocabulary cannot rescue confused organisation. Examiners are explicitly trained not to "round up" one criterion because of strength elsewhere.

For an LLM marker: produce four independent ratings *before* computing an average. Don't let one criterion bias another.

---

## 3. "Best fit" — not feature counting

The cover note on the official descriptors states: "A script must fully fit the positive features of the descriptor at a particular level."

In practice this is interpreted as a **best-fit** judgement:
- Read the descriptor for the candidate band and the band above and below.
- Decide which descriptor profile **most closely matches** the script as a whole.
- If the script "fully fits" the higher band's positive features and is free of that band's bolded negative features, award the higher band.

Examiners do not tally pluses and minuses. They form a holistic mental picture and check it against descriptors.

---

## 4. Bolded negative features are hard limiters

The official document marks certain phrases in **bold** and notes that "Bolded text indicates negative features that will limit a rating."

These are the **gating features** examiners watch for. The most consequential ones in Bands 2–6:

| Bolded feature | Effect |
|---|---|
| "Paragraphing may be inadequate or missing" (Task 2 CC, Band 5) | Caps CC at Band 5 |
| "Subordinate clauses are rare and simple sentences predominate" (Band 4 GRA) | Caps GRA at Band 4 |
| "The format may be inappropriate" (Band 4 TR) | Caps TR at Band 4 |
| "Unrelated to the task" (Band 4 LR) | Caps LR at Band 4 |
| "Length may be insufficient to provide evidence of control of sentence forms" (Band 3 GRA) | Caps GRA at Band 3 |
| "The entire response may be off-topic" (Band 2 CC) | Caps CC at Band 2 |
| "Responses of 20 words or fewer are rated at Band 1" | Hard floor |
| "Where there is proof that a candidate's answer has been totally memorised" (Band 0) | Band 0 |

For an LLM marker: scan for these limiters first. They override any positive features.

## 5. What examiners look for — by criterion

### 5.1 Task Achievement / Task Response

**First-pass questions an examiner asks:**
1. Does the script address all parts of the prompt? (For Task 1 GT, all three bullets; for Task 2, the full question; for Task 1 Academic, all major data features.)
2. Is the format appropriate? (Letter for GT; report-style for AC; essay for Task 2.)
3. Is the response on-topic?
4. **Task 1 Academic only:** Is there a clear overview?
5. **Task 1 GT only:** Is the tone consistent and appropriate? Is the purpose explicit?
6. **Task 2 only:** Is there a clear position? Are main ideas extended and supported with examples?

**Common Band 6 patterns examiners see:**
- Task 1 Academic: lists figures, no overview, or buries the overview.
- Task 1 GT: tone slips between formal and informal; bullets only listed not extended.
- Task 2: position stated then contradicted; one prompt part neglected; conclusion vague.

**Common Band 7 patterns examiners see:**
- Task 1 Academic: opening paragraph paraphrases the prompt; second paragraph delivers a clean overview; body paragraphs group data logically.
- Task 2: introduction explicitly states position; each body paragraph develops one main idea with a specific example; conclusion restates the position.

### 5.2 Coherence and Cohesion

The Cotton et al. (2011) IELTS-funded research shows that examiners find CC the hardest criterion to rate consistently. They spontaneously use auxiliary terms not in the descriptor:
- *flow* (≈ coherence)
- *structure* / *introduction* / *conclusion* / *topic sentence* (≈ paragraphing)
- *linking words* (≈ cohesive devices)

**First-pass questions:**
1. Can I follow the message without rereading?
2. Are the paragraphs logical with one main idea each?
3. Are cohesive devices flexible and accurate, or repetitive and mechanical?
4. Is reference clear ("this", "these", "such") or muddled?
5. Is the order of ideas within a paragraph logical?

**Bands 6 vs 7 cue:** Band 6 cohesion is *visible* and *mechanical*. Band 7 cohesion is *flexible* and includes substitution and reference, not just connectors.

### 5.3 Lexical Resource

**First-pass questions:**
1. Is the vocabulary range adequate to express the ideas?
2. Are there less common, topic-appropriate words?
3. Are collocations correct (e.g. *make a decision*, not *do a decision*)?
4. Are there spelling errors? How dense?
5. Are word forms correct (e.g. *economic* vs *economical*)?
6. Is there evidence of memorised template phrases?

**The "risk-taker" Band 6 note** is a key examiner concept: a candidate who tries advanced vocabulary but uses it incorrectly may end up at the same Band 6 as a candidate who plays safe with simpler vocabulary. The descriptor explicitly says: "If the writer is a risk-taker, there will be a wider range of vocabulary used but higher degrees of inaccuracy or inappropriacy."

### 5.4 Grammatical Range and Accuracy

**First-pass questions:**
1. Is there a variety of sentence structures (simple, compound, complex)?
2. Are complex sentences accurate, or do they break down?
3. How frequent are error-free sentences?
4. Do errors impede meaning?
5. Is punctuation managed (commas, apostrophes, semicolons)?

**Most diagnostic single signal:** examiner counts (mentally) the proportion of error-free sentences. Band 7 = "error-free sentences are frequent". Band 6 = a mix where complex sentences are usually wrong.

## 6. The first-30-seconds impression

Examiners read a script in full, but research and examiner self-reports indicate that an initial impression forms quickly. The features that shape that impression:

1. **Is the script roughly the right length?** (Visual scan.)
2. **Is there visible paragraphing?** (Multiple paragraphs vs a single block.)
3. **Does the introduction directly address the prompt?**
4. **Are early sentences grammatically stable?**
5. **Is the tone appropriate?**

A script that fails on any of these is unlikely to be revised upward as the examiner reads further. Conversely, a script that nails these items rarely falls below Band 6.

---

## 7. Common misinterpretations of the descriptors

These are the patterns examiners see in *self-assessed* candidates that cause inflated self-scores. An LLM marker should explicitly avoid these:

### "Long words = high vocabulary"
Wrong. The descriptor cares about **range, precision, collocation, style** — not difficulty for its own sake. Misusing *plethora*, *paramount*, *delve into* is worse than not using them.

### "Lots of linkers = high cohesion"
Wrong. **Mechanical, overused** linkers are explicitly a Band 6 feature. Cohesion is judged on flexibility, including reference (*this*, *these*) and substitution.

### "Long, complex sentences = high grammar"
Wrong. **Accurate** complex sentences score; **broken** ones don't. A Band 7 script may have many simple sentences alongside accurate complex ones.

### "More words = better"
Wrong. There is no upper limit, but errors and repetition tend to rise with length. Beyond ~290 words on Task 2 the marginal benefit is negative for most candidates.

### "Five paragraphs are required"
Not strictly true. Four paragraphs is the standard Task 2 essay shape (intro, two body, conclusion). Five works fine if each has a clear purpose. The descriptor cares that paragraphing is *logical* and supports coherence — not that it follows a specific count.

## 8. What examiners regard as off-criterion

These are things candidates worry about but examiners do not formally weigh:

- **Handwriting beauty.** As long as it's legible, it's fine.
- **Personal opinion strength.** Examiners don't agree or disagree with the candidate; they assess how the position is argued.
- **Topic familiarity.** A weak topic doesn't cap any criterion if the response is well argued.
- **Use of British vs American English.** Both are accepted, but the candidate must be consistent within a script.
- **Citing real statistics.** No sources required; invented but plausible examples are fine.
- **Title for the essay.** Not required; not penalised either way.

## 9. Quick descriptor-to-evidence mapping for LLM marking

When marking a script, an LLM should look for **textual evidence** for each band's features. Here is a compact mapping for the critical Band 5–7 range.

### Task Achievement / Task Response

| Look for | Evidence type |
|---|---|
| Clear position (Task 2) | Single, unambiguous statement of opinion in intro and conclusion |
| Clear overview (Task 1 AC) | Sentence(s) that summarise the main trend without numbers |
| All bullets covered (Task 1 GT) | Each of the three bullets has at least one developed sentence |
| Idea extension | Each main idea has at least one supporting sentence (reason / example / consequence) |
| Off-topic content | Sentences that don't connect to the prompt |
| Tone consistency (Task 1 GT) | No mixing of *Hi mate* with *I look forward to your prompt response* |

### Coherence & Cohesion

| Look for | Evidence type |
|---|---|
| Logical paragraphing | Each paragraph has one main idea; topic sentence usually present |
| Flexible cohesion | Use of *this*, *these*, *such*, *the latter*, *whereas* — not just *firstly/secondly* |
| Cohesion errors | Misused linkers (*Moreover* between two contrasting points; *Therefore* with no causal link) |
| Sentence-level flow | Sentences within a paragraph follow logically; no abrupt jumps |

### Lexical Resource

| Look for | Evidence type |
|---|---|
| Less common items | Topic-specific or sophisticated words used correctly |
| Collocations | Correct verb-noun pairs (*pose a threat*, *raise concerns*) |
| Spelling errors | Density per 100 words |
| Word formation | Correct part of speech (*economy* vs *economic* vs *economical*) |
| Memorised chunks | Phrases that don't fit the surrounding register |

### Grammatical Range & Accuracy

| Look for | Evidence type |
|---|---|
| Range | Use of relative clauses, conditionals, passive, participle clauses, modal verbs |
| Error-free sentences | Proportion of sentences with zero grammar/punctuation errors |
| Error severity | Errors that impede meaning vs those that don't |
| Punctuation | Comma splices, missing apostrophes, run-on sentences |

## 10. Practical examiner behaviours an LLM should emulate

1. **Read the whole script before scoring.** Initial impressions can be wrong.
2. **Score each criterion independently.** Don't anchor on the first criterion.
3. **Use best-fit, not feature-counting.** Pick the band that most closely matches.
4. **Check for bolded negative limiters first.** They cap the rating.
5. **Quote evidence from the script** when justifying a score (helps avoid hallucinated judgement).
6. **Do not over-reward effort.** A script that *attempts* complex vocabulary but uses it wrongly is at the band that matches its delivered output, not its ambition.
7. **Do not under-reward simplicity.** A clean Band 7 script with mostly correct simple-and-complex sentences is Band 7, even if it isn't flashy.
8. **Mark the script as written**, not as it would be with corrections.

## 11. Cotton et al. (2011) — what examiners actually said about CC

This IELTS-funded study tracked examiner think-aloud comments while marking. Key findings:

- Examiners were **conscientious** in applying the descriptors but found CC the **most difficult** criterion to consistently rate.
- They imported **auxiliary concepts** not in the descriptor: *flow*, *structure*, *introduction*, *conclusion*, *topic sentence*, *linking words*.
- These auxiliary concepts are **legitimate proxies** for descriptor terms (paragraphing, sequencing, cohesive devices) and have since informed how training treats CC.
- Examiners weighed **paragraph organisation more heavily** than the formal descriptor wording suggests.

Implication: an LLM marker should evaluate paragraph quality and topic sentences as part of CC, not just count linking words.

---

## 12. The role of word count in examiner judgement

Even with no automatic penalty:
- Underlength scripts trigger an immediate negative impression.
- The examiner expects to see idea development that simply isn't possible in 200 Task 2 words.
- LR and GRA descriptors at Band 3 explicitly mention "significantly underlength" as a possible cause for Band 3.

Practical examiner heuristics:
- Task 2 < 250 words → likely TR ≤ 5; CC may also drop.
- Task 2 < 200 words → likely TR ≤ 4; LR/GRA may drop.
- Task 2 < 100 words → likely Band 3 across the board.
