## Per-criterion justification
**Task Response / Achievement:** The essay covers all three countries and reports relevant figures: “11,000 kWh,” “6,000 kWh,” “300 kWh,” and “1,200 kWh.” However, the overview “electricity consumption in all three countries had a growth” is too broad because the USA and Germany both declined after their peaks. A Band 7 Task 1 needs a clear overview with main trends and comparisons, while Band 6 only requires an attempted overview (`contexts/01-band-descriptors/task1-academic-band-descriptors.md`). The response also needs stronger grouping: high-consuming USA/Germany versus low but rapidly rising India, as advised in `contexts/08-vocabulary-grammar/04-task-response-error-correction-bank.md`.

**Coherence and Cohesion:** Paragraphing is logical: introduction, overview, USA/Germany, then India. Progression is easy to follow, but cohesion is mechanical: “After this,” “However, then,” “after that,” and “In contrast” are used rather than smoother time-flow and reference links. Repetition of “consumption,” “figure,” and country names weakens lexical cohesion. This fits Band 6 CC, where cohesive devices may be mechanical, not Band 7’s flexible referencing (`contexts/01-band-descriptors/task1-academic-band-descriptors.md`; `contexts/09-instructor-tips/08-cohesion-upgrades-instructor.md`).

**Lexical Resource:** There is adequate chart vocabulary: “illustrates,” “approximately,” “reached a peak,” “decline,” “similar pattern,” and “rapidly.” But several collocations are Band-6-level: “had a growth,” “the figure of USA,” “all over the period,” “grew slow,” and “had reach to.” The vocabulary is also repetitive, especially “consumption/consumed” and “figure.” Band 7 needs more precise and flexible word choice; Task 1 trend banks recommend “rose,” “peaked at,” “declined slightly,” “stood at,” and “recorded” (`contexts/09-instructor-tips/03-trend-language-bank.md`; `contexts/08-vocabulary-grammar/02-vocabulary-error-correction-bank.md`).

**Grammatical Range & Accuracy:** The essay uses some complex structures, such as “which is much higher than Germany,” and a colon in “Germany had a similar pattern:”. However, errors are frequent: “USA’s consumption increase,” “it slightly decrease,” “grew slow,” and “had reach to.” Past-time charts require consistent past tense, so “which is much higher” should be “which was much higher” (`contexts/08-vocabulary-grammar/01-grammar-error-correction-bank.md`). Meaning is still clear, so this is Band 6 rather than Band 5, but the error density prevents Band 7.
