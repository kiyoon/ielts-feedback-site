# IELTS Writing — Band 6 vs Band 7 Comparison

The leap from Band 6 to Band 7 is the most consequential boundary on the IELTS Writing scale. Most universities, professional bodies, and visa categories require Band 7 (or 6.5) overall, and Band 7 in writing specifically. This document gives a **side-by-side, criterion-by-criterion** comparison using the official descriptors, highlights the gating differences in plain language, and gives concrete examples of what tips a script from Band 6 into Band 7.

> **Headline:** The shift from 6 to 7 is fundamentally a shift from **adequate, mechanical, sometimes-uneven communication** to **logical, controlled, varied, mostly-precise communication**. Band 7 is the first band where the descriptors stop talking about "limited", "restricted", "mechanical" and start talking about "flexible", "variety", "frequent error-free", "precise".

---

## Quick reference — the four shifts

| Criterion | Band 6 in one phrase | Band 7 in one phrase |
|---|---|---|
| Task Response/Achievement | Relevant but **uneven coverage** and **unclear conclusion** | **All main parts** addressed with a **clear developed position** |
| Coherence & Cohesion | Cohesive devices used **mechanically/over-used** | **Range of devices used flexibly**, including reference & substitution |
| Lexical Resource | **Generally adequate but restricted**, lacking precision | **Sufficient flexibility and precision**; some less common items |
| Grammatical R&A | **Mix of simple and complex** but complex less accurate | **Variety of complex structures**; **error-free sentences are frequent** |

## 1. Task Achievement (Task 1) / Task Response (Task 2)

### Task 1 (Academic) — Band 6 vs Band 7

| | Band 6 | Band 7 |
|---|---|---|
| Coverage | "Focuses on the requirements" | "Covers the requirements" |
| Overview | "A relevant overview is **attempted**" | "Presents a **clear** overview" |
| Categorisation | Information "appropriately selected and supported" | "Data are appropriately **categorised**, and main trends or differences are **identified**" |
| Highlighting | Key features "**adequately** highlighted" | Key features "covered and **clearly** highlighted" |
| Detail | "Some details may be missing (or excessive)" | Could be "more fully or more appropriately illustrated or extended" |
| Errors of fact | "Some irrelevant, inappropriate or inaccurate information" | "A few omissions or lapses" |

**The gating shift:** at Band 6 the overview is *attempted*; at Band 7 it is *clear*. A script that lists figures without ever stating the overall picture cannot reach Band 7 TA.

### Task 1 (General Training) — Band 6 vs Band 7

| | Band 6 | Band 7 |
|---|---|---|
| Bullet coverage | All bullet points "covered and **adequately** highlighted" | All bullet points "covered and **clearly** highlighted" |
| Purpose | "The purpose is **generally** clear" | "Presents a **clear** purpose" |
| Tone | "There may be **minor inconsistencies in tone**" | "The tone is **consistent and appropriate**. Any lapses are minimal" |
| Detail | "Some details may be missing (or excessive)" | Bullets could be more fully developed |

**The gating shift:** Band 6 tolerates **minor tone inconsistencies**; Band 7 requires **consistent appropriate tone** end-to-end. A formal letter that drops "Cheers!" in the closing falls to Band 6.

### Task 2 — Band 6 vs Band 7

| | Band 6 | Band 7 |
|---|---|---|
| Prompt coverage | "Main parts are addressed (**though some may be more fully covered than others**)" | "Main parts of the prompt are **appropriately** addressed" |
| Position | "A position is presented that is directly relevant" | "**A clear and developed position** is presented" |
| Conclusion | "**Conclusions drawn may be unclear, unjustified or repetitive**" | (no negative qualifier — implicit: conclusions are clear and justified) |
| Idea development | "Some main ideas may be **insufficiently developed** or may **lack clarity**" | "Main ideas are **extended and supported**" (weakness: may "over-generalise") |
| Supporting evidence | "Some supporting arguments and evidence may be **less relevant or inadequate**" | "Lack of focus and precision in supporting ideas/material" (still relevant) |

**The gating shift:** Band 7 requires that **all main parts are addressed** *with equivalent depth* and that the position is **clear and developed**. Band 6 allows uneven coverage and ambiguous conclusions.

## 2. Coherence & Cohesion (same descriptor for Task 1 and Task 2)

| | Band 6 | Band 7 |
|---|---|---|
| Overall organisation | "Information and ideas are **generally** arranged coherently and there is a clear overall progression" | "Information and ideas are **logically organised** and there is a **clear progression** throughout the response" |
| Number of lapses | (implicit: more than a few) | "**A few lapses** may occur" |
| Cohesive devices | Used "to some good effect but cohesion within and/or between sentences may be **faulty or mechanical** due to misuse, overuse or omission" | "A **range** of cohesive devices including reference and substitution is used **flexibly** but with some inaccuracies or some over/under use" |
| Reference / substitution | "May lack flexibility or clarity and result in some repetition or error" | "**Used flexibly** but with some inaccuracies" |
| Paragraphing (Task 2) | "Paragraphing **may not always be logical** and/or the central topic may not always be clear" | "Paragraphing is generally used effectively to support overall coherence, and the **sequencing of ideas within a paragraph is generally logical**" |

### Concrete behavioural difference

**Band 6 cohesion (mechanical/template):**
> "Firstly, education is important. Secondly, education helps people. Thirdly, education changes life. Moreover, education is good. In conclusion, education is important."

**Band 7 cohesion (flexible, with reference and substitution):**
> "Education provides individuals with the skills they need for the workplace. **This** preparation, however, **goes further than the technical**: it also fosters critical thinking. Beyond personal benefits, **such advantages** ripple outward to the wider society."

The Band 7 example uses *this*, *such advantages* (substitution), varies its connectors, and groups ideas within paragraphs.

## 3. Lexical Resource (same descriptor for Task 1 and Task 2)

| | Band 6 | Band 7 |
|---|---|---|
| Adequacy | "Resource is **generally adequate** and appropriate" | "Resource is **sufficient to allow some flexibility and precision**" |
| Range | "**Rather restricted range** or a lack of precision in word choice" | "Some ability to use **less common and/or idiomatic items**" |
| Style/collocation | (no positive mention) | "An **awareness of style and collocation is evident**, though inappropriacies occur" |
| Risk-taking | "If the writer is a risk-taker, **wider range** but **higher degrees of inaccuracy or inappropriacy**" | (positive ability to attempt and mostly hit less-common items) |
| Spelling/word formation | "**Some errors** in spelling and/or word formation, but these do not impede communication" | "**Only a few errors** in spelling and/or word formation, and they do not detract from overall clarity" |

### Concrete behavioural difference

**Band 6 vocabulary:**
> "Many people **have a lot of money** and they can **buy a lot of things**. They have a **good life**."

**Band 7 vocabulary:**
> "**Affluent** consumers can **indulge in luxuries** that fall well beyond the reach of most households, leading to lifestyles their peers find **enviable**."

Band 7 introduces precision (*affluent*, *indulge in*), collocation (*beyond the reach*), and topic-specific vocabulary (*enviable lifestyles*). Errors exist but don't detract from clarity.

## 4. Grammatical Range & Accuracy (same descriptor for Task 1 and Task 2)

| | Band 6 | Band 7 |
|---|---|---|
| Range | "A **mix of simple and complex** sentence forms is used but **flexibility is limited**" | "A **variety of complex structures** is used with some flexibility and accuracy" |
| Accuracy of complex sentences | "Examples of more complex structures are **not marked by the same level of accuracy** as in simple structures" | "**Error-free sentences are frequent**" |
| Errors | "Errors in grammar and punctuation occur, but rarely impede communication" | "A **few errors in grammar may persist**, but these do not impede communication" |
| Punctuation | (implicit: errors occur) | "Grammar and punctuation are **generally well controlled**" |

### Concrete behavioural difference

**Band 6 grammar (complex sentences faulty):**
> "Although technology is develop fast and people use them every day, but the children spend too much time on phone, what is bad for they health."

**Band 7 grammar (complex sentences mostly accurate):**
> "Although technology has developed at an unprecedented pace and is used daily, children's excessive screen time can be detrimental to their health."

Band 7 uses present perfect, passive, possessive, and a complex subordinate structure with no errors. Band 6 attempts the same structure but has multiple errors (*develop*, *but* after *although*, *what* used wrongly, *they health*).

## What concretely tips a script from 6 to 7?

These are the most common diagnostic gates, distilled from the descriptors and from examiner commentary:

### Task 1 Academic
1. Add **a clear, separate overview sentence** that summarises 1–3 main trends/differences without numbers.
2. **Group the data** ("the three highest categories rose, while the two lowest fell") rather than describing line by line.
3. Cite **specific figures** with units to support every main claim.

### Task 1 GT
1. Make the **purpose explicit** in the first sentence.
2. Maintain **consistent tone** — pick formal/semi-formal/informal and never mix.
3. **Extend each bullet** with one specific detail, reason, or example — not just stating it.

### Task 2
1. State a **clear position** in the introduction and reaffirm it in the conclusion.
2. **Address every part** of the prompt (e.g. "discuss both views and give your opinion" needs all three).
3. Develop ideas with **specific, relevant examples**, not vague generalisations.
4. Use **paragraphing logically**: 4 paragraphs is standard (intro, two body, conclusion) with one main idea each.

### Coherence & Cohesion (both tasks)
1. Use **reference and substitution** ("this trend", "such an outcome", "the latter") instead of repeating nouns.
2. **Vary** linking words — don't reuse "Moreover" four times.
3. Each paragraph should have a **topic sentence** and ideas inside should follow logically.

### Lexical Resource (both tasks)
1. Use **less common synonyms** appropriately (e.g. *exacerbate* instead of *make worse*).
2. Use **collocations** (e.g. *take steps to address*, *strike a balance*).
3. Spell common topic vocabulary correctly.

### Grammatical R&A (both tasks)
1. Use a **range of complex structures**: relative clauses, conditionals, passive voice, participle clauses, noun phrases.
2. Make sure **most sentences are error-free** — if every other sentence has an error, you're at Band 6.
3. Use **commas, semicolons, and apostrophes** correctly.

## Examiner-style summary of the gap (per criterion)

> **Task Achievement / Response.** A Band 6 script does the right thing but unevenly; a Band 7 script does the right thing in a clearly organised way. The 6 reader thinks "I get it"; the 7 reader thinks "This is well argued / well summarised."

> **Coherence & Cohesion.** A Band 6 script has scaffolding you can see — *Firstly, Secondly, In conclusion*. A Band 7 script has scaffolding you stop noticing because it works — *this trend, however, the same is not true of, by contrast*.

> **Lexical Resource.** A Band 6 script uses common words mostly correctly; a Band 7 script uses some less common words mostly correctly. Precision, not just range, is what scales.

> **Grammatical R&A.** A Band 6 script attempts complex sentences and breaks them; a Band 7 script attempts complex sentences and most of them work. Frequency of error-free sentences is the single best predictor.

---

## Band 6.5 — what is it?

There is **no separate descriptor for 6.5**. Half-bands arise from averaging four whole-band scores. A 6.5 in writing typically means:
- two criteria at 6, two at 7 → average 6.5
- three criteria at 7 and one at 6 (averages 6.75 → IELTS rounding rules disagree across sources, see `scoring-rules.md`)
- three criteria at 6 and one at 7 (averages 6.25 → 6.5)

In practice, a 6.5 candidate is one who shows clear Band 7 features in some areas (often vocabulary or grammar) but is held back by underdeveloped Task Response or weak cohesion (or vice-versa).
