# Grammar Upgrades — Band 6 → Band 7

> Band 7 grammar requires a **variety of complex structures** with **frequent error-free sentences**. The trap for Band 6 writers is overreaching: using complex grammar with errors lowers the score more than using simpler grammar accurately. The goal is to expand the **range** of structures you can use **accurately and naturally**.

---

## 1. The Band 7 grammar checklist

To score 7.0 on Grammatical Range and Accuracy, an essay should contain:

| # | Feature | Minimum target in a 250-word essay |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Defining relative clauses | 2+ |
| 2 | Non-defining relative clauses | 1+ |
| 3 | Conditional sentences (1st, 2nd, or 3rd) | 1+ |
| 4 | Participle clauses (-ing or -ed) | 1+ |
| 5 | Complex noun phrases (multi-word noun groups) | 5+ |
| 6 | Passive voice (used correctly, not over-used) | 1–2 |
| 7 | Modal verbs for hedging | 3+ |
| 8 | Variety of sentence openers (not all subject-first) | several |
| 9 | Error-free sentences | the **majority** of sentences |

## 2. Relative clauses — the most reliable Band 7 structure

### 2.1 Defining vs non-defining

| Type | Function | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Defining** (no commas) | Identifies which one | *Students **who study regularly** perform better.* |
| **Non-defining** (commas) | Adds extra information | *Modern smartphones, **which are now affordable**, have transformed daily life.* |

### 2.2 Common relative pronoun rules

- *who* → people
- *which* → things; non-defining clauses (never use *that* in non-defining)
- *that* → people or things in defining clauses (informal preference)
- *whose* → possession
- *where* → place
- *when* → time
- *why* → reason

### 2.3 Band 6 → Band 7 upgrade through relative clauses

| Band 6 (two short sentences) | Band 7 (one complex sentence) |
|---|---|
| *Many students don't understand grammar. They get low scores.* | *Many students **whose understanding of grammar is limited** end up with low scores.* |
| *My country has a problem. It is air pollution.* | *My country **faces a serious problem of air pollution**, **which has worsened over the past decade**.* |
| *Climate change is caused by humans. It is a global crisis.* | *Climate change, **which is largely human-induced**, has become a global crisis.* |
| *Education is important. It opens doors to opportunities.* | *Education, **which opens doors to a range of opportunities**, plays a crucial role in social mobility.* |

### 2.4 Reduced relative clauses (Band 7+)

Reduce a relative clause to a participle to vary structure:

| Full relative | Reduced (participle clause) |
|---|---|
| *The students who are studying abroad face many challenges.* | *Students **studying abroad** face many challenges.* |
| *The houses which were damaged by the fire will be demolished.* | *The houses **damaged by the fire** will be demolished.* |
| *People who live in cities pay higher rents.* | *People **living in cities** pay higher rents.* |
| *Policies that have been introduced recently are working.* | *Policies **introduced recently** are working.* |

## 3. Conditional sentences — full range

| Type | Form | Use | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Zero | *if + present, present* | General truths | *If governments invest in education, the economy benefits.* |
| First | *if + present, will/can* | Likely future | *If pollution continues to rise, public health **will deteriorate**.* |
| Second | *if + past, would/could* | Hypothetical present | *If governments **invested** more in renewable energy, emissions **could be reduced** drastically.* |
| Third | *if + past perfect, would/could have* | Hypothetical past | *If stricter laws **had been introduced** earlier, the crisis **would have been avoided**.* |
| Mixed | Mix of past and present hypothetical | Past condition with present consequence | *If we **had acted** twenty years ago, we **would not be facing** this crisis today.* |

### 3.1 Inverted conditionals (Band 7+ flair)

Drop the *if* and invert subject/verb. Highly formal — use 1× per essay maximum.

| Standard | Inverted |
|---|---|
| *If governments were to invest more, …* | ***Were governments to invest** more, …* |
| *If they had acted earlier, …* | ***Had they acted** earlier, …* |
| *If anyone should object, …* | ***Should anyone object**, …* |

---

## 4. Participle clauses — concise and formal

Participle clauses replace a finite clause with an *-ing*, *-ed*, or *having + V3* form. They reduce wordiness and add formality.

| Two-clause Band 6 | Participle clause Band 7 |
|---|---|
| *The company was influenced by global trends, and it changed its strategy.* | ***Influenced by global trends**, the company changed its strategy.* |
| *Because she had completed her studies, she applied for jobs.* | ***Having completed her studies**, she applied for jobs.* |
| *When you live in a big city, you face many challenges.* | ***Living in a big city**, you face many challenges.* |
| *If governments enforce these laws, they will protect the environment.* | ***Enforcing these laws**, governments would protect the environment.* |
| *Once they are educated about recycling, citizens behave differently.* | ***Once educated about recycling**, citizens behave differently.* |

**Warning — dangling participles:** the participle's implied subject must match the main clause's subject.
- ✗ *Walking to school, the rain started.* (the rain wasn't walking)
- ✓ *Walking to school, I was caught in the rain.*

## 5. Complex noun phrases (an under-rated Band 7 marker)

Building noun phrases out of multiple modifiers signals lexical-grammatical sophistication.

| Simple noun (Band 6) | Complex noun phrase (Band 7) |
|---|---|
| *traffic* | *the rising level of urban traffic congestion* |
| *jobs* | *the increasing number of part-time employment opportunities* |
| *pollution* | *the alarming rate of industrial air pollution* |
| *students* | *students from disadvantaged socioeconomic backgrounds* |
| *children* | *children growing up in single-parent households* |
| *technology* | *the rapid pace of technological change in the workplace* |
| *people who use the internet* | *individuals with regular access to the internet* |

A Band 7 sentence often has a **long noun phrase as subject + a precise verb + a long noun phrase as object**. Example:
*The widespread availability of affordable smartphones has fundamentally reshaped the way younger generations consume information.*

## 6. Passive voice — used correctly

Passive voice is appropriate when the agent is unknown, unimportant, or the focus is on the action.

| Active (often weaker) | Passive (often stronger in formal writing) |
|---|---|
| *People can solve this problem easily.* | *This problem **can be solved** easily.* |
| *Researchers have shown that…* | *It **has been shown** that… / Research **suggests** that…* |
| *Government should introduce strict laws.* | *Strict laws **should be introduced**.* |
| *Many people consider technology useful.* | *Technology **is widely considered** useful.* |

**Don't overdo it:** A whole essay in passive voice sounds awkward. Aim for 1–3 well-placed passives.

### Common passive errors to avoid
- ✗ *English is speak in many countries.* → ✓ *English **is spoken**…*
- ✗ *The internet is using by people.* → ✓ *The internet **is used** by people.*
- ✗ *It is believe that…* → ✓ *It **is believed** that…*

## 7. Modal verbs for hedging and sophistication

A Band 6 writer makes absolute claims. A Band 7 writer modulates claims with modals.

| Function | Modal | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Possibility | *may, might, could* | *This **may** be one of the underlying causes.* |
| Tentative claim | *would, could* | *This **would** suggest that…* |
| Necessity | *must, need to, ought to, should* | *Governments **must** address this issue urgently.* |
| Ability | *can, be able to* | *Citizens **can** play a meaningful role.* |
| Past speculation | *may have, might have, could have* | *Earlier policies **may have** contributed to the problem.* |

---

## 8. Cleft sentences — varying focus

Cleft sentences emphasise particular elements. Use 1× per essay for impact.

| Plain | Cleft |
|---|---|
| *Education matters most.* | ***It is education that** matters most.* |
| *Many people don't realise that pollution affects mental health.* | ***What many people don't realise is that** pollution affects mental health.* |
| *Government action is needed urgently.* | ***What is needed urgently is** government action.* |

---

## 9. Inversion (advanced — use carefully)

| Trigger | Plain | Inverted (formal) |
|---|---|---|
| *Not only* | *Technology is changing how we live, and it is also reshaping work.* | ***Not only is technology changing** how we live, but it is also reshaping work.* |
| *Rarely* | *Such a transformation rarely happens within a generation.* | ***Rarely does such a transformation happen** within a generation.* |
| *Only by* | *We can solve this only by working together.* | ***Only by working together can we solve** this.* |
| *Under no circumstances* | *Children should not be exposed to such content.* | ***Under no circumstances should children be exposed** to such content.* |
| *Seldom* | *We seldom see this level of cooperation.* | ***Seldom do we see** this level of cooperation.* |

## 10. Noun clauses (subject and object)

Noun clauses turn a question or fact into a sentence component. They diversify structure beyond *I think that…* / *It is true that…*

### As subject

| Plain | Noun clause as subject (Band 7) |
|---|---|
| *Many people don't understand the long-term effect.* | ***What many people fail to understand** is the long-term effect.* |
| *We need to act quickly. This is clear.* | ***That we need to act quickly** is now self-evident.* |
| *The reason is unclear.* | ***Why this trend has accelerated** remains unclear.* |

### As object

| Plain | Noun clause as object (Band 7) |
|---|---|
| *I don't know the answer.* | *I don't know **what the answer is**.* |
| *People wonder about it.* | *People wonder **whether such measures will succeed**.* |

## 11. Sentence-opener variety

Band 6 writers start ~80% of sentences with the subject. Band 7 writers vary openers.

| Opener type | Example |
|---|---|
| Subject (default) | *Pollution is rising in major cities.* |
| Adverb | *Increasingly, pollution is becoming a public-health concern.* |
| Prepositional phrase | *Across major cities, pollution levels continue to climb.* |
| Participle clause | *Driven by industrial expansion, pollution has reached alarming levels.* |
| Subordinate clause | *Although awareness has grown, pollution continues to rise.* |
| Cleft / inversion | *What is most concerning is the impact on children's lungs.* |
| It-cleft / dummy | *It is the impact on children's lungs that is most concerning.* |

---

## 12. Punctuation upgrades

| Punctuation | Use | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Comma after introductory clause | After adverbials and participle clauses | *In the long term, this approach is unsustainable.* |
| Comma around non-defining clause | Both sides | *Renewable energy, **which is now cost-competitive**, deserves greater investment.* |
| Semicolon | Joins two related independent clauses | *Pollution affects everyone**;** the wealthy can no longer escape it.* |
| Colon | Introduces explanation/list | *One factor stands out**:** government inaction.* |
| Em-dash (sparingly) | Emphasis or aside | *The solution—if it exists—lies in cooperation.* |

### Common punctuation errors to fix

- Comma splice: *I went home, I was tired.* → *I went home; I was tired.* / *I went home because I was tired.*
- Missing comma after intro: *In the modern world technology is essential.* → *In the modern world**,** technology is essential.*
- Apostrophe abuse: *the 1990's, government's are…* → *the 1990s, governments are…*

---

## 13. The "control beats complexity" principle

Examiners' guidance, in plain language: **a Band 7 essay with simpler grammar but no errors scores higher than a Band 6 essay with complex grammar and frequent errors.**

If a sentence feels uncertain, simplify. If a sentence is clearly correct, complexify (combine, embed a clause, add a participle).

---

## 14. 10 Band 7 sentence templates (steal these)

1. *X, which is widely regarded as Y, has nevertheless been criticised for Z.*
2. *Were governments to invest more in X, the long-term consequences for Y could be reduced significantly.*
3. *What is often overlooked in this debate is the fact that X.*
4. *X, driven largely by Y, has emerged as one of the most pressing concerns of the contemporary era.*
5. *While it is true that X, this argument fails to take into account Y.*
6. *Having considered both perspectives, I am inclined to believe that X.*
7. *Not only does X contribute to Y, but it also gives rise to Z.*
8. *One factor that deserves particular attention is the role of X in shaping Y.*
9. *If X had been addressed earlier, the present situation would arguably be far less serious.*
10. *On balance, although X presents certain drawbacks, the benefits it offers in terms of Y outweigh these concerns.*
