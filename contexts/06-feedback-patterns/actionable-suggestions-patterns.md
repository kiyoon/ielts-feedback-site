# Actionable Suggestions: Patterns

How examiners and teachers phrase "what to do next" suggestions. The hallmark of *good* IELTS feedback is that the student leaves with 2–3 specific, doable changes — not a vague "improve your grammar".

---

## 1. The five canonical sentence frames

Almost every actionable suggestion in the corpus uses one of these:

1. **Imperative**: "Do X."
   - "Plan, check and make sure you answer all of the question." (keytoielts.com)
   - "Use simple words and make no mistakes." (ieltsadvantage.com)
   - "Add at least two supporting sentences to each main idea." (synthesised)
2. **"You need to" / "You should" / "You must"**:
   - "You need to develop ALL of your main ideas to get Band 7." (ieltscharlie.com)
   - "You must explain why you think this." (ieltscharlie.com)
3. **"Try to" / "Consider" (softer)**:
   - "Try to balance the two sides equally." (synthesised from ieltsadvantage.com pattern)
   - "Consider using referencing words instead of connectors." (ieltsliz.com)
4. **"It is better to X than Y" (principle)**:
   - "It is better to use simple words and make no mistakes than use advanced words in error." (ieltsadvantage.com)
   - "It is better to write in a simple way and make no mistakes, than use complex grammar with frequent errors." (ieltsadvantage.com)
5. **"To reach Band X, you need to / focus on…"**:
   - "To move toward Band 7.5+, focus on using a wider range of cohesive devices beyond basic linking words." (cathoven.com)
   - "To reach Band 7 and beyond, you need to go into even more detail by ILLUSTRATING what you mean with a specific EXAMPLE." (ieltscharlie.com)

## 2. The "Original → Suggested" pattern (rewriting)

The most concrete, actionable form. Used by IELTS Podcast, Simon, IELTS Answers, ChatGPT-style graders, and writing9 hover cards.

### Sentence-level

| Original | Suggested | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| "individuals should contemplate the ramifications of their vehicular utilization" | "people should consider the effects of their car use" | Simpler is more accurate (babycode.org). |
| "before decide" | "before deciding" | Gerund after preposition (ieltspodcast.com). |
| "alot of peoples thinks" | "a lot of people think" | Spelling, word form, agreement. |
| "do games" | "play games" | Collocation (ieltsonlinetests.com). |
| "researches" | "research" | Uncountable noun (ieltsonlinetests.com). |
| "kids" | "children" | Register (ieltsonlinetests.com). |
| "unhappy" | "ominous" / "discontent" / "dissatisfied" | Range (ieltsonlinetests.com). |
| "loads" | "many / a great deal of" | Register (ielts470). |

### Paragraph-level (IELTS Simon's approach)

Show the original paragraph, list 2–3 issues, then write a Band-7 rewrite of the same paragraph. (https://www.ielts-simon.com/ielts-help-and-english-pr/2018/05/ielts-writing-task-2-rewriting.html)

Example skeleton:

> **Original (Band 6 paragraph):** [text]
>
> **Issues:** (1) topic sentence is missing; (2) one fragment ("Whereas X."); (3) "moreover" used incorrectly.
>
> **Rewrite (Band 7):** [text]
>
> **What changed:** topic sentence added; fragment merged into the previous sentence; "moreover" replaced with "in addition".

## 3. Pauline Cullen / "develop your ideas" patterns

The single largest band-6→7 lever. Stock phrasings:

- "The essay consists mostly of concluding statements rather than ideas that are developed to show how the conclusions were reached." — diagnosis (keytoielts.com).
- "This idea again sounds like a conclusion, not a further reason arguing for the benefits." — inline note (keytoielts.com).
- "Each main idea must be explained and illustrated." — directive (ieltscharlie.com).
- "You need to SHOW YOUR THINKING to the IELTS Examiner." — directive (ieltscharlie.com).
- "It's not enough to just say WHAT you think. You also need to explain WHY you think this." — principle (ieltscharlie.com).

## 4. Question-prompt feedback (ieltsetc.com style)

Instead of giving a directive, the examiner writes a *question* in the margin that prompts the student to develop the idea. Source: https://ieltsetc.com/2020/03/ielts-writing-correction/

| Margin prompt | Triggers |
|---|---|
| "which means…?" | clarification of vague claim |
| "So..?" | next-step / consequence |
| "in order to…?" | purpose |
| "And as a result…?" | causal chain |
| "For example…?" | concrete instance |
| "because…?" | reason |
| "Such as..?" | example |

This is a high-quality teaching technique — it forces the student to do the cognitive work themselves rather than receiving the answer.

## 5. Process-level recommendations

When the issue is bigger than a sentence (planning, time management, proofreading):

- "Take the first 3–5 minutes of the test to outline what you are going to write." (e2language.com)
- "Reduce the amount you write and focus on brainstorming, planning, organising and checking grammar." (ieltsbuddy.com)
- "Self-proofreading before submission could easily boost your score by at least 10%." (ielts470)
- "Spend the last 3 minutes proofreading specifically for articles, agreement, and fragments." (synthesised)
- "Apply the 100% rule — only use a word you are 100% sure of." (ieltsadvantage.com)

## 6. Prioritisation — "Top 3 actions" patterns

The closing block of most feedback. Conventions:

- **Numbered list** (not bulleted) — implies priority order.
- **Each item starts with an imperative verb**.
- **Each item is tagged with a criterion** in parentheses or bold prefix.
- **Item 1 is always the highest-leverage fix** (usually Task Response idea-development for band 6 students).
- **Maximum 3 items** — students cannot fix more.

Example skeleton:

```
1. (Task Response) For every main idea, write two extra sentences answering "why?" and "for example?".
2. (Coherence and Cohesion) Replace one connector per paragraph with a referencing word; never start more than one sentence per paragraph with a connector.
3. (GRA) Spend the last 3 minutes proofreading specifically for articles, agreement, and sentence fragments.
```

---

## 7. Closing forward-looking lines (verbatim from corpus)

- "In your next essay, focus on the first action — developing your ideas — and you should see a noticeable jump."
- "If you applied these changes, this essay would likely move into Band 7."
- "To move toward Band 7.5+, focus on using a wider range of cohesive devices." (cathoven.com)
- "Most students at Band 6–6.5 aiming for Band 7 do reach it with focused practice on idea development." (paraphrased correction-service marketing)
- "Self-proofreading before submission could easily boost your score by at least 10%." (ielts470)

---

## 8. Anti-patterns — what makes a suggestion *not* actionable

| Anti-pattern | Why it fails | Replace with |
|---|---|---|
| "Improve your grammar." | No specifics | "Spend 3 minutes proofreading articles in your final draft." |
| "Use better vocabulary." | No specifics | "Replace 'good' / 'bad' / 'important' with topic-specific terms." |
| "Develop your ideas more." | Tells what, not how | "For each main idea, add 2 supporting sentences answering 'why' and 'for example'." |
| "Use more linking words." | The opposite is usually true at band 6 | "Replace one connector per paragraph with a referencing word (this, these, it)." |
| "Write more." | Quantity ≠ quality | "Aim for 270–290 words; spend the saved time planning before you start." |
| "Try to be clearer." | Subjective | "Add a topic sentence to body paragraph 2 stating the main idea." |

---

## 9. The "if you fix this one thing" pattern

Used to give the student hope and focus. Examples:

- "If you developed each idea with two extra sentences, this essay would likely move into Band 7."
- "Self-proofreading before submission could easily boost your score by at least 10%." (ielts470)
- "Fix the article errors and you immediately move from Band 5.5 to Band 6 in GRA."
- "Master the overview sentence and you go from Band 6 to Band 7 in Task 1 Task Achievement."
