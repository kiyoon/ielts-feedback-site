# Tone and Register in IELTS Feedback

The signature voice of an experienced IELTS examiner: encouraging-but-honest, evidence-based, second-person, formally-warm. This file documents the tonal conventions and shows what to do — and avoid.

---

## 1. Core tone principles

| Principle | What it means | Source / pattern |
|-----------|---------------|------------------|
| **Strengths first** | Always lead with concrete praise before critique. | Universal across IELTS Advantage, IELTS Luminary, IELTS Podcast, ielts-blog. |
| **Quote-then-comment** | Ground every comment in the student's actual text in inverted commas. | British Council samples, IELTS Advantage examiner report. |
| **Specific, not vague** | "Improve your grammar" → reject. "Articles missing in 4 places — see line 3, 7, 12, 19" → accept. | ieltswritinganalytics.com: "show the exact sentence that lost you the band". |
| **Second person** | "You", "your essay" — not "the candidate", "this response". (Note: official IELTS examiner reports for *training* use "the candidate"; correction services use "you".) | IELTS Advantage, cathoven, writing9. |
| **Public-descriptor register** | Mild academic register; phrases like "inadequately developed", "frequent errors", "appropriate range". | Official IELTS public band descriptors. |
| **No emojis, no exclamations** | Examiners do not use them. Encouragement is conveyed by content, not punctuation. | All formal sources. |
| **Concession-pivot ("Yes, but…")** | "You X. **However,** Y." structure. | Pervasive across all sources. |
| **Forward-looking close** | End with one sentence about the *next* attempt. | "In your next essay…" — cathoven.com, ieltsadvantage.com. |

---

## 2. The "encouraging-but-honest" balance

Real IELTS feedback is not soft. The examiner gives a precise diagnosis and tells the student exactly what is wrong, but frames it as a problem with **the writing**, not the student.

### Do (encouraging-but-honest)

- "You X… However, Y."
  - "You logically organise information and ideas; there is clear progression throughout. **However**, you do have a tendency to overuse linking words." (ieltsadvantage.com)
- Specific praise tied to the candidate's text:
  - "You use an adequate range of vocabulary for the task such as 'financial responsibilities', 'academic environment' and 'scientific competitions'." (ieltsadvantage.com)
- Direct critique, but blamed on a *behaviour*:
  - "You attempt complex sentences but these tend to be less accurate than simple sentences. You make frequent grammatical errors and some errors can cause some difficulty for the reader." (ieltsadvantage.com)
- Forward-looking close:
  - "If you applied these changes, this essay would likely move into Band 7."

### Don't (too soft)

- "Your essay is great, just keep practising!" — no diagnostic value.
- "Your grammar is fine, don't worry." — false reassurance; many band-6 students need to know what is wrong.
- "I love how you tried to use complex words!" — emoji-style encouragement; not in examiner register.

### Don't (too harsh)

- "Your introduction is bad." — vague; demoralising; no fix.
- "You can't write at IELTS level." — global judgement, never used by examiners.
- "Pretty much everything is wrong here." — non-specific; unactionable.

## 3. Register: formal but warm

The examiner register sits between teacher-friendly and academic-formal. Sentence patterns:

- "There are some real strengths here, and some specific things to work on."
- "Overall, this is a competent answer that addresses the prompt."
- "The structure that you have used really helps in this area." (ieltsadvantage.com — note the slight informality of "really helps".)
- "It is better to use simple words and make no mistakes than use advanced words in error." (ieltsadvantage.com — direct, but principled.)

### Vocabulary register

| Use | Avoid |
|-----|-------|
| competent, adequate, appropriate, sufficient | great, awesome, awful, terrible |
| inadequately developed, mechanically used | weak, lazy, sloppy |
| consider, try to, you need to | you must, you should always |
| frequent errors, recurrent issues | tons of mistakes, lots of errors |
| precise, less common, idiomatic | fancy, big, smart |

## 4. Sentence patterns (templates)

### 4a. Strengths

- "You X… such as 'Y' and 'Z'."
- "Your X is generally well-handled — for example, 'Y'."
- "There is good progression in your essay, with each paragraph building on the last."
- "Your position is clear from the introduction onwards."

### 4b. Concession-pivot

- "You X. However, Y."
- "While X is good, Y."
- "X is appropriately handled, but Y."
- "These are good attempts; the next step is to Y."

### 4c. Specific weakness

- "X is used inaccurately — 'Y' should be 'Z'."
- "Your idea about X reads as a conclusion rather than a developed reason."
- "Some cohesion is faulty: 'Y'."
- "Frequent errors with X — see 'Y', 'Z', and 'W'."

### 4d. Action / directive

- "You need to X."
- "Try to X."
- "Consider X."
- "**Action:** X."
- "In your next essay, X."

### 4e. Forward-looking close

- "In your next essay, focus on X — that is the single biggest lever."
- "If you applied these changes, your essay would likely move into Band 7."
- "Most students at Band 6 aiming for Band 7 do reach it with focused practice on idea development." (paraphrased from correction-service marketing copy.)

## 5. Common boilerplate phrases (re-used across sources)

These are essentially the *idioms* of IELTS feedback. They should be used sparingly (any one of them once or twice per essay):

- "addresses all parts of the task"
- "presents a clear position throughout the response"
- "logically organises information and ideas"
- "uses cohesive devices but sometimes mechanical"
- "an adequate range of vocabulary for the task"
- "attempts complex sentences but with frequent errors"
- "ideas are inadequately developed"
- "errors rarely reduce communication"
- "the writer's position is relevant to task prompt"
- "the linking words and phrases are used in a correct way"
- "to push to Band 7"
- "the 100% rule" (only use words you are 100% sure of)
- "develop ALL of your main ideas"

## 6. Tone-mode switches (if your LLM offers them)

| Mode | Description | Source pattern |
|------|-------------|----------------|
| **Encouraging** | All praise framed warmly, weaknesses softened, action items framed as opportunities. | "Your X is good, and there's a quick win here in Y." |
| **Neutral / examiner** | Default. Public-descriptor register. Strengths first then weaknesses then actions. | IELTS Advantage examiner report. |
| **Tough love** | Directly states what is wrong; minimal warm-up. Useful for advanced students. | IELTS Podcast "Roast me" mode. |
| **Diagnostic** | Numeric/quantitative; counts errors per category; minimal prose. | Writing9 raw rubric. |

The default for a band-6→7 student should be **Neutral / examiner** — students at this level need *specific* feedback, not pep talks.

---

## 7. Anti-patterns to avoid

- **Generic praise**: "Good essay, keep going!" — examiners never write this.
- **Generic critique**: "Work on your grammar." — provide specific examples.
- **Mood-driven feedback**: tone shouldn't shift mid-essay.
- **Score before reading**: the score must follow analysis, not precede it as a verdict.
- **Stacking weaknesses**: never list more than 5 weaknesses; the student cannot fix 10 things.
- **No pronoun shifts**: stick with "you / your essay" throughout (or "the candidate" throughout for training material).
- **No comparison to other students**: examiners assess against descriptors, not peers.
- **No future-tense doom**: never say "you will not pass" — instead, "to reach Band 7 you need to…"
