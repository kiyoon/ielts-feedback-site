# How experienced tutors phrase speaking feedback (voice & structure for the LLM judge)

_Crawled by codex on 2026-05-19T07:29:21Z. Sources cited inline._

## Summary

This topic captures how IELTS Speaking feedback is usually packaged by official bodies, examiner-style sample reports, and regional prep providers: a score or level estimate, a concise overall judgement, criterion-by-criterion strengths and weaknesses, concrete examples from the answer, and next-step actions (https://oneielts.com/ielts-speaking/evaluation-sample; https://www.edmclass.com/ielts/product/13). For an LLM judge, the practical goal is to sound like a trained speaking teacher: specific, evidence-led, aligned to Fluency and Coherence, Lexical Resource, Grammatical Range and Accuracy, and Pronunciation, and careful not to over-score non-linguistic factors such as confidence, eye contact, or interesting ideas (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking; https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-fluency-coherence). Strong feedback should explain what held the score down, what already works, and what would move the candidate toward the next band, while avoiding vague AI-style advice such as "use better vocabulary" without examples (https://oneielts.com/ielts-speaking/evaluation-sample; https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).

## Authoritative material

### Official scoring frame

- IELTS states that certificated examiners assess the whole Speaking performance across four criteria: Fluency and Coherence, Lexical Resource, Grammatical Range and Accuracy, and Pronunciation (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking).
- IELTS explains Fluency and Coherence as speaking at normal speed without too much hesitation, ordering ideas logically, and using cohesive devices such as linking words, pronouns and conjunctions so the answer is not hard to follow (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking).
- IELTS explains Lexical Resource as range, accuracy and appropriacy of vocabulary, plus the ability to paraphrase around vocabulary gaps (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking).
- IELTS explains Grammatical Range and Accuracy as the range and accuracy of grammar used, so feedback should mention both variety and control rather than only counting errors (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking).
- IELTS explains Pronunciation as being understood without too much effort, so feedback should focus on intelligibility, stress, intonation, rhythm and sound clarity rather than whether the candidate has a British, American, or local accent (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking; https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-does-ielts-speaking-assess-your-accent).
- The official public band descriptors define next-band language that tutors often reuse in feedback: Band 7 Fluency can include some hesitation/repetition/self-correction if coherence is not affected, while Band 6 may lose coherence at times because of hesitation, repetition or self-correction (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).
- The official public band descriptors give useful feedback wording for vocabulary: Band 8 uses a wide resource flexibly and precise meaning, Band 7 has some less common and idiomatic items with occasional inappropriacies, Band 6 can discuss topics at length but vocabulary may be inappropriate while meaning remains clear, and Band 5 is sufficient but limited in flexibility (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).
- The official public band descriptors give useful feedback wording for grammar: Band 8 has a wide range used flexibly with a majority of error-free sentences, Band 7 uses a range flexibly with frequent error-free sentences, Band 6 mixes short and complex sentence forms with frequent errors in complex structures that rarely block communication, and Band 5 controls basic forms but complex attempts nearly always contain errors (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).
- The official public band descriptors give useful feedback wording for pronunciation: Band 8 can be easily understood throughout with flexible stress and intonation across long utterances, Band 6 is generally understood without much effort but rhythm, stress-timing, intonation or word/phoneme errors may be variable, and Band 4 includes frequent mispronunciation causing lack of clarity (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).

### What official teaching guidance implies for feedback voice

- British Council teacher guidance says Speaking is assessed on language criteria and not on appearance, body language or creativity of ideas, so feedback should not tell a candidate that a boring opinion, weak eye contact or unoriginal content caused a lower score unless it directly reduced language evidence (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-fluency-coherence).
- British Council teacher guidance warns against memorising large chunks before the test, so feedback should avoid pushing candidates toward rehearsed scripts and instead recommend flexible answer development, signposting, reasons and examples (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-fluency-coherence).
- British Council describes Fluency and Coherence as speaking at length, organising ideas logically, using signposting, and expressing/justifying/discussing opinions without long pauses or repetition; this supports feedback that says what the candidate did in Part 1, Part 2 and Part 3 rather than a single generic fluency label (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-fluency-coherence).
- Cambridge English's IELTS test-format page repeats the same four criteria and definitions, which supports using the official labels exactly rather than renaming criteria as "confidence", "ideas", "delivery" or "speaking skill" (https://www.cambridgeenglish.org/exams-and-tests/ielts/test-format/).
- Cambridge teacher material gives example comments mapped to criteria, such as comments about range of structures for grammar, appropriate vocabulary range for lexical resource, stress and intonation for pronunciation, and little hesitation plus linked ideas for fluency/coherence; this is a compact model for evidence-tagged feedback (https://assets.cambridgeenglish.org/webinars/3y06-ielts-speaking-002.pdf).
- Cambridge ELT guidance on pronunciation says the band descriptors assess range of pronunciation features, control of those features, and intelligibility, so pronunciation feedback should name the feature and its communication effect, not simply say "pronunciation needs work" (https://www.cambridge.org/elt/blog/2019/03/27/ielts-speaking-improving-pronunication/).
- IELTS.org's 2026 automarking article says automated speaking assessment is harder than writing because learner speech can be heavily accented and interaction/nuance matter, so an LLM judge should be explicit about evidence limits, especially if it sees only a transcript and not audio (https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/what-automarking-means-for-language-test-validity-and-integrity).
- IELTS.org's 2026 conversational-AI article says AI examiners may handle clarification and dialogue but can be difficult to standardise and may "help" too much, so feedback generated by an LLM should avoid coaching during assessment and should keep the scoring rationale separate from improvement advice (https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/exploring-the-potential-of-conversational-ai-in-speaking-assessment).

### Examiner-style sample feedback patterns

- OneIELTS publishes a public speaking evaluation sample with the structure `overall score`, short examiner feedback, `To aim higher` action bullets, then a per-criterion score report for Fluency and Coherence, Lexical Resource, Grammatical Range and Accuracy, and Pronunciation (https://oneielts.com/ielts-speaking/evaluation-sample).
- The OneIELTS sample phrases the overall judgement in balanced teacher voice: it first notes that the candidate can keep the conversation going and make answers clear, then says vocabulary repetition and grammar slips reduce accuracy, and then gives a short improvement list (https://oneielts.com/ielts-speaking/evaluation-sample).
- The OneIELTS sample uses a repeatable section pattern per criterion: `Strengths`, `Mistakes/Issues`, and `Improvements`; this is useful for an LLM judge because it forces evidence and advice to stay attached to the correct criterion (https://oneielts.com/ielts-speaking/evaluation-sample).
- The OneIELTS sample cites concrete answer evidence, including repeated wording, limited connectors, a subject-verb agreement error, an article error, and a pronunciation confusion between similar words; this is the expected specificity level for non-generic feedback (https://oneielts.com/ielts-speaking/evaluation-sample).
- IELTS-Blog's Band 7 sample feedback gives separate criterion scores and explains them with compact evidence: the candidate was fluent and coherent, answered capably, did not often use longer developed sentences, had good vocabulary access, made some grammar/collocation errors, and had a slight accent that did not affect communication (https://www.ielts-blog.com/ielts-preparation-tips/speaking-tips/ielts-speaking-band-7-full-test-with-examiners-feedback-aleks/).
- IELTS-Blog's sample illustrates a useful phrasing pattern: "X was good, but Y limited the score"; this helps the judge give credit and constraint together instead of writing either praise-only or error-only feedback (https://www.ielts-blog.com/ielts-preparation-tips/speaking-tips/ielts-speaking-band-7-full-test-with-examiners-feedback-aleks/).
- IELTS Buddy's speaking correction page says candidates submit an audio Part 2-style response plus transcript and receive analysis based on the official criteria, which supports using transcript examples plus audio-dependent pronunciation notes where available (https://www.ieltsbuddy.com/ielts-speaking-correction-service.html).
- IELTS Buddy's sample feedback page describes a major weakness as Fluency and Coherence and gives category comments for grammar, vocabulary and pronunciation; this is another public example of criterion-labelled feedback rather than a single overall paragraph (https://www.ieltsbuddy.com/support-files/ielts-speaking-correction-service-sample.pdf).
- IELTS-Academic's public feedback sheet uses checklist-style prompts under Fluency and Coherence, Lexical Resource, Grammar and Pronunciation, showing that many teachers operationalise feedback as observable yes/no or weak/strong items rather than abstract judgement (https://ielts-academic.com/2016/01/01/ielts-speaking-feedback-sheet/).

## Country-specific prep material

### Korea

- Korean provider edm IELTS sells a Writing/Speaking correction product that promises a score using the same assessment criteria as the real test, then teacher-written feedback; its speaking sample layout lists Fluency, Lexical Resources, Grammatical Range and Accuracy, Pronunciation and Band Score, which mirrors the official criterion breakdown (https://www.edmclass.com/ielts/product/13).
- edm distinguishes `Basic` correction, where the teacher writes feedback on a submitted speaking or writing task, from `Premium` correction, where a personalised video feedback lecture is provided; this shows a common Korean cram-school feedback model of score first, then direct correction, then personalised strategy (https://www.edmclass.com/ielts/product/13).
- edm states that Basic speaking feedback includes typed feedback documents for recorded answers and correction/evaluation for grammar, expression and pronunciation, so an LLM judge should produce written evidence comments rather than only a numerical band (https://www.edmclass.com/ielts/product/13).
- Korean online school Skybel says IELTS Speaking lessons use real-test-like practice, immediate oral/typed correction, band-score-based level feedback, and a post-class report, which suggests the Korean tutor voice often combines live correction, answer-structure coaching and concise after-class summaries (https://www.skybel.co.kr/sub/edpro_02.php).
- Skybel says its classes focus on answer structure, grammar, vocabulary and fluency in a balanced way, and student testimonials mention predicted scores plus lists of strengths and weaknesses, so Korean feedback commonly tells the student both "what was good" and "what was lacking" rather than only giving corrections (https://www.skybel.co.kr/sub/edpro_02.php).

### China

- IDP China hosts a Chinese public IELTS Speaking band descriptor PDF, so Chinese prep feedback should still map candidate performance to the same global four criteria rather than local-only categories (https://www.idp.cn/special/subject/IELTS/pdf/kscj_f2.pdf).
- New Channel Wuhan explains that the four Speaking dimensions are equally weighted and warns against blind practice without understanding what examiners score; a Chinese tutor-style feedback note should therefore identify which criterion the weakness belongs to before giving drills (https://wh.xhd.cn/info/ieltsSpeak/1059815.html).
- New Channel Wuhan emphasises balance across criteria and says candidates should not rely on one strong dimension to compensate for weak dimensions, which supports feedback that avoids saying "good vocabulary means overall high score" when grammar or pronunciation evidence is weak (https://wh.xhd.cn/info/ieltsSpeak/1059815.html).
- Gaodun's Chinese IELTS guide describes Band 7-style speaking as natural expression with occasional pauses that do not affect understanding, and it recommends connecting words plus supporting detail; this supports feedback that separates acceptable thinking pauses from answer-development gaps (https://www.gaodun.com/ielts/1629843.html).
- Talk915 reproduces lower-band descriptors in Chinese, including reliance on repetition/self-correction/slow speech and overuse of discourse markers at Band 5, so Chinese feedback often names repetition, slow delivery and marker overuse as fluency problems rather than simply saying "not fluent" (https://www.talk915.com/web/article-details/328).
- New Oriental Online explains pronunciation feedback in terms of clarity and natural intonation rather than requiring British or American accent, which aligns with IDP's global accent guidance and should guide Chinese-language feedback summaries (https://ielts.koolearn.com/20250710/866309.html; https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-does-ielts-speaking-assess-your-accent).

### Vietnam

- ZIM's Vietnamese guide explains that IELTS Speaking has four criteria and that the first article in its scoring series focuses on Fluency and Coherence plus Pronunciation; this is an example of local prep splitting feedback into official criterion modules for teachability (https://zim.vn/tieu-chi-cham-diem-ielts-speaking-phan-1).
- ZIM explains Fluency and Coherence as speaking with normal continuity, rate and effort while linking ideas into coherent connected speech, so Vietnamese tutor feedback should talk about continuity, idea linkage and effort rather than only "smoothness" (https://zim.vn/tieu-chi-cham-diem-ielts-speaking-phan-1).
- ZIM says pronunciation feedback should pay attention to loud/clear articulation, word stress, sentence stress and final sounds where they distinguish meaning in context; this supports concrete notes such as "final consonants reduce clarity" rather than generic "work on pronunciation" (https://zim.vn/tieu-chi-cham-diem-ielts-speaking-phan-1).
- ZIM's Speaking checklist for Band 7+ is based on IELTS criteria and public descriptors, which reflects a Vietnamese prep habit of turning criterion descriptors into checklists that learners can self-monitor (https://zim.vn/checklist-bai-thi-ielts-speaking).
- ZIM's vocabulary preparation page lists flexible use across topics, idiomatic/less common expressions, collocation and paraphrase as Lexical Resource targets, so feedback should identify whether vocabulary was inaccurate, repetitive, non-collocational or insufficiently flexible (https://zim.vn/phuong-phap-chuan-bi-tu-vung-cho-ielts-speaking).
- IELTS LangGo warns that Part 2 can become unfocused and hard to follow even when grammar and vocabulary are good, which supports feedback that says "language is accurate, but topic development was difficult to follow" when coherence is the limiting factor (https://www.reddit.com/r/u_LanggoIELTS/comments/uxcmo6).

### Iran / Persian-language prep

- Andishe Parsian's Persian IELTS article explains Speaking scoring through the same four areas: fluency/coherence, vocabulary range, grammatical accuracy/range and pronunciation, and warns that vocabulary or grammar alone cannot produce a high speaking score (https://www.andisheparsian.com/article/171/index.html).
- IELTS2's Persian band-descriptor guide frames feedback around the four criteria and recommends complex/compound sentences for grammar range, but it keeps the scoring labels tied to official terms such as Fluency and Coherence, Lexical Resource, Grammatical Range and Accuracy, and Pronunciation (https://ielts2.com/understanding-the-ielts-speaking-band-descriptors/).
- Hafez Education's Persian speaking tips says each of the four criteria is worth 25% and uses an example criterion-score calculation, which supports feedback that gives separate criterion bands and explains how a weak criterion pulls down the final speaking band (https://www.hafezedu.com/ielts-speaking-tricks/).
- Hafez Education presents discourse markers as tools for connecting and organising speech, so Persian tutor-style feedback may say a candidate needs clearer signposting rather than simply "more fluency" (https://www.hafezedu.com/ielts-speaking-tricks/).
- LELB Society offers recorded IELTS interview samples and correction by an online English teaching specialist; this supports a feedback format that pairs the candidate's recorded performance with post-hoc correction and examiner-style comments (https://lelb.net/ielts-interview-sample-correction/).

### India and Pakistan

- UpGrad India explains that IELTS Speaking receives a score for each of four criteria and that scores are averaged, so Indian prep feedback commonly reports a criterion table rather than only one band (https://www.upgrad.com/study-abroad/exam/ielts/speaking-band-score/).
- UpGrad India explains Speaking criteria as smooth/logical speaking, vocabulary range and accuracy, varied/correct structures and clear stress/intonation, which provides simple learner-facing wording for criterion labels (https://www.upgrad.com/study-abroad/exam/ielts/speaking/).
- LeapScholar India says Fluency and Coherence covers smooth speech, limited pauses/hesitations and logical flow, while Lexical Resource covers vocabulary range and expression of abstract ideas; this supports feedback that names both delivery and idea organisation inside FC (https://leapscholar.com/exams/ielts/speaking-band-score).
- LeapScholar's Band 6/7/8 comparison describes Band 6 as effortful with frequent hesitation/repetition, Band 7 as generally organised with some hesitation, and Band 8 as using a wide range with minor issues; this supports next-band feedback such as "to move from 6 toward 7, reduce repeated hesitation and organise ideas more clearly" (https://leapscholar.com/exams/ielts/speaking-band-score).
- British Council Pakistan hosts an IELTS Speaking Fluency and Coherence preparation video page, so Pakistan-facing feedback should still use the same British Council/IELTS criteria and not local informal categories such as "confidence marks" (https://www.britishcouncil.pk/exam/ielts/prepare/videos/fluency-coherence).
- Preply's India IELTS tutor profile describes practical, results-oriented speaking lessons with structured answers, fluency feedback and step-by-step guidance, which reflects a common South Asian tutoring emphasis on clear answer structure plus confidence-building, but scoring still needs official criterion evidence (https://preply.com/en/tutor/7668487; https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).

### Indonesia

- IDP Indonesia explains that Speaking tests the ability to communicate opinions and information on everyday topics, speak at length on a topic, and organise ideas coherently; Indonesian feedback should therefore mention whether the response met the part-specific communicative demand as well as the four criteria (https://ielts.idp.com/indonesia/prepare/article-ielts-speaking-tested-skills/en-gb).
- IDP Indonesia's local page lists Fluency and Coherence, Lexical Resource, Grammatical Range and Accuracy and Pronunciation as the tested skills, which supports using the official labels in Indonesian-market feedback (https://ielts.idp.com/indonesia/prepare/article-ielts-speaking-tested-skills/en-gb).
- An Indonesian university paper on IELTS mock speaking errors uses the same four assessment areas and keyword labels, which suggests Indonesian academic-prep feedback also frames speaking weaknesses by criterion rather than by general "English ability" (https://icels.fkip.uniska-kediri.ac.id/index.php/icels/article/download/163/30/474).
- IDP's global pronunciation article is especially useful for Indonesian candidates because it separates accent from pronunciation and says examiners listen for clarity, intelligibility, natural intonation/rhythm and word/sentence stress; feedback should not tell Indonesian speakers to remove an Indonesian accent unless the accent reduces intelligibility (https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-does-ielts-speaking-assess-your-accent).

### Russia and Kazakhstan

- IELTS-up, a Russian-language-oriented prep site in English, presents feedback-friendly definitions: FC is how clear and structured the speech is, LR is vocabulary, Pronunciation is how natural/easy the speech sounds, and GRA is grammar control; it also recommends linking ideas, extending answers, speaking smoothly and using pausing correctly (https://www.ielts-up.com/speaking/ielts-speaking-marking.html).
- IELTS Score 7's Russian guide explains that the final Speaking score is the average of four separate criteria, and it describes Fluency and Coherence as how smoothly the candidate speaks and how logically ideas are connected (https://ieltsscore7.com/ru/guides/ielts-speaking-scoring-criteria/).
- MyIELTS.kz advertises a Kazakhstan IELTS Speaking intensive with Part 3 practice, answer analysis, improvement advice and examiner-expectation training, which shows a local course structure of simulated response, analysis, and targeted improvement (https://myielts.kz/product/intensive-ielts-speaking).
- Russian and Kazakh materials found in this crawl translate or reuse the same official descriptor concepts, so the judge should not apply a separate regional standard for Russian/Kazakh speakers; it should use local materials only to enrich examples and phrasing (https://ieltsscore7.com/ru/guides/ielts-speaking-scoring-criteria/; https://myielts.kz/product/intensive-ielts-speaking).

### UK, Australian and Canadian native-perspective material

- British Council UK teacher guidance provides the clearest native-perspective boundary for feedback: assess only the four language criteria, discourage memorised chunks, and teach answer development through signposting, reasons, examples and discussion (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-fluency-coherence).
- IELTS Advantage's UK accent article says accent does not matter as long as pronunciation is clear and accurate, so feedback should say "clarity was reduced by word stress/final sounds" rather than "your accent is too strong" (https://www.ieltsadvantage.com/2019/06/10/ielts-speaking-accent/).
- IELTS-Blog's examiner-feedback sample gives an Australian-market example of concise examiner voice: score each criterion, then explain the score with specific strengths and limiting errors, including the statement that a slight accent did not affect communication (https://www.ielts-blog.com/ielts-preparation-tips/speaking-tips/ielts-speaking-band-7-full-test-with-examiners-feedback-aleks/).
- IDP, co-owner of IELTS and headquartered in Australia, says Speaking is marked on fluency/coherence, lexical resource, grammatical range/accuracy and pronunciation, and explains accent is not graded if clarity remains high; this supports feedback that prioritises intelligibility over accent imitation (https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-does-ielts-speaking-assess-your-accent).
- IELTS Vancouver's Canadian prep article says Fluency and Coherence is one of the four marking criteria and that speaking clarity is linked with pronunciation features such as individual sounds, word stress, sentence stress, connected speech and intonation; this supports Canadian-style feedback that connects fluency and pronunciation to listener effort (https://ieltsvancouver.com/2023/01/20/what-is-fluency-and-coherence-in-ielts-speaking/).

## Open-source tools and code

- SpeechSuper API Samples is a public GitHub repository under MIT license, with samples for a speech/pronunciation assessment API; its README says scripted assessment can return phoneme, syllable, word and sentence scores, fluency metrics, words-correct-per-minute, pause count and IELTS/PTE score estimates, and unscripted assessment can return overall, pronunciation, fluency, grammar, vocabulary and transcription outputs (https://github.com/speechsuper/SpeechSuper-API-Samples).
- SpeechSuper's repository page showed 22 commits, 63 stars, 8 forks, MIT license and no published releases in the crawled GitHub text; the crawled page did not expose a reliable last-updated date, so do not record one as fact (https://github.com/speechsuper/SpeechSuper-API-Samples).
- Speechat is a GitHub Pages project for spoken language assessment that extracts prosody features, uses ASR transcripts, and sends text to a language model for pragmatic/sentiment/mechanical analysis; it links to `my-voice-analysis` and `myprosody` repositories, but the crawl did not verify their license or update dates (https://shahabks.github.io/Speechat/).
- Speechat's page says its approach focuses on prosody, stress, intonation and structural speech features and uses expert-human graded audio in its broader project description, so it may inspire pronunciation/fluency feature extraction but should not be treated as a validated IELTS scorer (https://shahabks.github.io/Speechat/; https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/what-automarking-means-for-language-test-validity-and-integrity).
- Speechace's commercial Fluency API says it estimates IELTS/PTE fluency and pronunciation scores and returns segment-level scores and metrics, but it is an API rather than open-source code; it is relevant as a reference for feedback granularity such as segment-by-segment weakness reporting (https://www.speechace.com/hello-fluency-api/).
- IELTS.org warns that automarking must be at least as accurate as trained human examiners and that speaking is especially complex because of accent, interaction and nuance, so any open-source or API-derived features should be used as support evidence for an LLM judge, not as a replacement for descriptor-based reasoning (https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/what-automarking-means-for-language-test-validity-and-integrity).

## Practical signals for an LLM judge

- Use this default feedback shape: `Estimated band`, `Overall comment`, then four criterion sections with `strength`, `limiting evidence`, and `next action`, because OneIELTS, edm and UpGrad all model score-plus-criteria reporting and IELTS officially scores those four criteria (https://oneielts.com/ielts-speaking/evaluation-sample; https://www.edmclass.com/ielts/product/13; https://www.upgrad.com/study-abroad/exam/ielts/speaking-band-score/).
- Phrase strengths and weaknesses together: "You can keep the conversation going, but repetition and grammar slips limit the score" is closer to examiner/tutor feedback than "Good fluency, bad grammar", because public sample reports explicitly balance what works with what holds the band down (https://oneielts.com/ielts-speaking/evaluation-sample; https://www.ielts-blog.com/ielts-preparation-tips/speaking-tips/ielts-speaking-band-7-full-test-with-examiners-feedback-aleks/).
- Always attach feedback to transcript evidence when available: mention the repeated phrase, incorrect collocation, subject-verb agreement error, tense slip, overused connector, vague word choice, or unclear word contrast, following OneIELTS's sample pattern of issue plus example plus replacement (https://oneielts.com/ielts-speaking/evaluation-sample).
- Do not use non-criterion labels as scores: avoid "confidence 7", "ideas 8" or "body language weak" because IELTS and Cambridge list only Fluency and Coherence, Lexical Resource, Grammatical Range and Accuracy, and Pronunciation for Speaking marking (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking; https://www.cambridgeenglish.org/exams-and-tests/ielts/test-format/).
- Separate assessment from coaching: first justify the band from the performance, then give next steps, because IELTS.org warns AI assessment can distort standardisation if it helps too much during the assessment interaction (https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/exploring-the-potential-of-conversational-ai-in-speaking-assessment).
- Use next-band language from the public descriptors: for a Band 6-like answer, say what would move it toward Band 7, such as longer turns without noticeable effort, fewer coherence losses from hesitation, more flexible discourse markers, more frequent error-free sentences, or more controlled pronunciation features (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).
- For Lexical Resource, prefer precise comments such as "vocabulary was topic-related but repetitive", "collocations were inaccurate", "paraphrase worked", or "less common items were attempted but not always natural"; these map directly to IELTS lexical descriptors and OneIELTS sample comments (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf; https://oneielts.com/ielts-speaking/evaluation-sample).
- For Grammar, mention both range and accuracy: distinguish a candidate who uses mostly simple clauses accurately from one who attempts complex clauses but loses control, because official descriptors score variety and error impact together (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).
- For Pronunciation, never write "change your accent"; write "the examiner can follow you easily" or "word stress/final consonants/mumbling increase listener effort", because IDP and IELTS Advantage both state accent itself is not the scored issue when intelligibility is preserved (https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-does-ielts-speaking-assess-your-accent; https://www.ieltsadvantage.com/2019/06/10/ielts-speaking-accent/).
- If the judge only has a diarized transcript and no audio, state pronunciation evidence as limited unless the transcript includes phonetic notes, ASR uncertainty, repeated clarification, or explicit mishearing; IELTS.org says speaking automarking is complicated by speech and interaction, and pronunciation is officially about comprehensible speech (https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/what-automarking-means-for-language-test-validity-and-integrity; https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking).
- Avoid advice overload: give two or three priority actions tied to the limiting criteria, following OneIELTS's `To aim higher` pattern and edm/Skybel's targeted correction model rather than a long undifferentiated list (https://oneielts.com/ielts-speaking/evaluation-sample; https://www.edmclass.com/ielts/product/13; https://www.skybel.co.kr/sub/edpro_02.php).
- Do not reward memorised or ornate language for its own sake; British Council warns against memorised chunks and IELTS descriptors reward appropriate, flexible and precise language, so feedback should prefer natural collocation and relevance over rehearsed "band 9 phrases" (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-fluency-coherence; https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).
- Make feedback part-aware when possible: Part 1 may need fuller answers without becoming speeches, Part 2 needs sustained long-turn development, and Part 3 needs more abstract discussion, because British Council guidance separates the speaking parts and the skills they elicit (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-fluency-coherence).
- Use culturally neutral scoring: Korean, Chinese, Vietnamese, Persian, Indian, Pakistani, Indonesian, Russian, Kazakh, UK, Australian and Canadian sources in this crawl all refer back to the same official four criteria, so regional material should influence examples and phrasing but not create different standards (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking; https://www.idp.cn/special/subject/IELTS/pdf/kscj_f2.pdf; https://zim.vn/tieu-chi-cham-diem-ielts-speaking-phan-1; https://www.upgrad.com/study-abroad/exam/ielts/speaking-band-score/).
- End each criterion section with a concrete fix the candidate can apply in the next practice answer, such as "replace repeated `beautiful` with topic-specific adjectives", "combine two short clauses with because/which/although", or "record and check rose/rows contrasts", because public sample reports show replacements and drills rather than only diagnosis (https://oneielts.com/ielts-speaking/evaluation-sample).
