# Speaking-specific lexical resource and grammar — idiomatic chunks, collocations, paraphrase banks, fillers to drop

_Merged from codex + claude crawls on 2026-05-19T07:35Z._

## Summary

In IELTS Speaking, Lexical Resource (LR) and Grammatical Range & Accuracy (GRA) each weight 25% of the overall band, and from Band 7 upward the public descriptors explicitly require **less common and idiomatic vocabulary**, **collocation awareness**, **effective paraphrase**, and **a range of complex structures used with some flexibility** ([IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf); [IELTS.org speaking format](https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking); [IELTS Speaking Key Assessment Criteria PDF](https://ielts.org/cdn/Guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf); [Cambridge webinar PDF](https://assets.cambridgeenglish.org/webinars/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf)). The scoring distinction is not "advanced versus simple words" but whether the candidate expresses precise meaning across topics, uses style/collocation appropriately, paraphrases around lexical gaps, and produces flexible structures without errors that damage intelligibility ([IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf); [IELTS Speaking Key Assessment Criteria PDF](https://ielts.org/cdn/Guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf)). Fillers and self-correction are not automatically negative: official criteria allow appropriate discourse markers and content-planning hesitation, but penalise word-search pauses, false starts, backtracking, and overused markers when they reveal missing lexis/grammar or interrupt continuity ([IELTS Speaking Key Assessment Criteria PDF](https://ielts.org/cdn/Guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf)). This file maps the descriptors onto operational signals: which chunks examiners reward, which "memorised idiom" patterns trigger penalties, which complex-grammar moves (cleft, inversion, modal hedging, conditionals) yield Band 8+ signals, which fillers are acceptable as natural thinking-time, and the topical paraphrase banks Band 7 candidates from Korean, Chinese, Vietnamese, Iranian, Pakistani, Indian, Indonesian, Russian/Kazakh, and Anglophone cram material rely on. The judge should use these to distinguish genuine Band 7 from "memorised Band 5 with idiom decoration," a common failure pattern reported across regional academies.

## Authoritative material

### Official scoring anchors

- IELTS Speaking is marked by certificated examiners across four equally-weighted criteria — Fluency & Coherence, Lexical Resource, Grammatical Range & Accuracy, and Pronunciation — over an 11-14 minute three-part test, and a candidate is rated on **average performance across the whole test** rather than a single part ([IELTS.org speaking format](https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking); [IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf)).
- Lexical Resource is defined as range of vocabulary, topic coverage, precision of referential meaning, expression of attitude, appropriate style, **collocation including idiomatic expressions**, and the ability to paraphrase around a vocabulary gap ([IELTS Speaking Key Assessment Criteria PDF](https://ielts.org/cdn/Guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf); [IELTS.org speaking format](https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking)).
- Grammatical Range & Accuracy covers spoken-sentence length, subordinate clauses, verb-phrase complexity (auxiliaries, continuous/perfect aspect, modality, passive voice), phrase complexity through pre- and post-modification, and sentence patterns that move elements for information focus; accuracy is judged by **error density and communicative effect**, not by counting every slip equally ([IELTS Speaking Key Assessment Criteria PDF](https://ielts.org/cdn/Guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf)).
- The Fluency & Coherence descriptors are relevant to fillers and self-correction: Band 9 hesitation is content preparation (not word/grammar search); Band 8 has only occasional language-search hesitation; Band 7 may show some language-related hesitation/repetition/self-correction without harming coherence; Band 6 may lose coherence through hesitation/repetition/self-correction; Band 5 relies on repetition, self-correction, slow speech, and overused markers to keep going ([IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf); [IELTS Speaking Key Assessment Criteria PDF](https://ielts.org/cdn/Guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf)).

### Official band descriptors — Lexical Resource (Speaking)

Reproduced from the public IELTS Speaking band descriptor PDFs hosted by IELTS.org and British Council ([IELTS.org descriptors PDF](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf); [British Council descriptors PDF](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf); [Cambridge webinar PDF](https://assets.cambridgeenglish.org/webinars/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf)):

- **Band 9** — uses vocabulary with full flexibility and precision in all topics; uses idiomatic language naturally and accurately.
- **Band 8** — uses a wide vocabulary resource readily and flexibly to convey precise meaning; uses less common and idiomatic vocabulary skilfully, with occasional inaccuracies; uses paraphrase effectively as required.
- **Band 7** — uses vocabulary resource flexibly to discuss a variety of topics; uses some less common and idiomatic vocabulary and shows some awareness of style and collocation, with some inappropriate choices; uses paraphrase effectively.
- **Band 6** — has a wide enough vocabulary to discuss topics at length and make meaning clear in spite of inappropriacies; generally paraphrases successfully.
- **Band 5** — manages to talk about familiar and unfamiliar topics but uses vocabulary with limited flexibility; attempts to use paraphrase but with mixed success.
- **Band 4** — relies on mainly familiar-topic vocabulary, with frequent wrong word choice and rare paraphrase.

### Official band descriptors — Grammatical Range & Accuracy (Speaking)

- **Band 9** — uses a full range of structures naturally and appropriately; produces consistently accurate structures apart from "slips" characteristic of native speaker speech.
- **Band 8** — uses a wide range of structures flexibly; produces a majority of error-free sentences with only very occasional inappropriacies or basic/non-systematic errors.
- **Band 7** — uses a range of complex structures with some flexibility; frequently produces error-free sentences, though some grammatical mistakes persist.
- **Band 6** — uses a mix of simple and complex structures, but with limited flexibility; may make frequent mistakes with complex structures though these rarely cause comprehension problems.
- **Band 5** — produces basic sentence forms with reasonable accuracy; uses a limited range of more complex structures, but these usually contain errors.
- **Band 4** — rarely uses subordinate clauses and relies on short, repetitive structures with frequent errors.

(Sources: [IELTS.org descriptors PDF](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf); [British Council descriptors PDF](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf); [IELTS Tutors explainer](https://ieltstutors.org/speaking-band-descriptors/); [IDC aggregated PDF](https://idc.edu/IELTS-Speaking-Writing-Band-descriptors.pdf); [Love-to-Learn-English summary](https://lovetolearnenglish.com/tips-for-ielts-and-toefl/2024/9/12/detailed-summary-of-ielts-speaking-band-descriptors).)

### Idioms, collocations, chunks, and paraphrase — official guidance

The official IELTS.org article ([How to use idioms in the IELTS Speaking test](https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/how-to-use-idioms-in-the-ielts-speaking-test)) makes three operational claims:

1. Idiomatic language is rewarded **from Band 7 upward** — below 7, idioms are neither expected nor decisive.
2. Two specific failure modes lose marks: (a) topic mismatch (e.g., "raining cats and dogs" forced into a question about education), (b) over-used clichés that "sound forced." Examiners read forced idiom use as memorised script and downgrade LR for "inappropriate choices."
3. The rubric phrase is **"idiomatic language,"** not idioms. Phrasal verbs, fixed expressions, conventional metaphors, and natural collocations all count — a candidate with zero textbook idioms but rich phrasal verbs and collocation can still score Band 7 LR.

Other authoritative guidance:

- The British Council teacher resource on Lexical Resource highlights collocation, connotation, and word form as assessable features; canonical examples: "make a phone call" (not "do a phone call"); positive "laid back" vs negative "lazy" ([British Council LR video page](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-lexical-resource)).
- Cambridge ELT argues for teaching **chunks** — collocations, fixed expressions, sentence starters, multi-word items — because they support IELTS fluency and let a learner "buy thinking time" with a memorised but adaptable phrase ([Cambridge ELT chunks article](https://www.cambridge.org/elt/blog/2020/08/12/using-chunks-to-improve-your-ielts-students-band-scores/)).
- British Council's Part 1 LR/GRA classroom PDF frames upgrading as answer-replacement: substitute repeated/basic lexis ("children" → "toddler/kids"), use natural collocations ("spend time around"), use spoken expressions ("I'm really into," "loads of"), and vary grammar with present perfect continuous, "used to," and future forms ([British Council Part 1 LR/GRA PDF](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/speaking_part_1_grammatical_range_and_accuracy_lexical_resource.pdf)).
- The British Council grammar page says candidates need varied complex structures to show range, but IELTS does not expect 100% accuracy and self-correction is acceptable when a candidate notices an error; errors that block communication are more serious than passable slips ([British Council grammar page](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-grammar)).

### British Council — Top 20 IELTS Speaking idioms

The official British Council list ([Top English idioms](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/blog/top-english-idioms)) centres on safe, high-frequency, register-appropriate idioms: *break the ice, by the skin of your teeth, call it a day, a piece of cake, get the hang of, on top of the world, in the blink of an eye, once in a blue moon, cost an arm and a leg, hit the books, under the weather, miss the boat, the best of both worlds, blow off steam, cut corners, see eye to eye, speak of the devil, the ball is in your court, beat around the bush, let the cat out of the bag*. These are the "default" set examiners hear most often — using them is not penalised, but using only these gives no Band 8 lift either.

### IDP IELTS — topical idiom groupings

IDP groups idioms by topic and repeats the advice **"match the idiom to the topic; never force it"** ([IDP global idiom guide](https://ielts.idp.com/about/news-and-articles/article-use-idioms-ielts-speaking); [IDP Pakistan](https://ielts.idp.com/pakistan/about/news-and-articles/article-use-idioms-ielts-speaking); [IDP Iran best phrases](https://ielts.idp.com/iran/prepare/article-ielts-speaking-best-phrases); [IDP Nepal vocabulary](https://ielts.idp.com/nepal/about/news-and-articles/article-ielts-speaking-vocabulary)):

- Colour/emotion: *over the moon, in the red, green with envy, feeling blue, see red*
- Study/work: *pass with flying colours, drop out, hit the books, burn the midnight oil, learn the ropes*
- Family/childhood: *like a kid in a candy store, runs in the family, the apple of one's eye, like father like son*
- Health/COVID: *under the weather, on the mend, on its last legs, bounce back*
- IELTS-meta: *practice makes perfect, break a leg, take the plunge*

### Common high-frequency collocations expected at Band 7+

From the IDP Iran best-phrases article and the British Council phrasal-verbs list ([IDP Iran best phrases](https://ielts.idp.com/iran/prepare/article-ielts-speaking-best-phrases); [British Council phrasal verbs](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/blog/top-phrasal-verbs-for-IELTS)):

- **Verb + noun:** *take an exam* (NOT "make an exam"), *do homework, solve a problem, raise awareness, make a decision, reach a conclusion, foot the bill, pay attention, meet a deadline, take responsibility, lead a healthy lifestyle, cause concern*.
- **Adjective + noun:** *bright idea, strong criticism, heavy traffic, severe weather, fundamental difference, deep recession, vivid memory, key factor, hidden agenda*.
- **Adverb + adjective:** *highly successful, deeply concerned, widely available, completely different, perfectly natural, increasingly common*.
- **Phrasal verbs:** *bring up, look after, take after, hang out with, run out of, come up with, put up with, get along with, look forward to, deal with, end up*.

Candidates who pair a topic-specific collocation with a phrasal verb in the same Part 2 turn (e.g., *"I ended up taking responsibility for the project"*) consistently score Band 7+ for LR per ex-examiner analysis ([IELTS Podcast vocabulary](https://www.ieltspodcast.com/ielts-speaking/ielts-speaking-vocabulary/)).

### Complex grammar structures rewarded at Band 7-9

- **Conditionals (all three types + mixed)** — second conditional for hypothetical ("If I were…, I would…"), third conditional for past regret ("If I had known, I would have…"), mixed ("If I had studied harder, I'd be working there now"). Examiners specifically listen for at least one second or third conditional in Part 3 ([IELTS Podcast 10 Band 9 grammar features](https://www.ieltspodcast.com/audio-tutorials/10-ielts-band-9-grammar-features-3/); [IELTS.net grammatical structures](https://ielts.net/what-grammatical-structure-should-be-use-in-ielts/); [IELTS Buddy complex sentences](https://www.ieltsbuddy.com/complex-sentences.html); [New Oriental high-score structures](https://ielts.koolearn.com/20200109/835779.html)).
- **Cleft sentences** — *"It is X that…", "What I really enjoy is…", "The thing that matters most is…"*. Cited as a Band 9 grammar feature ([IELTS Podcast 10 Band 9 features](https://www.ieltspodcast.com/audio-tutorials/10-ielts-band-9-grammar-features-3/); [Keith Speaking Academy complex sentences](https://keithspeakingacademy.com/ielts-speaking-complex-sentences-grammar/)).
- **Inversion** — *"Not only did she write it, she also presented it", "Never have I felt so…", "Rarely do we see…"*. Optional even at Band 9; one well-placed inversion impresses, two or more in a 90-second turn usually reads as memorised ([IELTSMaterial emphatic structures](https://ieltsmaterial.com/advanced-grammar-for-ielts-emphatic-structures-and-inversion/); [IELTS Podcast advanced speaking](https://www.ieltspodcast.com/ielts-speaking/advanced-speaking/)).
- **Subjunctive / modal perfect** — *"He must have left already", "She should have known", "If I were in charge…"* ([IELTS Podcast 10 Band 9 features](https://www.ieltspodcast.com/audio-tutorials/10-ielts-band-9-grammar-features-3/)).
- **Relative clauses (defining and non-defining)** — *"my hometown, which is in the south", "a friend who works in tech"*. The single most common Band 6 → Band 7 lift per Keith Speaking Academy ([Keith Speaking Academy](https://keithspeakingacademy.com/ielts-speaking-complex-sentences-grammar/)); the New Oriental Chinese guide flags non-defining "which" clauses, indirect reported thought, and third-conditional counterfactuals as high-score targets ([New Oriental](https://ielts.koolearn.com/20200109/835779.html)).
- **Modal hedging** — *"It could be argued that…", "Many would suggest…", "It might depend on…", "There's a reasonable case for…", "I'd be inclined to say…", "Arguably…"* — supports Band 7-8 by avoiding over-generalisation and softening claims ([IELTS Mumbai hedging](https://ieltsmumbai.com/blog/ielts-vocabulary--grammar-using-hedging-language-appropriately-in-ielts); [TED IELTS hedging](https://ted-ielts.com/hedging-language/); [Astaachiever tentative language](https://www.astaachiever.com/mastering-the-art-of-tentative-language-in-ielts-writing-and-speaking)).
- **Ellipsis / substitution** — *"She asked if I wanted to go, but I didn't", "My brother loves it; so do I"*. Marker of native-like coherence at Band 9 ([IELTS Podcast 10 Band 9 features](https://www.ieltspodcast.com/audio-tutorials/10-ielts-band-9-grammar-features-3/)).
- **Layered complex sentences** — Keith's pattern: *"I live in Spain, which is a pretty big country, because the food is great, although I think they eat too much fish."* Three subordinators in one sentence is a fast Band 7 signal ([Keith Speaking Academy](https://keithspeakingacademy.com/ielts-speaking-complex-sentences-grammar/)).

### Self-correction, fillers, hesitation

Self-correction (sources: [IELTS Liz on self-correction](https://ieltsliz.com/ielts-speaking-self-correction/); [Practice9](https://practice9.co/self-correction-inielts-speaking/); [IFI Qatar](https://ifi.qa/self-correction-in-ielts-speaking-why-how/); [TED IELTS hesitation](https://ted-ielts.com/hesitation/)):

- **Correct only when meaning changes.** Liz: ignore minor grammar/lexical errors; correcting them costs more fluency marks than it gains in accuracy.
- **Method matters.** Don't word-for-word repeat. Use *"Sorry, I mean…"* or *"What I'm trying to say is…"* + paraphrase. Word-for-word repetition signals poor flexibility.
- **Cadence cap (Liz/practice-coach heuristic).** Liz and several coach sites recommend at most **one self-correction per 30-45 seconds**; more is treated as "noticeable hesitation." Official descriptors permit some self-correction at Band 7 but describe reliance on self-correction as Band 5 behaviour ([IELTS Speaking band descriptors](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf); [British Council grammar page](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-grammar)).
- **Non-English slips:** if you slip into L1, immediately define in English; turning a slip into a definition can demonstrate paraphrase ability and net positive for LR.

Acceptable thinking-time fillers (sources: [Quora — are fillers acceptable](https://www.quora.com/Are-fillers-like-umm-uh-etc-acceptable-in-IELTS-speaking-test); [Love-to-Learn-English](https://lovetolearnenglish.com/tips-for-ielts-and-toefl/2024/9/10/should-you-correct-yourself-or-keep-talking-in-the-ielts-speaking-exam); [SimplyIELTS 2026](https://simplyielts.com/ielts-speaking-common-mistakes-that-examiners-hate-2026/); [New Oriental scoring tips](https://ielts.koolearn.com/20211112/854112.html)):

- *"That's an interesting question."*
- *"I've never really thought about that before, but…"*
- *"Let me think for a second…"*
- *"That's a tough one — off the top of my head…"*
- *"Hmm, where do I start with this…"*
- *"I'd have to say…"*
- *"Now that you mention it…"*
- Natural transition markers such as *"I mean,"* *"well,"* *"to be honest,"* *"how shall I put it"* (New Oriental specifically recommends these in place of raw "erh"/"hmm" hesitation noises).

Penalised filler patterns: clusters of *"um, uh, er"* (>3 per turn); long English-L1-English code-switches; word-by-word L1-style hesitation ("how to say… how to say…"). Examiners treat one *um* as natural; three in a row triggers a Fluency drop.

## Country-specific prep material

### Korea — Hackers, ScoreFactory, TestGlider

Korean cram-school material treats Speaking 7.0 almost entirely as a **paraphrase + lexical upgrade** drill, distinct from grammar drilling which they treat as already-Band-6 plateau ([ScoreFactory 7.0 guide](https://scorefactory.io/ielts_board_1/1272750); [TestGlider band scoring guide](https://blog.testglider.com/%EC%95%84%EC%9D%B4%EC%97%98%EC%B8%A0-%EC%A0%90%EC%88%98-%EC%B1%84%EC%A0%90-%EB%B0%A9%EB%B2%95-%EA%B8%B0%EC%A4%80-%EC%B4%9D%EC%A0%95%EB%A6%AC/); [Hackers 6→7 guide](https://www.gohackers.com/?c=ielts/ielts_info2/ielts_method&type=url&uid=538610); [Hackers IELTS Speaking 6.5+](https://hackerseducation.com/publications/detail?BookID=38); [Hackers IELTS Speaking on YES24](https://www.yes24.com/Product/Goods/41050360); [Union IELTS Korean cram-school page](https://www.no1ielts.com/bbs/board.php?bo_table=1_1to4&page=1&wr_id=40)):

- The 6→7 jump is "유창성과 어휘" (fluency and vocabulary), not pronunciation or grammar — Korean candidates over-invest in grammar drilling and under-invest in lexical upgrades.
- The Hackers IELTS Speaking book is organised by Speaking part and by 20 topic units (job/study, accommodation, cities, leisure, art, fashion, sport, people, food/health, travel, weather/nature, events, daily life, experiences, education, communication, science/technology, media, history/tradition, society) and includes detail-adding expressions, Part 2 development phrases, Part 3 intro/conclusion phrases, "crisis expressions" for unexpected exam situations, common error points, and topic-specific ideas — supporting a judge-side expectation that strong LR is **topic-distributed**, not concentrated in a single memorised theme.
- Lexical upgrade list explicitly recommended: *famous → acclaimed / renowned / household name; happy → on cloud nine / over the moon / thrilled to bits; very good → second to none / top-notch / first-rate; very bad → abysmal / atrocious; lots of → a wealth of / a great deal of / a multitude of; important → pivotal / crucial / paramount; difficult → daunting / gruelling; people → individuals / the general public.*
- Hackers IELTS Speaking books target 6.5 and 5.5 explicitly — Band 7 candidates are coached to memorise **chunks**, not isolated words, and to deploy them only when contextually triggered (e.g., never produce "on cloud nine" in a Part 3 abstract question about economic policy).

### China — Zhihu, 新东方, 知乎 IELTS columns

Chinese forum and academy material ([Zhihu collocations](https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/609722578); [Zhihu Speaking + Writing collocations](https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/590612234); [New Oriental Speaking 7](https://ielts.xdf.cn/202304/13059517.html); [Zhihu phrasal verbs](https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/608561935); [Fanxuefei Speaking 7 vocab](http://www.fanxuefei.com/news/999902.htm); [Studytoura 30 phrases](https://www.studytoura.com/ielts-speaking-phrases/); [ZZYasi 4.5→7.5 differences](https://www.zzyasi.com/ielts-ielts/ielts-info/2024/0511/2728/); [New Oriental high-score structures](https://ielts.koolearn.com/20200109/835779.html); [New Oriental scoring tips](https://ielts.koolearn.com/20211112/854112.html); [IDP China bilingual descriptors PDF](https://www.idp.cn/special/subject/IELTS/pdf/kscj_f2.pdf)):

- 词汇搭配 (collocation) is the dominant lever for Band 7 — Zhihu commentators emphasise "verb+noun" collocations over isolated synonyms ("sustainable resources," "work out," "family member" are canonical).
- Phrasal verb drilling by topic: health (*work out, keep fit, cut down on*), study (*catch up on, fall behind, brush up on*), career (*end up, get ahead, take up*).
- 新东方 advice: at 6.0 candidates "describe" facts; at 7.0 candidates "evaluate" using hedged language — the lexical shift is from concrete nouns to abstract evaluations (*phenomenon, tendency, implication*). The scoring-focused tip page also warns that grammar accuracy beats showy complex structures that become tangled.
- A widely-circulated 30-phrase list on studytoura.com leans on connector chunks: *"It goes without saying that…", "Believe it or not…", "When all is said and done…", "More often than not…", "By and large…"* — Chinese Band 7 candidates over-use these openers; examiners notice the scripted Chinese pattern when 3+ appear in one turn.
- The IDP China public band-descriptor PDF mirrors the official English descriptors in Chinese and is useful for bilingual verification of Band 6 vs Band 7 LR cutoffs.

### Vietnam — IZONE, IDP Vietnam, MobiEdu, langgo, IELTS Fighter, Vietop, ECE, Smartcom

Vietnamese resources are among the most thorough for grouped idiom/collocation banks ([IZONE 120 idioms](https://www.izone.edu.vn/luyen-thi-ielts/idioms-for-ielts-speaking-band-7-ielts/); [IDP Vietnam idiom guide](https://ielts.idp.com/vietnam/about/news-and-articles/article-use-idioms-ielts-speaking); [MobiEdu](https://ielts.mobiedu.vn/news/phuong-phap-hoc/cach-dung-idioms-va-collocations-de-tang-band-speaking-ielts/344.html); [Langgo 90+ idioms](https://langgo.edu.vn/list-idioms-ielts-speaking-giup-tang-band-hieu-qua-nhat); [IELTS Fighter 120 idioms](https://ielts-fighter.com/tin-tuc/idioms-ielts-speaking_mt1508717634.html); [Vietop 8-week plan](https://vietop.edu.vn/blog/luyen-ielts-speaking-band-7-trong-8-tuan/); [Jolo collocation vs idiom](https://jolo.edu.vn/vi/thu-vien/bi-quyet-ielts/collocation-vs-idiom-dung-the-nao-cho-dung-trong-ielts-writing.html); [Smartcom 7.0 vocab](https://smartcom.vn/blog/tong-hop-tu-vung-ielts-7-0-speaking-hay-nhat/); [ZIM collocations](https://zim.vn/collocation-thong-dung); [IELTS Complete Part 2 collocations](https://ieltscomplete.com/20-collocations-ielts-xin-cho-speaking-part-2/); [ECE Vietnam high-score phrases](https://ece.edu.vn/cac-cum-tu-an-diem-trong-ielts-speaking/)):

- "Lạm dụng" (abuse) of idioms is consistently warned against: IZONE explicitly notes examiners will *deduct points* (sẽ bị trừ điểm) for misused or out-of-context idioms.
- ZIM frames collocation as a Band 7 trigger and defines it as two or more words that naturally co-occur (e.g., "pay tribute to"); wrong collocations confuse listeners and cost marks. Opinion-bank language should go beyond repeated "I think" using alternative collocational frames.
- IELTS Complete provides topic-linked Part 2 collocation banks: people/personality (*strong personality, excellent communication skills, positive outlook on life*); places (*breathtaking views, bustling city, tranquil atmosphere*). Accurate collocations reduce dependence on "um"/"uh."
- ECE separates the three by function: collocations fit Part 2 and Part 3; phrasal verbs fit Part 1/2 natural answers; idioms should appear only 1-2 times because memorised insertions are easy for examiners to spot. ECE's band map: Band 6 prioritises clarity; Band 7 adds variety and paraphrase with collocations/phrasal verbs; Band 8-9 emphasises natural flexible idiom/collocation use with few grammar errors.
- 90+ to 120-idiom banks are categorised by Speaking Part: Part 1 daily life (*a piece of cake, no big deal, in a nutshell, hit the sack*); Part 2 description (*out of the blue, once in a blue moon, the apple of my eye*); Part 3 abstract (*food for thought, the tip of the iceberg, a double-edged sword, the bigger picture, when push comes to shove*).
- IELTS Fighter and Vietop publish an **8-week graded plan**: weeks 1-2 master 20 high-frequency phrasal verbs; weeks 3-4 add 30 topic collocations; weeks 5-6 introduce **2 idioms per topic, no more**; weeks 7-8 wire idioms into spontaneous Part 2 narratives. The "≤2 idioms per topic" cap is the most-repeated heuristic across Vietnamese sources.

### Iran / Persian — IDP Iran, IELTS2, Cafe IELTS

IDP Iran's Persian/English bilingual articles ([IDP Iran best phrases](https://ielts.idp.com/iran/prepare/article-ielts-speaking-best-phrases); [IDP Iran idiom guide](https://ielts.idp.com/iran/about/news-and-articles/article-use-idioms-ielts-speaking)) repeat global IDP guidance plus an Iran-specific note about **register matching**: Iranian candidates often translate idiomatic Farsi proverbs literally, producing unintentionally formal or archaic English ("as the saying goes…") that drops LR.

IELTS2 ([Persian idioms PDF](https://ielts2.com/ielts-speaking-idioms-pdf/)) defines idiom as a figurative-meaning multiword unit and explicitly states that informal idioms are generally not recommended for Writing but can be useful in Speaking when they communicate meaning well; its list includes many high-risk memorisation targets (*break the ice, piece of cake, costs an arm and a leg, hit the nail on the head*), so reward these only when topic fit, pronunciation, grammar, and surrounding discourse are natural.

Cafe IELTS ([collocation guide](https://cafeielts.ir/ielts-cue-card-vocabulary-collocations-guide/)) frames collocations as more important than impressive single words and contrasts natural combinations with translated/unnatural ones — consistent with Band 7+ LR descriptors that include collocation awareness.

Treat the "1-2 collocations" target as a practice heuristic rather than an official IELTS threshold.
- Pakistani Band-7 prep tightly emphasises **modal verbs for hedging** (*could, might, may, would*) — local accent often produces flat assertions ("X is bad"), and hedged equivalents ("X could be problematic in certain contexts") are explicitly listed as Band 7 vocabulary upgrades.
- The Pakistan Languages and Humanities Review article discusses collocations and idioms as distinguishable lexical phenomena and notes Pakistani-context work on Urdu-English collocations — relevant when diagnosing L1 transfer errors (direct translation of word partnerships into English).

## Open-source tools and code

No mature open-source tool specifically scores IELTS Speaking lexical resource or grammatical range alone. The topic is typically tackled as a sub-component of full-essay scoring tools or upstream collocation/idiom detection NLP libraries. Useful adjacent resources:

- **TAASSC — Tool for the Automatic Analysis of Syntactic Sophistication and Complexity** ([tool page](https://www.linguisticanalysistools.org/taassc.html); [GitHub](https://github.com/kristopherkyle/TAASSC)) — measures classic syntactic complexity plus fine-grained phrasal/clausal complexity (adjectives per noun phrase, adverbials per clause, etc.), CSV output from plain text. Aligns directly with the GRA features IELTS lists (sentence length, subordinate clauses, verb-phrase complexity, phrase complexity, information-focus structures). Recent v2.0+ uses spaCy for POS and dependency parsing; verify repo metadata (license/last-updated) before redistribution.
- **TAALES — Tool for the Automatic Analysis of Lexical Sophistication** ([page](https://soletlab.asu.edu/taales/)) — 130+ lexical sophistication indices (frequency, range), batch CSV output. Useful for estimating LR breadth/sophistication from transcripts; should not be treated as a direct IELTS band scorer.
- **CALF — Complexity / Accuracy / Lexis / Fluency** ([page](https://www.censpothk.com/calf/)) — automatic analytical tool for speech and coded written data; spans all four production dimensions. Marked "Copyright 2018. HSUHK"; accessible tool rather than clearly open-source code.
- **PodcastFillers dataset** ([arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2203.15135)) — annotated filler words and vocal events in podcast speech; not IELTS-specific but useful as a filler-word detector to distinguish discourse markers from hesitation events before applying fluency criteria.
- **spaCy + Cambridge English Profile API** — common pipeline for tagging vocabulary by CEFR level (B2/C1/C2 → roughly Band 6/7/8). Used in academic IELTS automated scoring papers.
- **Penn-Treebank-style constituency parsers** (Stanza, spaCy benepar) — can detect cleft, inversion, and conditional patterns by matching tree shapes (`(SBAR (IN if)…)` for conditionals, `(SINV …)` for inversion).
- **NLTK collocation extractor (BigramAssocMeasures, TrigramAssocMeasures)** — can be tuned against COCA / BNC reference lists to flag ungrammatical collocations (e.g., *make exam* vs *take exam*).
- **Phrasal Verbs Project / Cambridge Phrasal Verbs Plus** — phrasal-verb annotation corpora used in IELTS coaching books; referenced widely (e.g., [Jackie Bolen Speaking Vocabulary Builder](https://www.amazon.com/IELTS-Speaking-Vocabulary-Builder-Band/dp/B09VRB6R5K)).

For feature-counting (idioms-per-minute, conditionals-per-Part-3, hedges-per-turn), a thin custom script over a Penn-tag parse plus a hand-curated idiom/collocation lexicon (200-300 entries from the lists above) is the standard approach. Treat any tool's output as supporting evidence — official IELTS scoring is holistic across all parts and requires descriptor matching, not maximisation of sophistication scores.

## Practical signals for an LLM judge

Ranked by directness for transcript scoring; thresholds drawn from prep-coach heuristics where indicated — treat as priors, not official IELTS rules.

1. **Idiom density and "idiomatic language" breadth.** The rubric word is **idiomatic language**, which covers idioms, phrasal verbs, fixed expressions, and conventional metaphors. **Conflict flagged:** IELTS Advantage observed Band 9 practice sessions averaging **1.2 idioms** with many Band 9 candidates producing none ([IELTS Advantage](https://www.ieltsadvantage.com/idioms-for-ielts-speaking/)), while a separate claude-side heuristic targets **1-3 idiomatic chunks per 90-second Part 2 turn** treating "chunk" as the broader category (idiom + phrasal verb + fixed expression). Both are compatible if "idiom" is interpreted narrowly in the first and "chunk" broadly in the second — but do not enforce an idiom-count quota; zero textbook idioms is consistent with Band 9 when phrasal verbs/collocations are rich. Cap LR at Band 6 only when there is **zero idiomatic language of any type across the whole test**. Flag >4 idioms per 90-second turn as memorised script ([IELTS.org idioms article](https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/how-to-use-idioms-in-the-ielts-speaking-test); [yourielts.ru idioms myths](https://yourielts.ru/prepare-for-ielts/vocabulary/idioms-in-ielts)).
2. **Topic-mismatched idioms** ("raining cats and dogs" in a tech question) — flag each; two or more across the test caps LR at Band 6 regardless of density, because official LR penalises "inappropriate choices."
3. **Cliché concentration.** Count idioms from the British Council Top 20 list. If >50% of all idiom uses come from that list, treat as cram-school memorisation; LR ceiling is Band 7. Mixing in less common idioms or contextual phrases (cleft + collocation) lifts to Band 7.5-8.
4. **Phrasal-verb floor.** Band 7 candidates should produce **at least 4-6 distinct phrasal verbs across the whole test**. Zero phrasal verbs combined with otherwise sophisticated vocabulary signals academic but non-spoken register → Band 6.
5. **Collocation accuracy.** Penalise wrong verb+noun partnerships (*do a research, say lies, make a decision* correct vs *do a decision* wrong) more than simple-but-correct wording, because wrong collocations make the answer sound translated and damage LR's appropriacy/style criteria. Coach heuristic: 0-1 collocation errors → Band 8; 2-3 → Band 7; 4-6 → Band 6.
6. **Paraphrase quality.** Reward paraphrase when the candidate preserves meaning and avoids long hesitation around a lexical gap; cap LR benefit if paraphrase changes the claim, forces an unnatural synonym, or breaks register. When the examiner uses a key word, 70%+ verbatim reuse caps LR at Band 6; mixing in synonyms with at least one less-common item per minute supports Band 7+.
7. **Conditional production.** Band 7+ candidates produce **at least one second or third conditional in Part 3** (hypothetical questions invite these). Absence is not fatal, but presence is a strong positive.
8. **Complex-sentence layering.** Count sentences with ≥2 subordinate clauses. Coach heuristic: Band 7 = at least 3 layered sentences per Part 2; Band 8 = 5+; Band 9 = layered sentences dominate without breakdown. Only count complex sentences as range when intelligible and mostly controlled — tangled subordinate clauses score nothing.
9. **Inversion / cleft as bonuses, not requirements.** One well-formed inversion or cleft signals Band 8. Two or more in a 90-second turn reads as memorised script and adds nothing further.
10. **Modal hedging frequency.** Band 7+ Part 3 answers should contain **~2 hedges per minute** (*might, could, tend to, arguably, it could be argued, I'd say*). Zero hedges = over-categorical claims = LR/GR ceiling at Band 6 because the candidate avoids the complexity hedging requires.
11. **Self-correction cadence.** Treat as neutral or mildly positive when quick and accuracy-improving without breaking coherence; negative when it becomes a support mechanism. Coach cap: **one self-correction per 30-45 seconds**; more than two per minute drops Fluency one band. Word-for-word repetition ("she went, she went, she went to…") is hesitation, not reformulation. Official descriptors permit some self-correction at Band 7 but flag reliance on it as Band 5 behaviour.
12. **Acceptable vs penalised fillers.** Accept content-bearing fillers (*"That's an interesting question," "Let me think for a second," "Off the top of my head," "I'd say…," "I mean," "well," "to be honest"*) as fluency-neutral. Penalise clusters of *um/uh/er* (>3 per turn), L1-style repetitions (*"how to say… how to say…"*), and long English-L1-English code-switches. One *um* is natural; three consecutive trigger a Fluency drop.
13. **Discourse marker variety.** Band 7+ candidates deploy **≥5 distinct discourse markers** across the test (sequencing, contrasting, exemplifying, generalising, opinion). Repeating only *and / also / so* caps Coherence at Band 6.
14. **Memorised-chunk red flags.** Formal multi-clause openers in Part 1 simple questions ("It goes without saying that…" / "When all is said and done…" in answer to "Do you like coffee?") are strong memorisation tells. Two or more in one test caps Fluency at Band 6 even with smooth delivery.
15. **Register matching.** Flag overly formal idioms in informal Part 1 ("I find it most agreeable when…") and overly informal slang in Part 3 ("yeah, kids these days are wack"). Register mismatch counts as "inappropriate choice" for LR per the Band 7 descriptor. South-Asian-English-specific idioms ("do the needful," "out of station") count as regional cliché and do not contribute to "less common idiomatic language."
16. **Topic distribution.** Strong LR is topic-distributed, not concentrated in one memorised theme. Korean (Hackers 20-unit), Vietnamese (8-week plan), and Kazakh sources all organise vocabulary by topic clusters; reward consistent topic-appropriate vocabulary across Parts 1-3, not bursts in a single domain.
17. **Tool outputs as evidence only.** If using TAASSC/TAALES/CALF/PodcastFillers, treat outputs as supporting features — IELTS scoring is holistic against the descriptors, not score maximisation against any single tool.

**Flagged conflicts:**

- *Idiom density per Part 2 turn.* IELTS Advantage's empirical Band 9 observation is **1.2 idioms average with many at zero** (i.e., quantity is not the lever) — see codex source. Claude's heuristic of **1-3 "chunks" per 90-second Part 2 turn** is compatible only if "chunk" is interpreted broadly (idiom + phrasal verb + fixed expression + conventional metaphor) and treated as a soft prior rather than a quota. Practical signal #1 keeps both with attribution; the judge should not enforce idiom-count quotas.
- *Self-correction cadence.* Codex frames self-correction permissively per Band 7 descriptor; claude pulls a specific Liz-Ferguson coach heuristic of one per 30-45 seconds. Both kept — official descriptors permit some self-correction at Band 7; the cadence cap is a coach prior, not an IELTS rule.
