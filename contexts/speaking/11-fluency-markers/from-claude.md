# Fluency & coherence — discourse markers, natural hesitation patterns, what counts as fluent vs hesitant

_Crawled by claude on 2026-05-19T00:00Z. Sources cited inline._

## Summary

Fluency & Coherence (F&C) is one of the four equally-weighted IELTS Speaking
criteria (25% of the band) and is the criterion most sensitive to **how**
speech is produced rather than what is in it. The official public band
descriptors hinge on three diagnostic axes: (1) the *type* of hesitation
(content-related vs language-related), (2) the *quality* of repetition and
self-correction (occasional and natural vs frequent and flow-breaking), and
(3) the *flexibility* with which spoken discourse markers, connectives, and
cohesive features are used. This document collects authoritative descriptors,
international prep-school perspectives, corpus-linguistic findings, and
operational signals an LLM judge can apply when reading a diarized Speaking
transcript.

## Authoritative material

### Public band descriptors (Fluency & Coherence, bands 4–9)

Quoted verbatim from the public version of the IELTS Speaking band
descriptors (Cambridge Assessment English / British Council / IDP).

- **Band 9** — "Speaks fluently with only rare repetition or self-correction;
  any hesitation is content-related rather than to find words or grammar.
  Speaks coherently with fully appropriate cohesive features. Develops topics
  fully and appropriately." [British Council PDF](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf), reproduced at
  [Agape IELTS Academy](https://ieltsagape.wordpress.com/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors/).
- **Band 8** — "Speaks fluently with only occasional repetition or
  self-correction; hesitation is usually content-related and only rarely to
  search for language. Develops topics coherently and appropriately."
- **Band 7** — "Speaks at length without noticeable effort or loss of
  coherence. May demonstrate language-related hesitation at times, or some
  repetition and/or self-correction. Uses a range of connectives and discourse
  markers with some flexibility."
- **Band 6** — "Is willing to speak at length, though may lose coherence at
  times due to occasional repetition, self-correction or hesitation. Uses a
  range of connectives and discourse markers but not always appropriately."
- **Band 5** — "Usually maintains flow of speech but uses repetition, self
  correction and/or slow speech to keep going. May over-use certain
  connectives and discourse markers. Produces simple speech fluently, but
  more complex communication causes fluency problems."
- **Band 4** — "Cannot respond without noticeable pauses and may speak
  slowly, with frequent repetition and self-correction. Links basic sentences
  but with repetitious use of simple connectives and some breakdowns in
  coherence."

The descriptors collapse into a clean decision tree: **what kind of hesitation
is happening?** Content-related = upper bands; language-related =
mid-to-lower; "noticeable pauses" = Band 4 or below. See also the official
descriptor explainer at [IELTS-GPT](https://ielts-gpt.com/blog/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors-explained).

### IDP's canonical functional inventory of discourse markers

From [IDP's official discourse-markers article](https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-discourse-markers-ielts-speaking),
which is the most authoritative non-descriptor list (IDP co-owns IELTS):

- **Sequencing**: first / first of all / firstly / to begin with / the next
  one / moving on to / secondly / thirdly / subsequently / on top of that /
  later / after this / finally.
- **Adding information**: another thing that comes to mind / also / and /
  besides / additionally / another good example of this is / another reason
  for this / and one more thing.
- **Opinion & attitude**: unfortunately / however / actually / to be honest /
  definitely / essentially / frankly / basically / clearly / I'm afraid / if
  you ask me / sadly / thankfully / in fact / seriously / as a matter of fact.
- **Comparing**: similarly / in the same way / equally / likewise / in a
  similar fashion / if I compare it to my country.
- **Contrasting**: however / although / instead of / despite / on one hand /
  on the other hand / in the opposite way / in contrast / whereas.
- **Giving examples**: a great example of this is / for example / for
  instance / a personal example is / in other words / a striking example of
  this / a classic example is / a clear example of this can be seen / such
  as / illustrated by.
- **Stalling** (the most-tested category for fluency): let me think about
  that / that's a difficult/interesting question / I haven't thought of that
  before / well… / actually / basically.
- **Result**: as a result / because of this / therefore / consequently / so /
  then.
- **Generalising**: generally / broadly speaking / as a rule / on the whole /
  it is often said that / in most cases / the vast majority of / a small
  minority of.

IDP explicitly warns that **repetitive use detracts from naturalness** — the
marker must "genuinely express your intended function rather than [be] used
mechanically."

### Hesitation typology: content-related vs language-related

The single most diagnostic distinction in the descriptors. Examiner-side
sources reduce it to:

- **Content-related** = pausing to formulate or evaluate an idea ("Let me
  think… I suppose the biggest reason would be…"). Tolerated at Band 8;
  expected at Band 9 (since native speakers do this too).
- **Language-related** = pausing to retrieve a word, conjugate a verb, or
  reorder a clause ("My favourite… eh… my favourite thing to… er… to do…
  is…"). Acceptable "at times" at Band 7; characteristic of Band 6;
  pervasive at Band 4–5.

See [Mastering Fluency for Band 9 (LinkedIn)](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/mastering-fluency-band-9-ielts-speaking-test-hesitation-shaji-ali),
the [Simply IELTS Band 8 guide](https://simplyielts.com/ielts-speaking-fluency-coherence-score-band-8/),
and [TED IELTS on hesitation](https://ted-ielts.com/hesitation/). The latter
adds a useful third rule: **L1 fillers are penalised harder than English
ones** ("那个", "음", "ano…" all read as L2 leakage to the examiner).

### Self-correction policy (Liz Tarp / ieltsliz.com)

Liz Tarp's [self-correction guidance](https://ieltsliz.com/ielts-speaking-self-correction/),
widely cited by examiners, gives two operational rules:

1. **Never stop to correct minor grammar or vocabulary errors.** "You will
   only show the examiner that you struggle with fluency and need to keep
   stopping to correct your English."
2. **Correct only if you misstated the meaning** — and when you do,
   *paraphrase*, never repeat verbatim. "Use different language as well as
   altering the meaning."

This matches the band descriptors: at Band 7+, self-corrections are
"occasional"; at Band 5 they "keep [the speaker] going" — i.e., they're
crutches, not flourishes.

## Country-specific prep material

### South Korea (한국)

- [Skybel — 연결어 오답 유형 (connective mistakes)](https://blog.skybel.co.kr/blog/%EC%95%84%EC%9D%B4%EC%97%98%EC%B8%A0%20%EC%8A%A4%ED%94%BC%ED%82%B9/%EC%95%84%EC%9D%B4%EC%97%98%EC%B8%A0-%EC%8A%A4%ED%94%BC%ED%82%B9-%EA%B3%A0%EB%93%9D%EC%A0%90%EC%9D%84-%EB%A7%89%EB%8A%94-%EC%97%B0%EA%B2%B0%EC%96%B4-%EC%98%A4%EB%8B%B5-%EC%9C%A0%ED%98%95-%ED%97%B7%EA%B0%88%EB%A6%AC%EB%8A%94-%ED%91%9C%ED%98%84-%EC%A0%95%EB%A6%AC)
  flags four high-frequency Korean-L1 errors that drag F&C scores:
  *because…so* redundancy ("Because I was tired, so I went to bed" — the
  paired causal+result calque from 왜냐하면…그래서); *in addition to…also*
  doubling; *despite* used as a conjunction ("Despite I was tired"); and
  *although ↔ however* confusion (clausal vs sentential). The blog stresses
  that 연결어를 무작정 끼워 넣기보다는 (rather than blindly inserting
  connectives), accuracy and contextual fit matter more than inventory.
- [Hackers — 6.0→7.0 jump tips](https://www.gohackers.com/?c=ielts/ielts_info2/ielts_method&type=url&uid=538610)
  and [Score Factory Part 2 vocabulary](https://scorefactory.io/ielts_board_5/1497081)
  emphasise replacing 음/어 (Korean filler equivalents of "um") with English
  stalling phrases ("That's a good point", "Let me think for a second"),
  since L1 fillers are immediately flagged.
- Skybel's [Linking Words 15선](https://blog.skybel.co.kr/blog/%EC%95%84%EC%9D%B4%EC%97%98%EC%B8%A0%20%EC%8A%A4%ED%94%BC%ED%82%B9/%EC%95%84%EC%9D%B4%EC%97%98%EC%B8%A0-%EC%8A%A4%ED%94%BC%ED%82%B9-%EC%8B%A4%EC%A0%84-%ED%91%9C%ED%98%84-%EA%B3%A0%EB%93%9D%EC%A0%90%EC%9D%84-%EB%B6%80%EB%A5%B4%EB%8A%94-Linking-Words-15%EA%B0%80%EC%A7%80-%EC%99%84%EB%B2%BD-%EC%A0%95%EB%A6%AC)
  recommends a tight Band-7 set: *however, in contrast, on the other hand,
  for instance, as a result, on top of that, to be honest, what I mean is*.

### China (中国)

- [新东方 — 雅思口语上7谈何容易](https://ielts.xdf.cn/202210/12339985.html)
  argues the Band-6→7 gap is mainly about *flexibility* of discourse markers,
  not vocabulary. Chinese 9-band tutors push students to drop *first /
  second / and then* triplets (the calque of 首先…然后…) and adopt varied
  phrasing ("to begin with", "on top of that", "what's more").
- [知乎 — Connectives and Discourse markers](https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/121677680)
  splits markers into 逻辑关系连接词 (logical connectives: firstly, however,
  therefore) and 话语标记词 (pragmatic markers: well, you know, I mean,
  actually, the thing is). The post warns that Chinese candidates over-rely
  on the first set and under-use the second — the inverse of native speakers.
- [知乎 — 雅思口语评分标准详解](https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/595039373)
  notes that "keep going" replaced "speak at length" in the 2023 descriptor
  refresh: examiners reward sustained delivery even with imperfect grammar,
  whereas stop-start speech that's grammatically clean still caps at Band 6.

### Vietnam (Việt Nam)

- [Net English — Discourse Markers Band 7.0+](https://netenglish.edu.vn/discourse-markers-trong-ielts-speaking/)
  organises markers into three functional clusters: *connecting ideas*
  (sequencing, contrast, illustration, result), *managing conversation*
  (stalling, rephrasing, topic shifts), and *expressing attitude*
  (certainty/uncertainty/surprise/emphasis). Warns against direct
  Vietnamese-to-English calques ("after that then" from *sau đó thì*).
- [Zim.vn — IELTS Speaking Band Descriptors](https://zim.vn/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors)
  paraphrases the public descriptors for Vietnamese learners and stresses
  that at Band 6–7, hesitation/stuttering are similar in *amount* but differ
  in *cause* — the Band-7 speaker hesitates while choosing among options,
  the Band-6 speaker hesitates while retrieving any option.
- [Izone — Discourse markers là gì](https://www.izone.edu.vn/luyen-thi-ielts/discourse-markers-la-gi/)
  flags that overuse ("the more, the better") produces robotic speech that
  examiners immediately recognise.

### India / Pakistan / Bangladesh

- [British Council India — top tips](https://www.britishcouncil.in/blog/top-tips-get-better-ielts-speaking-score)
  notes most Indian candidates underperform on Speaking due to anxiety and
  L1-to-English translation latency, not lexis.
- [PND IELTS — Band 7 Speaking Strategy 2025](https://pndielts.in/blog/ielts-speaking-tips-for-band-7/)
  and [Turbo IELTS — Sound natural, not scripted](https://turboielts.com/ielts-speaking-band-7-tips-sound-natural/)
  recommend a memorised filler set: *er, em, well, actually, basically, you
  know, I mean, the thing is, that's an interesting question, let me think
  about that for a moment*. The target pace is 130–150 wpm — slower reads as
  hesitation, faster reads as memorised.
- [Kadamb Overseas — 12 techniques](https://kadamboverseas.com/ielts-speaking-techniques-band-7-confidence/)
  pushes the "1 idea + extension" formula: state, justify, exemplify, so the
  candidate is never reaching for a filler to buy thinking time.
- [IELTS Mumbai — advanced discourse markers](https://ieltsmumbai.com/blog/ielts-speaking-advanced-discourse-markers-for-natural-speech-flow)
  curates a Band-8 set: *having said that, on the flip side, what's more, to
  put it another way, that being said, by the same token, more often than
  not*.

### Iran (ایران)

- [Hafez Edu — speaking tricks for 7+](https://www.hafezedu.com/ielts-speaking-tricks/)
  and [Sara English School — what reduces F&C](https://saraenglishschool.com/fluency-coherence-ielts/)
  identify Persian-L1 fillers (*خب…، یعنی…، چی بگم…*) as the single biggest

- [Charbzaban — 20 speaking techniques](https://charbzaban.com/%D8%A2%D9%85%D9%88%D8%B2%D8%B4-%D8%B2%D8%A8%D8%A7%D9%86-%D8%A7%D9%86%DA%AF%D9%84%DB%8C%D8%B3%DB%8C/%D8%A2%DB%8C%D9%84%D8%AA%D8%B3/%D8%AA%DA%A9%D9%86%DB%8C%DA%A9-%D9%87%D8%A7%DB%8C-%D8%A7%D8%B3%D9%BE%DB%8C%DA%A9%DB%8C%D9%86%DA%AF-%D8%A2%DB%8C%D9%84%D8%AA%D8%B3.html)
  push *consequently, furthermore, as a result* as the minimum logical
  connectives a Band-7 candidate must own.
- [IDP Iran's Speaking phrasebook](https://ielts.idp.com/iran/prepare/article-ielts-speaking-best-phrases/fa-ir)
  emphasises stalling phrases as the cheapest way to gain content-related
  hesitation credit.

### Indonesia (Bahasa Indonesia)

- [AUG Study — Tips Speaking IELTS Band 7](https://augstudy.com/rahasia-tips-speaking-ielts-band-7-agar-skor-naik-drastis/)
  notes the Band-6→7 gap is rarely grammatical — it's about spontaneous
  development of answers in Part 3 and abstract reasoning. Recommends
  starters (*Well, Actually, To be honest*), additives (*On top of that,
  What's more*), contrasts (*Having said that, On the flip side*), examples
  (*For instance*), summaries (*So basically, All in all*).
- [OSEE — 15 cara jitu](https://osee.co.id/ielts-speaking-tips-skor-tinggi/)
  warns Indonesian candidates to keep their accent but eliminate L1 fillers
  (*eee…, anu…, gimana ya…*) entirely.

### UK / Australia / Canada (native-perspective)

- [3D Academy (Cebu) — How to reach Band 7](https://3d-universal.com/en/blogs/how-to-reach-band-7-in-ielts-speaking-strategies-for-success.html)
  and [IELTS Advantage — Band 8/9](https://www.ieltsadvantage.com/ielts-speaking/)
  emphasise that native examiners are listening for *naturalness* — a
  candidate who deploys advanced markers (*by and large, more often than
  not, that being said*) on cue but mispronounces or mis-collocates them
  signals memorisation and is capped below Band 7.
- [IELTS Liz — self-correction](https://ieltsliz.com/ielts-speaking-self-correction/)
  is the most-cited native-examiner blog; her rule (paraphrase, never repeat
  verbatim) is canonical.
- [Love to Learn English — filler-word impact](https://lovetolearnenglish.com/tips-for-ielts-and-toefl/2024/9/12/mastering-ielts-speaking-the-impact-of-filler-words-and-how-to-minimize-their-use)
  states the practical threshold: occasional *um* is invisible; filler in
  every sentence is recorded by the examiner as hesitation and depresses
  F&C directly.

## Open-source tools and code

- [jjordanoc/robust-english-speech-fluency-classification](https://github.com/jjordanoc/robust-english-speech-fluency-classification)
  — L2 English fluency classifier using SVM/MLP on speech rate, filled-pause
  rate, phonation ratio, effective speech rate, and MFCCs. Useful prior-art
  for what audio-side fluency features map to band differences. Not
  IELTS-trained but uses the same feature family.
- [pariajm/joint-disfluency-detector-and-parser](https://github.com/pariajm/joint-disfluency-detector-and-parser)
  — disfluency detection (false starts, repeats, repairs, filled pauses)
  trained on PTB-3 Switchboard. Outputs reparandum / interregnum / repair
  tags, which align cleanly with the descriptors' "repetition" and
  "self-correction" categories.
- [pariajm/deep-disfluency-detector](https://github.com/pariajm/deep-disfluency-detector)
  — Auto-Correlational NN variant.
- [SpeechAce API samples](https://github.com/speechace/speechace-api-samples)
  and [SpeechSuper API samples](https://github.com/speechsuper/SpeechSuper-API-Samples)
  — commercial APIs (closed weights, open client samples) that surface
  words-correct-per-minute, pause count, and IELTS/PTE estimated bands.
- Academic baseline: *Phonetic and Prosody-aware Self-supervised Learning
  Approach for Non-native Fluency Scoring* —
  [arXiv 2305.11438](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2305.11438) — handcrafted vs
  SSL feature ablations on non-native fluency scoring.
- Corpus-linguistic priors for "what looks native":
  [A Corpus-Based Study on Discourse Markers in Native and Non-native
  Spoken English](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/368973740_A_Corpus-Based_Study_on_Discourse_Markers_in_Native_and_Non-native_Spoken_English)
  and [Frontiers — "you know" and "I mean" in mediatized English](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/communication/articles/10.3389/fcomm.2024.1427062/full).
  Native speakers produce ~5× the rate of pragmatic markers per turn that L2
  learners do, and the ratio shifts toward interpersonal/interactive use
  (vs textual use) as L2 proficiency approaches C1.

## Practical signals for an LLM judge

When reading a diarized transcript, these are concrete, citable rules. Bands
in brackets are calibrated against the public descriptors above.

1. **Hesitation type triage.** Tag every hesitation as content-related
   ("hmm, let me think… I suppose…") or language-related ("the… er… the
   thing… that I…"). Ratio of content:language ≥ 4:1 supports Band 8+; ≤ 1:1
   supports Band 6 or below.
2. **L1 filler check.** Any non-English filler (음, 那个, ano, эээ, چی بگم,
   anu) in the transcript is a hard penalty — caps F&C at Band 6 even if
   other features are stronger ([TED IELTS](https://ted-ielts.com/hesitation/)).
3. **Discourse-marker inventory size.** Count distinct markers used
   meaningfully. Band 7 expects ≥ 6 distinct markers across at least 3 of
   IDP's 9 functional categories (sequencing, adding, contrast, example,
   result, opinion, comparing, stalling, generalising). Band 6 typically
   shows 2–4 distinct markers concentrated in 1–2 categories (usually
   sequencing + result). ([IDP](https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-discourse-markers-ielts-speaking))
4. **Marker over-repetition.** If a single marker (commonly *and*, *so*,
   *also*, *because*) accounts for > 40% of all discourse-marker tokens,
   penalise toward Band 5–6 ("may over-use certain connectives and
   discourse markers" — Band 5 descriptor verbatim).
5. **Marker–function fit.** Misused or mechanical markers ("on the other
   hand" introducing a similar idea; "for example" not followed by an
   example) signal Band 6 use, not Band 7. ([Skybel](https://blog.skybel.co.kr/blog/%EC%95%84%EC%9D%B4%EC%97%98%EC%B8%A0%20%EC%8A%A4%ED%94%BC%ED%82%B9/%EC%95%84%EC%9D%B4%EC%97%98%EC%B8%A0-%EC%8A%A4%ED%94%BC%ED%82%B9-%EA%B3%A0%EB%93%9D%EC%A0%90%EC%9D%84-%EB%A7%89%EB%8A%94-%EC%97%B0%EA%B2%B0%EC%96%B4-%EC%98%A4%EB%8B%B5-%EC%9C%A0%ED%98%95-%ED%97%B7%EA%B0%88%EB%A6%AC%EB%8A%94-%ED%91%9C%ED%98%84-%EC%A0%95%EB%A6%AC))
6. **Pragmatic vs textual markers.** Presence of pragmatic markers (*well,
   actually, I mean, you know, the thing is, to be honest*) used at clause
   boundaries — not just sequencing connectives — pushes toward Band 7+,
   because native L1-English corpora show ~5× higher pragmatic-marker rates
   than L2. ([Corpus study](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/368973740_A_Corpus-Based_Study_on_Discourse_Markers_in_Native_and_Non-native_Spoken_English))
7. **Self-correction quality.** Count self-corrections. Meaning corrections
   ("I went to the bank — well, I mean the river bank") read as fluency
   *strength*. Grammar/word-search corrections ("I goed — went — I went")
   read as fluency *weakness*. Three or more language-related corrections
   per Part-2 turn drops F&C to Band 6. ([Liz](https://ieltsliz.com/ielts-speaking-self-correction/))
8. **Repetition without function.** Functionless word/phrase repeats ("the
   the the", "I think I think") are disfluency markers and should be
   counted separately from rhetorical repetition ("very, very important").
   ([Disfluency detector reference](https://github.com/pariajm/joint-disfluency-detector-and-parser))
9. **Part-2 long-turn endurance.** A Band-7 Part-2 turn fills ≥ 90 seconds
   of the available 2 minutes without examiner intervention. Stopping at
   30–60 s reads as "willingness to speak at length" (Band 6 wording) only;
   running the full 2 minutes coherently is a Band 7+ signal.
   ([Edmicro](https://edmicro.edu.vn/ielts/ielts-speaking-band-7/))
10. **Speech pace.** Target 130–150 wpm in transcript ([Kadamb](https://kadamboverseas.com/ielts-speaking-techniques-band-7-confidence/)).
    Below 100 wpm with frequent gaps suggests Band 5–6; above 170 wpm
    typically signals memorised script, which examiners discount.
11. **Pause length.** Any single mid-utterance pause inferred from the
    transcript longer than ~5 s without a content reason (e.g., recalling a
    specific fact) is a Band 5–6 signal. Pauses at clause boundaries are
    invisible; mid-clause pauses are penalised. ([Hafez Edu](https://www.hafezedu.com/ielts-speaking-tricks/))
12. **Topic-shift cohesion.** When the examiner changes topic, a Band 7+
    candidate uses an explicit transition ("Moving on to that…", "When it
    comes to X, I'd say…"). A jump straight into the new topic without
    transition is a Band 6 signal. ([IDP](https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-discourse-markers-ielts-speaking))
13. **Stalling quality.** "Let me think about that for a moment" or "That's
    an interesting question, I haven't thought about it before" earn
    content-related hesitation credit. Bare *um/er/uh* sequences do not.
    Repeated use of the same stalling phrase across turns (mechanical) is
    itself a Band 6 signal. ([Essential IELTS](https://www.essentialielts.com/ielts-speaking-7-fluency-markers))
14. **Verbatim repetition under correction.** If the candidate corrects by
    repeating the same phrase word-for-word ("I went there — I went there
    last year"), that is not a fluency strength even when the second
    version is "right" — Liz's paraphrase rule. ([Liz](https://ieltsliz.com/ielts-speaking-self-correction/))
15. **Cohesion via reference, not just markers.** Band 8–9 speakers use
    pronouns and substitution ("it", "that one", "do so") to bind
    utterances, not only explicit markers. Over-reliance on explicit
    markers without pronoun/ellipsis cohesion reads as Band 6–7.
    ([Cambridge descriptor PDF](https://assets.cambridgeenglish.org/webinars/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf))
