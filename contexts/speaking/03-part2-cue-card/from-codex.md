# Part 2 (long turn) — cue card format, prep strategies, recent cue card banks

_Crawled by codex on 2026-05-19T07:33:23Z. Sources cited inline._

## Summary

IELTS Speaking Part 2 is the individual long turn: the examiner gives a task/cue card, the candidate has one minute to prepare with paper and pencil, then speaks for one to two minutes before one or two short follow-up questions (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking; https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/take-ielts/prepare/free-ielts-english-practice-tests/speaking/part-2). For scoring, Part 2 is the clearest transcript sample of sustained candidate speech, so it is useful for judging whether the candidate can keep going, organise a mini-monologue, develop a personal example, and show lexical/grammatical/pronunciation range under time pressure (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf). Cue-card banks are useful as prep and corpus topic coverage, but they are third-party reported/practice resources unless published by IELTS owners; they should not be treated as official disclosed test inventories (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking; https://ieltsliz.com/ielts-speaking-part-2-topics/).

## Authoritative material

### Official format and task mechanics

- IELTS.org describes Speaking Part 2 as the "individual long turn": the examiner gives a task card with a topic, points to include, and an instruction to explain one aspect of the topic; the candidate has one minute to prepare and receives pencil and paper for notes (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking).
- IELTS.org says the candidate is expected to use the task-card points and preparation notes to structure the talk and keep speaking for two minutes; the examiner stops the candidate when time is up and may ask one or two questions on the same topic (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking).
- British Council's official practice Part 2 page states the same sequence: task card with key points, one minute preparation, pencil and paper for notes, one to two minutes of speaking, then one or two same-topic questions; Part 2 takes three to four minutes in total (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/take-ielts/prepare/free-ielts-english-practice-tests/speaking/part-2).
- The British Council sample card "Describe something you own which is very important to you" shows the canonical cue-card shape: a main "Describe..." prompt, three factual bullets, an "explain why..." bullet, a one-minute preparation instruction, and a one-to-two-minute speaking instruction (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/take-ielts/prepare/free-ielts-english-practice-tests/speaking/part-2).
- IDP's official speaking-practice article says the topic is handed on a card, the candidate receives paper and pen, the examiner uses a timer, the candidate has exactly one minute for notes, the examiner stops the talk after two minutes, and candidates should try to keep talking for the full two minutes (https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-free-speaking-practice-questions).
- IDP Indonesia gives the same format in Indonesian: Part 2 gives a topic card, one minute to make notes using pencil and paper, then two minutes to speak, followed by Part 3 discussion connected to the Part 2 topic (https://ielts.idp.com/indonesia/prepare/speaking).
- IELTS research by Elder and Wigglesworth includes interviewer administration wording for one-minute planning: candidates are told they will have one minute to think, may make notes, receive paper and pencil, and are reminded they have one to two minutes and should not worry if stopped when time is up (https://cdn.ielts.org/Research/investigation-of-effectiveness-and-validity-of-planning-time-in-part-2-of-ielts-speaking-test-elder-et-al-2006.pdf).

### What Part 2 tests

- IELTS.org says Part 2 tests the ability to speak at length on a given topic, use appropriate language, organise ideas logically, and draw on personal experiences to complete the long turn (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking).
- The public IELTS Speaking Band Descriptors apply across the full Speaking test, but Part 2 is particularly informative for Fluency and Coherence because Band 7 requires candidates to "readily produce long turns" without noticeable effort, Band 6 requires willingness to produce long turns though coherence may be lost, and Band 5 often relies on repetition/self-correction/slow speech to keep going (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).
- The same descriptors make Part 2 useful for Lexical Resource because Band 7+ expects flexible topic vocabulary, some less common/idiomatic items and effective paraphrase, while Band 5 has limited flexibility and unsuccessful paraphrase attempts; the long turn gives enough speech to see whether vocabulary range is sustained beyond short answers (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).
- The descriptors also make Part 2 useful for Grammatical Range and Accuracy because a long turn can reveal whether the candidate uses a mix of simple and complex structures, past/present/future references, subordination and error control under sustained production (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).
- Pronunciation in Part 2 should be judged by intelligibility, chunking, rhythm, stress, intonation and connected speech across an extended turn; Band 8 can sustain rhythm and flexible stress/intonation across long utterances, while Band 6 has variable phonological control and rhythm may be affected by stress timing or rapid speech rate (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).

### Official preparation-time guidance

- IELTS.org's preparation-time guide recommends first checking the task-card topic and asking the examiner immediately if a word on the card is not understood, because waiting until the end of the one-minute preparation wastes planning time (https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/how-to-use-the-preparation-time-in-ielts-speaking-part-2).
- The same IELTS.org guide recommends choosing the first example that the candidate can describe in English, slightly adapting the topic if necessary while making the adaptation clear at the start, and preferably using a real example because invented stories often run out of content or become incoherent (https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/how-to-use-the-preparation-time-in-ielts-speaking-part-2).
- IELTS.org advises notes in English, phrases rather than full sentences, notes in the intended speaking order, clear handwriting, and one or two useful phrases if time remains; these rules are operationally relevant because full-sentence notes often lead to reading or delayed planning rather than spontaneous speech (https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/how-to-use-the-preparation-time-in-ielts-speaking-part-2).
- IDP Canada recommends three preparation steps: understand the topic and keywords, brainstorm at least two key ideas for each task-card point, and note relevant vocabulary/expressions because examiners assess range and precision of vocabulary as well as organisation (https://ielts.idp.com/canada/prepare/article-did-you-say-2-minutes-speaking-test).
- IDP's long-turn strategy article recommends drawing a cross or dividing the note paper into four columns for the three bullets plus final explanation, focusing on question words such as where/how long/what/why, and continuing to write notes until the examiner asks the candidate to begin (https://ielts.idp.com/turkey/prepare/article-ielts-speaking-test-long-turn-part-2/en-gb/).
- IDP's same article warns against speaking too fast through nerves, recommends a measured pace and breathing between sentences, and suggests using hesitation devices such as "well, let me think" or "another thing I remember..." to keep the long turn moving when content momentarily stalls (https://ielts.idp.com/argentina/prepare/article-ielts-speaking-test-long-turn-part-2/).
- British Council's PPF Strategy lesson plan presents Past-Present-Future as a Part 2 strategy to help candidates speak at length and demonstrate more complex grammar structures, especially for topics that can be extended through earlier experience, current situation and future possibility (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/speaking_part_2_-_ppf_strategy.pdf).
- Cambridge English's classroom activity has students practise a short talk first without planning, then with one minute planning, and finally extend to two minutes; the teacher notes explicitly ask students to use preparation time to find a real personal experience, write key ideas or vocabulary, and expand when ready (https://www.cambridgeenglish.org/images/168248-ielts-speaking-part-2.pdf).

### Recent and historical cue-card banks

- Official sample cards are sparse and for practice format, not a live question bank; British Council's public sample uses "Describe something you own which is very important to you" and two rounding-off questions about value and replacement (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/take-ielts/prepare/free-ielts-english-practice-tests/speaking/part-2).
- IDP's sample-task page provides official-style Part 2 prompts and states that the task-card points help candidates think of what to say and keep speaking for the full two minutes (https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-free-speaking-practice-questions).
- IELTS Liz maintains a long-running Part 2 topic list, groups common topics as objects, people, places, events/occasions and activities, warns not to memorise full cue-card answers, and says common topics often repeat year to year, including for 2026 prep (https://ieltsliz.com/ielts-speaking-part-2-topics/).
- IELTS Buddy's historical Part 2 collection presents example cards and reminds learners that real topics may differ because there are many examiner-selected topics and topics change (https://www.ieltsbuddy.com/ielts-speaking-topics.html).
- Cathoven's "May-August 2026" page is a recent third-party reported/practice bank with Part 2 cue cards, model answers, prep notes and follow-up questions, published March 16, 2026 and last updated April 29, 2026 (https://resources.cathoven.com/ielts-speaking/part-2-cue-cards).
- Cathoven also publishes a Jan-Apr 2026 cue-card bank with 55 Part 2 topics and Part 3 follow-up discussion questions, useful for corpus coverage of likely person/place/object/event/experience categories (https://resources.cathoven.com/ielts-speaking/cue-card-bank).
- IELTS International's 2026 multilingual bank lists 100+ practice Part 2 topics grouped by categories such as people, places, objects, events and activities, and repeats the one-minute preparation plus one-to-two-minute speaking format in English, Korean, Chinese and Russian versions (https://www.ielts.international/ielts-speaking-part-2-cue-cards; https://www.ielts.international/ko/ielts-speaking-part-2-cue-cards; https://www.ielts.international/zh/ielts-speaking-part-2-cue-cards; https://www.ielts.international/ru/ielts-speaking-part-2-cue-cards).

- AllThingsIELTS publishes 2026 cue-card topics with model answers and a preparation strategy of brief notes, timer use and an introduction-main-points-conclusion structure (https://allthingsielts.com/ielts-speaking/part-2-cue-cards.php; https://allthingsielts.com/ielts-speaking/cue-cards-2026).
- Historical Makkar-style cue-card PDFs such as a May-August 2020 collection show how many prep markets circulate seasonal cue-card lists by window; these are useful for historical topic taxonomy, not official disclosure (https://magicaloverseas.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/06/Makkar-IELTS-Speaking-May-to-Aug-2020.pdf).

### Korea

## Practical signals for an LLM judge

- Detect the Part 2 segment by examiner script patterns such as a card/topic handover, one-minute preparation, "start speaking now", and "thank you" after the long turn; Part 2 should be one candidate monologue plus one or two short follow-up questions before Part 3 begins (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/take-ielts/prepare/free-ielts-english-practice-tests/speaking/part-2; https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-free-speaking-practice-questions).
- If timestamps are available, treat under 60 seconds of candidate speech as strong negative Fluency/Coherence evidence because the official task asks for one to two minutes; treat 60-90 seconds as potentially acceptable but often thin, and 90-120 seconds as a stronger opportunity to demonstrate long-turn control, noting that the 90-second target is a coaching heuristic from prep sources rather than an official band cutoff (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking; https://learnenglishweekly.com/ielts/speaking/part-2-cue-cards; https://www.ielts.international/ko/ielts-speaking-part-2-cue-cards).
- Do not penalise the candidate merely because the examiner stops them at two minutes; official sources say the examiner will stop the talk when time is up, and IDP says being stopped at two minutes is normal because the candidate has shown a lot of language (https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-free-speaking-practice-questions; https://ielts.idp.com/indonesia/prepare/ielts-academic-preparation/ielts-academic-speaking/en-gb).
- Score prompt coverage as a coherence aid, not as a checklist-only task: the card points help candidates structure the talk, but higher scoring should depend on sustained relevant development, logical order, appropriate language and the four official criteria (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking; https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).
- Reward a clear opening that names the chosen person/place/object/event and quickly anchors time/place/relationship, because IELTS.org advises choosing an example early and making any topic adaptation clear at the start (https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/how-to-use-the-preparation-time-in-ielts-speaking-part-2).
- Treat specific personal details, sensory description, emotions and reflection as positive evidence for Fluency/Coherence and Lexical Resource because official prep guidance recommends real examples and IDP Canada recommends brainstorming key ideas and relevant vocabulary for each card point (https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/how-to-use-the-preparation-time-in-ielts-speaking-part-2; https://ielts.idp.com/canada/prepare/article-did-you-say-2-minutes-speaking-test).
- Watch for "notes read aloud" or memorised-answer signals: unnaturally essay-like phrasing, abrupt topic mismatch, no adaptation to the actual card, or a polished block followed by weak follow-up answers; IELTS Liz explicitly warns not to memorise cue-card answers, and IELTS.org recommends phrases/keywords rather than full sentences in notes (https://ieltsliz.com/ielts-speaking-part-2-topics/; https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/how-to-use-the-preparation-time-in-ielts-speaking-part-2).
- Treat flexible time-frame movement as positive grammar evidence in Part 2: a strong answer often uses past experience, present evaluation and future consequence/plan, matching British Council's PPF strategy for length and complex grammar range (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/speaking_part_2_-_ppf_strategy.pdf).
- Penalise running out of content through repetition more than a normal brief pause: Band 6 can keep going but may lose coherence through hesitation/repetition/self-correction, while Band 5 relies on repetition/self-correction/slow speech to continue (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).
- For Lexical Resource, evaluate whether the candidate produces topic-specific vocabulary appropriate to the card category, such as personality and relationship language for people, sensory/spatial language for places, function/value language for objects, sequence/emotion language for events, or ability/process language for skills; official descriptors require flexible vocabulary use and less common/idiomatic items at higher bands (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf; https://www.ieltsbuddy.com/ielts-speaking-topics.html).
- For Pronunciation, use the sustained monologue to judge phrase chunking, rhythm, stress and intonation across long utterances; do not penalise accent identity if intelligibility is maintained, because IDP Indonesia says accent is only a problem when it prevents understanding and the official descriptors focus on intelligibility and phonological control (https://ielts.idp.com/indonesia/prepare/ielts-academic-preparation/ielts-academic-speaking/en-gb; https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).
- If the candidate asks about a word on the cue card at the beginning of preparation, do not treat that alone as a score penalty; IELTS.org explicitly recommends asking immediately if a card word is unclear (https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/how-to-use-the-preparation-time-in-ielts-speaking-part-2).
- Do not infer that recent cue-card-bank topics are official expected answers; use them only to ensure evaluation data covers common topic families and seasonal practice distributions across people, places, objects, events, activities and abstract follow-up themes (https://ieltsliz.com/ielts-speaking-part-2-topics/; https://resources.cathoven.com/ielts-speaking/part-2-cue-cards; https://www.ielts.international/ielts-speaking-part-2-cue-cards).
