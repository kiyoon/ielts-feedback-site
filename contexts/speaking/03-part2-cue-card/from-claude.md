# Part 2 (long turn) — cue card format, prep strategies, recent cue card banks

_Crawled by claude on 2026-05-19T00:00:00Z. Sources cited inline._

## Summary

Part 2 — the "individual long turn" — is the only part of the IELTS Speaking
test where the candidate must hold the floor alone, with 1 minute of prep and
1–2 minutes of unbroken monologue from a printed cue card. It is the single
richest data point for a judge: long enough to exhibit cohesion, lexical range,
grammatical variety, and pronunciation stamina; structured enough (4 bullet
prompts on the card) that organisation can be assessed directly. Because the
test's question pool refreshes on a 4-month cycle (Jan–Apr, May–Aug, Sep–Dec),
most non-native test-takers in Asia walk in having drilled the current cue-card
bank, so judges should expect rehearsed openings on familiar topics and judge
"fluency under load" by whether candidates can sustain natural delivery past
their memorised script.

## Authoritative material

**Format (ielts.org)** — Part 2 lasts "3–4 minutes, including the preparation
time." Candidates receive a task card stating a topic plus 3–4 sub-points,
1 minute to prepare with pencil and paper, then must speak for "1 to 2 minutes"
until the examiner stops them. The examiner then asks "one or two questions on
the same topic" (the "rounding-off" questions, *not* Part 3 yet).
[ielts.org Academic Speaking format](https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking)

**What it assesses (ielts.org)** — "Your ability to speak at length on a
given topic, using appropriate language and organising your ideas logically.
You will need to think about your own experiences to complete the long turn."
Identical format for Academic and General Training.
[ielts.org test types](https://www.ielts.org/how-to-use-ielts-results/four-skills/speaking/format)

**Scoring (ielts.org / British Council)** — The four criteria — Fluency &
Coherence, Lexical Resource, Grammatical Range & Accuracy, Pronunciation — are
**equally weighted, each 25%** of the speaking band, averaged across the whole
test (Parts 1+2+3). Part 2 is not separately scored, but is widely regarded as
the highest-leverage section because it is the only sustained sample.
[ielts.org scoring detail](https://ielts.org/take-a-test/your-results/ielts-scoring-in-detail) ·
[British Council Speaking Band Descriptors PDF](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf)

**Topic-development research (Cambridge / IELTS Research Reports)** —
Seedhouse et al. (2011) "Topic development in the IELTS Speaking Test"
explicitly examines how candidates at different bands manage the long turn;
the paper is one of the few peer-reviewed sources directly on Part 2 dynamics
and is hosted on ielts.org.
[ielts.org research PDF](https://ielts.org/cdn/Research/topic-development-in-ielts-speaking-test-seedhouse-et-al-2011.pdf)

**No "task achievement" criterion** — Several teacher-side sources note a
counter-intuitive but official fact: the candidate is *not* penalised for
content truth or for skipping a bullet on the card. "There is no marking
criterion for 'answering the question perfectly' in the Speaking Test"; the
examiner scores how the candidate communicates, not whether the story is true.
That said, ignoring a bullet usually shows up as thin coherence and a short
turn. [IELTSetc — examiner perspective](https://ieltsetc.com/ielts-speaking-part-2-long-turn/)

**IDP official (Vietnam)** — Recommends: introduce topic, address each bullet
in order with linking words (`and / so / but / or`), and if you run out of
ideas use any of seven stalling techniques (re-read the card aloud and
rephrase; "let me think about that for a minute"; always say "yes" to "is
there anything else?"; never produce filler `eh / uh / um`). Examiner cuts you
off exactly at 2 minutes regardless of completeness.
[IDP Vietnam long turn](https://ielts.idp.com/vietnam/about/news-and-articles/article-ielts-speaking-test-long-turn-part-2)

**British Council practice set** — Free official practice Part 2 cards with
audio.
[takeielts practice Part 2](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/take-ielts/prepare/free-ielts-english-practice-tests/speaking/part-2)

## Country-specific prep material

### Korea (한국)

**TestGlider — B-E-F-L 구조** (Background → Experience → Feeling → Lesson).
The blog claims this four-block framework naturally produces 1m 50s – 2m 10s
of speech, and prescribes specific second budgets: B 20–30s, E 30–40s,
F 20–30s, L 20–30s. Prep-minute timing: 0–15s underline the four bullets,
15–40s write keywords only under each B/E/F/L slot, 40–60s mentally rehearse
the opening sentence.
[TestGlider B-E-F-L](https://blog.testglider.com/%EC%95%84%EC%9D%B4%EC%97%98%EC%B8%A0-speaking-part2-%ED%81%90%EC%B9%B4%EB%93%9C-b-e-f-l-%EA%B5%AC%EC%A1%B0/)

**Skybel cram school** — pushes a `when → where → who → what → why → feeling`
flow with single-word notes per slot, e.g. *Topic: a trip / When: last
summer / Where: Jeju / Who: friend / Why: relax / Feeling: happy*. Explicit
warning that writing full sentences during the prep minute triggers a
memorisation reflex that "kills naturalness and speed" once speaking begins.
[Skybel 1분 전략](https://blog.skybel.co.kr/blog/%EC%95%84%EC%9D%B4%EC%97%98%EC%B8%A0%20%EC%8A%A4%ED%94%BC%ED%82%B9/%EC%95%84%EC%9D%B4%EC%97%98%EC%B8%A0-%EC%8A%A4%ED%94%BC%ED%82%B9-Part-2-1%EB%B6%84%EC%9D%98-%EA%B8%B0%EC%A0%81-%EB%81%8A%EA%B8%B0%EC%A7%80-%EC%95%8A%EB%8A%94-%EB%8B%B5%EB%B3%80%EC%9D%84-%EB%A7%8C%EB%93%9C%EB%8A%94-%ED%95%B5%EC%8B%AC-%EC%A0%84%EB%9E%B5)

### Vietnam

**ZIM, DOL, VUS, IDP Vietnam** all teach a five-block expansion: *what / when /
who / how / feeling / conclusion*. ZIM and DOL specifically push the
"detail explosion" tactic — every bullet must be elaborated with at least one
sensory detail or specific year/place/name, not a generic statement. Common
advice across all VN sources: speak the full 2 minutes; stopping early auto-
caps Fluency near band 6.

### Iran (فارسی)

**Iranian academies (LMAP, Deed Language, IELTS Teachers)** emphasise a
mind-map approach in the 1-minute prep: write the topic in the centre,
branch into *who / where / when / why*. The single most-repeated rule:
"بهترین روش...تنها کلمات کلیدی...یادداشت کنید" (best method = keywords only;
writing sentences takes more than a minute). For band-7+ candidates, Iranian
guides recommend memorising 4–5 "advanced anchor phrases" per topic family
(e.g. for a *place* card: *off the beaten track, hidden gem, stumble upon*).

### Native-perspective UK / AUS / CAN

**Simon (ex-IELTS examiner)** — quality over quantity: "Realistically you can
only write 5–6 words/phrases" in a minute, so make every one a *high-band*
word. Don't write `nice / fun / good`; write `entrepreneurial, charismatic,
inspirational`. Headings to organise notes: *introduction, past, description,
opinion, future*.
[IELTSpodcast — notes on cue cards](https://www.ieltspodcast.com/ielts-speaking/making-notes-on-cue-cards/) ·
[Vietop hosted PDF of Simon's 40 tips](https://vietop.edu.vn/wp-content/uploads/2019/12/40-TIPS-FOR-IELTS-SPEAKING-PART-2.pdf)

**Keith Speaking Academy (UK)** — 3-step frame: brief direct intro → develop
with adjectives + examples + stories + connectors → optional micro-conclusion.
Two operational rules: (a) if you don't know the topic, "talk around it"
(`I imagine it would be... / I suppose if I had...`); (b) read the *tense*
of each bullet (`would like / used to / will`) and match it.
[Keith Speaking Academy Part 2](https://keithspeakingacademy.com/ielts-speaking-part2-cue-card-topics-tips/)

**IELTS Advantage** — Band 8+ separator is *storytelling*: narrative arc
(set the scene → describe → outcome → reflection), not a list of facts.
[IELTS Advantage Part 2 strategy](https://www.ieltsadvantage.com/2015/03/03/ielts-speaking-task-2-strategy/) ·

## Practical signals for an LLM judge

Apply when reading a Part 2 transcript. Numeric thresholds are pragmatic
heuristics derived from cram-school + examiner sources cited above, not from
the official band descriptors (which are qualitative); treat as priors, not
hard cutoffs.

1. **Duration is a hard prior on Fluency & Coherence.** Under 60s of speech
   ≈ Band ≤5 territory regardless of language quality; 60–90s with prompting
   ≈ Band 6; sustained 90–120s with no examiner re-prompt is a band-7+
   precondition. A candidate stopping at ~30–40s with the examiner forced to
   say "anything else?" is the diagnostic example of Band 4.5 in published
   guides.

2. **Coverage of bullet points is *not* officially scored but functions as a
   coherence proxy.** A response that skips 2+ bullets typically reads as
   poorly organised even when fluent. Count bullet hits and weight Coherence
   accordingly; do *not* directly deduct for "missing content."

3. **Detect rehearsed openings.** Sentences like "The person I am going to
   talk about today is..." or any opener that exactly mirrors the cue-card
   stem in formal register suggest memorisation. Look for a drop in fluency
   or grammatical accuracy in the *second half* of the turn as the script
   runs out — that delta is the real Fluency signal.

4. **Discourse markers per minute are a band marker.** Band 7+ candidates
   typically deploy ≥4 cohesive devices per 90s (e.g. *what's more, on top
   of that, having said that, I suppose, the thing is*). Sub-2 per 90s with
   over-reliance on `and / so / but` ≈ Band 6.

5. **Stalling phrases ≠ filler sounds.** "Let me think about that for a
   moment" is band-neutral or band-positive; sequences of `um / er / uh`
   over 3+ in a 30-second window are a clear Fluency penalty per IDP and
   ieltsetc.com guidance.

6. **Tense control across the talk.** Person/place/event cards almost
   always require a past-tense narrative with a present-tense reflection
   ("…and to this day I still…") and a hypothetical/future closing
   ("…I'd love to do it again"). A turn locked in a single tense throughout
   caps Grammatical Range below Band 7.

7. **Idiomatic chunks per turn.** Band 7+ candidates typically produce
   ≥2 idiomatic expressions per 2-minute turn (e.g. *a hidden gem, off the
   beaten track, broaden my horizons, out of the blue*). Zero idioms + fully
   literal phrasing usually = Band 6 Lexical Resource.

8. **Topic substitution / "talk around it" tactic.** If the candidate openly
   admits "I've never actually done X, but I imagine…" and proceeds to a
   coherent hypothetical narrative, do *not* penalise — Keith Speaking
   Academy and IDP both endorse this. Penalise only if the substitution is
   off-topic.

9. **Reading from notes.** Robotic delivery that mirrors note bullets
   exactly ("when… last summer… where… Jeju… who… my friend…") signals
   note-dependence; Fluency rarely scores above 6.

10. **The rounding-off question is the candour test.** Examiners ask 1–2
    short questions after the long turn. A candidate who scripted the main
    turn will frequently produce shorter, less fluent answers here. Compare
    fluency between the main turn and the round-off — a large drop suggests
    the long turn was rehearsed, and you should anchor the speaking score
    closer to the round-off / Part 3 performance.

11. **High-band openers are short and specific.** "Today I'd like to tell
    you about my grandfather" (band-9 idiom) beats "Today I am going to
    speak about a person who has had a major influence on my life and that
    person is my grandfather" (over-formal, often-memorised). Brevity in
    the intro frees seconds for development — itself a band-7+ habit.

12. **Speech rate.** Native-norm range cited by examiner blogs is ~130–160
    wpm; rates above 180 wpm typically lose pronunciation marks (slurred
    word boundaries); rates below 90 wpm typically lose fluency marks
    (excessive pausing).

13. **Self-correction is band-positive up to a point.** A single fluent
    self-correction ("…I went to… I mean, I had been to…") demonstrates
    grammatical control. Repeated mid-clause restarts (>3 per minute) drop
    Fluency.

14. **Don't reward the formal conclusion.** "So that is the person I admire
    most" is not required and often signals a memorised template. Lack of
    explicit conclusion ≠ incoherence; examiners cut at 2 min regardless.

15. **Topic-family priors for vocabulary expectations.** Use the recent
    cue-card bank above to calibrate: a "describe a place" card should
    surface place-specific lexis (*bustling, scenic, off the beaten
    track*); a "describe a person" card should surface character lexis
    (*down-to-earth, charismatic, role model*). Generic adjectives
    (`nice, good, big`) across *any* topic family = Lexical Resource ≤ 6.
