# Pronunciation criteria — segmental and suprasegmental features, common L1-specific errors

_Merged from codex + claude crawls on 2026-05-19T08:00:00Z._

## Summary

IELTS Pronunciation is one of the four equally-weighted Speaking criteria (25%) and is assessed against a fixed set of phonological features rather than against any "British/American/Australian" accent target (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking; https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf; https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-does-ielts-speaking-assess-your-accent). Examiners listen for the candidate's **range** of features (segmental: individual phonemes, word stress; suprasegmental: sentence stress, weak forms, connected speech/linking/elision, rhythm, intonation, chunking), the **flexibility/control** with which they are deployed, and the resulting **intelligibility** — i.e., how much listener effort the speech requires.

L1 background is not penalised in itself, but L1 transfer typically surfaces as a predictable cluster of errors (Korean L↔R, F↔P, V↔B, epenthesis on final clusters and onset clusters, syllable-timing; Japanese vowel epenthesis ("katakana English"), R/L flap, mora-timing; Chinese final-consonant deletion and word-level pitch transfer; Arabic /p/↔/b/, /v/↔/f/, vowel-quality undershoot; Spanish schwa-loss and prothetic /e/ before s+C; Vietnamese final-consonant elision and tone-on-word transfer; Persian /w/↔/v/; Hindi-Urdu /w/↔/v/ collapse to /ʋ/; etc.).

For a transcript-only judge, pronunciation evidence is **indirect**. Rely on explicit transcript signals (examiner repair, [inaudible] spans, phonetic spellings, marked pauses/chunking, contractions/weak forms, ALL-CAPS/italics for stress) and avoid inferring L1-specific errors from nationality alone. The judge should score using **descriptor anchor phrases** ("wide range, flexibly, easy to understand"), not invented language.

## Authoritative material

### Official band descriptors (public version)

The IELTS public Speaking Band Descriptors define Pronunciation around four operational axes: **range of pronunciation features**, **flexibility/control**, **intelligibility (listener effort / mother-tongue effect)**, and **mispronunciation patterns** (frequency and whether they affect clarity). The headline phrases per band, as reproduced across IELTS.org, British Council, Cambridge, and IDP:

- **Band 9** — "Uses a full range of pronunciation features with precision and subtlety. Sustains flexible use of features throughout. Is effortless to understand." (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf; https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf)
- **Band 8** — "Uses a wide range of pronunciation features. Sustains flexible use of features, with only occasional lapses. Is easy to understand throughout; L1 accent has minimal effect on intelligibility."
- **Band 7** — "Shows all the positive features of Band 6, and some, but not all, of the positive features of Band 8." (Band 7 is the only band defined by reference.)
- **Band 6** — "Uses a range of pronunciation features with mixed control. Shows some effective use of features but this is not sustained. Can generally be understood throughout, though mispronunciation of individual words or sounds reduces clarity at times."
- **Band 5** — "Shows all the positive features of Band 4, and some, but not all, of the positive features of Band 6."
- **Band 4** — "Uses a limited range of pronunciation features. Attempts to control features but lapses are frequent. Mispronunciations are frequent and cause some difficulty for the listener."
- **Band 2** — mispronunciations "severely affect" understanding (https://ieltsagape.wordpress.com/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors/).

The official descriptors note that a candidate must fully fit the positive features of a descriptor at a level and is rated on **average performance across all parts of the test**, so a judge should not over-score a single memorised or polished answer if later speech loses control (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).

### The assessable feature set ("range of features")

The IELTS Speaking Key Assessment Criteria define Pronunciation as accurate and sustained use of phonological features to convey meaningful messages, and identify the following indicators (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf). Cambridge, British Council, and IDP examiner-training material paraphrase the same feature set (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-pronunciation; https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-pronunciation-for-the-ielts-speaking-test-discover-our-new-video-series; https://assets.cambridgeenglish.org/webinars/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf):

1. **Phonemes** — the ~44 sounds of English (20 vowels, 24 consonants), including pairs absent from many L1s: /θ ð/, /ɪ iː/, /ʊ uː/, /æ e/, /v w/, /r l/, /ʃ ʒ/, /tʃ dʒ/ (https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-improve-pronunciation-ielts-speaking; https://ieltsetc.com/ielts-pronunciation-tips-speaking-test/).
2. **Word stress** — the syllable that bears prominence, e.g. `PHO-to-graph` vs `pho-TO-graph-y` vs `pho-to-GRA-phic`; also stress-shift pairs like *DESert/deSERT*, *PREsent/preSENT* that change part of speech (https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-does-ielts-speaking-assess-your-accent).
3. **Sentence stress and weak forms / vowel reduction** — prominence on content words to mark meaning; function words reduced to schwa (*and→/ən/*, *to→/tə/*, *for→/fə/*, *of→/əv/*, *was→/wəz/*); ability to shift prominence for contrast (e.g. "I lost **ALL** my money" vs "I lost all **MY** money"). Loss of weak forms is a reliable marker pulling a candidate from Band 7 to Band 6 on rhythm (https://eflmagazine.com/pronunciation-ielts-speaking/; https://keithspeakingacademy.com/ielts-speaking-pronunciation-features-practice/).
4. **Connected speech / linking** — C→V linking (*an apple* → /əˈnæpəl/), elision (*next day* → /neksdeɪ/), assimilation (*ten bikes* → /tem baɪks/), intrusion (*go on* → /ɡəʊwɒn/) (https://ieltsetc.com/ielts-pronunciation-tips-speaking-test/).
5. **Chunking** — pausing at phrase boundaries rather than mid-phrase; "delivering chunks of information…with short silences in between" (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-pronunciation).
6. **Intonation** — falling for completed declaratives and wh-questions, rising for yes/no questions and list-non-final items, fall-rise for contrast/uncertainty (https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-i-is-for-intonation).
7. **Rhythm** — English is stress-timed: stressed syllables roughly equidistant, intervening syllables compressed. Syllable-timed L1s (French, Spanish, Korean, Vietnamese, Italian, Mandarin) characteristically over-articulate every syllable, producing a "machine-gun" rhythm that pulls bands down (https://eflmagazine.com/pronunciation-ielts-speaking/).

### Accent vs. pronunciation marking principles

- **Accent is not penalised in itself.** "No specific accent required. IELTS is international English… If your accent requires significant effort to understand, your score drops" (https://ieltsliz.com/ielts-speaking-accent-british-us-or-other/; https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-does-ielts-speaking-assess-your-accent; https://ielts.idp.com/canada/prepare/article-accents-ielts-speaking-scores).
- **Mother-tongue effect is explicitly invoked at Band 8** ("L1 accent has minimal effect on intelligibility") — L1 accent CAN exist at Band 8/9 but must not force the listener to back-translate (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).
- **Range × control separates 7 from 8.** Band 7 *shows* features (some sentence stress, some weak forms, some linking) but not consistently and not all; Band 8 *sustains* a wide set across the whole test (https://ielts.com.au/australia/about/news-and-articles/article-speaking-band-6-vs-band-7).
- **Memorised/over-rehearsed answers can backfire.** Pronunciation is designed to assess natural production rather than rehearsed display — over-rehearsed answers may sound fluent in a transcript but still fail if delivery is monotone or not interactive (https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/demystifying-the-ielts-speaking-test; https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-pronunciation).

### IELTS research base

Isaacs, Trofimovich, Yu, and Munoz Chereau's IELTS research report examined which linguistic factors discriminate the upper levels of the revised IELTS Pronunciation scale using segmental, prosodic, fluency, and lexicogrammatical measures rated by accredited IELTS examiners (https://ielts.org/researchers/our-research/research-reports/examining-the-linguistic-aspects-of-speech-that-most-efficiently-discriminate-between-upper-levels-of-the-revised-ielts-pronunciation-scale; https://cdn.ielts.org/Research/examining-linguistic-aspects-of-speech-isaacs-et-al-2015.pdf).

Cambridge English ELT classroom guidance recommends recording and speech-recognition transcription as a practical activity to notice pronunciation features and intelligibility problems — relevant for automated corpus-building from diarized transcripts (https://www.cambridge.org/elt/blog/2019/03/27/ielts-speaking-improving-pronunication/).

## Country-specific prep material

### Korea (한국어)

Korean L1 transfer produces a tightly-clustered error profile and is the most relevant for this corpus.

- **British Council Korea**'s Korean-language pronunciation page lists word stress, sentence stress, intonation, and chunking as the assessed features (https://www.britishcouncil.kr/exam/ielts/advice-resources/ielts-videos/pronunciation). Korean prep listings for IELTS Speaking academies highlight linking, rules, and intonation practice in Parts 2 and 3, reflecting local cram-school attention to connected speech rather than only vocabulary scripts (https://whereisyourwork.com/education-english-ielts-speaking-academy/).
- **L vs R**: Korean has one liquid ㄹ that surfaces as a flap intervocalically and as a lateral in coda. Word-final /r/ and /l/ collapse ("four"→"fall", "berry"≈"belly"); /r/ often produced as a tap or with insufficient retroflex/bunched articulation (https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/pronunciation-problems-koreans-face-when-learning-alison-tomlinson; https://hadarshemesh.com/magazine/english-for-korean-speakers/).
- **F/V → P/B; Z → J/S; TH → S/T/D**: e.g. *very*→*berry*, *fine*→*pine*, *thanks*→*sanks*/*tanks*, *doesn't*→*doe-chn't* (https://pronunciationstudio.com/korean-speakers-english-pronunciation-errors/; https://hadarshemesh.com/magazine/english-for-korean-speakers/).
- **Final-stop devoicing/insertion**: Korean codas neutralise voicing (B/D/G→P/T/K) and prohibit released stops; speakers either devoice (*bag*→*bak*) or epenthesise an /ɯ/ (*bus*→*ba-su*, *milk*→*mil-ku*) (https://www.academia.edu/6019976/L1_to_L2_Transfer_of_Korean_Learners_of_English).
- **Consonant-cluster epenthesis**: Korean syllables ban onset clusters, so *school*→*su-kul*, *strike*→*sŭ-tŭ-ra-ik* — increases syllable count and breaks rhythm.
- **Vowel mergers**: /iː ɪ/ → /i/ (*sheep*≈*ship*); /uː ʊ/ → /u/ (*pool*≈*pull*); /æ/ → /e/ (*pat*→*pet*) (https://mountainscholar.org/bitstreams/7933c70e-a329-4458-8463-c49096c6ef9f/download).
- **Flat stress / syllable-timing**: Korean has no lexical stress and roughly equal-prominence syllables; speakers stress every syllable equally in English, killing weak forms and rhythm — a strong Band-6 ceiling marker.
- **TALK Schools** lists Korean-speaker issues with sentence rhythm, intonation, stress, diphthongs, consonant pairs, and long/short vowel contrasts (https://blog.talk.edu/learn-english/common-pronunciation-errors-korean-speakers/). Korean KCI research summaries report Korean EFL learners' problematic areas include vowels, stress, pause, rhythm, and intonation (https://www.kci.go.kr/kciportal/landing/article.kci?arti_id=ART002219699).
- **Korean cram-school perspective** (https://ieltskorea.org/korea/results/scores/speaking; https://blog.testglider.com/ielts-speaking-%EC%99%84%EB%B2%BD-%EA%B0%80%EC%9D%B4%EB%93%9C-%EC%9C%A0%ED%98%95%EB%B3%84-%EB%B6%84%EC%84%9D-%EB%B0%8F-%EA%B3%A0%EB%93%9D%EC%A0%90-%EC%A0%84%EB%9E%B5/): mark word stress for every new word, shadow for rhythm rather than for accent, drill /æ/–/e/ and /iː/–/ɪ/ minimal pairs, drill final consonants without an epenthetic vowel.

### Mainland China (中文)

- The Chinese IELTS official site provides a Chinese translation of the Speaking Band Descriptors but states that the English version is authoritative (https://backoffice.ielts.chinaielts.org/api/assets/ielts-cms/0c5929f5-faad-45ca-83f5-ce5c8384ec95/%E8%AF%84%E5%88%86%E6%A0%87%E5%87%86-speakingfinal.pdf?version=1).
- **Final-consonant deletion or schwa insertion**: Mandarin syllables end in vowel, /n/, or /ŋ/. Result: *wood*→/wʊdə/ or *glass*→*gla*; "dark L" /ɫ/ retracted to /u/ or /w/, *ball*→*bau* (https://www.fluentu.com/blog/english/english-pronunciation-for-chinese-speakers/; https://www.callearning.com/blog/2013/12/pronunciation-errors-for-chinese-speakers/).
- **Tone interference**: Mandarin uses lexical pitch on each syllable; speakers transfer this onto English so each *word* carries pitch movement, blocking the sentence-level intonation contour. The dominant Band-6-vs-7 cue for Mandarin candidates (https://blog.talk.edu/grammar/the-top-6-english-pronunciation-errors-made-by-native-chinese-speakers/; https://blog.talk.edu/learn-english/common-pronunciation-errors-chinese-speakers/).
- **Stress over-distribution**: Chinese speakers tend to stress too many English syllables and give weak syllables full vowels (https://sites.duke.edu/writingstudio/files/2013/12/Learner-English-Chinese.pdf).
- **/v/–/w/**, **/n/–/l/** (regional, especially southern), **/θ ð/ → /s z/ or /f v/** (https://magoosh.com/ielts/english-pronunciation-resources-native-chinese-speakers/).
- **新东方 New Channel / Xindongfang** says IELTS pronunciation is not only word accuracy but also linking, appropriate pauses, word stress, and natural intonation, and lists common errors as wrong word stress, ignored connected speech and weak forms such as reduced "and", and unnatural intonation (https://www.xhd.cn/ielts/kouyu/161942.html; https://km.xhd.cn/m/info/ieltsSpeak/934657.html; https://ielts.xdf.cn/201706/10683720.html — Chinese candidates' strongest dimension is vocabulary; weakest is FC and PR; most common deduction is "only word-level stress, no sentence-level stress").
- **沪江 hujiang** lists "speaking too fast without pauses" and "stressing every word equally" as top pronunciation-related deductions (https://liuxue.hujiang.com/ielts/speaking/p367455/).
- **TestDaily 厚朴优学** walks through descriptor language band-by-band, noting Chinese candidates are typically stuck at Band 6 on PR because they treat each Mandarin character as a stressed unit (https://www.testdaily.cn/ielts-speak-20201020/).

### Vietnam

- **DOL English** (Vietnamese IELTS provider) defines IELTS Pronunciation as clear individual sounds, sound-linking, rhythm, sentence stress, chunking, and intonation, noting that Vietnamese tone (rather than English lexical stress) is a disadvantage (https://www.dolenglish.vn/blog/tieu-chi-pronunciation-trong-ielts-speaking). A FLYER PDF lists word stress, linking, effective use of features, flexibility, intelligibility, and limited L1 effect as higher-band features (https://flyer.vn/wp-content/uploads/2024/05/TIEU-CHI-P-IELTS.pdf?x30689=).
- **Final-consonant omission** is the #1 issue: Vietnamese codas are limited and unreleased — *fine* loses /n/, *five* loses /v/, *fights* loses /t/, *cold*→*co*, *right*→*ri* (https://zim.vn/nhung-loi-phat-am-pho-bien-nhat-trong-bai-thi-ielts-speaking-phan-1; https://pasal.edu.vn/cac-loi-phat-am-trong-ielts-speaking-cua-nguoi-trinh-do-duoi-60-n1523.html; https://blog.talk.edu/learn-english/common-pronunciation-errors-vietnamese-speakers/).
- **/θ ð/ → /t d s z/**, **/dʒ/ → /z/ or /d/**, **/j/ dropped** (*year*→*ear*) (https://zim.vn/nhung-loi-phat-am-pho-bien-nhat-trong-bai-thi-ielts-speaking-phan-1).
- **Mis-syllabification**: monosyllables split into two — *green*→*gờ-rin* (https://ieltsmentor.edu.vn/blog/cam-nang-speaking/ielts-speaking-cac-loi-phat-am-phan-lon-nguoi-viet-deu-mac.html).
- **Plural -s mis-rule**: default /s/ for all endings, missing /z/ and /ɪz/ (https://onthiielts.com.vn/loi-phat-am-tieng-anh-cua-nguoi-viet/).
- **Tone transfer**: Vietnamese has 6 lexical tones; speakers sometimes give English words a tonal contour that fragments sentence intonation (https://ccsenet.org/journal/index.php/elt/article/download/0/0/50828/55087).
- **Short /i/, sound lengthening**, and word stress also flagged by Eduling (https://www.eduling.org/post/three-common-pronunciation-features-vietnamese-learners-need-to-improve). Empirical study reports final consonants, consonant clusters, vowel distinctions, stress, intonation, rhythm, and linking as recurring problem areas (https://www.ijsshr.in/v7i5/Doc/74.pdf).
- **ZIM Academy** (Vietnam's largest IELTS publisher) explicitly states linking ("nối âm") is the dominant Band-6→7 marker for Vietnamese candidates (https://zim.vn/cac-loi-phat-am-o-nguoi-hoc-ielts-trinh-do-duoi-6-0; https://zim.vn/nhung-loi-phat-am-pho-bien-nhat-trong-bai-thi-ielts-speaking-phan-2).

### Japan

- **British Council Japan**'s IELTS pronunciation page lists word stress, sentence stress, intonation, and chunking as assessed aspects (https://www.britishcouncil.jp/en/exam/ielts/prepare/test-tips/speaking-pronunciation). Improve Your Accent says Japanese speakers should work on English stress because Japanese tends to give syllables more equal weight (https://improveyouraccent.co.uk/pronunciation-tips-japanese-speakers/).
- **R/L collapse to flap ɾ**: only one liquid in Japanese (ら-row); both English /r/ and /l/ realised as a tap (https://www.hpu.edu/research-publications/tesol-working-papers/2014/new-with-metadata/tesol-wps-2014-lesley.pdf).
- **CV epenthesis ("katakana English")**: every English consonant gets a moraic vowel attached — *strike*→/sɯ.toɾai.kɯ/, *first*→/ɸaː.sɯ.to/. Inflates syllable count and demolishes rhythm (https://www.tofugu.com/japanese/katakanization/; https://blog.talk.edu/learn-english/common-pronunciation-errors-japanese-speakers/; https://www.jstage.jst.go.jp/article/ast/31/5/31_5_320/_pdf).
- **/v/→/b/** (no /v/ in native Japanese), **/θ ð/→/s z/**, **/f/→/ɸ/ or /h/**, **/si/→/ʃi/** (*see*→*shee*) (https://www.tefl.net/elt/articles/home-abroad/japanese-english-pronunciation-changes/; https://pronunciationstudio.com/japanese-speakers-english-pronunciation-errors/).
- **Mora-timed rhythm**: Japanese is mora-timed (each mora equal), pulling speakers further from English stress-timing than even syllable-timed L1s.

### Spanish-speaking regions

- **IDP** says many Spanish-speaking learners struggle with English vowel length, with *ship* and *sheep* often collapsed because Spanish has fewer vowel contrasts (https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-does-ielts-speaking-assess-your-accent). **IDP Mexico** publishes pronunciation guidance on word stress, sentence stress, and intonation (https://ielts.idp.com/mexico/prepare/article-pronunciation-skills-word-sentence-stress-intonation-ielts-speaking).
- **No schwa** — Spanish has 5 clear vowels, no reduction; speakers articulate every vowel fully, killing weak forms (*for example* → /fɔɾ eksampl/ instead of /fər ɪɡˈzɑːmpl/) (https://pronunciationstudio.com/spanish-speakers-english-pronunciation-errors/; https://tefltrainer.com/pronunciation/pronunciation-problems-for-spanish-learners-of-english/).
- **/ɪ iː/, /ʊ uː/, /æ e/ mergers**; **/b v/ confusion** (Spanish /b/ and /v/ are allophones).
- **Prothetic /e/ before s+C onsets**: *Spain*→*Espain*, *student*→*estudent* (https://languagelearnershub.com/blog/english-pronunciation/; https://www.tefl.net/elt/articles/teacher-technique/spanish-speaker-pronunciation-problems/).
- **Voiceless stops un-aspirated**: *pat*/*bat*, *tin*/*din* can collapse word-initially because Spanish /p t k/ lack the English aspiration that listeners use as a voicing cue.
- **Packard Communications**: two primary factors for Spanish-speaker English pronunciation are word stress and speech-sound differences; English word-final consonant clusters contrast with Spanish patterns (https://packardcommunications.com/pronunciation-english-spanish/).
- Other segmental/prosodic patterns include connected-speech treatment of /h/ in function words (https://pronunciationstudio.com/spanish-speakers-english-pronunciation-errors/).

### Arabic-speaking regions

- **British Council Egypt**'s IELTS pronunciation page lists word stress, sentence stress, intonation, and chunking as assessed features (https://www.britishcouncil.org.eg/en/exam/ielts/courses-resources/videos/pronunciation).
- **No /p/** — substituted with /b/; *park*/*bark* collapse (https://blog.talk.edu/learn-english/common-pronunciation-problems-arabic-speakers/; https://boldvoice.com/blog/english-pronunciation-arabic-speakers).
- **/v/ confused with /f/** (no /v/ in Modern Standard Arabic): *very*→*ferry*.
- **Vowel inventory mismatch**: MSA has 3 short + 3 long vowels (/a i u/ × length); English 12 monophthongs collapse onto these. Particular trouble: /ɒ ʌ/ (*lock*/*luck*), /eɪ əʊ/ shortened to /e ɒ/ (https://www.britishaccentacademy.com/most-common-english-pronunciation-errors-made-by-arabic-speakers/).
- **Cluster-breaking via vowel insertion**: *spring*→*sipring*, *children*→*chiledren* (https://www.researchgate.net/publication/309290691_PRONUNCIATION_DIFFICULTIES_FOR_ARAB_LEARNERS_OF_ENGLISH).
- **Dark-L and /ŋ/** often back-realised; **/r/** trilled or tapped.
- **TH substitution, epenthetic vowels, stress shifts** documented as confusing/ambiguous output (https://bspace.buid.ac.ae/bitstreams/cf83a81a-bbb1-46ff-a4a1-5e8885dc54ed/download; https://www.eajournals.org/wp-content/uploads/English-Pronunciation-Problems-Encountered-by-Basic-School-Pupils-in-Sudan.pdf).

### Iran and Persian/Farsi speakers

- **IDP IELTS Iran** advises focus on stress, intonation, and rhythm because these features communicate meaning and influence intelligibility, not native accent (https://ielts.idp.com/iran/prepare/article-accents-ielts-speaking-scores). IDP Iran's 5 tips: practice sounds missing from L1; karaoke for intonation; drill with diagrams of articulatory muscles; repeat for muscle memory; speak with native speakers outdoors (https://ielts.idp.com/iran/prepare/article-5-tips-for-pronunciation-ielts-speaking). Tahasoni's Iranian IELTS instructor-training material includes pronunciation, intonation, and word stress (https://tahasoni.ir/ttc/download/ielts_ttc_syllabus_speaking.pdf).
- **/w/→/v/** (no /w/ in Persian): *wonder*→*vonder*, *we*→*ve* (https://boldvoice.com/blog/english-pronunciation-persian-speakers; https://www.speechactive.com/our-top-5-english-pronunciation-tips-for-farsi-speakers/).
- **/θ ð/→/t d/ or /s z/**, **/ŋ/→/ng/** (cluster realised) (https://www.academia.edu/81520546/A_Survey_on_Several_Potentially_Problematic_Areas_of_Pronunciation_for_Iranian_Efl_Learners).
- **Prothetic vowel before s+C**: like Spanish, *school*→*eskool*, *sport*→*esport*.
- **Vowel substitutions**: /æ/→/e/, /ɑː/→/ɔː/, /ʊ/→/uː/, /ɪə/→/eə/, /aɪ/→/ɔɪ/ (https://www.researchgate.net/publication/260602889_An_Analysis_of_Iranian_EFL_Learners'_Pronunciation_Errors; https://cdn.pronunciationpro.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/Farsi-NEW.pdf).

### India, Hindi, and Urdu

- **EEC Global** (Indian IELTS coaching): Indian English may use flat intonation; recommends shadowing for rhythm, stress, intonation (https://eecglobal.com/blog/ielts-speaking-tips-part-1-2-3-2026). **IELTS Mumbai**: stress and intonation help speech sound natural and are factors examiners listen for (https://ieltsmumbai.com/blog/ielts-speaking-the-role-of-stress-and-intonation-in-ielts-speaking).
- **/w v/ collapse to bilabial /ʋ/**: *wine*/*vine* indistinguishable to many listeners (https://hadarshemesh.com/magazine/english-for-urdu-and-hindi-speakers/).
- **Retroflex /ʈ ɖ/ for English alveolar /t d/**: gives the characteristic "Indian English" timbre but is *intelligible* — does not pull bands unless paired with other features (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hindustani_phonology).
- **No aspiration distinction** the way English uses it in /ph th kh/ initially; voicing perception in plosives can be unreliable for listeners.
- **Syllable-timing**: South Asian English is famously syllable-timed; vowels are not reduced, weak forms weak or absent → flat rhythm. Indian-IELTS prep emphasises **stress placement** as the single biggest lift (e.g. `de-VEL-op-ment` not `DE-vel-OP-ment`).
- **/θ ð/ → /t̪ d̪/** (dental stops) — usually intelligible.
- Over-stressing too many words, and TH substitutions are additional flags (https://hadarshemesh.com/magazine/english-for-urdu-and-hindi-speakers/).

### Pakistan and Urdu/Punjabi-influenced English

- **IELTSNOVA** says intonation and stress affect clarity, meaning, naturalness, and engagement; recommends listening, imitation, script marking, recording, and stress/intonation exercises (https://www.ieltsnova.com/ielts-speaking-intonation-and-stress/).
- **Pakistan Journal of Social Research** paper on Pakistani English says content/function word stress differences are often overlooked and links that to rhythmic differences from Received Pronunciation (https://pjsr.com.pk/wp-content/uploads/2023/02/97.-Vol.-4-No.-1-March-2022-Jadoon-Ahmad-Pakistani-English-A-Glimpse-of-Phonetics-and-Phonological-Features.pdf).
- A Pakistani EFL pronunciation study reports speakers may stress the wrong syllable or use wrong intonation and discusses /v/ and /w/ articulation, so meaning confusion such as "west" vs "vest" needs explicit transcript evidence (https://archives.palarch.nl/index.php/jae/article/download/6300/6153).

### Turkey

- **No /θ ð/, no /w/ vs /v/ contrast**; vowel-harmony habits push speakers toward articulating each vowel fully (https://blog.talk.edu/learn-english/common-pronunciation-errors-turkish-speakers/; https://boldvoice.com/blog/english-pronunciation-turkish-speakers).
- **Diphthong simplification or doubling**: *pain* either flattened to /pen/ or over-pronounced as /pa.ɪn/.
- **Final-obstruent devoicing**: *dog*→*dok*, *bag*→*bak*.

### Indonesia / Malay

- **IDP Indonesia common mistakes** highlights pronunciation as a recurring issue area (https://ielts.idp.com/indonesia/about/news-and-articles/article-common-mistakes-in-ielts-speaking-test-you-should-avoid). General IDP pronunciation guidance applies directly to Indonesian candidates (https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-improve-pronunciation-ielts-speaking).
- **Phonemes absent**: /θ ð/, /ʃ ʒ/, /v/, sometimes /z/. Substitutions: *think*→*tink*, *very*→*fery* or *wery* (https://globalenglish.co.id/6-kesalahan-umum-pelafalan-kosakata-bahasa-inggris/; https://www.zenius.net/blog/belajar-pronunciation-bahasa-inggris/).
- **No diphthongs**: /eɪ əʊ aɪ aʊ ɔɪ/ tend to be monophthongised.
- **Spelling-to-sound** is direct in Indonesian, so candidates pronounce English the way it's spelled (*vehicle*→/ve.hi.kel/ instead of /ˈviːəkl/).
- **Syllable-timed**, each syllable roughly equal.
- Indonesian EFL research reports L1 Indonesian/Gayo/Acehnese learners' English segmental errors concentrate in final stops, final sibilants, affricates, and interdentals, with common realisations including elision, devoicing, and lack of release (https://bridges.monash.edu/articles/journal_contribution/Errors_in_pronunciation_of_consonants_by_learners_of_English_as_a_foreign_language_whose_first_languages_are_Indonesian_Gayo_and_Acehnese/5063614). Indonesian TEFL material flags vowel epenthesis to break clusters and difficulty with final consonants (https://repository.uhamka.ac.id/48477/1/E-BOOK_Metodologi%20Pengajaran%20Bahasa%20Inggris%20sebagai%20Bahasa%20Asing%20%28TEFL%29_November%202025.pdf). Indonesian-accented English research notes listener distraction from consonant devoicing and final -ed issues (https://jurnal.uisu.ac.id/index.php/languageliteracy/article/download/3738/pdf).

### Russia / CIS

- **Russian-language IELTS prep** says intonation and word stress can affect the Speaking pronunciation result and notes Pronunciation is 25% of the Speaking score (https://www.ielts.international/ru/ielts-preparation-tips/speaking-guide).
- **Word-final devoicing**: /d→t, g→k, z→s, v→f/ — *good*→*goot* (https://pronunciationstudio.com/russian-speakers-english-pronunciation-errors/; https://www.engvid.com/english-pronunciation-for-russian-speakers/).
- **Dark-L palatalised; clear-L palatalised before front vowels** → distinctive L-quality.
- **/w/→/v/**, **/θ ð/→/s z/**, **/ŋ/→/ng/**.
- **Heavy consonant articulation**; Russian *has* unstressed vowel reduction, which is an advantage for English schwa (https://www.britishaccentacademy.com/most-common-english-pronunciation-errors-made-by-russian-speakers/).
- Russian has far fewer vowel sounds than GB English, so attention to schwa and weak syllables is encouraged.

### Kazakhstan

- Nazarbayev University research: Kazakh-Russian bilinguals' acquisition of English lexical stress is affected by differences between Russian, English, and Kazakh stress/vowel-reduction systems, with Kazakh described as fixed-stress without vowel reductions (https://research.nu.edu.kz/en/publications/vowel-reductions-in-the-acquisition-of-english-lexical-stress-by-).

## Open-source tools and code

- **Speechocean762** — open non-native English speech corpus for pronunciation assessment: 5,000 utterances from 250 non-native speakers, expert annotation at sentence/word/phoneme levels; CC BY 4.0 (https://arxiv.org/abs/2104.01378; https://github.com/jimbozhang/speechocean762; https://huggingface.co/datasets/mispeech/speechocean762/blob/7b69e51b3d13bd5e7d3026174c5ca857ee71c1d5/speechocean762.py).
- **GOPT** — BSD-3-Clause PyTorch implementation of Goodness Of Pronunciation Feature-Based Transformer for multi-aspect, multi-granularity non-native English pronunciation assessment; reports phone/word/sentence-level Pearson correlations on SpeechOcean762 (https://github.com/YuanGongND/gopt; https://arxiv.org/abs/2205.03432).
- **Kaldi `gop_speechocean762` recipe** — phoneme-level Goodness of Pronunciation feature extraction; requires audio and forced alignment (https://github.com/kaldi-asr/kaldi/tree/master/egs/gop_speechocean762).
- **SpeechSuper API Samples** — MIT-licensed sample code for scripted and IELTS-oriented spontaneous-speech pronunciation assessment at phoneme/syllable/word/sentence levels, with fluency metrics, rhythm/speed, syllable stress, liaison, loss of plosion, rising/falling sentence tone; 8 languages (https://github.com/speechsuper/SpeechSuper-API-Samples).
- **Speechace** — commercial pronunciation and fluency assessment API: phoneme/syllable feedback, lexical stress, intonation, pausing, pitch, speaking/articulation rate, IELTS/CEFR/PTE/TOEIC scoring, spontaneous open-ended speaking (https://www.speechace.com/api-plans/; https://www.postman.com/speechace/speechace-s-public-workspace/documentation/fwsskik/speechace-api).
- **CMUSphinx pronunciation evaluation** — classic forced-alignment + edit-distance scoring (Goodness-of-Pronunciation, GOP); BSD-style licence (https://cmusphinx.github.io/wiki/pronunciation_evaluation/).
- **IELTS_PracticeAndEvaluation** — educational project combining OpenAI + Azure Speech Studio to produce a band estimate; reference architecture only (https://github.com/ZainabZaman/IELTS_PracticeAndEvaluation).
- **GitHub topic `pronunciation-scoring`** — academic implementations including "Score-balanced Loss for Multi-aspect Pronunciation Assessment" (Interspeech 2023) and multi-aspect GOP variants (https://github.com/topics/pronunciation-scoring).
- **Korean L2-English error corpus** — "Comparison of L2 Korean pronunciation error patterns from five L1 backgrounds by using automatic phonetic transcription"; baseline of L1-specific phonemic substitution probabilities (https://arxiv.org/pdf/2306.10821).
- **Automatic pronunciation assessment surveys & multimodal LLM approaches** — generally require learner audio + prompt text, not transcript only (https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.13974; https://arxiv.org/abs/2407.09209; https://arxiv.org/abs/2101.06396).

For a transcript-only LLM judge these tools are reference-only; the judge cannot run acoustic models, but the *error catalogues* are usable to predict likely phonetic errors from spelling-only hints in a transcript (e.g., a Korean candidate writing "berry" where context demands "very" is a hint of F→B/V→B transfer).

## Practical signals for an LLM judge

1. **Use descriptor anchor phrases verbatim** when justifying a band: Band 6 ↔ "range of features with mixed control"; Band 7 ↔ "all positive features of Band 6, *some* of Band 8"; Band 8 ↔ "wide range, sustained flexibly, easy to understand"; Band 9 ↔ "full range, precision and subtlety." Avoid inventing language outside the official set.
2. **Without audio, do not score pronunciation in isolation higher than 7.** A clean transcript cannot verify a "wide range of pronunciation features." Cap at 7 unless transcribed prosodic cues (caps for stress, ellipses for pauses, contractions, weak-form spellings) explicitly show stress, weak forms, chunking, or contrastive intonation. Band 8 requires sustained features across long utterances which transcript-only text usually cannot demonstrate.
3. **Do not award or penalise Pronunciation from ordinary spelling, vocabulary, or grammar** in a clean manual transcript unless the transcript explicitly preserves phonetic spellings, inaudibility, repairs, or examiner misunderstanding (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf).
4. **Treat repeated examiner repair prompts** ("Sorry?", "Could you repeat that?", "Do you mean…?") or transcript tags like [inaudible]/[unclear] as strong evidence of reduced intelligibility. Band 8-9 should be easy/effortless to understand throughout; Band 4 allows patches that cannot be understood. **Cap pronunciation at 6** when the transcript shows persistent meaning-changing mispronunciations or repeated examiner repair.
5. **Separate accent from intelligibility.** An L1 accent with clear pronunciation, natural rhythm, and effective stress/intonation should not be penalised simply for sounding Korean, Chinese, Indian, Spanish, Arabic, Russian, etc. Use the **"mother tongue effect" rule as the Band 7↔8 hinge**: Band 7 may show L1 features that *occasionally* require listener effort; Band 8 must show only *minimal* L1 effect. Operational test: would a non-trained native English listener need to re-listen / back-translate to understand any utterance? If yes, ceiling is 7.
6. **Treat repeated misspellings of common words in a transcript as L1-transfer evidence**, but only as diagnostic hypotheses — not certainties from name/nationality. Examples: *berry/very, sink/think, ship/sheep, light/right, este/study, tank/thank*. If the candidate was *understood despite* substitutions, that's Band-6 territory at best.
7. **Chunking signals.** Commas placed at semantic boundaries, em-dashes, and explicit `[pause]` annotations correlate with Band 7+ chunking. Long run-on sentences without breaks, or unnatural pause breaks inside phrases, correlate with Band 6 chunking and weak rhythm/stress timing.
8. **Flat-rhythm cues.** If the transcript shows every content word lexically full (no contractions, `wanna`, `gonna`, `kinda`, `sorta`, `dunno`) and no marked pauses, that is consistent with syllable-timed rhythm and missing weak forms — a Band-6 ceiling marker, especially for L1s whose prep flags equal stress (Korean, Japanese, Indian, Spanish, Indonesian, Mandarin).
9. **Reward contrastive-stress and linking markers.** ALL-CAPS, italics, or `*emphasis*` markup on a content word suggests sentence-level stress for contrast (Band 7/8 feature). Phrases transcribed without word boundaries (*lotta, gotta, outta, kindof, sortof, whaddya, gonna*) are direct evidence of connected speech.
10. **Range vs. control distinction.** "Uses some weak forms but only on common collocations" = Band 7 (some features). "Uses weak forms, linking, contrastive stress, and chunking flexibly across all parts" = Band 8 (wide range, sustained).
11. **Word-stress errors matter most when in stress-shift pairs** that change part of speech (*PREsent* noun vs *preSENT* verb). Arbitrary syllable-stress drift on content words is a Band-6 marker but does not by itself drop below 6 unless it impedes understanding.
12. **Segmental errors matter most when they reduce clarity or change lexical identity.** Examples: Korean /f/-/p/ or /v/-/b/, Korean L/R collapse, Korean epenthesis; Chinese final-consonant loss and tone-on-word; Vietnamese final-consonant deletion; Japanese vowel epenthesis ("katakana English"); Spanish /s/-cluster prothetic /e/ and schwa absence; Arabic /p/-/b/ and vowel undershoot; Hindi-Urdu /w/-/v/ and TH→/t̪ d̪/; Indonesian final-stop/sibilant elision; Persian /w/→/v/.
13. **Do not infer L1-specific errors automatically from the candidate's name, nationality, or topic content.** Use them only as diagnostic hypotheses when transcript evidence, ASR alternatives, or examiner repairs show a matching intelligibility problem.
14. **Use Part 2 and Part 3 as pronunciation stress tests** because they create longer utterances where chunking, rhythm, stress timing, intonation, and connected speech can be sustained or break down. IELTS says the final score is based on average performance across all parts.
15. **If pronunciation problems lead to semantic confusion** in the transcript, count the listener-impact under Pronunciation even when the same surface issue also looks lexical or grammatical — official descriptors foreground whether individual words/phonemes require effort and whether mispronunciation reduces clarity.
16. **Band 6 vs Band 4 calibration.** A candidate with occasional isolated word/phoneme errors but generally understandable speech with some effective stress/intonation fits Band 6, not Band 4. Band 4 requires frequent mispronunciation with sustained listener effort.
17. **L1-aware weighting priors** (use only when L1 is signalled, never to penalise accent itself): Korean/Japanese — weight L/R, F/V, final clusters, rhythm; Mandarin — weight final consonants and word-level pitch transfer; Arabic — weight /p b/ and vowel quality; Spanish — weight schwa absence and prothetic /e/; Vietnamese — weight final-consonant deletion and linking; Hindi/Urdu — weight /w v/ and rhythm.
