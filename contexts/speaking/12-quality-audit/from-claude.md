# Speaking corpus audit

_Audited by claude on 2026-05-19T08:17:22Z._

## Overview

The corpus comprises 11 curated `final.md` files (topics 01–11) covering band descriptors, the three test parts, the 6→7 boundary, feedback voice, similar-repos/tooling, vocabulary/grammar, instructor tips, pronunciation, and fluency markers — roughly 5,050 lines of dense merged-crawl prose with each topic structured identically (Summary → Authoritative material → Country-specific prep material → Open-source tools and code → Practical signals for an LLM judge → Sources → Crawl notes). Every topic is a "codex + claude" merge with explicit crawl-notes calling out unique contributions, conflicts, and missed sections, so the corpus already documents its own provenance. The single most important strength is the extraordinary cross-regional reach (Korea, China, Vietnam, Iran, India, Pakistan, Indonesia, Russia/Kazakhstan, Japan, Brazil, Saudi Arabia/Gulf, UK/AU/Canada — each with native-language sources, L1-transfer diagnoses, and named cram-school tactics) anchored against the official IELTS / British Council / Cambridge descriptors. The single most important weakness is the proliferation of numeric "Band 7 vs Band 6" coaching heuristics (idiom density, discourse-marker counts, pause-length thresholds, wpm targets, error-free-sentence ratios, hedge density) that are quoted across topics with **mutually inconsistent** numbers and only sometimes flagged as non-official priors — a judge that mechanically applies them will produce false precision the official descriptors explicitly reject.

## Contradictions and conflicts

- **Idiom-density target for Band 7.** `02-part1-interview/final.md → ## Practical signals for an LLM judge #15` and `11-fluency-markers/final.md → ## Practical signals for an LLM judge #11` both target 1 natural idiom per ~90s; `04-part3-discussion/final.md → ## Practical signals for an LLM judge #13` says 1–3 idiomatic chunks per Part 3 answer; `01-band-descriptors/final.md → ## Practical signals for an LLM judge #11` says 1–2 per Part 2 turn (≥4–5 = memorised); `08-vocabulary-grammar/final.md → ## Practical signals for an LLM judge #1` cites IELTS Advantage's empirical Band-9 average of **1.2 idioms per practice session with many at zero**. The IELTS Advantage empirical observation is the most credible — quantity is not the lever and the official descriptors say so.
- **Idiom-density cap (Band 6 ceiling on zero idioms).** `01-band-descriptors/final.md → ## Practical signals #11` says "zero idiomatic items across the whole test caps LR at Band 6"; `08-vocabulary-grammar/final.md → ## Practical signals #1` says cap only when there is "zero idiomatic language of any *type*" (idiom + phrasal verb + fixed expression + conventional metaphor); the topic-08 framing is closer to the rubric ("idiomatic *language*", not idioms) and is more credible.
- **Discourse-marker count for Band 7.** `01-band-descriptors/final.md → ## Practical signals #9` says "Band 7 ≥ 5 distinct markers used appropriately"; `03-part2-cue-card/final.md → ## Practical signals #8` says "Band 7+ candidates typically deploy ≥4 cohesive devices per 90s"; `05-band-6-to-7/final.md → ## Practical signals #4` says "at least 4-6 distinct flexible markers" across the test; `09-instructor-tips/final.md → ## Practical signals #8` warns 0 markers ≈ Band 5–6 but ">5 marker types crammed in" ≈ Band 6 with memorisation flag; `11-fluency-markers/final.md → ## Practical signals #6` says "Band 7 expects ≥ 6 distinct markers across ≥ 3 categories". The topic-11 framing (count *and* category spread) is the most defensible because it captures the descriptor's "flexibility" requirement that the others lose.
- **Maximum acceptable pause length.** `11-fluency-markers/final.md → ### Conflicts and contested heuristics` lays this out explicitly: Chinese prep ≥3s, Iranian prep ≥5s, British Council classroom drill ≥10s; `09-instructor-tips/final.md → ## Practical signals #4` says ">2s mid-clause" caps FC at Band 6. None of these are official. The descriptors give no numerical cutoff (`01-band-descriptors/final.md → ### Public band descriptor matrix`); the BC 10s figure is a classroom-game stop condition, not a band cutoff, and is the only one with a primary source.
- **Speech rate.** `03-part2-cue-card/final.md → ## Practical signals #12` cites native-norm 130–160 wpm and >180 = loses pronunciation; `05-band-6-to-7/final.md` does not name numbers; `09-instructor-tips/final.md → ## Practical signals #7` says 130–160 wpm with >170 = clarity collapses; `02-part1-interview/final.md → ## Practical signals #10` says Band-7-ish ≈ 3.5–4.5 *syllables*/sec; `07-similar-repos/final.md → ## Practical signals #2` cites Speechace's commercial threshold of ≥120 wpm as "fluent". The 130–160 wpm range is consistent across coaching sources; the Speechace number is a commercial vendor's lower bar, not a Band 7 floor — topic 07 correctly flags this as vendor evidence, others slide past the distinction.
- **Part 3 answer length.** `04-part3-discussion/final.md → ## Practical signals #8` flags this as an explicit unresolved conflict: Korean ed:m says 2–3 sentences, Chinese New Oriental says 4–5, Western coaching (Keith/Speechful) says 30–60s / 70–150 words. The reconciliation in the same signal ("answer fully, don't pad") is the correct read — none is an absolute limit.
- **Self-correction cadence.** `01-band-descriptors/final.md → ## Practical signals #7` says "≤ 2 self-corrections per Part 2 turn is healthy / Band 7 compatible; ≥ 4 in one turn becomes a coherence cost"; `08-vocabulary-grammar/final.md → ## Practical signals #11` cites Liz Ferguson's "one self-correction per 30–45 seconds" cadence cap; `11-fluency-markers/final.md → ## Practical signals #4` says "three or more language-related corrections per Part-2 turn caps at Band 6". These are roughly compatible — about 2–3 per 2-minute Part 2 turn — but published as independent numbers across topics.
- **Error-free sentence ratio for Band 7.** `01-band-descriptors/final.md → ## Practical signals #13` says "≥ ~50% of complex sentences ... → Band 7-compatible; under 30% → Band 6"; `05-band-6-to-7/final.md → ## Practical signals #8` says "~30–40% of clauses across the transcript should contain subordination" and "~80% error-free speech" target from DOL Vietnam. The 50% and 80% targets are measuring different things (error-free *complex* sentences vs overall error-free clauses) but a judge mechanically applying both will double-count.
- **Hypothetical-question frequency in Part 3.** `04-part3-discussion/final.md → ### Canonical Part 3 question-type taxonomy` flags this explicitly: prep blogs treat hypothetical as one of 7 standard types, but the UsingEnglish 365-question Cambridge-book analysis found only 3 hypotheticals (~1%). The UsingEnglish count is the only empirical number and is more credible — coaching emphasis on hypothetical conditionals is over-weighted relative to the real test.
- **Part 3 "interesting question" stalling phrases.** `04-part3-discussion/final.md → ## Practical signals` permits "That's an interesting question" as content-bearing stall; `06-feedback-patterns/final.md → ## Practical signals #14` explicitly forbids motivational/stalling formulas that signal memorisation when used by the *examiner-style judge*, not the candidate — these are addressing different speakers and not in conflict, but topics 04 and 09 do not flag this asymmetry.

## Operational-signal review

- **Total count of "Practical signals for an LLM judge" bullets across all 11 topics:** topic 01 = 21; topic 02 = 18; topic 03 = 19; topic 04 = 22; topic 05 = 18; topic 06 = 21; topic 07 = 18; topic 08 = 17; topic 09 = 20; topic 10 = 17; topic 11 = 20. **Total ≈ 211 operational bullets** across the corpus.
- **Number of duplicates and near-duplicates:** at least ~80 bullets restate the same descriptor-anchored rule. Recurring restatements include: "accent is not penalised, intelligibility is" (10+ topics), "do not infer pronunciation from a clean transcript without audio" (topics 01, 02, 04, 06, 07, 10, 11), "memorisation is detected and downgraded" (topics 01, 02, 03, 04, 05, 06, 08, 09), "score the four criteria, do not invent labels like confidence" (topics 06, 07, 09), "Band 7 = sustained, Band 6 = intermittent" (topics 01, 05, 09), "do not auto-deduct for examiner interruption" (topics 02, 04, 09), "L1 fillers are penalised harder than English ones" (topics 06, 08, 09, 11), "report sub-scores then round the unrounded average" (topic 01 only, oddly not echoed). Net non-overlapping operational guidance is probably ~50–60 distinct rules wearing different labels.
- **Contradictions in operational signals:** the eight conflicts listed in the previous section are all in the "Practical signals" sections. The most acute are the discourse-marker count (≥4 vs ≥5 vs ≥6 vs 4–6 across topics 01/03/05/09/11), the idiom-density target, and the pause-length threshold.
- **Top non-overlapping operational signals (~15) the judge should actually apply** (verbatim with source):

  1. "**Average across all three parts.** Treat descriptors as 'average performance across all parts' — do not let one strong answer dominate" (`01-band-descriptors/final.md → ## Practical signals for an LLM judge #2`).
  2. "**Sustained vs intermittent is the single most reliable Band 6 ↔ 7 discriminator.** Do the desirable features (range, complexity, idiomaticity, prosody) appear throughout the transcript or only in flashes? Intermittent → Band 6; sustained → Band 7" (`01-band-descriptors/final.md → ## Practical signals for an LLM judge #4`).
  3. "**Cluster scoring, not one hard rule.** IELTS research found overlap between candidates at the same level and no single feature that separates all bands; combine pause burden, coherence, vocabulary flexibility, grammar range/accuracy and pronunciation evidence" (`01-band-descriptors/final.md → ## Practical signals for an LLM judge #5`).
  4. "**Classify hesitation by function (FC).** Content-planning hesitation ... is compatible with high bands. Mid-sentence searches for basic vocabulary/grammar, followed by silence or repeated function words, are Band 5–6 evidence" (`01-band-descriptors/final.md → ## Practical signals for an LLM judge #6`).
  5. "**Answer-the-exact-question discipline.** Seedhouse and Harris: high-scoring candidates understand the question, answer it, identify its inherent topic, and develop that topic. A fluent but off-question response should lower FC" (`04-part3-discussion/final.md → ## Practical signals for an LLM judge #1`).
  6. "**Personal vs. general framing.** If Part 3 answers contain ≥50 % first-person personal-narrative content ... cap FC and LR at Band 6 regardless of vocabulary, unless the question explicitly invited it" (`04-part3-discussion/final.md → ## Practical signals for an LLM judge #2`).
  7. "**Question-type ↔ grammar match.** Reward when a *future* question elicits modal/future forms ...; *hypothetical* elicits conditionals ...; *past-vs-present* elicits present perfect or `used to`; *comparison* elicits `whereas / while / by contrast`. Mismatches ... flag GRA at Band 6" (`04-part3-discussion/final.md → ## Practical signals for an LLM judge #3`).
  8. "**Duration as a hard prior on Fluency & Coherence.** <60s of candidate speech is strong negative evidence (≈ Band ≤5); 60–90s is acceptable but often thin (≈ Band 6 with prompting); sustained 90–120s with no examiner re-prompt is a Band 7+ precondition. ... Do **not** penalise the candidate merely for being stopped at two minutes" (`03-part2-cue-card/final.md → ## Practical signals for an LLM judge #2`).
  9. "**The rounding-off question is the candour test.** Compare fluency between the main turn and the 1–2 short follow-ups: a large drop suggests the long turn was rehearsed, and the speaking score should anchor closer to the round-off / Part 3 performance" (`03-part2-cue-card/final.md → ## Practical signals for an LLM judge #4`).
  10. "**Tense control across the talk.** Person/place/event cards typically need past-tense narrative + present-tense reflection ... + a hypothetical/future closing .... A turn locked in a single tense throughout caps Grammatical Range below Band 7" (`03-part2-cue-card/final.md → ## Practical signals for an LLM judge #6`).
  11. "**Anchor every error claim to a specific transcript span** in the format `Q<n> '<verbatim candidate phrase>' should be '<correction>'` or `Q<n> '<verbatim>' → '<better>'`. ... No anonymous 'you sometimes…' without an example" (`06-feedback-patterns/final.md → ## Practical signals for an LLM judge #3`).
  12. "**Hedge band predictions with 'estimated'** ('Your estimated score: Band 7') in the prose; commit to an integer only inside the JSON score field" (`06-feedback-patterns/final.md → ## Practical signals for an LLM judge #5`).
  13. "**Use the official four-criterion frame in fixed order: Fluency & Coherence → Lexical Resource → Grammatical Range & Accuracy → Pronunciation.** Never collapse, re-order, or invent labels such as 'confidence', 'ideas', 'delivery', or 'speaking skill'" (`06-feedback-patterns/final.md → ## Practical signals for an LLM judge #1`).
  14. "**Without audio, do not score pronunciation in isolation higher than 7.** A clean transcript cannot verify a 'wide range of pronunciation features.' Cap at 7 unless transcribed prosodic cues (caps for stress, ellipses for pauses, contractions, weak-form spellings) explicitly show stress, weak forms, chunking, or contrastive intonation" (`10-pronunciation-features/final.md → ## Practical signals for an LLM judge #2`).
  15. "**Hesitation type triage.** Tag every hesitation as content-related ... or language-related .... Heuristic: content:language ratio ≥ 4:1 supports Band 8+; ≤ 1:1 supports Band 6 or below" (`11-fluency-markers/final.md → ## Practical signals for an LLM judge #1`).
  16. "**L1 filler check.** Any non-English filler (음, 那个, ano, эээ, چی بگم, anu, eee) is a hard penalty — caps F&C at Band 6 even when other features are stronger" (`11-fluency-markers/final.md → ## Practical signals for an LLM judge #3`).
  17. "**Memorised-chunk detection.** Watch for register-incongruent fluent bursts: sudden flawless multi-clause sentences with low-frequency vocabulary that don't match surrounding speech texture" (`04-part3-discussion/final.md → ## Practical signals for an LLM judge #16`).
  18. "**Report sub-scores then round.** Report all four sub-scores separately and compute the rounded overall (nearest half band) from the unrounded average — do not pre-round per criterion. Worked example: 7/7/6/7 → 6.75 → **7.0**; 7/6/6/7 → 6.5" (`01-band-descriptors/final.md → ## Practical signals for an LLM judge #21`).

## Judge-prompt readiness verdict

The corpus is fit for purpose as judge context for a diarized speaking transcript, but only if the judge prompt actively filters the noise: it must privilege the cluster-scoring framing from `01-band-descriptors/final.md → ## Practical signals #5` over the parade of numeric heuristics; it must use the qualitative descriptor language from `06-feedback-patterns/final.md → ## Authoritative material` over any commercial-vendor threshold; and it must apply the pronunciation cap from `10-pronunciation-features/final.md → ## Practical signals #2` because the user's input is transcript-only. The judge will struggle most where the corpus already flags conflict: idiom density, discourse-marker count, pause-length threshold, speech rate, Part 3 answer length, and self-correction cadence — if the judge prompt names a single number for each, it must explain *why* it picked that number rather than averaging across topics. The judge will also struggle on Part 3 register decisions for Japanese, Brazilian, Saudi, Turkish, and Spanish-speaking candidates because the regional 6→7 deltas for those L1s are simply absent. Finally, the corpus's coverage of *examiner-style feedback voice* (topic 06) is the strongest single asset for an LLM judge that needs to emit feedback in a believable Cambridge register — that topic alone is high enough quality to anchor the writing voice of the judge output. Net: usable with care; rewrite the numeric heuristics into a single conflict-resolved table before shipping.
