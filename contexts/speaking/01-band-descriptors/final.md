# Band descriptors (0–9) for all four speaking criteria, plus Band 6 vs 7 deltas and examiner perspective

_Merged from codex + claude crawls on 2026-05-19T07:37:53Z._

## Summary

IELTS Speaking is a face-to-face, recorded 11–14 minute interview marked by a certificated examiner against four publicly published criteria — Fluency & Coherence (FC), Lexical Resource (LR), Grammatical Range & Accuracy (GRA), and Pronunciation (P) — each weighted equally at 25% (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/your-results/ielts-scoring-in-detail; https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking). The reported speaking score is the unrounded average of the four sub-scores, rounded to the nearest half band (https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-understanding-the-ielts-speaking-band-descriptors; https://ieltstutors.org/speaking-band-descriptors/).

Examiners apply a "best-fit" approach: the candidate is placed at the band where the bulk of features in their performance sit, even if individual features dip up or down by a band, and the rating reflects average performance across all three parts of the test (https://ielts.com.au/australia/about/news-and-articles/article-speaking-band-6-vs-band-7; https://cdn.ielts.org/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).

The most operationally important boundary is Band 6 vs Band 7. The single most reliable discriminator is **sustained vs intermittent**: Band 6 candidates show Band 7 traits in flashes (one strong Part 2 answer, occasional flexible vocabulary, intermittent complex grammar), while Band 7 candidates hold those traits across the whole interview — long turns without noticeable effort or loss of coherence, frequent error-free sentences, flexible discourse markers, and sustained prosodic control (https://cdn.ielts.org/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf; https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/differences-between-ielts-6-and-7-for-productive-skills).

For an automated judge, descriptors must be applied as a **cluster of evidence** across the full test, not by a single feature such as speed, idiom count, or grammar error rate. IELTS research finds high variation among candidates at the same level and overlap between adjacent levels, and no single speaking feature distinguished all bands in a 60-recording mixed-methods study (https://ielts.org/researchers/our-research/research-reports/candidate-discourse-in-the-revised-ielts-speaking-test; https://ielts.org/researchers/our-research/research-reports/the-relationship-between-speaking-features-and-band-descriptors-a-mixed-methods-study).

## Authoritative material

### Official scoring frame

- IELTS Speaking is a face-to-face interview, recorded, with certificated IELTS examiners assessing performance throughout against the four criteria (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking).
- Each criterion is equally weighted; the IELTS Speaking score is the average of the four sub-scores, rounded to the nearest half band, so a judge must not let one strong criterion mask weak evidence in another (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/your-results/ielts-scoring-in-detail; https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-understanding-the-ielts-speaking-band-descriptors).
- Rounding worked example: a candidate scoring 7/7/6/7 averages 6.75 → reported as **7.0**, while 7/6/6/7 averages 6.5. The judge should report the four sub-scores separately **and** the rounded overall, not pre-round per criterion (https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-understanding-the-ielts-speaking-band-descriptors; https://ieltstutors.org/speaking-band-descriptors/).
- The public descriptor PDF requires a candidate to "fully fit" the positive features of a band level and rates them on **average** performance across all parts, so a single good Part 2 answer cannot by itself justify a high band if Part 1 and Part 3 show weaker average performance (https://cdn.ielts.org/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).
- The Speaking test has three parts: Part 1 familiar topics, Part 2 a prepared long turn of up to two minutes after one minute of notes, and Part 3 a more abstract discussion linked to Part 2 (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking).
- Part 2 specifically tests the ability to speak at length, use appropriate language and organise ideas logically; Part 3 tests explaining opinions, analysing, discussing and speculating, so evidence of abstract language and complex grammar is especially likely in Part 3 (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking).
- IELTS NZ notes the recording can also be re-marked if the candidate queries the score, reinforcing that the band reflects the **whole** interview, not a moment-in-time impression (https://ielts.co.nz/newzealand/results/ielts-band-scores/speaking-band-score).

### Official construct definitions and key indicators

From the official IELTS Speaking Key Assessment Criteria document (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf):

- **Fluency and Coherence** means speaking with normal continuity, rate and effort, and linking ideas and language into coherent connected speech. Key indicators: speech rate, speech continuity, logical sequencing, relevant spoken sentences, appropriate pausing, spoken discourse markers, fillers, cohesive devices.
- IELTS defines a "spoken sentence" as the closest speech equivalent of a written sentence, but it may include elliptical or verbless structures if they perform a sentence-like function — a transcript judge should not penalise natural spoken ellipsis simply because it would be incomplete in writing.
- **Lexical Resource** means the range of vocabulary available, the precision with which meaning and attitude are conveyed, appropriacy for referential meaning/style/collocation, idiomatic expression, and the ability to paraphrase around a vocabulary gap.
- **Grammatical Range and Accuracy** means accurate and appropriate use of syntactic forms plus enough grammatical range to express complex propositions. Key indicators: spoken sentence length, subordinate clauses, verb phrase complexity (aspect/modality/passive voice), other phrase complexity such as modification, sentence-structure range for information focus, error density, and the communicative effect of errors.
- **Pronunciation** means accurate and sustained use of phonological features to convey meaning. Key indicators: meaningful chunking, rhythm, stress timing, linking/elision, emphatic or contrastive stress, intonation, word stress, vowel/consonant production, listener effort, and the effect of accent on intelligibility.

### Band 6 vs Band 7 deltas by criterion

| Criterion | Band 6 evidence | Band 7 evidence | Operational scoring delta |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fluency & Coherence | Willing to speak at length, but coherence is occasionally lost through hesitation/repetition/self-correction; connectives and discourse markers are present but not always appropriate. | Speaks at length **without noticeable effort or loss of coherence**; only language-related hesitation; discourse markers/connectives/cohesion are flexible. | Move 6 → 7 when hesitation and repairs remain local rather than derailing the answer, and when linking is flexible rather than mechanical or misused. |
| Lexical Resource | Enough vocabulary to discuss topics at length; inappropriate choices occur but meaning remains clear; paraphrase is generally successful. | **Flexible** vocabulary across varied topics; some less common/idiomatic items; awareness of style/collocation, with some inappropriate choices still possible; paraphrase is effective. | Move 6 → 7 when the candidate goes beyond sufficient topic vocabulary into flexible, precise, collocationally aware choices and recovers lexical gaps cleanly by paraphrase. |
| Grammatical Range & Accuracy | Simple and complex forms mixed with **limited flexibility**; complex structures often contain errors, rarely blocking communication. | Range of complex structures used with some flexibility; **frequently** produces error-free sentences, though some basic errors persist. | Move 6 → 7 when complex structures are not just attempted but frequently controlled, and when error-free clauses/sentences appear often enough to show flexible command. |
| Pronunciation | Range of phonological features with **mixed/variable control**; effective use is not sustained; rhythm/stress timing affected; occasional mispronunciation reduces clarity but the candidate is generally understood. | All Band 6 positives plus some Band 8 positives: sustained control of rhythm, stress, intonation, connected speech, and consistently easy understanding. | Move 6 → 7 when intelligibility is not merely adequate but consistently easy and at least some higher-band prosodic control is evident; do not require a native accent. |
| **Overall heuristic** | Right features **sometimes**. | Same features **sustained throughout**. | Sustained > intermittent is the single most reliable Band 6 ↔ 7 discriminator. |

### Examiner perspective and scoring behaviour

- IELTS Writing and Speaking examiners must have a related undergraduate degree, a recognised English-language teaching qualification, and at least three years of English-language teaching experience mostly with adults (https://ielts.idp.com/canada/prepare/article-understanding-ielts-examiners-and-how-they-score-tests).
- Examiner certification involves standardised training, practice marking, consistent and accurate marking, regular monitoring after certification, and **re-certification every two years** against Cambridge ESOL standardisation samples — this is what keeps the four sub-scores comparable globally (https://ielts.idp.com/canada/prepare/article-understanding-ielts-examiners-and-how-they-score-tests; https://ielts.ch/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/assessing-IELTS-speaking.pdf).
- The assessment is **best-fit**: when a candidate's features straddle two adjacent bands, the examiner picks the band where the majority of features sit, then asks whether enough features of the higher band are present to bump it (https://ielts.com.au/australia/about/news-and-articles/article-speaking-band-6-vs-band-7; https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/differences-between-ielts-6-and-7-for-productive-skills).
- Self-correction is **not** automatically penalised — examiners explicitly treat occasional self-correction as evidence of language awareness; it only hurts when it becomes frequent enough to cost coherence (https://blog.myieltsclassroom.com/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors/; https://ielts.com.au/australia/about/news-and-articles/article-speaking-band-6-vs-band-7).
- "Pausing to gather thoughts is acceptable; pausing to find words is not" — examiner shorthand for the FC distinction between content-related and language-related hesitation, mirrored almost verbatim in Band 7/8 descriptors (https://blog.myieltsclassroom.com/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors/; https://cdn.ielts.org/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).
- IELTS.org's own 6 → 7 productive-skills piece says Band 7 requires the speaker to "express nuanced opinions, handle interruptions or follow-up questions and use a broader vocabulary," whereas a Band 6 speaker "can communicate adequately but may find longer academic discussions challenging" (https://ielts.org/news-and-insights/differences-between-ielts-6-and-7-for-productive-skills).
- Common candidate-side traps that cap GRA at Band 6: tense slippage between past/present/future within one response, and reverting to mostly simple sentences once Part 3 abstraction increases (https://blog.myieltsclassroom.com/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors/).
- Native British/American accent is **not** a scoring factor — pronunciation is about intelligibility and suprasegmental features (stress, intonation, chunking, weak forms); L1 accent only matters if it actually obscures meaning (https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-does-ielts-speaking-assess-your-accent; https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-understanding-the-ielts-speaking-band-descriptors; https://blog.myieltsclassroom.com/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors/).
- Brown's IELTS research validated the analytic four-scale system across operational IELTS administrations; most measures increased in the expected direction over levels, but with high variation among candidates at the same level and overlap between levels — supporting multi-feature cluster scoring rather than rigid one-metric rules (https://ielts.org/researchers/our-research/research-reports/candidate-discourse-in-the-revised-ielts-speaking-test).
- Seedhouse, Harris, Naeb and Ustunel studied 60 IELTS Speaking recordings (15 each at Bands 5, 6, 7, 8) and found that **no single speaking feature distinguished all bands**; a cluster of assessable features leads toward a score. Pause length per 100 words showed significant differences between Bands 5 and 8 but did not by itself separate adjacent bands (https://ielts.org/researchers/our-research/research-reports/the-relationship-between-speaking-features-and-band-descriptors-a-mixed-methods-study).
- British Council teacher guidance: memorising large chunks is not a good Speaking strategy and is unlikely to impress the examiner — transcript evidence of memorised-but-poorly-integrated answers should not be rewarded as flexible language control (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-fluency-coherence).
- Candidates are assessed on the four language criteria, not on appearance, body language, creativity of ideas, or whether opinions are right or wrong (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-fluency-coherence; https://ielts.com.au/australia/prepare/article-ielts-for-native-english-speakers).

## Country-specific prep material

### South Korea
- IDP IELTS Korea explains Speaking is assessed by the four criteria and directs candidates to the public Speaking band descriptors for details by band; the Korean-language descriptor page positions Band 6.5 as the practical floor for university admission and frames 6 → 7 as "some inaccuracies or misunderstandings remain, but the candidate can adapt flexibly" (https://ieltskorea.org/korea/results/band-score-calculation; https://ieltskorea.org/korea/results/how-ielts-is-marked).
- **Hackers Academy (해커스)** markets a dedicated "6.0 → 7.0 jump" pathway; one published student case study attributes the breakthrough specifically to integrating **idioms and chunked expressions** rather than to grammar work or accent training, and explicitly notes accent is not scored and asking the examiner to repeat is not penalised (https://www.gohackers.com/?c=ielts/ielts_info2/ielts_method&type=url&uid=538610; https://www.hackers.ac/contents/?m=landing&v=ielts/overall7).
- **Skybel** summarises the 6.0 → 7.0 gap as moving from basic continuity, repeated vocabulary and unstable complex sentences toward natural speed, coherent development, topic-group collocations, varied sentence structures, recording-based error review, and pronunciation clarity through stress/rhythm/intonation practice (https://blog.skybel.co.kr/blog/%EC%95%84%EC%9D%B4%EC%97%98%EC%B8%A0%20%EC%8A%A4%ED%94%BC%ED%82%B9/%EC%95%84%EC%9D%B4%EC%97%98%EC%B8%A0-%EC%8A%A4%ED%94%BC%ED%82%B9-%EC%A4%80%EB%B9%84-%EB%8F%85%ED%95%99-%EB%A3%A8%ED%8B%B4%EC%9C%BC%EB%A1%9C-60%EC%97%90%EC%84%9C-70-%EC%8B%A4%EC%A0%84-%EC%A0%90%EC%88%98-%ED%96%A5%EC%83%81-%EC%A0%84%EB%9E%B5-%EA%B3%B5%EA%B0%9C).
- **TestGlider** (widely shared in 학원 circles) emphasises that fluency is the fastest criterion to move short-term, and advises that getting stuck on one word should trigger paraphrase rather than silence — operationally consistent with the LR Band 6 descriptor (https://blog.testglider.com/%EC%95%84%EC%9D%B4%EC%97%98%EC%B8%A0-band-6-5%EB%AA%A9%ED%91%9C-%ED%9A%A8%EA%B3%BC%EC%A0%81%EC%9D%B8-%EC%98%81%EC%97%AD%EB%B3%84-%EA%B3%B5%EB%B6%80%EB%B2%95/).
- **Korean-language ELT Japan** describes the 6 → 7 boundary as less mechanical linking, more flexible connectors, natural idioms/collocations, more frequent error-free complex sentences, and intelligibility despite an L1 accent (https://www.eltschool.jp/ko/column/ielts-speaking-band-7-tips).

### China
- IELTS Chinese official site reiterates the four criteria; IDP China hosts a Chinese public-version Speaking descriptor PDF that mirrors the Band 7 requirement for detailed coherent expression with flexible connectives, less common/idiomatic vocabulary, flexible complex grammar, and pronunciation showing all Band 6 positives plus some Band 8 positives (https://www.chinaielts.org/prepare/understand-ielts-score/assessment-speaking; https://www.idp.cn/special/subject/IELTS/pdf/kscj_f2.pdf).
- **Zhihu "口语5/6/7/8分的区别"** thread (widely circulated): Band 7 candidates produce noticeably less language-related hesitation, self-correction and repetition than Band 6 candidates, and Band 7 grammar shows "more flexible deployment of complex structures with persistent but non-blocking errors" (https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/34529961). A separate Zhihu IELTS article frames the public descriptors as a guard against blind template memorisation (https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/1945919027349266867).
- **新通教育 (Xintong)** frames the 6 → 7 LR shift as: Band 6 = "wide enough vocab to discuss broad topics, can use some uncommon items or idioms though not always accurately"; Band 7 = "rich vocabulary deployed naturally and idiomatically across all topics" (https://www.igo.cn/kp/info/details/323203.shtml).
- **新航道 (New Channel) Wuhan** summarises Band 7+ as stable performance across all four criteria with fewer mistakes, natural expression and strong logic; treats "Part 2 can fill two minutes" as coaching evidence (not an official IELTS cutoff) (https://wh.xhd.cn/info/cjzx/1058456.html).
- **New Oriental (新东方)** warns that fluency is not simply non-stop fast speech — excessive speed causes more grammar errors and long hesitation slows delivery; vocabulary must be usable in speech, not merely memorised in lists (https://ielts.xdf.cn/202004/11052541.html).
- **老烤鸭 (Laokaoya)** Band 7 guide emphasises flexibility: flexible topic discussion, less common/idiomatic vocabulary with style/collocation awareness, flexible complex grammar, and pronunciation that preserves meaning even with Chinese-accented speech (https://www.laokaoya.com/17054.html).
- **学为贵 (Xueweigui)** "万年5.5中招原因" ("why people get stuck at 5.5 forever") attributes the cap to overuse of memorised templates and absence of complex structures in Part 3 — a diagnostic signal that maps onto Band 5 GRA wording (https://ielts.guixue.com/article/2889.shtml).
- **TestDaily 厚朴优学** highlights that of the four criteria, **fluency** is the most short-term-improvable and most often the lever that lifts a 6.5 to 7.0 (https://www.testdaily.cn/ielts-speak-20201020/).

### Vietnam
- IDP Vietnam explains discourse markers as phrases that connect ideas and support fluent, coherent transmission of ideas, explicitly linking them to FC (25% of the Speaking score); says discourse markers can buy thinking time, e.g. "Let me think about that for a minute" is preferable to silence or repeated "umm/err" — useful for distinguishing functional fillers from disfluent searching. IDP Vietnam also stresses the Band 6 → 7 P delta is about **sustaining** prosodic features (stress, intonation, chunking) rather than acquiring a new feature inventory (https://ielts.idp.com/vietnam/about/news-and-articles/article-discourse-markers-ielts-speaking; https://ielts.idp.com/vietnam/prepare/article-understanding-the-ielts-speaking-band-descriptors/en-gb).
- **RES.edu.vn** explains FC through longer answers, discourse markers/conjunctions, coherent sentence flow, stable speed and limited self-correction; a minimal Part 1 answer such as "Yes, of course" is too short — candidates should add explanation or an example (https://res.edu.vn/unit-2-tieu-chi-cham-diem-ielts-speaking/).
- **IELTS LangGo** says Band 7+ candidates use varied discourse markers reasonably and flexibly, while lower-band speakers often overuse simple markers (https://langgo.edu.vn/discourse-markers-la-gi-cach-su-dung-hieu-qua-trong-ielts-speaking).
- **IELTS Power-up** operationalises the LR Band 6 → 7 jump as "from clear meaning despite inappropriacies → flexible deployment of less common and idiomatic items with awareness of style," with paraphrase drills as the bridging exercise (https://ielts-powerup.com/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors/).

- **Gurully** (Indian academy): practical operational rule — **1–2 well-chosen idioms per Part 2 response** is the deployment density associated with Band 7 LR; more risks sounding rehearsed and triggers the "memorised utterances" red flag in the Band 3 descriptor (https://www.gurully.com/blog/ielts-speaking-tips-band-7-or-higher/). **IELTS-blog.com** lists concrete Band 7+ expressions (https://www.ielts-blog.com/ielts-preparation-tips/speaking-tips/10-expressions-to-help-you-get-band-7-in-ielts-lesson-4/).

### Indonesia
- **IDP Indonesia** says Speaking is assessed through the four criteria; explains fluency as speaking without excessive pauses/hesitation/repetition; coherence as logical sequencing; LR as range and contextual use of vocabulary, including phrasal verbs, figurative language and paraphrasing; GRA as accurate, complex and appropriate grammar rather than playing safe with only short simple sentences (https://ielts.idp.com/indonesia/prepare/article-ielts-speaking-tested-skills/en-gb; https://ielts.idp.com/indonesia/results/how-ielts-is-marked/band-score-calculator/en-gb).
- **AUG Study**, **OSEE**, **Lister**: Indonesian prep consensus that the Band 6 → 7 gap is rarely grammar and almost always about (a) developing answers spontaneously and (b) handling abstract Part 3 questions. Concrete advice: record yourself and listen for unfinished sentences, flat intonation, and "umm/eee" fillers (https://augstudy.com/rahasia-tips-speaking-ielts-band-7-agar-skor-naik-drastis/; https://osee.co.id/ielts-speaking-tips-skor-tinggi/; https://lister.co.id/blog/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors/).
- Pronunciation framing: word stress (PHOtograph vs phoTOGraphy), sentence stress, and rising vs falling intonation are the three measurable features Indonesian schools drill; Indonesian accent itself is explicitly stated not to be penalised (https://osee.co.id/ielts-speaking-tips-skor-tinggi/).

### UK, Australia, Canada, New Zealand (native-perspective material)

## Open-source tools and code

No official open-source IELTS Speaking scorer was identified in either crawl; any automated system should be validated against human-rated IELTS-style data before using these descriptors operationally (https://ielts.org/researchers/our-research/research-reports/the-relationship-between-speaking-features-and-band-descriptors-a-mixed-methods-study). The projects below are feature-extraction, preprocessing, or reference-architecture components — not IELTS scoring authorities.

### Audio / transcript feature extraction
- **WhisperX** — ASR, alignment and word-level timestamps for estimating candidate speech rate, pause placement and turn timing when audio is available (https://github.com/m-bain/whisperX). License / last-updated not verified.
- **pyannote-audio** — speaker diarization toolkit, necessary if the system must separate examiner and candidate speech before applying IELTS criteria (https://github.com/pyannote/pyannote-audio). License / last-updated not verified.
- **openSMILE** — acoustic and prosodic feature extraction for pronunciation, rhythm, pause and stress-timing analysis; the IELTS score must still be tied to descriptor evidence rather than raw acoustic features alone (https://github.com/audeering/opensmile). License / last-updated not verified.
- **Parselmouth** — Python interface to Praat for pitch, duration, intensity and pause measurements (https://github.com/YannickJadoul/Parselmouth). License / last-updated not verified.
- **deep_disfluency** (CLP Research) — detects filled pauses, restarts and repairs in spontaneous speech transcripts, mapping to IELTS fluency indicators such as false starts, backtracking, repetition and self-correction (https://github.com/clp-research/deep_disfluency). License / last-updated not verified.

### Reference IELTS-scoring pipelines and methodological templates
- **dustland/talk** — AI-powered IELTS Speaking practice platform with real-time feedback and scoring; useful as a reference architecture (https://github.com/dustland/talk).
- **ZainabZaman/IELTS_PracticeAndEvaluation** — All four modules with band-score calculation; the speaking module runs a conversational bot then a relevance score before evaluation (https://github.com/ZainabZaman/IELTS_PracticeAndEvaluation).
- **hubeiqiao/IELTS-Speaking-Simulator** — Simulates Parts 1/2/3 then auto-scores; includes prompt-engineering of the four criteria (https://github.com/hubeiqiao/IELTS-Speaking-Simulator/blob/main/README_EN.md).
- **chillestt/Automated-IELTS-essay-evaluation** — Writing (not speaking), but the QLoRA fine-tuning of Mistral-7B / Llama-2-7B / Gemma-7B on band-score data is a methodological template that transfers to spoken-transcript scoring (https://github.com/chillestt/Automated-IELTS-essay-evaluation).

### Academic / patent references for feature selection
- **Suzuki & Kormos (2023)**, "What do the measures of utterance fluency employed in automatic speech evaluation tell us about oral proficiency?" — utterance-fluency measures (articulation rate, mid-clause pause frequency, repetition frequency) together predict functional adequacy; breadth of lexical knowledge is the main predictor of articulation rate; these account for ~60% of variance in functional adequacy among 60 Chinese IELTS Part 2 takers (https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/15434303.2023.2283839).
- **PMC 11952242** — comparative study of automated evaluation systems vs human raters for spoken English proficiency, useful as a calibration baseline (https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC11952242/).
- **USPTO patent 8,457,967** — "Automatic evaluation of spoken fluency" — formant-based filled-pause detection, exact/inexact repeat N-grams, normalized distance between consecutive N-grams; usable as fluency features for an LLM judge with a timestamped transcript (https://image-ppubs.uspto.gov/dirsearch-public/print/downloadPdf/8457967).

## Practical signals for an LLM judge

Operational rules for scoring a Part 1 / Part 2 / Part 3 transcript. Apply each per criterion, then combine via best-fit.

1. **Score only candidate speech.** IELTS Speaking is an interview, but the score is awarded for the test taker's performance across the full test (https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking; https://cdn.ielts.org/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).
2. **Average across all three parts.** Treat descriptors as "average performance across all parts" — do not let one strong answer dominate (https://cdn.ielts.org/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).
3. **Best-fit, not penalty-counting.** Place the candidate at the band where the **bulk** of evidence sits per criterion; do not deduct from a Band 7 transcript every time a feature dips into Band 6 (https://ielts.com.au/australia/about/news-and-articles/article-speaking-band-6-vs-band-7; https://ielts.ch/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/assessing-IELTS-speaking.pdf).
4. **Sustained vs intermittent is the single most reliable Band 6 ↔ 7 discriminator.** Do the desirable features (range, complexity, idiomaticity, prosody) appear throughout the transcript or only in flashes? Intermittent → Band 6; sustained → Band 7 (https://cdn.ielts.org/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf; https://ielts.com.au/australia/about/news-and-articles/article-speaking-band-6-vs-band-7).
5. **Cluster scoring, not one hard rule.** IELTS research found overlap between candidates at the same level and no single feature that separates all bands; combine pause burden, coherence, vocabulary flexibility, grammar range/accuracy and pronunciation evidence (https://ielts.org/researchers/our-research/research-reports/candidate-discourse-in-the-revised-ielts-speaking-test; https://ielts.org/researchers/our-research/research-reports/the-relationship-between-speaking-features-and-band-descriptors-a-mixed-methods-study).
6. **Classify hesitation by function (FC).** Content-planning hesitation (often signalled by content rephrasing like "…what I mean is…", "…actually…") is compatible with high bands. Mid-sentence searches for basic vocabulary/grammar, followed by silence or repeated function words, are Band 5–6 evidence (https://cdn.ielts.org/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf; https://blog.myieltsclassroom.com/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors/).
7. **Self-correction is not free, but is not auto-penalised.** ≤ 2 self-corrections per Part 2 turn is healthy / Band 7 compatible; ≥ 4 in one turn becomes a coherence cost and pulls FC toward Band 6 or below (https://ielts.com.au/australia/about/news-and-articles/article-speaking-band-6-vs-band-7; https://blog.myieltsclassroom.com/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors/).
8. **Yes/no responses are a hard cap.** Multiple single-word or under-one-sentence answers anywhere cap FC at Band 5 — the descriptor requires "willing to speak at length" from Band 6 upward (https://www.divelang.ru/blog/useful/kak-podgotovitsya-k-ielts-speaking-chtoby-sdat-na-otlichno/; https://cdn.ielts.org/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).
9. **Discourse marker range (FC).** Count distinct discourse markers used appropriately ("however", "for example", "as a result", "on the other hand", "consequently", "well", "I mean", "you know"). Heuristic: **Band 7 ≥ 5 distinct markers used appropriately**; Band 6 typically 2–4 with at least one inappropriate use (https://www.britishcouncil.in/blog/journey-ielts-band-5-band-7-ielts-speaking-test; https://blog.myieltsclassroom.com/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors/).
10. **Lexical Resource: appropriacy > rarity.** Do not reward rare words by themselves; reward precise, appropriate, collocationally natural choices and effective paraphrase. Distinguish Band 6 "meaning clear despite inappropriate vocabulary" from Band 7 "flexible vocabulary with some less common/idiomatic items and style/collocation awareness" (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf; https://cdn.ielts.org/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).
11. **Idiom density target (LR, Part 2).** Heuristic: **1–2 well-collocated idiomatic chunks per ~90-second Part 2 turn** as a Band 7 LR floor. Zero idiomatic items across the whole test caps LR at Band 6. **More than 4–5 forced idioms** in a single short response is a "memorised utterances" red flag and pulls LR *down* (https://www.gurully.com/blog/ielts-speaking-tips-band-7-or-higher/; https://cdn.ielts.org/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).
12. **Paraphrase is the Band 6+ floor.** If the candidate stalls at a word search without producing a workable paraphrase, LR floors at Band 5 (https://blog.myieltsclassroom.com/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors/; https://cdn.ielts.org/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).
13. **GRA error-free sentence ratio.** Band 7 requires "frequently produces error-free sentences." Heuristic: ≥ ~50% of complex sentences (those with subordination/coordination) free of grammatical error → Band 7-compatible; under 30% → Band 6 (https://cdn.ielts.org/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf).
14. **GRA complex-structure inventory.** Tag the transcript for conditionals (any type), passive voice, reported speech, perfect aspects, relative clauses, cleft sentences. Heuristic: Band 7 needs ≥ 3 of these 6 categories present and mostly accurate; Band 6 typically shows ≤ 2 categories or shows them with high error rate (https://salamzaban.com/blog/ielts-speaking-7-8/; https://www.britishcouncil.in/blog/journey-ielts-band-5-band-7-ielts-speaking-test).
15. **Communication-blocking errors weigh more than minor slips.** IELTS key criteria include the communicative effect of errors (https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf; https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-grammar).
16. **Part 3 abstraction test.** A clear drop in sentence complexity, idea-development length, or vocabulary range from Part 1 → Part 3 caps the candidate at Band 6 regardless of strong Part 1 performance (https://augstudy.com/rahasia-tips-speaking-ielts-band-7-agar-skor-naik-drastis/; https://blog.myieltsclassroom.com/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors/).
17. **Pronunciation without audio: low confidence, transcript artefacts only.** Score P from: (a) examiner-flagged comprehension breakdowns ("Sorry?", "Could you repeat?"); (b) overt mispronunciation calls in the transcript; (c) homophone-induced word-choice errors; (d) the candidate's own pronunciation repairs. Absent any of these signals, default P to the same level as LR/GRA per best-fit rather than artificially low (https://cdn.ielts.org/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf; https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-understanding-the-ielts-speaking-band-descriptors).
18. **Accent is never a deduction by itself.** A transcript that flags "speaks with [L1] accent" with no comprehension breakdown should not move P at all (https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-does-ielts-speaking-assess-your-accent; https://osee.co.id/ielts-speaking-tips-skor-tinggi/).
19. **Articulation-rate proxy.** With timestamps, use words-per-minute as a fluency proxy: Suzuki & Kormos find articulation rate, mid-clause pause frequency and repetition frequency together predict functional adequacy in IELTS Part 2 (https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/15434303.2023.2283839).
20. **Treat memorised chunks cautiously, and ignore non-language signals.** Polished memorised language without flexible adaptation should not be credited as broad control. Ignore content truth, opinion quality, body language and charisma unless they affect language evidence (https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/teach-ielts/teaching-resources/videos/speaking-fluency-coherence; https://ielts.com.au/australia/prepare/article-ielts-for-native-english-speakers).
21. **Report sub-scores then round.** Report all four sub-scores separately and compute the rounded overall (nearest half band) from the unrounded average — do not pre-round per criterion. Worked example: 7/7/6/7 → 6.75 → **7.0**; 7/6/6/7 → 6.5 (https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-understanding-the-ielts-speaking-band-descriptors; https://ieltstutors.org/speaking-band-descriptors/).
