# Concrete tactics to lift each speaking criterion from Band 6 to Band 7

_Crawled by claude on 2026-05-19T07:32:34Z. Sources cited inline._

## Summary

The official descriptor gap between IELTS Speaking Band 6 and Band 7 is small in wording but large in effect: Band 6 candidates can sustain long turns but lose coherence under load and over-rely on safe vocabulary and simple structures; Band 7 candidates sustain length "without noticeable effort or loss of coherence", deploy a range of connectives and discourse markers with flexibility, use some less-common and idiomatic lexis, and produce frequent error-free complex sentences with intelligible prosody and only minor L1 interference [1][2][3]. Crucially every criterion is marked separately and the overall band is averaged, so a single criterion stuck at 6 caps the whole score at roughly 6.5 — the most common Band-6 ceiling is Lexical Resource or Grammatical Range, not Fluency [4][10]. The international tutor consensus (Korea, China, Vietnam, Iran, India, Pakistan, Indonesia, Kazakhstan, UK/AU) converges on the same upgrade levers: extend Part-1 answers to 2-3 sentences with a reason and example; tell mini-stories in Part 2 rather than reciting bullets; vary connectives beyond "and/but/because"; deploy 2-3 well-collocated less-common items per response (not 5-7 forced ones); produce conditional and relative-clause complex sentences; and trade speed for clear chunking and sentence stress [5][6][7][8][14][16][20][22].

## Authoritative material

### Official descriptors: Band 6 vs Band 7 side-by-side

The British Council/IDP/Cambridge Speaking Band Descriptors PDF and IELTS.org "Differences between IELTS 6 and 7" page are the canonical references [1][2][3].

**Fluency and Coherence**
- Band 6: "is willing to speak at length, though may lose coherence at times due to occasional repetition, self-correction or hesitation"; "uses a range of connectives and discourse markers but not always appropriately" [2][3].
- Band 7: "speaks at length without noticeable effort or loss of coherence"; "may demonstrate language-related hesitation at times, or some repetition and/or self-correction"; "uses a range of connectives and discourse markers with some flexibility" [2][3].
- Key shift: hesitations at Band 7 are "language-related" (searching for the right word or grammar), not "content-related" (searching for an idea). Band 6 hesitations are typically content-driven [1][2][9].

**Lexical Resource**
- Band 6: "has a wide enough vocabulary to discuss topics at length and make meaning clear in spite of inappropriacies"; "generally paraphrases successfully" [3].
- Band 7: "uses vocabulary resource flexibly to discuss a variety of topics"; "uses some less common and idiomatic vocabulary and shows some awareness of style and collocation, with some inappropriate choices"; "uses paraphrase effectively" [2][3][14].
- Key shift: presence of less-common and idiomatic vocabulary plus collocation awareness, not just paraphrase capacity [12][14].

**Grammatical Range and Accuracy**
- Band 6: "uses a mix of simple and complex structures, but with limited flexibility"; "may make frequent mistakes with complex structures, though these rarely cause comprehension problems" [3].
- Band 7: "uses a range of complex structures with some flexibility"; "frequently produces error-free sentences, though some grammatical mistakes persist" [2][3][22].
- Key shift: "frequently produces error-free sentences" is the explicit Band-7 trigger; examiners listen for whole complex sentences with no error [4][22].

**Pronunciation**
- Band 6: "uses a range of pronunciation features with mixed control"; "can generally be understood throughout, though mispronunciation of individual words or sounds reduces clarity at times" [2][3].
- Band 7: shows "all the positive features of Band 6 and some, but not all, of the positive features of Band 8" — Band 8 features include effective use of features for delivering meaning, sustained flexible use of features, and only occasional lapses [2][3][15].
- Official line on accent: "L1 accent has minimal effect on intelligibility" at Band 7. Noticeable, intrusive L1 features that force the examiner to work to understand prevent Band 7 [15][17].

### IELTS.org and British Council guidance

IELTS.org's "Journey from Band 5 to Band 7" explicitly identifies the differentiators as: (a) speaking at length without effort, (b) flexible discourse markers, (c) use of less-common vocabulary and idioms with awareness of collocation, (d) frequent error-free complex sentences, and (e) intelligible pronunciation throughout with sustained use of stress and intonation [11]. The article's recommended drill: record a 2-minute Part-2 response, transcribe it, mark every filler, every repetition, every self-correction, every basic word; then re-record substituting one less-common word and one complex structure per sentence until natural [11].

### IELTS Australia / IELTS.com.au

The IDP-published comparison piece states the single largest practical difference is "consistency": Band 6 candidates "show the right features some of the time", while Band 7 candidates "sustain those features throughout the test" — meaning a candidate can hit Band 7 lexis or grammar in Part 1 but drop to Band 6 in Part 3 under topic pressure, and that drop is what caps the score [3][4].

### IELTS Simon (UK ex-examiner)

Simon's "Band 7 Vocabulary" post argues against memorising "fancy" words. The Band 7 lexical marker is "less common words plus correct collocations plus maybe some idiomatic language", used naturally inside answers that directly address the question. Forced idioms with broken collocation ("relish low temperature", "under this kind of weather") are diagnostic of Band 6 [12]. He also warns that two or three accurate uses of less-common items per long turn is Band-7 territory; five forced uses without natural collocation actually signals Band 6 [12][14].

### IELTS Liz

Liz's pronunciation page emphasises that the four pronunciation features the examiner is listening for are word stress, sentence stress, intonation/rise-fall, and chunking (grouping words into meaningful units with a brief pause). Accent itself is not marked; sustained correct use of these four features is [15]. Her general guidance for Band 7+ candidates: "Band 7 shows all features of Band 6 and only some of the positive features of Band 8" — so a single criterion sustained at full Band 7 is harder than aggregating partial 7s [15].

### IELTS Podcast / Lexical Resource episode

The Band-7 Lexical Resource lesson from IELTS Podcast lists concrete substitutions: "I like my job" → "I find my work quite fulfilling"; "funny" → "hilarious / hysterical / side-splitting"; "not much free time" → "limited spare time"; "big" → "significant / substantial" [13][14]. The lesson stresses that band-7 vocabulary should appear across all three parts, not clustered in Part 2 only [13][14].

### Engvarta / All Ears English / 3D Academy (Cebu)

Engvarta's "game-changing trick" is structural: answer → reason → example → mini-conclusion, with a transition word at each seam ("because", "for instance", "so overall") [5]. All Ears English's "8 Actions" lesson narrows it to four operational levers (one per criterion) and rejects memorised answers, scripted idioms, and over-fast delivery [6]. 3D Academy (Cebu) frames the Band-7 fluency target as "appropriate pacing with meaningful pauses" rather than rapid continuous speech, and recommends rhythm/pause practice for L1 speakers of syllable-timed languages [7][8].

### Engvarta and Miss Kay (mini-stories)

Miss Kay's "Secret to Band 7+" argues that the highest-leverage move is not perfect grammar but switching from generic answers to "tiny, clear stories" — one specific instance, one specific person, one specific time. Mini-stories naturally trigger past tenses, time-sequencing connectives, attitudinal lexis, and intonational variation in one move, lifting all four criteria simultaneously [5][7].

### Academic / corpus evidence

Huang, Lin & Gráf (LINDSEI corpus) show that C1 learners (the CEFR equivalent of IELTS 7-7.5) use significantly more discourse markers than B2 learners, and discourse-marker frequency rises with fluency level toward near-native rates [18]. Dumont (2018, UCLouvain PhD) provides a non-native vs native disfluency-profile comparison the LLM judge can use as a B2/C1 reference [18]. The Engvarta meta-claim that "speakers who use linking phrases appropriately can enhance their coherence scores by as much as 20%" mirrors this corpus finding qualitatively, though the exact number is marketing [5][18].

## Country-specific prep material

### Korean (한국 IELTS 학원)

Korean cram-school consensus (Skybel, Lexis, Testglider, Engoo, Wall Street English, Grace IELTS, Super English) [19][20][21][29][30][31]:
- Above 6.0, every 0.5 increment "becomes exponentially harder"; the 6.0→6.5 jump is the smallest and 6.5→7.0 the largest tactical leap because Lexical Resource and Grammatical Range start gating the score [19].
- Tactic: deliberately deploy memorised "어려운 단어들과 부사들" (advanced words and adverbs) you have drilled — but inside live sentences, not slotted in [19][30]. The Korean failure mode is sounding "수능 영어" (CSAT-style stiff) rather than conversational.
- Pronunciation strand: most Korean academies hire Commonwealth (UK/Ireland/AU/NZ) instructors and target British prosody, because Korean L1 lacks English-style sentence stress and rise-fall [19][20].
- Part-2 advice: do NOT cover every bullet on the cue card — Korean students lose marks chasing all four points and running shallow on each. Pick one point, drop a 30-second mini-story, then ladder to the others [20][21].
- Mind-set tactic widely repeated: "친구와 카페에서 수다 떨듯이" (treat the examiner like a friend at a café, not an interrogator). Stiff, monotone delivery is the Korean signature Band-6 cap [20][30].

### Chinese (雅思口语 7分)

Zhihu, Earthedu, EIC, Kouyuxia, Hanlin, FCGvisa forum consensus [22][23][24][25][26][27][28]:
- 6分到7分的 explicit gap: "6分能交流但有重复和自我改正、词汇有不恰当之处; 7分没有明显停顿、能用较难词汇和习语、语法错误较少" [22][24].
- 句型多样化 (sentence pattern diversity) is the most-repeated Chinese tactic: deliberately force yourself off your default S+V+O scaffolding into conditionals, relative clauses, cleft sentences, and inversions — even when the simple form would suffice — as practice [22].
- 输入大于输出 (input > output): the consensus warns that Chinese 6-band candidates plateau because they output without re-inputting. Daily large-volume input of native podcasts/talks is required before output drills will lift the band [22][26].
- Pronunciation: 一成不变的音律 (monotone, unchanging melody) is the diagnostic Chinese Band-6 signature; conscious 抑扬顿挫 (rise-fall) practice with shadowing material is recommended [24][27].
- Part-1 length: 1-3 sentences, never one-word "yes" — and never a memorised 5-line monologue, which the examiner will detect and downgrade [24].
- 心态 (mindset): 自信放松 (relaxed confidence) is repeatedly listed as a Band-7 prerequisite [22][24][27].

### Vietnamese (luyện thi IELTS Speaking band 7)

DOL English, IELTS-Powerup, KTDC, Thanh Loan, IELTS Mentor, IELTS Ngocbach, Hot Courses consensus [16][17][32][33][34][35]:
- Explicit 6.0→7.0+ differentiator: 7.0 candidates "trình bày một câu trả lời dài mà không đánh mất yếu tố mạch lạc" (deliver long answers without losing coherence) versus 6.0's ấp úng, lặp lại, tự sửa (stalling, repeating, self-correcting) [16][32].
- Flexible connectors recommended for Band 7+: "to be more specific", "I guess this is because", "I have to say that", "what stood out for me", "as well as" — used in mid-turn, not just at sentence starts [16][32].
- Idioms must be "close-knit" with the topic — Vietnamese tutors specifically warn against the "close-knit relationship" type swap-in if it doesn't fit the cue card [16][32].
- Grammar lever: practice "I can't help + Ving", "not only…but also", third conditional, mixed conditional, and reduced relative clauses as drill targets [16][32].
- Filler-buying time: when hit with an unfamiliar topic, candidates should not panic — use stalling phrases ("That's a tricky question, let me think…") rather than fall silent [16][32].

### Iranian / Persian (آیلتس اسپیکینگ بند ۷)

Hafez Edu, Zandi English, Charb Zaban, Irsafam, GMT Academy consensus [36][37][38][39][40]:
- Four criteria each weigh 25%, so Persian academies push a balanced regimen, not pronunciation-only [36].
- Lexical "Activation" technique: stop rote-memorising word lists; learn each new word inside 2-3 sentences and a collocation, then deploy that sentence in next speaking practice within 48 hours [36][37].
- High-frequency-topic preparation: see which questions historically score highest and pre-build answers — but only as scaffolding, never word-for-word memorised, because Iranian examiners' #1 complaint is the "robotic memorised Part 1" tell [36][38].
- Idioms are non-negotiable above 7: "اگر می‌خواهید نمره ۷ یا بالاتر بگیرید باید از اصطلاح ها استفاده نمایید" (you must use idioms) [36][38].
- Grammatical variety explicitly framed: all-short-simple-sentences produces a broken rhythm that signals 6 [36][37].

### Indian (IELTS coaching academies)

British Council India, PND IELTS, USpeak, Kadamb, Leverage Edu consensus [11][14][41][42]:
- British Council India's "Journey from Band 5 to Band 7" defines Band 7 as: "fairly smooth speech with only minor hesitation, clear organization, range of connectives, and idiomatic vocabulary used appropriately" [11].
- The Indian academy emphasis: phrasal verbs are a "key sign of 7 or above" for vocabulary [14][41]. A response packed with single-word formal Latinate vocabulary but zero phrasal verbs reads as essay-like and caps at 6.5.
- Communication-strategy marker: "showing the skill of communicating meaning when you don't know the word for what you mean" — i.e. graceful paraphrase under pressure — is itself a Band-7 signal because it shows lexical flexibility [11][41].
- Part-3 specific: never always agree. "It depends on…" and "There are two sides to this…" demonstrate analytical thinking and unlock complex grammar (concession, hypothetical) [11][41].

### Pakistani (IELTS Pakistan: Lahore / Karachi / Islamabad)

AEO Pakistan, Omni Academy, JNS Edu, TheUnigrad, Sat Prep Tutors consensus [43][44][45][46][47]:
- Pakistani academies recommend 45-minute daily speaking practice minimum, with at least 4-6 timed full mock tests before exam day [43][44].
- Trainer-selection criterion repeatedly stated: trainer's own IELTS Speaking score should be 8.0+ before you trust their model answers [43][46].
- Pakistani candidates' L1 (Urdu/Punjabi) interference often shows up as stress on the wrong syllable in 3+ syllable Latinate words (e.g. "PHO-tograph" vs "pho-TOG-raphy" confusion) and dental /θ ð/ → /t̪ d̪/ substitution — both correctable through stress-pattern drilling [44].
- Cost-anchored point: PKR 15,000-50,000 buys 8-12 weeks of structured coaching with small batches of 6-10 [43][46].

### Indonesian (IELTS Indonesia)

IDP Indonesia, IALF, Educenter BSD, SUN Education, AUG, Edupac, Kampung Inggris consensus [48][49][50][51][52]:
- The Indonesian Part-3 differentiator: "Jangan takut untuk think out loud" (don't be afraid to think out loud); natural fillers like "well", "actually", "that's a good question" are explicitly better than silence or rushed incoherent answers [48][49].
- Intonation: Indonesian candidates' Band-6 trap is reading-news flatness. AUG's tactic: smile while speaking — physically changes mouth shape, lifts pitch range, makes intonation more dynamic [49].
- Eye contact framing: "anggap penguji sebagai teman ngobrol di kafe, bukan polisi interogasi" (treat examiner as a café friend, not an interrogating cop) — reduces freeze that drops F&C from 7 to 6 [49].
- IALF's "IELTS Speaking Plus" 1-week intensive specifically targets features that challenge Indonesian L1 candidates [52].

### Russian / Kazakh (IELTS 7 баллов)

Medium (Kulibraeva), English with Experts, YourIELTS, MBA Consult, vc.ru, Caspian Training Group, Top Tier, A Level, Smart School, NGEC, EduBridge consensus [53][54][55][56][57][58][59][60]:
- Russian-language coaches emphasise that Band 7 requires "уверенное использование сложных грамматических конструкций, устойчивых выражений, фразовых глаголов" (confident use of complex grammar, fixed expressions, phrasal verbs) [53].
- Standard structural template: 7-10 logically connected sentences per Part-3 answer, with introductory phrase + reasoning + supporting opinion + closing [53][54].
- Common Russian/Kazakh L1 issue: monotone delivery with falling intonation at the end of every clause — Kazakh academies (Caspian Training Group, Smart School) drill rising intonation on non-final clauses and sentence-stress placement [57][58][60].
- Кaspian / Top Tier recommendation: prepare not full answers but "связки" — bridge phrases that buy thinking time without sounding rehearsed (e.g., "It's an interesting question because…", "Well, I haven't thought about that before, but…") [57][58].
- Mirror practice if no partner available [54][55].

### UK / Australian / Canadian native-tutor perspective

Keith Speaking Academy (UK), Oxford Online English (UK), CanLanguage (Canada), New Cambridge (Bangkok with UK tutors), Yana Immis, IELTS Focus consensus [9][22][32][61][62][63]:
- Native tutor framing: the most under-rated Band-7 lever is "speak at length without effort" — examiners can hear effort in micro-pauses before content words, even when the words are correct. The fix is shadowing native podcasts at native speed, then producing 2-minute monologues with a stopwatch [9][61][62].
- "Selectively and mostly accurate" complex sentences: Keith Speaking Academy frames Band 7 grammar not as "use complex structures often" but as "use complex structures when they're the right tool, and get them right when you do" — over-using conditionals where simple past works actually drops the score [22][61].
- Conditional/relative-clause Band-7 example given: "If governments had invested earlier in public health education, the rise in chronic diseases might have been prevented" — third conditional + passive, two complex features in one sentence [22].
- IELTS Focus (UK examiner blog): Band 7 candidates can have a few mistakes but must "express yourself naturally with different tenses and complex sentences" [9].

## Practical signals for an LLM judge

Reading a Speaking transcript (with diarized turns and optional filler/timing annotation), the judge should treat the following as operational rules. "Per minute" assumes Part 2 is ~2 minutes of monologue and Part 1/3 turns are 15-30 s each.

1. **Hesitation type, not count, gates F&C.** Pauses before *content* words (the speaker is searching for an idea) cap F&C at 6; pauses before *specific* words (searching for a word/grammar form) are compatible with Band 7. Transcript proxy: filler ("um", "uh") immediately followed by a topic noun = content hesitation; filler followed by a precise/less-common word or grammatical reformulation = language hesitation [1][2][9].
2. **Self-correction rhythm.** Band 6 self-corrections break rhythm ("I goed — I mean I went — to the…"). Band 7 corrections preserve flow ("I went, or rather I made my way to the…"). Look for repair markers ("or rather", "I mean", "what I meant was") used as discourse markers, not as broken restarts [2][3][9].
3. **Discourse-marker variety.** Count distinct markers used flexibly across the transcript. Band 6 transcripts repeat "and / but / because / so" plus maybe "however"; Band 7 transcripts mix in at least 4-6 of: actually, well, you know, I mean, anyway, in fact, having said that, on the other hand, what's more, to be honest, mind you, the thing is, I suppose, frankly. Fewer than 4 distinct flexible markers across a full test = Band 6 signal [11][14][18].
4. **Less-common vocabulary density.** Target ~2-4 well-collocated less-common items per minute of Part 2 monologue and ~1-2 per Part 1/3 turn. Zero = Band 6 cap. Five+ forced ones with bad collocation ("relish low temperature", "indulge sleep") = also Band 6 cap, not Band 7 [12][14].
5. **Phrasal-verb presence.** At least 2-3 idiomatic phrasal verbs (look up, get on with, put up with, cut down on, look forward to) per Part-2 turn is a strong Band-7 signal in lexical resource [14][41]. An answer dominated by single-word formal Latinate vocabulary with zero phrasal verbs reads as essay-like and caps at 6.5.
6. **Idiomatic chunks.** 2-4 idiomatic chunks per Part-2 monologue is Band 7+; fewer than 1 across the whole test is Band 6 [12][14]. Forced or off-register idioms (e.g. business idiom in a holiday cue card) downgrade.
7. **Complex-sentence ratio.** At least ~30-40% of clauses across the transcript should contain subordination (because/although/which/that/while/if) — Band 7 needs "a range of complex structures with some flexibility". Pure parataxis (string of simple sentences joined by "and/so") = Band 6 [22][23].
8. **Error-free-sentence frequency.** Look for runs of 3+ consecutive sentences with no grammar error including agreement, tense, articles, and prepositions. Band 7 requires "frequently produces error-free sentences"; a transcript where every long sentence has at least one error caps at 6 [2][3][22].
9. **Conditional/relative-clause deployment.** At least one well-formed conditional (second or third) AND one relative clause in the full transcript is a Band-7 marker. Zero of either across all three parts = Band 6 [22][32].
10. **Answer length scaffold.** Part 1 answers consistently of 2-3 sentences (answer + reason + example) = Band 7. One-sentence answers throughout = Band 6 cap regardless of vocabulary [5][24][41].
11. **Mini-story presence in Part 2.** Look for one specific instance with a time anchor ("last summer", "back in 2019"), a named person, and at least one past-tense narrative sequence. Generic Part-2 ("I think it's good because…") without a concrete instance is Band 6 [5][7].
12. **Pronunciation proxies in text.** Pure pronunciation can't be scored from text, but transcripts surface proxies: high ASR-confidence variance, frequent transcriber `[unintelligible]` tags, mistranscribed common words, and listener-side clarifying questions ("Sorry, could you repeat that?") all indicate Band-6 pronunciation. A clean transcript with the speaker's intended meaning never queried = compatible with Band 7 [15][17].
13. **Chunking proxy.** Punctuated transcripts (when produced by a chunking-aware ASR) showing many short syntactic chunks with commas inside long sentences are compatible with Band 7 chunking. Long undifferentiated runs without commas, or commas placed inside noun phrases, indicate poor chunking [15][17].
14. **Topic consistency across parts.** A common Band-6.5 ceiling: Band-7 features appear in Part 1 (familiar topics) but vanish in Part 3 (abstract topics). Score the three parts separately for lexical density, complex-sentence ratio, and discourse-marker variety; consistency across all three = Band 7, inconsistency = Band 6.5 cap [3][4][11].
15. **Memorised-answer detection.** Robotic openers ("Well, that's a very interesting question. Let me tell you about…"), perfectly structured 4-bullet Part-2 monologues, and Part-1 answers longer than 6 sentences are memorisation tells. Examiners downgrade these regardless of language quality [22][27][36].
