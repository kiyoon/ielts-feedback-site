# Part 1 (4–5 min interview) — format, topic categories, examiner question banks

_Crawled by claude on 2026-05-19T00:00:00Z. Sources cited inline._

## Summary

Part 1 is the 4–5 minute opening interview of the IELTS Speaking test. The examiner reads from a tightly controlled "Interlocutor Frame" and asks 7–12 questions across 2–3 familiar topic sets — the first set is always Work/Study (or Home/Hometown if the candidate does neither). It is explicitly designed as a warm-up: the official goal is to "set the test taker at ease" by talking about familiar topics, and ex-examiners agree the bulk of the band evidence is actually gathered in Parts 2–3. Because IELTS Speaking is scored **holistically across all three parts** on four equally-weighted criteria (Fluency & Coherence, Lexical Resource, Grammatical Range & Accuracy, Pronunciation), an LLM judge that sees only Part 1 must phrase its output as evidence-of-tendency, not a final band.

## Authoritative material

### Format and timing
- Total Speaking test: **11–14 minutes**; three parts; face-to-face, audio-recorded, with a certified examiner ([ielts.org Academic format](https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking)).
- Part 1: **4–5 min**; Part 2: 3–4 min (1 min prep + ~2 min long turn + 1–2 follow-ups); Part 3: 4–5 min abstract discussion ([ielts.org](https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking)).
- Speaking format is **identical** for Academic and General Training — "no provision for examiners to conduct the test any differently" (Read 2022, *Language Testing*, [SAGE](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/02655322221086211)).
- Structure of Part 1: **2–3 topic sets × 3–4 questions ≈ 7–12 questions total** ([British Council practice](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/take-ielts/prepare/free-ielts-english-practice-tests/speaking/part-1), [Keith Speaking Academy](https://keithspeakingacademy.com/ielts-speaking-test-format/), [My IELTS Classroom](https://blog.myieltsclassroom.com/how-long-talk-ielts-speaking-part-1/)).
- Inclusive question-slot rate: ~27 seconds per Q+A pair on average ([My IELTS Classroom](https://blog.myieltsclassroom.com/how-long-talk-ielts-speaking-part-1/)).
- **First topic is always Work or Study** (one of the two); examiners default to Home/Hometown if the candidate does neither ([IELTS Advantage](https://www.ieltsadvantage.com/2023/02/19/ielts-speaking-questions/)).

### Examiner script — the Interlocutor Frame (IF)
- The Part 1 script is **prescribed verbatim** by the IF, introduced by Cambridge ESOL in the early 1990s "to ensure that all test events conform to the original test design" ([ielts.org research report on IF deviation](https://ielts.org/researchers/our-research/research-reports/the-impact-on-candidate-language-of-examiner-deviation-from-a-set-interlocutor-frame-in-the-ielts-speaking-test)).
- Read 2022 verbatim: the IF "prescribed the questions to be asked verbatim" with limited options, and Part 1 / Part 2 "are quite stable in terms of deviations, with relatively few noted" — i.e. examiners rarely paraphrase in Part 1 ([SAGE](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/02655322221086211)).
- Standard opening script (cross-validated across published transcripts at [IELTS Yasi](https://ielts-yasi.englishlab.net/The_Introduction_Phase.htm), [IELTS Liz greeting page](https://ieltsliz.com/ielts-speaking-greeting-the-examiner/), and the [Scribd IELTS Speaking test script](https://www.scribd.com/document/522807390/S-Speaking-test-script)):
  - "Good morning/afternoon. My name is X. Can you tell me your full name, please?"
  - "And what should I call you?"
  - "Can I see your identification, please?"
  - Transition into Part 1: "Now, in this first part, I'd like to ask you some more questions about yourself, OK?"
- Topic-shift cues are drawn from a small fixed inventory: *"Let's talk about…"*, *"Let's move on to…"*, *"Now I'd like to ask you about…"*, *"And…?"*, *"What about…?"*, *"How about…?"* ([British Council practice questions](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/take-ielts/prepare/free-ielts-english-practice-tests/speaking/part-1), [IELTS-Blog Asia transcript PDF](https://www.ielts-blog.com/resources/asia-interview-transcript.pdf)).
- Examiners **may interrupt mid-answer in Part 1** for time/coverage or to break up memorised material — this is not a penalty signal ([IELTS Liz on examiner stops](https://ieltsliz.com/why-the-ielts-speaking-examiner-stops-your-answer/), [My IELTS Classroom on interruption](https://blog.myieltsclassroom.com/why-did-the-ielts-examiner-interrupt-me-in-my-speaking-test/)).

### Topic categories
The official wording on both [Academic](https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking) and [General Training](https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-general-training-test/ielts-general-training-format-speaking) pages: "the examiner asks you general questions on some familiar topics, such as **home, family, work, studies or interests**."

Working topic bank, cross-compiled from ex-examiner aggregators:
- Core four: **Work, Study, Hometown, Home/Accommodation** (always the first topic).
- High-frequency extensions: Family, Friends, Daily routine, Food, Cooking, Hobbies, Music, Sport, TV, Films, Reading, Newspapers, Internet, Social media, Mobile phones, Transport, Shopping, Clothes, Weather, Seasons, Holidays, Childhood, Pets, Flowers, Languages, Sleep, Patience, Happiness, Noise ([IELTS Liz topic list](https://ieltsliz.com/ielts-speaking-part-1-topics/), [Breakout English 120-question bank](https://breakoutenglish.com/ielts/ielts-speaking-part-1-questions/)).
- January–May 2026 rotation explicitly includes **Robots, Social media, Cinema-going, Sleep, Stars/planets, Tea-or-coffee with guests** ([Simply IELTS](https://simplyielts.com/recent-ielts-speaking-part-1-questions-january-2026-confirmed-topics/), [Preptical](https://ielts.preptical.com/speaking/ielts-speaking-questions-2026/), [Speaking9](https://speaking9.com/ielts-speaking-questions/0), [IELTS Liz 2026 topics](https://ieltsliz.com/ielts-speaking-topics-2026/)).

### Verbatim examiner question wordings
From the [British Council practice Part 1](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/take-ielts/prepare/free-ielts-english-practice-tests/speaking/part-1) (official):
- Hometown set: "Where is your hometown? What do you like about it? What do you not like about it? How important is your hometown to you?"
- Accommodation set: "Tell me about the kind of accommodation you live in. Does the place you live in have many amenities? Is there anything you would like to change about the place you live in? Do you plan to live there for a long time?"

From [Breakout English's compiled bank](https://breakoutenglish.com/ielts/ielts-speaking-part-1-questions/) (~120 documented questions sourced from test reports):
- Work: "What do you do?" / "What is the most important part of your job?" / "Do you work with other people or alone?" / "Why did you choose your job?" / "What is your dream job?"
- Study: "What do/did you study?" / "Why do/did you want to study that?" / "How long have you been studying English?" / "What was your worst subject at school?"
- Family: "Do you have any brothers or sisters?" / "Tell me about somebody in your family." / "Who do you admire in your family?"
- Daily routine: "What do you normally do in the morning?" / "Is exercise part of your daily routine?"
- Food: "What is your favourite food?" / "Do you enjoy cooking?" / "What is a traditional meal in your country?" / "Have you ever been on a diet?"
- Technology: "How often do you use a computer?" / "Is it OK for children to have mobile phones?" / "Is it bad to use the Internet too much?"
- Hobbies: "Do you have any creative hobbies like painting, writing, or music?" / "Do you prefer to do your hobbies alone or with other people?"
- Childhood: "Tell me about your school when you were a child." / "What was your favourite toy as a child?"
- [IELTS Mentor's bank](https://www.ielts-mentor.com/speaking-sample/speaking-part-1) adds verbatim Clothes/Food set: "Where do you buy most of your clothes? How often do you buy new clothes? Who normally does the cooking in your home?"

### Band descriptors as they apply to Part 1
The four criteria are **equally weighted at 25% each** ([ielts.org scoring](https://ielts.org/take-a-test/your-results/ielts-scoring-in-detail)), and the test is scored **holistically across all three parts** — Part 1 is not scored separately ([IELTS Ninja on scoring weight](https://ieltsninja.com/content/score-percentage-weight-part-1-2-3-ielts-speaking-test/)).

Fluency & Coherence (public version, [British Council PDF](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf)):
- **Band 9**: Fluent with only very occasional repetition/self-correction; any hesitation prepares content of the next utterance, not search for words/grammar; topic development fully coherent and appropriately extended.
- **Band 8**: Fluent with occasional repetition/self-correction; hesitation is usually content-related, only rarely to search for language.
- **Band 7**: Speaks at length without noticeable effort or loss of coherence; some language-related hesitation, some repetition; uses a range of connectives and discourse markers with some flexibility.
- **Band 6**: Willing to speak at length but may lose coherence due to occasional repetition, self-correction or hesitation; uses connectives but not always appropriately.
- **Band 5**: Maintains flow using repetition, self-correction or slow speech; may over-use certain connectives.
- **Band 4**: Noticeable pauses; ideas disjointed.

The load-bearing distinction the descriptors enforce: **content-related hesitation** (acceptable, characteristic of Band 8–9 even at Band 9) vs **language-related hesitation** (penalised; characteristic of Band 6 and below). This is the single most important signal to extract from a Part 1 transcript.

Pronunciation: "L1 accent has minimal effect on intelligibility" is the Band 7 criterion — a Korean (or any L1) accent is not a ceiling provided stress, intonation and consonant clusters are intelligible ([CareerWise English band breakdown](https://careerwiseenglish.com.au/ielts-speaking-part-1-band-score-guide/)).

### Part 1 vs Parts 2/3 scoring weight
- Scoring is holistic — "the 3 parts are not scored separately; the examiner rates the candidate's performance as a whole" ([IELTS Ninja](https://ieltsninja.com/content/score-percentage-weight-part-1-2-3-ielts-speaking-test/)).
- Part 1 is "a warm-up" — a candidate cannot earn a high band on Part 1 alone ([IELTS Ninja](https://ieltsninja.com/content/score-percentage-weight-part-1-2-3-ielts-speaking-test/)).
- Simon (ex-examiner): "Consider Part 2 as the core of your speaking test. It's the point at which most examiners get a clear idea of what score to give you" ([IELTS Simon](https://www.ielts-simon.com/ielts-help-and-english-pr/2019/01/ielts-speaking-which-part-is-the-most-important.html)).
- Inter-rater reliability for Speaking: **0.90** (Read 2022, citing Cambridge re-rating data, [SAGE](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/02655322221086211)). Examiners are trained over 2 days initially and recertified every 2 years.
- Memorised answers are penalised — examiners are trained to detect them ([IELTS Advantage](https://www.ieltsadvantage.com/2023/02/19/ielts-speaking-questions/), [Cullen / Key to IELTS Speaking](https://keytoielts.com/category/speaking/)).
- Content is not scored: "You will only be assessed on your English language skills, not on the content of what you actually say" ([IELTS Jacky](https://www.ieltsjacky.com/ielts-speaking-part-1.html)).
- Register: Part 1 expects **conversational small-talk English**, not academic register ([Keith Speaking Academy](https://keithspeakingacademy.com/ielts-speaking-test-format/), [Cullen / Key to IELTS](https://keytoielts.com/category/speaking/)). Over-formal vocabulary in Part 1 is itself a red flag for memorisation.

### Academic / validity framing
- Read 2022: the IF "favours reliability at the expense of construct validity" — the test "differs fundamentally from natural conversation" ([SAGE](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/02655322221086211)).
- 2001 revision shifted Speaking from holistic to **analytic** rating across the four criteria ([SAGE](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/02655322221086211)).
- Lazaraton (1992, 2001, 2002) — foundational discourse-analysis work showing examiners do deviate from IF but Cambridge research finds the impact on candidate language "essentially negligible in practical terms" in Parts 1 and 2 ([ielts.org research](https://ielts.org/researchers/our-research/research-reports/the-impact-on-candidate-language-of-examiner-deviation-from-a-set-interlocutor-frame-in-the-ielts-speaking-test)).
- "Reviewing the IELTS Speaking test in East Asia" (Springer, 2018) flags that Part 1's question-only format gives candidates **no opportunity to demonstrate topic-initiation or questioning skill**, so its scoring evidence is necessarily *responsive only* ([Language Testing in Asia 2018](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s40468-018-0056-5)).
- Other peer review: [Critical Review of IELTS Speaking — JL3T](https://doi.org/10.32505/jl3t.v9i2.7161); [Cognitive diagnostic approach](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s40468-024-00311-2); [Critical Review (ResearchGate)](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/376063204_A_Critical_Review_of_IELTS_Speaking_Test).

## Country-specific prep material

### Korea (한국)
Sources: [Hackers (해커스) Speaking Basic](https://www.hackers.co.kr/?m=bookmanager&quick=N&uid=488), [Gohackers mentor post](https://www.gohackers.com/?c=ielts%2Fielts_info2%2Fielts_method&type=url&uid=588365), [Siwon School LAB](https://ielts.siwonschool.com/?s=products_book), [Skybel Part 1 strategy guide](https://blog.skybel.co.kr/blog/아이엘츠-스피킹/아이엘츠-스피킹-Part-1-예상-질문과-답변-전략-총정리), [IDP Korea tips for Korean speakers](https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-ielts-tips-korean-language).

Key tactics: Hackers' Speaking Basic drills "답변 추가 표현" (answer-extension phrases) and "답변 셀프 체크 포인트" (self-check points). Siwon publishes a **빈출 질문 리스트** (frequently-asked question list) per topic that is treated as a **floor** every student must rehearse. Skybel's Part 1 template: **Direct answer → Reason → Personal example → Connector** (`For example / Actually / In addition / However`). Korean students are taught never to say "I study business" alone — always pair with *why-chosen* + *future goal*.

Cultural pitfalls warned about: memorisation is the single biggest risk — IDP and Hackers mentors both note Korean classroom English is "teacher-focused, grammar/vocab heavy with speaking practiced only by repeating the teacher or memorizing scripts," which examiners detect immediately. L1 pronunciation drill targets: /f/–/p/, /r/–/l/, /θ/, final consonant clusters.

### China (中国)
Sources: [雅思哥 (IELTS Bro) seasonal bank](https://ieltsbro.com/) and [seasonal oral bank](https://next.ieltsbro.com/oral_season/), [小站雅思 prediction article 1](https://m.zhan.com/ielts/rumen119863.html), [小站雅思 prediction article 2](https://m.zhan.com/ielts/rumen120190.html), [Zhihu strategy post 1](https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/379910939), [Zhihu strategy post 2](https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/445755673), [Zhihu ex-examiner's "5 misconceptions"](https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/355887162), [Zhihu Study/Work corpus](https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/353674116), [Gter forum thread](https://bbs.gter.net/thread-1881344-1-1.html), [老烤鸭雅思](http://www.laokaoya.com/59256.html), [朗阁 common mistakes](https://www.longre.com/yasi/speaking/183136.shtml).

The famous **题库 (tíkù, "question bank") system** is unique to the Chinese market. The bank resets on a **tri-quarterly 换题季** ("topic-change season") cycle: Jan–Apr, May–Aug, Sep–Dec. Each rotation swaps 30–50% of questions; of new ones, only 10–20% are genuinely new while the rest are **旧题回库题** (recycled retired questions) or **旧题改编题** (edited variants). Banks are categorised as **必考题 / 新题 / 保留题 / 回库题**. Cram apps update **weekly** from candidate exam-room recall (考场回忆). 雅思哥 claims coverage >90% of real exam-room questions.

Operationalisable Part 1 rules Chinese sites consistently teach: answer length floor ≥20 seconds / 4–5 complete sentences (老烤鸭); never carry Part 3 complexity into Part 1; classic Mandarin-L1 grammar leaks to watch for — he/she confusion, missing past tense, missing present perfect, singular/plural mismatch. Despite selling templates, top Zhihu posts and 朗阁 explicitly warn that examiners flag "套路化" (formulaic) speech and **背诵 (memorised recitation) is the most common downgrade trigger**.

### Vietnam (Việt Nam)
Sources: [IELTS Fighter — 42 topics PDF](https://ielts-fighter.com/tin-tuc/42-chu-de-IELTS-Speaking-Part-1-pho-bien-nhat-va-mau-tra-loi_mt1462076210.html), [IELTS Fighter Part 1 question set](https://ielts-fighter.com/speaking/ielts-speaking-part-1-question_mt1547599191.html), [IELTS Vietop Part 1 strategy](https://vietop.edu.vn/blog/chien-luoc-lam-bai-speaking-part-1/), [Vietop common mistakes](https://vietop.edu.vn/blog/common-mistakes-in-ielts-speaking), [DOL English](https://www.dolenglish.vn/blog/cach-tra-loi-ielts-speaking-part-1).

The IELTS Fighter **42 chủ đề** (42 topics) PDF + MP3 bundle is the canonical Vietnamese flashcard set. Vietop's named "lỗi sai cấp quốc gia" (national-level mistake): forgetting verb tenses, defaulting to present simple when narrating past events — Vietnamese has no verbal tense morphology. Tonal-language monotone problem: Vietnamese tones at syllable level means candidates produce flat sentence-level intonation; Vietop drills English stress-timing as a Vietnamese-specific intervention. Vietop also flags "echo trap" (parroting examiner wording) and "speed-fluency confusion" (rushing to sound fluent, mispronouncing instead). Vocabulary is told to **stay simple** — advanced words in Part 1 read as unnatural.

### Iran (ایران)
Sources: [Iran-Europe Language Institute](https://iran-europe.net/ساختار-بخش-اسپیکینگ-آیلتس/), [Zandi English](https://zandienglish.ir/ielts-speaking-part-1-questions-and-answers/), [IELTS Tehran](https://ieltstehran.com/blog/چگونه-در-پارت-اول-اسپیکینگ-بهتر-جواب-ده/), [IELTS2 Part 1 questions](https://ielts2.com/سوالات-اسپیکینگ-آیلتس-پارت-اول/), [زبان‌ساز frequent questions](https://zaban-saz.ir/frequent-ielts-speaking-questions/).

Iranian schools sell *The Key to IELTS Speaking* (Cullen-derived کتاب) as the canonical Part 1 textbook. The "parrot mistake" (تکرار مثل طوطی) — repeating examiner stems verbatim — is the hammered warning. Persian-L1 specific advice: accent is fine, but consonant clusters and syllable stress must be correct since Persian is syllable-timed and English stress-timed. Tense errors when narrating past events are flagged as the top grammar leak. Regional overcorrection: many Iranian guides ban slang and informal register even in Part 1, which is actually stricter than examiners require.

### India / South Asia
Sources: [IELTS Ninja — Indian struggles](https://ieltsninja.com/content/indians-struggle-ielts-speaking/), [IELTS Material Part 1 guide](https://ieltsmaterial.com/speaking/ielts-speaking-part-1/), [IELTS Buddy Part 1 technique](https://www.ieltsbuddy.com/tips-and-technique-for-ielts-speaking-part-1.html), [IDP India on accent](https://ieltsidpindia.com/blog/accent-in-ielts-speaking-test), [IDP India common topics](https://ieltsidpindia.com/blog/common-ielts-speaking-topics), [Aspire Square MTI neutralisation](https://aspiresquare.com/how-to-neutralise-mother-tongue-influence-mti-while-speaking-english/), [Clapingo MTI](https://clapingo.com/blog/mother-tongue-influence-problem-english).

The dominant Indian frame is **MTI ("Mother Tongue Influence") neutralisation** — almost every Indian prep site organises Part 1 prep around reducing MTI rather than topic-banking. Standard drill: 15–20 min/day read-aloud + record + playback, focusing on linking phrases (*well, you know, actually, I'd say*) and natural stress patterns. IDP India is explicit that the test grades **pronunciation (intelligibility, stress, intonation), NOT accent** — Indian English accent is acceptable; mumbled stress is not. Named failure mode: the "translate from L1 in head" trap — candidates think in regional language, mentally translate, then speak, producing unnatural English even with accurate grammar.

### Indonesia
Sources: [IDP Indonesia Part 1 prep](https://ielts.idp.com/indonesia/about/news-and-articles/article-preparation-for-ielts-speaking-part-1), [ElevaU tips](https://elevau.id/speaking/tips-speaking-ielts-part-1/), [ElevaU frequent topics](https://elevau.id/speaking/topik-speaking-ielts-yang-sering-muncul/), [Sun English](https://sunenglish.co.id/tips-menjawab-ielts-speaking-part-1/), [Schoters](https://blog.schoters.com/pertanyaan-speaking-ielts), [IELTSpresso](https://ieltspresso.com/tips-ielts-speaking-part-1/).

Signature Indonesian formula: **Jawaban Langsung + Alasan + Contoh Singkat** (Direct Answer + Reason + Brief Example) — short, three-part, drilled per topic. Indonesian guides specifically warn against **robotic intonation from script-reading** — even with good pronunciation, flat robot-reading is auto-detected and downgraded. Confidence/anxiety drills (breathing, visualisation) feature unusually prominently — test anxiety treated as primary obstacle.

### Russia / Kazakhstan
Sources: [yourielts.ru Speaking](https://yourielts.ru/prepare-for-ielts/ielts-speaking), [yourielts.ru secrets](https://yourielts.ru/prepare-for-ielts/ielts-speaking/ielts-speaking-secrets), [Anglo-Repetitor](https://anglo-repetitor.ru/blog/ielts/article-943830.html), [ITEC School](https://www.itec-school.ru/articles/podrobno-pro-ustnuyu-chast-ekzamena-ielts-speaking/), [Expert Language](https://expertlanguage.ru/useful/podgotovka-k-ielts-speaking-osnovnye-sovety/), [IELTS-GPT for Kazakh students](https://ielts-gpt.com/blog/ielts-preparation-tips-kazakh-students).

Russian-language prescription is unusually **terse** — a 2–3 sentence answer floor (shorter than Chinese/Korean), framed as "natural under time pressure." Mandatory **contractions drill** (*I'd, can't, don't*) because Russian doesn't contract; full forms read as translated. Russian guides emphasise asking for clarification — Russian candidates tend to bluff/freeze on unexpected questions. IELTS-GPT for Kazakh students names the canonical Russophone failure mode: "fluency holds until an unexpected question, then candidate freezes into one-word answers because they only prepared scripts."

### Japan (日本)
Sources: [Solo IELTS TOEFL Part 1 100-sample guide](https://solo-ielts-toefl.com/speaking-part1-sample/), [atsueigo Speaking testimonial](https://knowledge.atsueigo.com/ieltsspeaking/), [Berkeley House blog](https://berkeleyhouse.co.jp/blog/ielts/ielts-speaking-flow/), [IELTS Score Book](https://ielts-score.net/speaking), [Best Teacher](https://www.best-teacher-inc.com/blog/ielts-example-questions).

Japanese benchmark: **5 sentences per Part 1 answer** with reasons attached to any Yes/No. Standard study format is the 100-question topic deck. Named pitfall: **棒読み ("stick-reading", mechanical recitation) caps you at band 5.5**. Speed myth — Japanese candidates believe speaking faster = higher fluency. Atsueigo and Solo IELTS debunk: fast = sounds memorised = downgrade. Berkeley House notes Japanese candidates underprepare Part 1 thinking it's "easy," missing that this is where examiners set the band-impression anchor.

### Brazil / Lusophone
Sources: [British Council Brazil](https://www.britishcouncil.org.br/exame/ielts/blog/ingles-para-conversacao), [Estudar Fora](https://www.estudarfora.org.br/ielts-speaking-respostas-completas/), [Hotcourses BR](https://www.hotcourses.com.br/study-abroad-info/applying-to-university/prepare-se-para-a-secao-de-conversacao-do-ielts/), [British Institute BR](https://britishinstitute.com.br/blog/ielts-speaking-guia-completo-das-partes-1-2-e-3), [EnglishPrep CLB9](https://englishprep.com.br/clb9-ielts-speaking/), [Magoosh PT](https://magoosh.com/ielts/parte-1-teste-de-conversacao-ielts-introducao-e-entrevista/).

Core Brazilian prescription: **expandir a resposta** (expand the answer) while staying on topic — Estudar Fora frames this as Brazilians' biggest unlock since the conversational style is already expressive. Brazilian guides skew toward **CLB9** (Canadian immigration band) more than UK university audiences. Less anti-memorisation framing than in Asian markets; more anti-stiffness framing — "forced English sounds forced."

### Arabic-speaking world (Saudi Arabia / Gulf)
Sources: [IDP Saudi Arabia Part 1](https://ielts.idp.com/saudiarabia/prepare/article-ielts-speaking-test-part-1), [IDP Saudi free practice](https://ielts.idp.com/saudiarabia/prepare/article-free-speaking-practice-questions), [Saad Al-Dehaimi](https://saadadehaimi.com/اختبار-الآيلتس/مهارة-المحادثة-speaking-skill/), [Dalilk4IELTS](https://library.dalilk4ielts.com/دليلك-لقسم-المحادثة-في-اختبار-الايلتس/), [IESCO](https://iesco.my/ar/أقسام-اختبار-الايلتس-وطريقة-التحضير/), [EnglishCentral AR](https://www.englishcentral.com/blog/ar/ielts-speaking-questions-أسئلة-المحادثة-في-اختبار-الآيلتس/).

Saad Al-Dehaimi (popular Saudi IELTS YouTuber/site) prescribes **academic register only — no slang, no عامية (dialect informalities) even in Part 1** — a regional overcorrection since real Part 1 tolerates casual register, but it's the dominant Gulf teaching. Top Arabic-L1 grammar leak: tense collapse to present when narrating past, because Arabic aspect doesn't map 1:1 to English past-perfect / past-simple distinctions. Saudi guides give unusually prominent same-day prep advice (sleep, breakfast).

| Echoing/parroting examiner words | Iran, Vietnam, India |
| Single-word / under-2-sentence answers | China (20 s floor), Korea, Japan (5-sentence target), Russia (2–3 sentences) |
| Hometown question = describing the city instead of *your experience of it* | Pan-regional, hardest-called in Chinese 知乎 posts |
| Over-formal academic register in Part 1 | Saudi/Gulf, India |
| Speed = fluency myth | Japan, Vietnam |
| Quarterly question-bank prediction | **China only** (换题季 system) — unique market behaviour |

## Open-source tools and code

No GitHub repo is purpose-built for IELTS Part 1 scoring on spontaneous speech. The open work splits into four reusable blocks; a Part 1 scorer would compose them.

**Read-speech pronunciation scoring** (foundational, limited but reusable):
- [speechocean762](https://github.com/jimbozhang/speechocean762) — 5,000 sentences from Mandarin-L1 speakers, 5-expert scores on accuracy/fluency/completeness/prosody. v1.2.0, Jul 2022. Free for commercial & non-commercial. Mirror: [HuggingFace](https://huggingface.co/datasets/mispeech/speechocean762); paper [arXiv 2104.01378](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2104.01378); [OpenSLR-101](https://www.openslr.org/101/).
- [GOPT (Gong, ICASSP 2022)](https://github.com/YuanGongND/gopt) — Transformer over Kaldi GOP features → phoneme/word/utterance scores. BSD-3-Clause, updated Jan 2023. r=0.743 utt-level on SO-762. Read-speech only.
- [L2-ARCTIC](https://psi.engr.tamu.edu/l2-arctic-corpus/) — 24 non-native speakers including **Korean L1**, 27.1 h read speech, mispronunciation-annotated. CC BY-NC 4.0. [Interspeech 2018 paper](https://psi.engr.tamu.edu/wp-content/uploads/2018/08/zhao2018interspeech.pdf).

**Spontaneous / dialogue-level scoring (closer to Part 1)**:
- [EvalYaks (Talking-Yak)](https://github.com/Talking-Yak/EvalYaks) — 6 models for the 4 sections of a CEFR B2 speaking exam + vocabulary CEFR + text CEFR. ~96% acceptable-accuracy. Apache-style. The "Introduction" section model maps almost 1:1 onto Part 1; B2 ≈ IELTS 5.5–6.5.
- [deep_pron (Vyas Raina)](https://github.com/rainavyas/deep_pron) — closest open re-implementation of Cambridge in-house Linguaskill-style grader, based on Kyriakopoulos et al. (Interspeech 2018).
- [Speechat (Shahabks)](https://github.com/Shahabks/Speechat) — feature extractor for prosody/intonation/stress.

**Disfluency / filler detection** (the most operationally relevant block for Part 1):
- [CrisperWhisper](https://github.com/nyrahealth/CrisperWhisper) + [model on HF](https://huggingface.co/nyrahealth/CrisperWhisper) — Whisper fine-tune emitting verbatim transcripts including `[UM]`/`[UH]` tokens with corrected word-level timestamps; SOTA on filler-event detection. CC BY-NC 4.0. Paper [arXiv 2408.16589](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2408.16589). Single most useful single tool for Part 1.
- [WhisperD](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2505.21551) — 2025 Whisper fine-tune scoring Filler Inclusion Rate (FIR).
- [joint-disfluency-detector-and-parser (Jamshid Lou)](https://github.com/pariajm/joint-disfluency-detector-and-parser) — self-attentive parser on Switchboard for filled-pauses/restarts/repetitions/revisions.
- [disfluency_detection_from_audio (Romana)](https://github.com/amritkromana/disfluency_detection_from_audio) — multimodal (audio + text) demo, active 2023.
- [DisfluencySpeech dataset on HF](https://huggingface.co/datasets/amaai-lab/DisfluencySpeech) — filled pauses, editing terms, discourse markers annotated.

**Voice-activity / pause detection** (for Tavakoli-style breakdown fluency):
- [Silero VAD](https://github.com/snakers4/silero-vad) — MIT, pretrained on 6000+ languages, 87.7% TPR @ 5% FPR.
- [py-webrtcvad](https://github.com/wiseman/py-webrtcvad) — MIT, lightweight fallback.

**Lexical-diversity / linguistic-complexity tooling** (transcript side):
- [TAALED (Kyle / LCR-ADS-Lab)](https://github.com/LCR-ADS-Lab/TAALED) — TTR, MATTR, MTLD, HDD indices.
- [LexicalRichness (Shen)](https://github.com/LSYS/LexicalRichness) — MIT, mature pip-installable.
- [TAALES (Kyle)](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/The-tool-for-the-automatic-analysis-of-lexical-2.0-Kyle-Crossley/4d7f35aca70d3cb8a205feedc87f50ae46627188) — lexical sophistication, n-gram frequency, age-of-acquisition.
- L2SCA (Lu, 2010) — 14 syntactic complexity measures, validated for L2 spoken production ([Modern Language Journal article on L2SCA](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/modl.12907)).

**Foundational papers for designing the scoring function**:
- Tavakoli (2018) *Fluency in Second Language Testing: Insights From Different Disciplines* — [tandfonline PDF](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/pdf/10.1080/15434303.2018.1477780). Canonical breakdown/speed/repair fluency operationalisation.
- Tavakoli & Skehan triadic framework: silent pause threshold **≥ 250 ms** (sometimes ≥ 400 ms); mid-clause pauses more diagnostic than end-clause; articulation rate, MLR, repair rate. See [de Jong LANGSNAP PDF](https://blog.soton.ac.uk/langsnap/files/2013/04/LANGSNAP_dejong.pdf) and [EEJPL temporal measurements paper](https://eejpl.vnu.edu.ua/index.php/eejpl/article/view/905).
- Bannò & Matassoni (2022) *Proficiency assessment of L2 spoken English using wav2vec 2.0* — [arXiv 2210.13168](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2210.13168). Wav2vec graders beat BERT-on-transcript on grammar/lexical/pronunciation/fluency.
- *Assessment of L2 Oral Proficiency using Speech LLMs* — Interspeech 2025, [ISCA Archive](https://www.isca-archive.org/interspeech_2025/ma25b_interspeech.pdf).
- *Session-Level Spoken Language Assessment with a Multimodal Foundation Model* — [arXiv 2509.16025](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2509.16025).
- *Impact of ASR on Linguistic Complexity Analysis* — [arXiv 2104.08529](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2104.08529). Quantifies which complexity measures survive imperfect transcripts.

**Caveat**: ETS SpeechRater ([myspeakingscore.com](https://www.myspeakingscore.com/speechrater)) and Cambridge Linguaskill are closed-source; no open production-grade IELTS auto-grader exists.

## Practical signals for an LLM judge

These are operationalisable rules a judge can apply when reading a diarized Part 1 transcript. Numbers are anchored to research or examiner-blog consensus cited above.

1. **Question-count sanity check.** Expect 7–12 examiner questions across 2–3 topic sets in 4–5 min. Fewer than 7 ⇒ candidate over-extended (compute average answer length; very long Part 1 answers are themselves a downgrade signal — Liz, Jacky). More than 12 ⇒ candidate gave one-word answers and was prompted heavily.
2. **First-topic frame check.** The first topic set should be Work, Study, Home, or Hometown. If anything else, the diarisation is wrong or this is not actually Part 1.
3. **Answer-length target.** ~2–3 sentences / ~10–30 seconds per answer is the band-7-ish target (Keith, Jacky, My IELTS Classroom). Single-clause answers across multiple questions ⇒ Band ≤ 5 evidence. Multi-paragraph Part 1 answers (>45 s) suggest the candidate is over-elaborating in the wrong test stage — flag as misallocated effort.
4. **Hesitation classifier — the load-bearing signal.** Tag each hesitation as **content-related** (thinking what to say; OK at all bands) vs **language-related** (searching for words/grammar; penalised). Repeated language-related hesitations within a single answer ⇒ Band 6 evidence.
5. **Silent-pause threshold ≥ 250 ms.** (Tavakoli / de Jong canonical cut-off.) Weight **mid-clause** pauses much higher than end-clause pauses — mid-clause is the diagnostic L2 marker.
6. **Filler rate (`um`, `uh`, `like`, `you know`, `er`)**: Band-7-ish ≈ < 5/min; Band-6-ish ≈ ~10/min. Use CrisperWhisper `[UM]`/`[UH]` tokens as ground truth if audio is available.
7. **Articulation rate (excluding pauses)**: Band-7-ish ≈ 3.5–4.5 syllables/sec; below 3.0 typically reads as Band ≤ 6. Above 5.5 with no variation often signals memorised recitation.
8. **Mean length of run (MLR) between pauses**: Band 7 ≈ 6–8 syllables; Band 6 ≈ 3–5.
9. **Repair rate**: more than ~3 self-corrections/min is a Band 6 marker (Tavakoli "repair" dimension).
10. **Discourse-marker range.** Acceptable spoken DMs (NOT counted as hesitation): *well, I mean, you know, actually, to be honest, anyway*. Band 7 requires "flexible use of spoken discourse markers" — count distinct DMs used; <3 distinct over the whole Part 1 ⇒ Band 6 evidence; mechanical reuse of one DM ("uh I think" before every answer) ⇒ Band 5–6.
11. **Memorised-answer detector.** Flag any of: (a) suspiciously low filler rate combined with high lexical sophistication in Part 1 specifically; (b) academic register (`furthermore`, `consequently`, `it is widely acknowledged that`) in Part 1; (c) verbatim repetition of fixed multi-clause templates across topic changes; (d) answer that doesn't actually address the specific question wording. Examiners are trained to interrupt these and the descriptor implicitly downgrades them.
12. **Echo / parroting check** (Iranian, Vietnamese, Indian L1 pattern). If the candidate begins a meaningful fraction of answers with the examiner's stem verbatim (`"Where is my hometown? My hometown is…"`), that is a Band 5–6 marker — the descriptor at Band 7 requires paraphrasing and sentence restructuring.
13. **Tense-collapse check** (Mandarin/Vietnamese/Persian/Arabic L1 pattern). Past-narrative questions ("What was your favourite toy as a child?") should produce past-tense morphology. Present-simple narration of past events ⇒ Grammatical Range & Accuracy band ≤ 6 evidence.
14. **Lexical diversity** (transcript side). MTLD ≥ 50 is a Band 7 floor for Part 1-sized samples; MTLD is unstable on < 50-token samples, so use MATTR window 20 for very short answers. Match against TAALES bands for sophistication.
15. **Holistic-only framing in output.** Because IELTS Speaking is scored across all three parts as a whole (IELTS Ninja, Read 2022), the judge must phrase Part 1-only output as *"Part 1 evidence indicates band-X tendency on criterion Y"* — never as a final band. The Part 1 sample is informative for FC and Pronunciation, weaker evidence for Lexical Resource and Grammatical Range, since Part 2's long turn is where those are best demonstrated (Simon).
