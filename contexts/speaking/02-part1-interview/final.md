# Part 1 (4–5 min interview) — format, topic categories, examiner question banks

_Merged from codex + claude crawls on 2026-05-19T12:00:00Z._

## Summary

IELTS Speaking Part 1 is the 4–5 minute opening interview of an 11–14 minute three-part Speaking test. After identity/name check, the examiner reads from a tightly controlled **Interlocutor Frame (IF)** — scripted verbatim by Cambridge ESOL since the early 1990s "to ensure that all test events conform to the original test design" — and asks short question-answer prompts about the candidate's own life and familiar everyday topics such as home, family, work, studies, interests, weather, cooking, music, movies, hobbies, and daily routines ([ielts.org Academic format](https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking), [IDP free practice questions](https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-free-speaking-practice-questions), [IF deviation research](https://ielts.org/researchers/our-research/research-reports/the-impact-on-candidate-language-of-examiner-deviation-from-a-set-interlocutor-frame-in-the-ielts-speaking-test)).

It matters for scoring because it gives early evidence of whether the candidate can answer directly, extend briefly but naturally, maintain continuity, use topic-specific everyday vocabulary, shift tenses when the question requires it, and stay intelligible under scripted examiner control ([key assessment criteria](https://ielts.org/cdn/Guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf), [Seedhouse et al. 2011 topic-development research](https://cdn.ielts.org/Research/topic-development-in-ielts-speaking-test-seedhouse-et-al-2011.pdf)). However, IELTS Speaking is scored **holistically across all three parts** on four equally-weighted criteria (Fluency & Coherence, Lexical Resource, Grammatical Range & Accuracy, Pronunciation), so a Part 1-only judge must phrase its output as evidence-of-tendency, not a final band ([IELTS Ninja on scoring weight](https://ieltsninja.com/content/score-percentage-weight-part-1-2-3-ielts-speaking-test/), [Simon ex-examiner](https://www.ielts-simon.com/ielts-help-and-english-pr/2019/01/ielts-speaking-which-part-is-the-most-important.html)).

## Authoritative material

### Format and timing
- Total Speaking test: **11–14 minutes**; three parts; face-to-face, audio-recorded, with a certified examiner ([ielts.org](https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking), [IELTS Canada](https://www.ielts.ca/ielts-speaking-test/)). IDP Australia notes some centres offer video-call Speaking, still interactive ([ielts.com.au](https://ielts.com.au/australia/prepare/speaking)).
- Part 1: **4–5 min**; Part 2: 3–4 min (1 min prep + ~2 min long turn + 1–2 follow-ups); Part 3: 4–5 min abstract discussion ([ielts.org](https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking)).
- Speaking format is **identical** for Academic and General Training — "no provision for examiners to conduct the test any differently" ([Read 2022, *Language Testing*](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/02655322221086211); [GT Speaking format](https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-general-training-test/ielts-general-training-format-speaking)).
- IDP says Part 1 starts with name/ID, then current life status (where you live, work/study), then 1–2 more familiar topics; examiner may prompt "why?" / "why not?" if answers are too short ([IDP](https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-free-speaking-practice-questions)).

### Examiner script — the Interlocutor Frame (IF)
- The Part 1 script is **prescribed verbatim** by the IF; Read 2022 confirms Part 1 / Part 2 "are quite stable in terms of deviations" — examiners rarely paraphrase ([SAGE](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/02655322221086211), [IF deviation report](https://ielts.org/researchers/our-research/research-reports/the-impact-on-candidate-language-of-examiner-deviation-from-a-set-interlocutor-frame-in-the-ielts-speaking-test)).
- Standard opening script (cross-validated across [IELTS Yasi](https://ielts-yasi.englishlab.net/The_Introduction_Phase.htm), [IELTS Liz](https://ieltsliz.com/ielts-speaking-greeting-the-examiner/), [Scribd transcript](https://www.scribd.com/document/522807390/S-Speaking-test-script)):
  - "Good morning/afternoon. My name is X. Can you tell me your full name, please?"
  - "And what should I call you?"
  - "Can I see your identification, please?"
  - Transition: "Now, in this first part, I'd like to ask you some more questions about yourself, OK?"
- Topic-shift cues from a small fixed inventory: *"Let's talk about…"*, *"Let's move on to…"*, *"Now I'd like to ask you about…"*, *"And…?"*, *"What about…?"*, *"How about…?"* ([British Council](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/take-ielts/prepare/free-ielts-english-practice-tests/speaking/part-1), [IELTS-Blog Asia transcript PDF](https://www.ielts-blog.com/resources/asia-interview-transcript.pdf)).
- **First topic is always Work or Study** (one of the two); examiners default to Home/Hometown if the candidate does neither ([IELTS Advantage](https://www.ieltsadvantage.com/2023/02/19/ielts-speaking-questions/)).
- Examiners **may interrupt mid-answer in Part 1** for time/coverage or to break up memorised material — this is not a penalty signal by itself ([IELTS Liz on interruption](https://ieltsliz.com/why-the-ielts-speaking-examiner-stops-your-answer/), [My IELTS Classroom](https://blog.myieltsclassroom.com/why-did-the-ielts-examiner-interrupt-me-in-my-speaking-test/), [IDP Australia](https://ielts.com.au/australia/prepare/speaking)).
- Research framing: Part 1 is a succession of topic-based question-answer adjacency pairs; topic shifts are **examiner-controlled**, not candidate-controlled ([Seedhouse et al. 2011](https://cdn.ielts.org/Research/topic-development-in-ielts-speaking-test-seedhouse-et-al-2011.pdf)). A low-scoring example in the report shows minimal "yeah" responses that answer the question but fail to develop the topic.

### Topic categories
The official wording on both [Academic](https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-academic-test/ielts-academic-format-speaking) and [General Training](https://ielts.org/take-a-test/test-types/ielts-general-training-test/ielts-general-training-format-speaking) pages: "the examiner asks you general questions on some familiar topics, such as **home, family, work, studies or interests**."

- **Core four** (always one is the first set): Work, Study, Hometown, Home/Accommodation.
- **High-frequency extensions**: Family, Friends, Daily routine, Food, Cooking, Hobbies, Music, Sport, TV, Films, Reading, Newspapers, Internet, Social media, Mobile phones, Transport, Shopping, Clothes, Weather, Seasons, Holidays, Childhood, Pets, Flowers, Languages, Sleep, Patience, Happiness, Noise, Festivals, Travelling, Ambitions ([IELTS Liz topic list](https://ieltsliz.com/ielts-speaking-part-1-topics/), [Breakout English 120-question bank](https://breakoutenglish.com/ielts/ielts-speaking-part-1-questions/), [British Council lexical-resource worksheet](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/speaking_part_1_familiarisation_and_lexical_resource.pdf)).
- **January–May 2026 rotation** explicitly includes Robots, Social media, Cinema-going, Sleep, Stars/planets, Tea-or-coffee with guests ([Simply IELTS](https://simplyielts.com/recent-ielts-speaking-part-1-questions-january-2026-confirmed-topics/), [Preptical](https://ielts.preptical.com/speaking/ielts-speaking-questions-2026/), [Speaking9](https://speaking9.com/ielts-speaking-questions/0), [IELTS Liz 2026](https://ieltsliz.com/ielts-speaking-topics-2026/)).

### Verbatim examiner question wordings
From the official [British Council Part 1 practice](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/take-ielts/prepare/free-ielts-english-practice-tests/speaking/part-1):
- **Hometown**: "Where is your hometown? What do you like about it? What do you not like about it? How important is your hometown to you?"
- **Accommodation**: "Tell me about the kind of accommodation you live in. Does the place you live in have many amenities? Is there anything you would like to change about the place you live in? Do you plan to live there for a long time?"

From [Breakout English](https://breakoutenglish.com/ielts/ielts-speaking-part-1-questions/) (~120 questions sourced from test reports):
- **Work**: "What do you do?" / "What is the most important part of your job?" / "Do you work with other people or alone?" / "Why did you choose your job?" / "What is your dream job?"
- **Study**: "What do/did you study?" / "Why do/did you want to study that?" / "How long have you been studying English?" / "What was your worst subject at school?"
- **Family**: "Do you have any brothers or sisters?" / "Tell me about somebody in your family." / "Who do you admire in your family?"
- **Daily routine**: "What do you normally do in the morning?" / "Is exercise part of your daily routine?"
- **Food**: "What is your favourite food?" / "Do you enjoy cooking?" / "What is a traditional meal in your country?" / "Have you ever been on a diet?"
- **Technology**: "How often do you use a computer?" / "Is it OK for children to have mobile phones?" / "Is it bad to use the Internet too much?"
- **Hobbies**: "Do you have any creative hobbies like painting, writing, or music?" / "Do you prefer to do your hobbies alone or with other people?"
- **Childhood**: "Tell me about your school when you were a child." / "What was your favourite toy as a child?"

[IELTS Mentor](https://www.ielts-mentor.com/speaking-sample/speaking-part-1) adds verbatim Clothes/Food: "Where do you buy most of your clothes? How often do you buy new clothes? Who normally does the cooking in your home?"

### Scoring — the four criteria

The Speaking test uses nine bands and four criteria: **Fluency and Coherence, Lexical Resource, Grammatical Range and Accuracy, Pronunciation** — **each weighted 25%** ([key assessment criteria PDF](https://ielts.org/cdn/Guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf), [ielts.org scoring detail](https://ielts.org/take-a-test/your-results/ielts-scoring-in-detail)). The 2001 revision shifted Speaking from holistic to **analytic** rating across these four criteria ([Read 2022](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/02655322221086211)).

**Fluency & Coherence.** Definition: continuity, rate, effort, linking ideas into coherent connected speech. Penalised: excessive false starts, backtracking, functionless repetition, word-search pausing. Coherence indicators: logical sequencing, appropriate pausing/discourse markers, relevance to turn purpose, connectors, pronouns, conjunctions ([criteria PDF](https://ielts.org/cdn/Guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf)). Public band descriptors ([PDF](https://ielts.org/cdn/ielts-guides/ielts-speaking-band-descriptors.pdf), [British Council PDF](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/ielts_speaking_band_descriptors.pdf)):
- **Band 9**: only very occasional repetition/self-correction; hesitation prepares content of next utterance, not search for words/grammar.
- **Band 8**: occasional repetition/self-correction; hesitation usually content-related, only rarely to search for language.
- **Band 7**: speaks at length without noticeable effort or loss of coherence; some language-related hesitation, some repetition; range of connectives and discourse markers used with some flexibility.
- **Band 6**: willing to speak at length but may lose coherence due to repetition, self-correction, or hesitation; connectives used but not always appropriately.
- **Band 5**: maintains flow using repetition, self-correction, or slow speech; may over-use certain connectives.
- **Band 4**: noticeable pauses; ideas disjointed.

The load-bearing distinction: **content-related hesitation** (acceptable, characteristic even at Band 9) vs **language-related hesitation** (penalised; characteristic of Band 6 and below). This is the single most important signal to extract from a Part 1 transcript.

**Lexical Resource.** Definition: vocabulary range, precision of meaning and attitude, appropriacy of style, collocation including idiomatic expressions, paraphrasing around a vocabulary gap ([criteria PDF](https://ielts.org/cdn/Guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf)). Band descriptors: Band 8 — wide, flexible resource with skillful less-common/idiomatic use and effective paraphrase; Band 7 — flexible topic vocabulary, some less-common/idiomatic items, collocation awareness, effective paraphrase; Band 6 — enough resource to discuss topics at length but possible inappropriate vocabulary; Band 5 — limited flexibility on unfamiliar topics.

**Grammatical Range & Accuracy.** Range = sentence length, subordinate clauses, verb phrase complexity (aspect/modality/passive), phrase modification, varied sentence structures. Accuracy = error density and communicative effect of errors. Band 8 vs 7 separated by width/flexibility of structures and by most sentences being error-free; Band 6 mixes simple and complex forms with frequent errors in complex structures that rarely impede communication.

**Pronunciation.** Sustained use of phonological features to convey meaning: chunking speech into meaningful utterances, rhythm, stress timing, sound linking/elision, contrastive stress, intonation, word/phoneme production, listener effort, and accent's effect on intelligibility ([criteria PDF](https://ielts.org/cdn/Guides/ielts-speaking-key-assessment-criteria.pdf)). High bands: flexible phonological features, easy/effortless intelligibility. Lower bands: limited stress/intonation control, frequent mispronunciations, patches requiring listener effort. **Band 7 criterion: "L1 accent has minimal effect on intelligibility"** — accent itself is not a ceiling provided stress, intonation, and consonant clusters are intelligible ([CareerWise English](https://careerwiseenglish.com.au/ielts-speaking-part-1-band-score-guide/)).

### Part 1 vs Parts 2/3 scoring weight
- Scoring is **holistic** — "the 3 parts are not scored separately; the examiner rates the candidate's performance as a whole" ([IELTS Ninja](https://ieltsninja.com/content/score-percentage-weight-part-1-2-3-ielts-speaking-test/)).
- Part 1 is a **warm-up**; a candidate cannot earn a high band on Part 1 alone. Simon (ex-examiner): "Consider Part 2 as the core of your speaking test. It's the point at which most examiners get a clear idea of what score to give you" ([IELTS Simon](https://www.ielts-simon.com/ielts-help-and-english-pr/2019/01/ielts-speaking-which-part-is-the-most-important.html)).
- **Inter-rater reliability for Speaking: 0.90** (Read 2022, citing Cambridge re-rating; examiners trained 2 days initially, recertified every 2 years).
- **Memorised answers are penalised** — examiners are trained to detect them ([IELTS Advantage](https://www.ieltsadvantage.com/2023/02/19/ielts-speaking-questions/), [Cullen / Key to IELTS Speaking](https://keytoielts.com/category/speaking/), [British Council worksheet](https://takeielts.britishcouncil.org/sites/default/files/speaking_part_1_familiarisation_and_lexical_resource.pdf)).
- **Content is not scored**: "You will only be assessed on your English language skills, not on the content of what you actually say" ([IELTS Jacky](https://www.ieltsjacky.com/ielts-speaking-part-1.html)).
- **Register**: Part 1 expects **conversational small-talk English**, not academic register. Over-formal vocabulary in Part 1 is itself a red flag for memorisation ([Keith Speaking Academy](https://keithspeakingacademy.com/ielts-speaking-test-format/), [Cullen](https://keytoielts.com/category/speaking/)).

### Academic / validity framing
- Read 2022: the IF "favours reliability at the expense of construct validity" — the test "differs fundamentally from natural conversation" ([SAGE](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/02655322221086211)).
- Lazaraton (1992, 2001, 2002) — foundational discourse-analysis work showing examiners do deviate from IF but Cambridge research finds the impact on candidate language "essentially negligible in practical terms" in Parts 1 and 2 ([IF research](https://ielts.org/researchers/our-research/research-reports/the-impact-on-candidate-language-of-examiner-deviation-from-a-set-interlocutor-frame-in-the-ielts-speaking-test)).
- "Reviewing the IELTS Speaking test in East Asia" (Springer, 2018): Part 1's question-only format gives candidates **no opportunity to demonstrate topic-initiation or questioning skill**, so its scoring evidence is necessarily *responsive only* ([Language Testing in Asia 2018](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s40468-018-0056-5)).
- Additional peer review: [Critical Review of IELTS Speaking — JL3T](https://doi.org/10.32505/jl3t.v9i2.7161); [Cognitive diagnostic approach](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s40468-024-00311-2); [Critical Review (ResearchGate)](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/376063204_A_Critical_Review_of_IELTS_Speaking_Test).

## Country-specific prep material

### South Korea (한국)
Sources: [Hackers Speaking Basic](https://www.hackers.co.kr/?m=bookmanager&quick=N&uid=488), [Gohackers mentor post](https://www.gohackers.com/?c=ielts%2Fielts_info2%2Fielts_method&type=url&uid=588365), [Siwon School LAB](https://ielts.siwonschool.com/?s=products_book), [Skybel Part 1 strategy](https://blog.skybel.co.kr/blog/아이엘츠-스피킹/아이엘츠-스피킹-Part-1-예상-질문과-답변-전략-총정리), [IDP Korea Korean-language tips](https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-ielts-tips-korean-language), [ed:m IELTS Speaking handout](https://www.edmclass.com/ielts/lib/download/free_lecture/sp_lec_202002.pdf), [ed:m Stella Lee lecture](https://www.edmclass.com/ielts/lib/download/free_lecture/2021/sp_upfile_20210423082751.pdf).

Key tactics: ed:m advises Part 1 candidates to answer only the question, keep answers short at roughly **1–3 sentences**, answer directly, and include a reason to avoid extra follow-up pressure. Stella Lee frames Part 1 answers as core answer + short elaboration within **2–4 lines** via reason/example/addition/contrast. Hackers' Speaking Basic drills "답변 추가 표현" (answer-extension phrases) and "답변 셀프 체크 포인트" (self-check points). Siwon publishes a **빈출 질문 리스트** (frequently-asked question list) per topic treated as a floor every student must rehearse. Skybel's template: **Direct answer → Reason → Personal example → Connector** (`For example / Actually / In addition / However`). Korean students are taught never to say "I study business" alone — always pair with *why-chosen* + *future goal*. Topic bank: home/accommodation, hometown, breaks, work/study, travel, transport, history, animals, planning, tea/coffee, languages, concentration, numbers, laughter, science class, scenery, movies, art, reading, parks, local area, cake, running, haircuts, markets, music, public holidays, photos, water sports, walking, friends, voice, maths, time alone; shopping/fashion collocations include value-for-money, hand-me-down, tailor-made, dress code, luxury goods, impulse buying.

Cultural pitfalls: **memorisation is the single biggest risk** — IDP and Hackers mentors both note Korean classroom English is "teacher-focused, grammar/vocab heavy with speaking practiced only by repeating the teacher or memorizing scripts," which examiners detect immediately. L1 pronunciation drill targets: /f/–/p/, /r/–/l/, /θ/, final consonant clusters.

### China (中国) / Hong Kong
Sources: [雅思哥 (IELTS Bro)](https://ieltsbro.com/) and [seasonal oral bank](https://next.ieltsbro.com/oral_season/), [小站雅思 1](https://m.zhan.com/ielts/rumen119863.html) / [2](https://m.zhan.com/ielts/rumen120190.html), [Zhihu strategy posts](https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/379910939) ([2](https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/445755673), [3 — ex-examiner](https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/355887162), [4 — Study/Work corpus](https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/353674116)), [Gter forum](https://bbs.gter.net/thread-1881344-1-1.html), [老烤鸭](http://www.laokaoya.com/59256.html), [朗阁](https://www.longre.com/yasi/speaking/183136.shtml), [IDP Hong Kong (Simplified Chinese)](https://ielts.idp.com/hongkong/prepare/article-free-speaking-practice-questions/zh-hans), [IELTSDay weather/season bank](https://www.ieltsday.com/433.html), [EasyIELTS.cn](https://easyielts.cn/speaking), [Zhihu Part 1 length answer](https://www.zhihu.com/question/269054280/answer/3181743459).

The famous **题库 (tíkù, "question bank") system** is unique to the Chinese market. The bank resets on a **tri-quarterly 换题季** ("topic-change season") cycle: Jan–Apr, May–Aug, Sep–Dec. Each rotation swaps 30–50% of questions; of new ones, only 10–20% are genuinely new while the rest are **旧题回库题** (recycled retired questions) or **旧题改编题** (edited variants). Banks are categorised as **必考题 / 新题 / 保留题 / 回库题**. Cram apps update **weekly** from candidate exam-room recall (考场回忆). 雅思哥 claims coverage >90% of real exam-room questions. IDP Hong Kong's Simplified Chinese page mirrors the IDP English format and notes scripted questions plus "why/why not" extension prompts.

Operationalisable Part 1 rules Chinese sites consistently teach: **answer length floor ≥20 seconds / 4–5 complete sentences** (老烤鸭); never carry Part 3 complexity into Part 1; classic Mandarin-L1 grammar leaks — he/she confusion, missing past tense, missing present perfect, singular/plural mismatch. Topic-card categories visible on EasyIELTS.cn: talents, mirrors, old buildings, lost and found, emails, ads, photos, cars, street markets, websites, watches. Despite selling templates, top Zhihu posts and 朗阁 explicitly warn that examiners flag "套路化" (formulaic) speech and **背诵 (memorised recitation) is the most common downgrade trigger**.

### Vietnam (Việt Nam)
Sources: [IELTS Fighter 42 topics PDF](https://ielts-fighter.com/tin-tuc/42-chu-de-IELTS-Speaking-Part-1-pho-bien-nhat-va-mau-tra-loi_mt1462076210.html), [IELTS Fighter Part 1 set](https://ielts-fighter.com/speaking/ielts-speaking-part-1-question_mt1547599191.html), [Vietop strategy](https://vietop.edu.vn/blog/chien-luoc-lam-bai-speaking-part-1/) and [common mistakes](https://vietop.edu.vn/blog/common-mistakes-in-ielts-speaking), [Vietop sample bank](https://vietop.edu.vn/blog/tong-hop-bai-mau-ielts-speaking/), [DOL English](https://www.dolenglish.vn/blog/cach-tra-loi-ielts-speaking-part-1), [ECE Vietnam](https://ece.edu.vn/cach-hoc-ielts-speaking/), [Ace of English](https://aceofenglish.com/blog/ielts-practice/cach-tra-loi-cau-hoi-part-1-2-3-ielts-speaking).

The IELTS Fighter **42 chủ đề** (42 topics) PDF + MP3 bundle is the canonical Vietnamese flashcard set. ECE presents Part 1 as 4–5 minutes with three topics and 3–4 questions per topic; sample "where you live now" set covers house/apartment, favourite room, what makes home pleasant, neighbours. Vietop recommends **concise answers of roughly 10–20 seconds**; Ace of English teaches **Answer → Reason → Example → optional Feeling** with explicit warnings against one-word answers (recommending 2–3 sentences).

Vietop's named "lỗi sai cấp quốc gia" (national-level mistake): **forgetting verb tenses, defaulting to present simple when narrating past events** — Vietnamese has no verbal tense morphology. Tonal-language monotone problem: Vietnamese tones at syllable level mean candidates produce flat sentence-level intonation; Vietop drills English stress-timing as a Vietnamese-specific intervention. Vietop also flags "echo trap" (parroting examiner wording) and "speed-fluency confusion" (rushing to sound fluent, mispronouncing instead). Vocabulary is told to **stay simple** — advanced words in Part 1 read as unnatural.

### Iran / Persian-speaking prep (ایران)
Sources: [Iran-Europe](https://iran-europe.net/ساختار-بخش-اسپیکینگ-آیلتس/), [Zandi English](https://zandienglish.ir/ielts-speaking-part-1-questions-and-answers/), [IELTS Tehran](https://ieltstehran.com/blog/چگونه-در-پارت-اول-اسپیکینگ-بهتر-جواب-ده/), [IELTS2 — questions](https://ielts2.com/سوالات-اسپیکینگ-آیلتس-پارت-اول/) and [audio samples](https://ielts2.com/ielts-speaking-samples-part-1/), [Zaban-Saz](https://zaban-saz.ir/frequent-ielts-speaking-questions/).

IELTS2.com says Part 1 is mainly introduction/warm-up before harder parts, lasts no more than 4–5 minutes, and expects **short 10–15 second answers** to simple examiner questions; it provides 100 Part 1 audio samples including work, accommodation, university, family, and Tehran/public-transport prompts. Iranian schools sell *The Key to IELTS Speaking* (Cullen-derived کتاب) as the canonical Part 1 textbook. The **"parrot mistake"** (تکرار مثل طوطی — repeating examiner stems verbatim) is the hammered warning. Persian-L1 specific advice: accent is fine, but consonant clusters and syllable stress must be correct since Persian is syllable-timed and English stress-timed. **Tense errors when narrating past events** are flagged as the top grammar leak. Regional overcorrection: many Iranian guides ban slang and informal register even in Part 1, which is actually stricter than examiners require.

### India / South Asia
Sources: [PW Live](https://www.pw.live/study-abroad/ielts/exams/ielts-speaking-part-1-topics), [IELTS Ninja on Indian struggles](https://ieltsninja.com/content/indians-struggle-ielts-speaking/), [IELTS Material](https://ieltsmaterial.com/speaking/ielts-speaking-part-1/), [IELTS Buddy](https://www.ieltsbuddy.com/tips-and-technique-for-ielts-speaking-part-1.html), [IDP India on accent](https://ieltsidpindia.com/blog/accent-in-ielts-speaking-test) and [common topics](https://ieltsidpindia.com/blog/common-ielts-speaking-topics), [Aspire Square on MTI](https://aspiresquare.com/how-to-neutralise-mother-tongue-influence-mti-while-speaking-english/), [Clapingo on MTI](https://clapingo.com/blog/mother-tongue-influence-problem-english).

PW Live describes Part 1 as ~4–5 minutes with **12–13 questions from three topic areas** covering home, study, hobbies, habits. The dominant Indian frame is **MTI ("Mother Tongue Influence") neutralisation** — almost every Indian prep site organises Part 1 prep around reducing MTI rather than topic-banking. Standard drill: 15–20 min/day read-aloud + record + playback, focusing on linking phrases (*well, you know, actually, I'd say*) and natural stress patterns. **IDP India is explicit that the test grades pronunciation (intelligibility, stress, intonation), NOT accent** — Indian English accent is acceptable; mumbled stress is not. Named failure mode: the "translate from L1 in head" trap — candidates think in regional language, mentally translate, then speak, producing unnatural English even with accurate grammar.

### Pakistan / Urdu-English prep
Sources: [IDP Pakistan Urdu-localised](https://ielts.idp.com/pakistan/prepare/article-question-types-ielts-speaking/ur-pk), [Shahi Academy](https://www.shahiacademy.com/speaking).

IDP Pakistan's Urdu-localised page gives the official format in an Urdu interface but retains the key Part 1 wording in English: 4–5 minutes, name/ID, general questions, familiar topics (music/cooking/weather/movies), scripted questions, "why/why not" prompts for too-short answers. Shahi Academy presents Part 1 as 4–5 minutes, **5–6 questions, 1–2 topics, 15–20 seconds per answer** (treat counts as local heuristic, not official); its do/don't list: extend answers, avoid yes/no answers, avoid going off topic, avoid no answer, avoid speaking too quietly. Sample banks include friends, flowers/plants, food/cooking, gifts, games, telephoning, clothing, reading, accommodation, drinks, colour, neighbours, leisure, television.

### Indonesia
Sources: [IDP Indonesia Part 1 prep](https://ielts.idp.com/indonesia/about/news-and-articles/article-preparation-for-ielts-speaking-part-1) and [question-types page](https://ielts.idp.com/indonesia/prepare/article-question-types-ielts-speaking), [ElevaU tips](https://elevau.id/speaking/tips-speaking-ielts-part-1/) and [frequent topics](https://elevau.id/speaking/topik-speaking-ielts-yang-sering-muncul/), [Sun English](https://sunenglish.co.id/tips-menjawab-ielts-speaking-part-1/), [Schoters](https://blog.schoters.com/pertanyaan-speaking-ielts), [IELTSpresso](https://ieltspresso.com/tips-ielts-speaking-part-1/).

IDP Indonesia's Indonesian-language page states Part 1 is 4–5 minutes with scripted questions and "mengapa/mengapa tidak" extension prompts. Signature Indonesian formula: **Jawaban Langsung + Alasan + Contoh Singkat** (Direct Answer + Reason + Brief Example) — short, three-part, drilled per topic. Indonesian guides specifically warn against **robotic intonation from script-reading** — even with good pronunciation, flat robot-reading is auto-detected and downgraded. Confidence/anxiety drills (breathing, visualisation) feature unusually prominently — test anxiety treated as primary obstacle.

### Russia / Kazakhstan
Sources: [Lingust.ru](https://lingust.ru/faq/english/2157-17), [MyIELTS.kz](https://myielts.kz/archives/15434), [yourielts.ru](https://yourielts.ru/prepare-for-ielts/ielts-speaking) and [secrets](https://yourielts.ru/prepare-for-ielts/ielts-speaking/ielts-speaking-secrets), [Anglo-Repetitor](https://anglo-repetitor.ru/blog/ielts/article-943830.html), [ITEC School](https://www.itec-school.ru/articles/podrobno-pro-ustnuyu-chast-ekzamena-ielts-speaking/), [Expert Language](https://expertlanguage.ru/useful/podgotovka-k-ielts-speaking-osnovnye-sovety/), [IELTS-GPT for Kazakh students](https://ielts-gpt.com/blog/ielts-preparation-tips-kazakh-students).

Lingust.ru recommends **full but not overlong 2–3 sentence answers** and warns against memorising full texts for common topics (work, study, hometown, family, hobbies). MyIELTS.kz describes Part 1 as a warm-up of 4–5 minutes with **~10–12 questions in three blocks**: occupation/place of living/hometown, then two additional topic blocks; it emphasises **everyday/non-academic vocabulary, topic-specific lexis over forced idioms**, possible past/future grammar questions, and ~3–5 sentence answers as a conditional guideline.

Russian-language prescription is unusually **terse** — a 2–3 sentence answer floor (shorter than Chinese/Korean), framed as "natural under time pressure." Mandatory **contractions drill** (*I'd, can't, don't*) because Russian doesn't contract; full forms read as translated. Russian guides emphasise asking for clarification — Russian candidates tend to bluff/freeze on unexpected questions. IELTS-GPT for Kazakh students names the canonical Russophone failure mode: "fluency holds until an unexpected question, then candidate freezes into one-word answers because they only prepared scripts."

### Japan (日本)
Sources: [Solo IELTS TOEFL 100-sample guide](https://solo-ielts-toefl.com/speaking-part1-sample/), [atsueigo](https://knowledge.atsueigo.com/ieltsspeaking/), [Berkeley House](https://berkeleyhouse.co.jp/blog/ielts/ielts-speaking-flow/), [IELTS Score Book](https://ielts-score.net/speaking), [Best Teacher](https://www.best-teacher-inc.com/blog/ielts-example-questions).

Japanese benchmark: **5 sentences per Part 1 answer** with reasons attached to any Yes/No. Standard study format is the 100-question topic deck. Named pitfall: **棒読み ("stick-reading", mechanical recitation) caps you at band 5.5**. Speed myth — Japanese candidates believe speaking faster = higher fluency. Atsueigo and Solo IELTS debunk: fast = sounds memorised = downgrade. Berkeley House notes Japanese candidates underprepare Part 1 thinking it's "easy," missing that this is where examiners set the band-impression anchor.

## Open-source tools and code

No GitHub repo is purpose-built for IELTS Part 1 scoring on spontaneous speech. The open work splits into reusable blocks; a Part 1 scorer would compose them.

**Read-speech pronunciation scoring** (foundational, limited but reusable):
- [speechocean762](https://github.com/jimbozhang/speechocean762) — 5,000 sentences from Mandarin-L1 speakers, 5-expert scores on accuracy/fluency/completeness/prosody. Free for commercial & non-commercial. Mirror: [HuggingFace](https://huggingface.co/datasets/mispeech/speechocean762); paper [arXiv 2104.01378](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2104.01378); [OpenSLR-101](https://www.openslr.org/101/).
- [GOPT (Gong, ICASSP 2022)](https://github.com/YuanGongND/gopt) — Transformer over Kaldi GOP features → phoneme/word/utterance scores. BSD-3-Clause. r=0.743 utt-level on SO-762. Read-speech only.
- [L2-ARCTIC](https://psi.engr.tamu.edu/l2-arctic-corpus/) — 24 non-native speakers including **Korean L1**, 27.1 h read speech, mispronunciation-annotated. CC BY-NC 4.0. [Interspeech 2018 paper](https://psi.engr.tamu.edu/wp-content/uploads/2018/08/zhao2018interspeech.pdf).

**Spontaneous / dialogue-level scoring (closer to Part 1)**:
- [EvalYaks (Talking-Yak)](https://github.com/Talking-Yak/EvalYaks) — 6 models for the 4 sections of a CEFR B2 speaking exam plus vocabulary/text CEFR; instruction-tuned LoRA over Mistral 7B; expert-validated synthetic transcripts; ~96% acceptable accuracy. The "Introduction" model maps almost 1:1 onto Part 1; B2 ≈ IELTS 5.5–6.5. Paper [arXiv 2408.12226](https://arxiv.org/abs/2408.12226).
- [deep_pron (Vyas Raina)](https://github.com/rainavyas/deep_pron) — closest open re-implementation of Cambridge in-house Linguaskill-style grader, based on Kyriakopoulos et al. (Interspeech 2018).
- [Speechat (Shahabks)](https://github.com/Shahabks/Speechat) — feature extractor for prosody/intonation/stress.
- [Cambridge automated conversational-test pipeline](https://www.repository.cam.ac.uk/items/003ce1d5-6fe7-4d27-bbb1-91020f474b6d) — peer-reviewed paper combining diarization, speaker assignment, ASR, Longformer text models and wav2vec2 audio models on Occupational English Test data.

**Disfluency / filler detection** (the most operationally relevant block for Part 1):
- [CrisperWhisper](https://github.com/nyrahealth/CrisperWhisper) + [HF model](https://huggingface.co/nyrahealth/CrisperWhisper) — Whisper fine-tune emitting verbatim transcripts including `[UM]`/`[UH]` tokens with corrected word-level timestamps; SOTA on filler-event detection. CC BY-NC 4.0. Paper [arXiv 2408.16589](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2408.16589). **Single most useful tool for Part 1.**
- [WhisperD](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2505.21551) — 2025 Whisper fine-tune scoring Filler Inclusion Rate (FIR).
- [joint-disfluency-detector-and-parser](https://github.com/pariajm/joint-disfluency-detector-and-parser) — self-attentive parser on Switchboard for filled-pauses/restarts/repetitions/revisions.
- [disfluency_detection_from_audio](https://github.com/amritkromana/disfluency_detection_from_audio) — multimodal (audio + text) demo.
- [DisfluencySpeech dataset](https://huggingface.co/datasets/amaai-lab/DisfluencySpeech) — filled pauses, editing terms, discourse markers annotated.

**Voice-activity / pause detection** (for Tavakoli-style breakdown fluency):
- [Silero VAD](https://github.com/snakers4/silero-vad) — MIT, pretrained on 6000+ languages, 87.7% TPR @ 5% FPR.
- [py-webrtcvad](https://github.com/wiseman/py-webrtcvad) — MIT, lightweight fallback.

**Lexical-diversity / linguistic-complexity tooling** (transcript side):
- [TAALED (Kyle / LCR-ADS-Lab)](https://github.com/LCR-ADS-Lab/TAALED) — TTR, MATTR, MTLD, HDD indices.
- [LexicalRichness (Shen)](https://github.com/LSYS/LexicalRichness) — MIT, mature pip-installable.
- [TAALES (Kyle)](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/The-tool-for-the-automatic-analysis-of-lexical-2.0-Kyle-Crossley/4d7f35aca70d3cb8a205feedc87f50ae46627188) — lexical sophistication, n-gram frequency, age-of-acquisition.
- L2SCA (Lu, 2010) — 14 syntactic complexity measures, validated for L2 spoken production ([MLJ article](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/modl.12907)).

**Commercial APIs (closed-source; cited for reference)**:
- [SpeechSuper API Samples](https://github.com/speechsuper/SpeechSuper-API-Samples) — public sample code (MIT), `test_type = "ielts"` for English spontaneous speech; the API returns pronunciation, fluency, grammar, vocabulary, transcription, pause markers, pause-filler frequency, speaking rate, word-level pronunciation.
- [Speechace API](https://www.speechace.com/api-plans/) + [Fluency API blog](https://www.speechace.com/hello-fluency-api/) — proprietary; advertises IELTS/CEFR/PTE/TOEIC speaking scores, pronunciation/fluency scoring, speech rate, pausing, hesitation, length of run, words-correct-per-minute.
- [ETS SpeechRater](https://www.myspeakingscore.com/speechrater) and Cambridge Linguaskill — closed-source; no open production-grade IELTS auto-grader exists.

**Foundational papers for designing the scoring function**:
- Tavakoli (2018) *Fluency in Second Language Testing: Insights From Different Disciplines* — [tandfonline PDF](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/pdf/10.1080/15434303.2018.1477780). Canonical breakdown/speed/repair fluency operationalisation.
- Tavakoli & Skehan triadic framework: silent pause threshold **≥ 250 ms** (sometimes ≥ 400 ms); mid-clause pauses more diagnostic than end-clause; articulation rate, MLR, repair rate. See [de Jong LANGSNAP PDF](https://blog.soton.ac.uk/langsnap/files/2013/04/LANGSNAP_dejong.pdf), [EEJPL temporal measurements paper](https://eejpl.vnu.edu.ua/index.php/eejpl/article/view/905).
- Bannò & Matassoni (2022) *Proficiency assessment of L2 spoken English using wav2vec 2.0* — [arXiv 2210.13168](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2210.13168). Wav2vec graders beat BERT-on-transcript on grammar/lexical/pronunciation/fluency.
- *Assessment of L2 Oral Proficiency using Speech LLMs* — Interspeech 2025, [ISCA Archive](https://www.isca-archive.org/interspeech_2025/ma25b_interspeech.pdf).
- *Session-Level Spoken Language Assessment with a Multimodal Foundation Model* — [arXiv 2509.16025](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2509.16025).
- *Impact of ASR on Linguistic Complexity Analysis* — [arXiv 2104.08529](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2104.08529).

## Practical signals for an LLM judge

Operationalisable rules a judge can apply when reading a diarized Part 1 transcript. Numbers are anchored to research or examiner-blog consensus cited above.

1. **Identify Part 1 scope before scoring.** Separate identity/name confirmation and examiner setup from scored interview answers. The first scored topic set should be Work, Study, Home, or Hometown — if anything else, suspect bad diarisation or that this is not Part 1.
2. **Question-count sanity check.** Expect 7–13 examiner questions across 2–3 topic sets in 4–5 min (sources disagree on exact numbers; this is the inclusive range). Fewer than 7 ⇒ candidate over-extended (long Part 1 answers are themselves a downgrade signal). More than 13 ⇒ candidate gave one-word answers and was prompted heavily.
3. **Score directness first.** For each examiner prompt, check whether the candidate answers the actual question before adding reasons or examples ([Seedhouse et al.](https://cdn.ielts.org/Research/topic-development-in-ielts-speaking-test-seedhouse-et-al-2011.pdf) — answer the question AND develop the topic inherent in it).
4. **Answer-length target ≈ 2–3 sentences / ~10–30 seconds.** Single-clause answers across multiple questions ⇒ Band ≤ 5 evidence. Multi-paragraph Part 1 answers (>45 s) suggest misallocated effort. Country-specific floors vary (Russia 2–3 sentences, China ≥20 s / 4–5 sentences, Japan 5 sentences) — calibrate but don't mechanise.
5. **Treat repeated "why/why not" prompts as underdevelopment evidence.** Examiners ask why/why not if an answer is too short ([IDP](https://ielts.idp.com/prepare/article-free-speaking-practice-questions)); repeated extension prompts indicate weak topic development, not examiner hostility.
6. **Do not punish examiner interruptions by default.** Examiners may interrupt for time, coverage, or to break up memorised material ([IELTS Liz](https://ieltsliz.com/why-the-ielts-speaking-examiner-stops-your-answer/), [IDP Australia](https://ielts.com.au/australia/prepare/speaking)). Judge whether the candidate had supplied a coherent answer before being cut.
7. **Hesitation classifier — the load-bearing signal.** Tag each hesitation as **content-related** (thinking what to say; OK at all bands) vs **language-related** (searching for words/grammar; penalised). Repeated language-related hesitations within a single answer ⇒ Band 6 evidence.
8. **Silent-pause threshold ≥ 250 ms** (Tavakoli / de Jong canonical cut-off). Weight **mid-clause** pauses much higher than end-clause pauses — mid-clause is the diagnostic L2 marker.
9. **Filler rate** (`um`, `uh`, `like`, `you know`, `er`): Band-7-ish ≈ <5/min; Band-6-ish ≈ ~10/min. Use CrisperWhisper `[UM]`/`[UH]` tokens as ground truth if audio is available.
10. **Articulation rate (excluding pauses)**: Band-7-ish ≈ 3.5–4.5 syllables/sec; below 3.0 ⇒ Band ≤ 6; above 5.5 with no variation often signals memorised recitation. **Mean length of run (MLR)**: Band 7 ≈ 6–8 syllables; Band 6 ≈ 3–5. **Repair rate** >3 self-corrections/min ⇒ Band 6 marker.
11. **Discourse-marker range.** Acceptable spoken DMs (NOT counted as hesitation): *well, I mean, you know, actually, to be honest, anyway*. Band 7 requires flexible use; <3 distinct DMs across all of Part 1 ⇒ Band 6 evidence; mechanical reuse of one DM before every answer ⇒ Band 5–6.
12. **Memorised-answer detector.** Flag any of: (a) suspiciously low filler rate combined with high lexical sophistication in Part 1 specifically; (b) academic register (`furthermore`, `consequently`, `it is widely acknowledged that`) in Part 1; (c) verbatim repetition of fixed multi-clause templates across topic changes; (d) answer that doesn't address the specific question wording. Examiners are trained to interrupt these.
13. **Echo / parroting check** (Iranian, Vietnamese, Indian L1 pattern). If the candidate begins a meaningful fraction of answers with the examiner's stem verbatim (`"Where is my hometown? My hometown is…"`), Band 5–6 marker — Band 7 requires paraphrasing and sentence restructuring.
14. **Tense-collapse check** (Mandarin/Vietnamese/Persian/Arabic L1 pattern). Past-narrative questions ("What was your favourite toy as a child?") should produce past-tense morphology. Present-simple narration of past events ⇒ Grammatical Range & Accuracy band ≤ 6 evidence. More broadly, test whether the candidate shifts tense/aspect/modality when asked about childhood, recent events, future plans, habits, preferences.
15. **Lexical Resource: reward topic-specific everyday vocabulary over forced idioms.** Natural phrasal verbs and topic-specific lexis are stronger evidence than idioms inserted everywhere ([MyIELTS.kz](https://myielts.kz/archives/15434)). MTLD ≥ 50 is a Band 7 floor for Part 1-sized samples; MTLD is unstable on <50-token samples, so use MATTR (window 20) for very short answers.
16. **Accent is not a penalty by itself.** IELTS Pronunciation criteria focus on listener effort and clarity, not native accent. **Band 7 criterion: "L1 accent has minimal effect on intelligibility."** Use ASR confidence, inaudible tags, or examiner-repair markers cautiously as intelligibility evidence, not accent bias.
17. **Pronunciation from transcripts alone is low-confidence.** Plain transcripts cannot directly reveal rhythm, stress, intonation, linking, phoneme accuracy, or listener effort. If audio or transcript annotations exist, use them; otherwise flag Pronunciation as low-evidence.
18. **Holistic-only framing in output.** IELTS Speaking is scored across all three parts as a whole, so a Part 1-only judge must phrase output as *"Part 1 evidence indicates band-X tendency on criterion Y"* — never as a final band. Part 1 is informative for **Fluency & Coherence** and **Pronunciation** but **weaker evidence for Lexical Resource and Grammatical Range**, since Part 2's long turn is where those are best demonstrated ([Simon](https://www.ielts-simon.com/ielts-help-and-english-pr/2019/01/ielts-speaking-which-part-is-the-most-important.html), [IELTS Ninja](https://ieltsninja.com/content/score-percentage-weight-part-1-2-3-ielts-speaking-test/)).
