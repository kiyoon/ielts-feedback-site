# Topic: Science and Research

## Sub-topics commonly seen on the IELTS exam

- Government funding of scientific research.
- Space exploration: justified expense or unjustifiable luxury?
- The ethics of scientific experimentation, including animal testing.
- Whether research should be driven by curiosity or by application.
- Scientific literacy and the public's understanding of science.
- The role of science in addressing climate change.
- Genetic engineering and biotechnology.
- Funding pure vs applied research.
- The use of animals in scientific research.
- The relationship between science and society / policy.

## Sample real exam prompts

1. *Some people argue that the money spent on space exploration could be better used to address problems on Earth. To what extent do you agree?*
2. *Some people believe that scientific research should be carried out by governments, while others think it should be left to private companies. Discuss both views.*
3. *Some argue that science classes should be compulsory at school, while others believe students should be free to choose their subjects. Discuss both views.*
4. *Many people argue that animals should not be used for scientific research. To what extent do you agree?*
5. *Some people believe that scientists should only work on research that has practical applications, while others think pure scientific curiosity is just as valuable. Discuss both views.*
6. *In recent years, the public has become increasingly distrustful of scientific findings. What are the causes, and what can be done to address this?*
7. *Some people argue that scientific progress brings as many problems as benefits. To what extent do you agree?*
8. *Some governments are investing heavily in artificial intelligence and biotechnology. Are these positive or negative developments?*

## Core arguments FOR (e.g. fund science, support pure research, defend space exploration, animal testing)

| # | Argument | Supporting reason | Concrete example | Useful phrase |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Science underpins long-run economic growth | Most productivity gains trace to past research | Endogenous-growth theory makes this case explicit | "underpin productivity growth over the long run" |
| 2 | Pure research yields unexpected, transformative applications | Many breakthroughs began as curiosity-driven work | Lasers, GPS and the internet all began as pure research | "produce unforeseen applications across decades" |
| 3 | Space technology has produced extensive spillovers | Satellite navigation, weather forecasting, medical imaging | NASA-developed materials are now standard in many industries | "deliver spillover benefits beyond the original mission" |
| 4 | Public funding insulates science from short-term commercial pressure | Markets prefer near-term returns | Most fundamental physics is publicly funded | "preserve the long horizons that fundamental science requires" |
| 5 | Carefully regulated animal research has saved many human lives | Vaccines, insulin and antibiotics were tested on animals | Most major medical advances rest on animal studies | "underpin medical advances that have saved millions of lives" |
| 6 | Science-literate populations are better equipped to make policy choices | Citizens who understand evidence vote more thoughtfully | Several democracies link science literacy with civic education | "support democratic deliberation on technical questions" |
| 7 | Public funding reduces dependence on industry-influenced research | Independent science is more credible | Tobacco-funded studies illustrate the alternative | "guarantee independence from commercial agendas" |
| 8 | International scientific collaboration tackles global problems | Climate, pandemic and AI safety cross borders | The IPCC, CERN and human-genome work illustrate this | "co-ordinate response to inherently global problems" |
| 9 | Investment in science attracts and retains talent | Great labs draw researchers to a country | The US, UK and Switzerland routinely top science-talent indices | "anchor the talent pipeline of advanced economies" |
| 10 | Science education in schools foster critical thinking generally | Hypothesis-testing transfers across domains | Most STEM-curriculum reviews emphasise this | "cultivate transferable habits of careful reasoning" |

## Core arguments AGAINST / counter (e.g. against space spending, against unrestricted research, against animal testing)

| # | Argument | Supporting reason | Concrete example | Useful phrase |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Public money should address poverty, disease and education first | Earthbound problems are visible and urgent | Critics of space programmes raise this consistently | "compete with more pressing terrestrial priorities" |
| 2 | Science-policy decisions can ignore distributional consequences | Who benefits from new technology is uneven | The early history of GMOs illustrates the concern | "neglect the distributional consequences of technological change" |
| 3 | Unfettered research raises ethical issues that governance is slow to address | Genetic editing and AI are obvious examples | Governance frameworks for CRISPR and AI remain in flux | "advance more rapidly than the governance designed to contain them" |
| 4 | Animal experimentation imposes suffering that alternatives can increasingly replace | Computer modelling and tissue cultures are advancing | Several jurisdictions phase out specific animal tests | "rest on assumptions undermined by emerging in-vitro alternatives" |
| 5 | Excessive deference to scientific authority can stifle public deliberation | Not every policy question is purely scientific | Pandemic-era debates illustrated this tension | "blur the line between scientific evidence and democratic choice" |
| 6 | "Pure" research can absorb resources without clear public benefit | The link between curiosity and application is sometimes oversold | Critics of basic-physics funding raise this | "produce limited identifiable public benefit" |
| 7 | Industry-driven research is responsive to demand | The market signals where applied work has value | The pharmaceutical R&D model exemplifies this | "respond directly to demonstrated market need" |
| 8 | Public mistrust of science is often rooted in genuine institutional failures | Past errors (e.g. tobacco, opioids) make scepticism reasonable | Some "anti-science" sentiment reflects experience, not ignorance | "reflect, in part, legitimate institutional failures" |
| 9 | Scientific progress has produced new harms alongside benefits | Plastic pollution and antibiotic resistance are both science-enabled | These illustrate the dual-use character of progress | "carry intrinsic dual-use risks alongside benefits" |
| 10 | Compulsory advanced science in schools may not suit every learner | Some students will find their strengths in arts or vocations | Rigid science requirements can produce disengagement | "fail to accommodate varied learner aptitudes" |

## Concession patterns (band-7 nuance)

1. *While space exploration looks like an extravagance against urgent earthbound problems, the spillover technologies it has generated are too significant to be dismissed.*
2. *Granted that pure research can seem detached from immediate need, the historical record of unforeseen applications cautions against reducing science to its current uses.*
3. *It is true that animal research raises serious ethical concerns; equally, ending it abruptly without replacement methods would also have a moral cost in foregone medical progress.*
4. *Critics rightly worry about commercial influence on research; the alternative, leaving science wholly to industry, would not obviously improve matters.*
5. *Even those most enthusiastic about scientific progress tend to concede that not every advance has been unambiguously beneficial.*
6. *To be sure, public scepticism of science is sometimes ill-informed, but it is sometimes a justified response to past institutional failures.*
7. *Although AI and biotechnology bring new possibilities, the governance frameworks designed to manage them are still developing — and that is a legitimate cause for caution rather than panic.*

## Ready-to-adapt thesis statements

1. **(Opinion)** *I largely agree that public investment in space exploration remains worthwhile, although the case rests less on the romance of discovery than on the substantial spillover benefits to terrestrial life.*
2. **(Discuss both views)** *Although both pure and applied research deserve support, in my view governments should specifically protect basic science, since markets reliably under-fund precisely that work.*
3. **(Advantages / disadvantages)** *While the rapid expansion of biotechnology offers genuine medical and agricultural benefits, the ethical and ecological risks it raises, on balance, justify a more cautious regulatory framework.*
4. **(Problem / solution)** *Declining public trust in science is driven by a confluence of misinformation, past institutional failures and weak science communication; rebuilding it requires action across journalism, education and research integrity.*
5. **(Two-part question)** *This essay will first identify why public confidence in scientific findings has waned before considering which interventions are most likely to restore it.*

## Sample fully-developed body paragraph #1 (band 7+)

> *(Topic sentence)* The most defensible argument for sustained public funding of basic, curiosity-driven research is that the link between such research and eventual public benefit, although unpredictable in the short run, is unusually strong over decades. *(Explanation)* Discoveries in fundamental physics, chemistry and biology have repeatedly produced applications that no funding panel could have anticipated at the time of the original work; lasers, magnetic-resonance imaging, the internet and modern vaccine platforms all began as projects whose immediate practical use was unclear. Markets, by their nature, are poorly suited to fund this kind of work, since the returns are too distant, too diffuse and too hard to capture by any single firm. *(Concrete example)* The development of mRNA vaccines, which underwrote much of the COVID-19 response, rested on decades of publicly funded basic biology that had attracted only modest interest from commercial sponsors during its early years. *(Mini-conclusion)* The lesson, in other words, is not that all blue-skies research will pay off — many will not — but that retaining the public infrastructure of basic science is the only reliable way to ensure that the next round of breakthroughs eventually arrives.

## Sample fully-developed body paragraph #2

> *(Topic sentence)* Critics of large-scale public investment in science are nevertheless right to insist that scientific progress does not, on its own, equate to social progress. *(Explanation)* New technologies have repeatedly arrived alongside new harms — plastic, leaded petrol, antibiotic resistance and certain digital platforms are all stories of progress whose downsides emerged only later — and the institutional capacity to anticipate and govern such harms has consistently lagged the pace of discovery. The result is a recurring pattern in which the public absorbs new risks while the gains are appropriated by a smaller group, eroding the very trust on which future scientific work depends. *(Concrete example)* The current regulatory scramble around generative AI illustrates the pattern in real time, with deployment outpacing governance in a way that several scientific bodies themselves have begun to flag. *(Mini-conclusion)* Defending public investment in science is therefore not the same as defending science from criticism; on the contrary, the strongest case for funding it is the case for taking the responsibilities that come with it equally seriously.

## Common pitfalls / clichés to avoid

- "Science has changed our lives." — Vague.
- "Necessity is the mother of invention." — Cliché.
- "Scientists are saving the world." — Sweeping.
- "Animals have rights." — Polemical without development.
- Confusing *science* (the practice) with *technology* (its applications).
- "Space is the final frontier." — Cliché.
- "We must use science responsibly." — Empty if not specified.
- Citing fabricated R&D statistics.
- Confusing *experiment* (noun) and *experiment* (verb stress).
- "Without science, we would still live in caves." — Reductive.
