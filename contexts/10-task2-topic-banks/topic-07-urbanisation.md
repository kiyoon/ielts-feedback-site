# Topic: Urbanisation

## Sub-topics commonly seen on the IELTS exam

- Migration from rural to urban areas: causes, effects.
- Affordable housing and the housing crisis.
- Public transport, traffic congestion and pollution.
- Urban planning, green spaces, mixed-use development.
- Slums and informal settlements in rapidly growing cities.
- Rural depopulation and how to revive rural areas.
- Smart-city solutions to urban problems.
- Whether urbanisation is, on balance, positive or negative.
- Tackling urban crime and overstretched services.
- Suburbanisation, gentrification and the cost of living.

## Sample real exam prompts

1. *More and more young people are leaving rural areas to live and work in cities. What are the reasons, and do the advantages outweigh the disadvantages?*
2. *Some people think that urbanisation is increasing every year. What do you think — is this a positive or negative development?*
3. *Many cities suffer from severe traffic congestion. What are the main causes, and what measures can be taken to address it?*
4. *In many cities, the lack of affordable housing is becoming a serious problem. Why is this happening, and how can it be solved?*
5. *In many countries, more people are moving to cities, putting pressure on infrastructure. What are the main problems caused by overpopulation in urban areas, and how can governments address them?*
6. *Some people believe that public transport should be free for all. To what extent do you agree?*
7. *Some argue that governments should focus on developing rural areas instead of cities. Discuss both views.*
8. *In many cities, green spaces are disappearing as construction increases. What problems does this cause, and what can be done to address it?*

## Core arguments FOR (e.g. urbanisation has benefits, public-transport investment, mixed-use planning)

| # | Argument | Supporting reason | Concrete example | Useful phrase |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Cities concentrate jobs and economic opportunity | Density allows specialisation and matches workers to roles | Most national GDP is generated in a handful of metropolitan regions | "metropolitan areas as engines of national output" |
| 2 | Cities offer better access to education and healthcare | Universities and specialist hospitals cluster in cities | Most teaching hospitals are urban | "concentrate specialist services in metropolitan centres" |
| 3 | Public transport investment reduces emissions and congestion | High-capacity transit moves more people per square metre of road | Cities such as Tokyo and Vienna are routinely cited | "expand high-capacity public-transport networks" |
| 4 | Mixed-use planning reduces commuting | Living, working and shopping in proximity cuts car-dependence | Many European cities are restructuring streets in this direction | "promote walkable, mixed-use neighbourhoods" |
| 5 | Density supports cultural and social vitality | Theatres, restaurants, sports and minorities sustain themselves only above a critical mass | Major capitals retain rich cultural ecosystems | "sustain a critical cultural mass" |
| 6 | Cities have lower per-capita carbon footprints | Apartment living and short journeys consume less | Several climate analyses confirm urban residents have smaller footprints | "deliver lower per-capita emissions through density" |
| 7 | Smart-city technologies optimise services in real time | Sensors balance traffic, water and energy | Singapore is the canonical example | "harness real-time data for service optimisation" |
| 8 | Migration to cities lifts rural living standards too | Remittances and reduced rural unemployment follow | Several developing economies show this dynamic | "ease pressure on rural labour markets" |
| 9 | Compact cities preserve farmland and natural areas | Sprawl, by contrast, paves over countryside | Green-belt policy in the UK rests on this rationale | "limit sprawl and protect surrounding countryside" |
| 10 | Cities accelerate innovation through chance encounters | Talent that meets, collaborates | Tech and finance clusters depend on this proximity | "facilitate the serendipitous encounters that drive innovation" |

## Core arguments AGAINST / counter (e.g. costs of urbanisation, housing crisis, congestion, rural decline)

| # | Argument | Supporting reason | Concrete example | Useful phrase |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Rapid urban growth outstrips the capacity of housing markets | Construction lags demand, prices spiral | Cities such as Vancouver, Sydney and London face acute affordability crises | "outpace the capacity of housing markets to respond" |
| 2 | Traffic congestion harms productivity and air quality | Hours lost in traffic and chronic respiratory illness | Several megacities lose noticeable GDP to congestion | "exact a substantial cost in productivity and public health" |
| 3 | Slums and informal settlements emerge where formal housing fails | Migrants without options self-build | Mumbai's Dharavi is the textbook example | "give rise to informal settlements that lack basic services" |
| 4 | Rural areas suffer brain drain and demographic collapse | Young people leave; services close | Many European villages have lost schools and clinics | "leave hollowed-out rural communities" |
| 5 | Mental-health problems are reportedly higher in urban populations | Noise, isolation amid crowds, loss of green | Multiple studies link urban living to elevated anxiety and depression | "associated with elevated rates of psychological distress" |
| 6 | Gentrification displaces long-term residents | Rising rents push out the working class | Districts in Berlin and New York illustrate this clearly | "displace existing communities through rent pressure" |
| 7 | Urban inequality is starkly visible | Affluent and impoverished neighbourhoods often sit side by side | Most major cities exhibit this pattern | "expose stark spatial inequalities" |
| 8 | Cities concentrate vulnerability to crises | Pandemics, heatwaves and supply shocks hit cities harder | COVID-era mortality clustered in dense urban districts | "concentrate exposure to systemic shocks" |
| 9 | Overstretched services degrade quality of life | Schools, transport and hospitals become congested | Several megacities operate well above design capacity | "operate beyond design capacity" |
| 10 | Cultural homogenisation: chain stores replace local character | Globalised retail looks the same everywhere | High streets in many countries now look interchangeable | "erode the distinctive character of local high streets" |

## Concession patterns (band-7 nuance)

1. *While urbanisation has clearly delivered economic gains, the social costs — particularly in housing and mental health — should not be airbrushed out of the picture.*
2. *Granted that traffic congestion is largely a consequence of car ownership, the answer is rarely simply to ban cars; substitutes must be in place first.*
3. *It is true that rural areas suffer when young people leave, yet attempting to reverse this entirely would mean foregoing the economic benefits of urban concentration.*
4. *Critics rightly point out that gentrification displaces long-term residents; equally, allowing neighbourhoods to decay further is no kinder.*
5. *Even those who celebrate the cultural vitality of cities tend to concede that this vitality can be smothered when housing is unaffordable for ordinary residents.*
6. *To be sure, public-transport investment is expensive; ignoring that cost ignores why cash-strapped governments delay it.*
7. *Although smart-city technology is often presented as a panacea, the underlying problems are typically political and financial rather than technological.*

## Ready-to-adapt thesis statements

1. **(Opinion)** *I largely agree that, despite their costs, large cities remain a positive development overall, since they concentrate the economic, cultural and educational opportunities that smaller settlements cannot match.*
2. **(Discuss both views)** *Although both rural and urban development have their advocates, in my view a balanced strategy — investing in cities while reviving rural infrastructure — is the only sustainable response to current demographic pressures.*
3. **(Advantages / disadvantages)** *While the rapid growth of cities brings real challenges in housing and congestion, the benefits in employment, services and innovation outweigh them, provided that planning keeps pace.*
4. **(Problem / solution)** *Urban traffic congestion is driven by a long history of car-centred planning and inadequate transit; addressing it requires sustained investment in public transport combined with disincentives for private-car use.*
5. **(Two-part question)** *This essay will first identify why cities continue to draw young migrants from the countryside before assessing whether the resulting urbanisation, on balance, brings more benefits than drawbacks.*

## Sample fully-developed body paragraph #1 (band 7+)

> *(Topic sentence)* The most pressing consequence of rapid urbanisation is the housing crisis that has crystallised in almost every major city. *(Explanation)* When migration into a city outpaces the construction of new homes, the limited supply is bid up by those with the highest incomes, while existing residents on modest wages find themselves either commuting from ever further afield or sharing increasingly cramped accommodation. The problem is structural rather than incidental: zoning rules, planning delays and slow public-housing provision combine to keep supply from responding. *(Concrete example)* Cities as varied as Vancouver, Sydney and Lisbon have seen median rents rise considerably faster than median wages over the past decade, with the gap widening rather than narrowing. *(Mini-conclusion)* Without deliberate policy intervention — denser zoning, faster permitting and renewed public-housing programmes — urbanisation will continue to deliver economic dynamism only to those who can afford to live near it.

## Sample fully-developed body paragraph #2

> *(Topic sentence)* That said, simply pointing to the costs of urbanisation tells only half the story; cities also remain the principal venues in which modern economies actually function. *(Explanation)* Density allows highly specialised workers to find suitable employers, supports the universities and hospitals that smaller communities cannot sustain, and produces the chance encounters that feed innovation. Public transport, when adequately funded, can serve a metropolitan population at a fraction of the per-capita carbon cost of suburban car-dependence. *(Concrete example)* Cities such as Vienna and Tokyo, with their integrated transit and substantial public-housing stock, demonstrate that high density and high quality of life are not incompatible. *(Mini-conclusion)* The choice, in other words, is not between cities and the countryside, but between cities run well and cities allowed to drift — and the historical record suggests strongly that the former is achievable when political will exists.

## Common pitfalls / clichés to avoid

- "Cities are concrete jungles." — Cliché.
- "The hustle and bustle of city life." — Tired.
- "Country life is peaceful; city life is stressful." — Reductive.
- Confusing *city* and *town* without thought.
- Citing population statistics without source.
- "Villages are dying." — Overstated; hedge.
- Treating "urbanisation" as if it were a recent invention.
- "Public transport is the only solution." — Overstated.
- Listing problems without solutions.
- Using *infrastructures* (rare in BrE academic writing) — prefer *infrastructure*.
