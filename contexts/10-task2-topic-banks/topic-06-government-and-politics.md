# Topic: Government and Politics

## Sub-topics commonly seen on the IELTS exam

- How public money should be spent (health, education, defence, the arts, transport).
- Taxation: progressive vs flat, sin taxes, redistribution.
- Whether services should be free at the point of use or means-tested.
- Freedom of speech vs hate speech, censorship and media regulation.
- Democracy vs other systems; voter engagement.
- Regulation of business: environmental, health, employment, financial.
- Whether voting should be compulsory.
- Crime, policing and civil liberties.
- The role of government in shaping individual behaviour (nudges, bans, taxes).
- International cooperation vs national sovereignty.

## Sample real exam prompts

1. *Some people think that taxation should be kept to a minimum, as the money people earn should be theirs to do with as they wish. Others believe that people should pay a significant level of tax to support the public services we all depend on. Discuss both views.*
2. *Governments should focus their spending on public services such as healthcare and education rather than on the military or space exploration. To what extent do you agree?*
3. *In some countries, voting is compulsory. To what extent do you think this is a good idea?*
4. *Some argue that freedom of speech should be absolute, while others believe it must be restricted to protect minorities. Discuss both views and give your own opinion.*
5. *In many countries, governments are introducing more rules and regulations to protect their citizens. Are these regulations a positive or a negative development?*
6. *Some people believe that governments should provide free health care for everyone, while others argue that individuals should pay for their own treatment. Discuss both views.*
7. *Some people believe that international cooperation is essential for solving global problems, while others argue that countries should focus on their own problems first. Discuss both views.*
8. *Governments should make it illegal to drive cars in city centres. To what extent do you agree or disagree?*

## Core arguments FOR (e.g. active government, progressive taxation, regulation, public spending on services)

| # | Argument | Supporting reason | Concrete example | Useful phrase |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Taxation funds the public goods that no individual can provide | Roads, courts, defence and emergency services depend on collective contribution | Even the most market-friendly countries fund these collectively | "fund the public goods that markets cannot provide" |
| 2 | Progressive taxation reduces inequality without crippling growth | Higher earners can absorb higher rates with less harm to consumption | Several Nordic states combine high taxes with sustained growth | "couple progressive taxation with sustained growth" |
| 3 | Regulation corrects market failures | Pollution, monopoly and consumer fraud are not self-correcting | Antitrust action against major tech firms reflects exactly this view | "address market failures through proportionate regulation" |
| 4 | Strong public services create equality of opportunity | Free schools and healthcare give all children a fair start | The public-school systems of many European countries are still credited for high mobility | "underpin equality of opportunity" |
| 5 | Compulsory voting deepens civic participation | Higher turnout produces governments more representative of the whole population | Australia's compulsory-voting system delivers very high turnout | "broaden the legitimacy of elected government" |
| 6 | Regulation of speech is necessary to protect minorities | Pure laissez-faire allows hate speech that undermines democracy itself | Many democracies criminalise incitement without becoming authoritarian | "balance free expression with protection from incitement" |
| 7 | Government can shift behaviour cheaply through "nudges" | Default options, labelling and modest taxes have measurable effects | Default-on organ donation has lifted donor rates in several jurisdictions | "leverage behavioural-economic insights at scale" |
| 8 | International cooperation is required for transboundary issues | Climate, pandemics, financial crises and crime cross borders | The Paris Agreement, the WHO and Interpol are obvious examples | "coordinate action on inherently transboundary challenges" |
| 9 | Public investment in infrastructure pays for itself | Roads, broadband and clean water raise productivity | OECD analyses link infrastructure investment to long-run growth | "yield substantial returns on public investment" |
| 10 | Universal services strengthen national resilience | Crises (pandemics, recessions) hit weak public systems hardest | Several countries with strong public health systems weathered COVID better | "build the systemic resilience that crises expose" |

## Core arguments AGAINST / counter (e.g. small government, low taxes, free speech absolutism, deregulation)

| # | Argument | Supporting reason | Concrete example | Useful phrase |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | High taxes can dampen entrepreneurship and investment | Capital and talent are mobile and respond to incentives | Several high-tax economies have seen entrepreneurs relocate | "blunt the incentives that drive enterprise" |
| 2 | Bureaucracy and regulation impose hidden costs on small firms | Compliance is proportionally heavier for SMEs | Many small businesses cite regulation as a top concern | "impose disproportionate compliance costs on smaller firms" |
| 3 | State spending tends to expand beyond its mandate | Once a programme exists, it is rarely abolished even when redundant | Comparative public-finance studies trace this "ratchet effect" | "exhibit a long-term tendency to expand beyond original scope" |
| 4 | Compulsory voting forces uninformed citizens to decide | Disengaged voters may dilute, rather than enhance, democratic quality | Critics of Australia's system raise exactly this objection | "extract participation without informed consent" |
| 5 | Speech regulation is open to political abuse | What counts as "hate" can shift with the government | Several states have used hate-speech laws against legitimate dissent | "vulnerable to politicised application" |
| 6 | "Nanny-state" regulation infantilises adults | Soda taxes and exercise mandates restrict adult choice | Libertarian commentators routinely raise this concern | "encroach on the sphere of legitimate adult choice" |
| 7 | International institutions can encroach on democratic accountability | Decisions made in Brussels or Geneva escape national voters | Brexit was, in part, an articulation of this view | "displace decisions from accountable national institutions" |
| 8 | Means-testing is more efficient than universal benefits | Targeting saves resources for those who need them | Many welfare reforms have shifted in this direction | "concentrate finite resources on those most in need" |
| 9 | Markets allocate resources more efficiently than central planning | Prices aggregate information that no agency can match | The mid-20th-century socialist-calculation debate articulated this view | "price signals as a more efficient information system" |
| 10 | Governments make poor venture capitalists | State-picked "winners" routinely fail | Several "industrial policy" initiatives have produced costly disappointments | "lack the discipline of a profit-and-loss test" |

## Concession patterns (band-7 nuance)

1. *While higher taxation does carry some economic cost, the alternative — under-funded schools, hospitals and infrastructure — is hardly more attractive.*
2. *Granted that compulsory voting can produce uninformed ballots, voluntary systems can produce equally unrepresentative ones.*
3. *It is true that regulation imposes compliance costs, yet markets without regulation are demonstrably prone to crises.*
4. *Critics rightly worry about state over-reach, but treating every regulation as paternalism collapses important distinctions.*
5. *Free speech is rightly defended; whether it should extend to incitement against minorities is a separate question.*
6. *To be sure, public spending is not infinite, but framing every social problem as a budget question obscures the genuine choices involved.*
7. *Even those most sceptical of international institutions tend to concede that climate, pandemics and crime cross borders in ways that purely national action cannot resolve.*

## Ready-to-adapt thesis statements

1. **(Opinion)** *I largely agree that taxation should be progressive and substantial, since the public goods it funds — health, education and infrastructure — are essential to a functioning society and cannot be efficiently delivered by markets alone.*
2. **(Discuss both views)** *Although there are reasonable arguments on both sides, in my view government should retain a leading role in regulating markets, since unregulated markets have repeatedly produced outcomes that democracies have judged unacceptable.*
3. **(Advantages / disadvantages)** *While extensive regulation imposes genuine compliance costs, the protective benefits — for consumers, workers and the environment — on balance outweigh them.*
4. **(Problem / solution)** *Voter disengagement reflects a confluence of perceived elite distance, weak civics education and disillusioning policy outcomes; remedies must therefore work at all three levels.*
5. **(Two-part question)** *This essay will first weigh the case for progressive taxation against its critics' concerns and then consider how a fair tax system might best be designed.*

## Sample fully-developed body paragraph #1 (band 7+)

> *(Topic sentence)* The clearest case for substantial public spending rests on the existence of goods that the market, left to itself, will systematically under-provide. *(Explanation)* Roads, courts, basic research, public health and national defence share two awkward features: their benefits cannot be confined to those who pay, and their costs are too large for any single buyer to bear. In these circumstances, individual rationality leads predictably to collective under-investment, and only a state with the power to tax can break the impasse. *(Concrete example)* The early development of vaccines, satellite navigation and the internet itself was funded largely through public expenditure, before private firms scaled what governments had built. *(Mini-conclusion)* Far from being a drag on the economy, then, well-targeted public spending is in many cases the precondition for the private innovation that comes later.

## Sample fully-developed body paragraph #2

> *(Topic sentence)* Critics of expansive government nevertheless make an important point about the limits of state action. *(Explanation)* Programmes funded with public money are more easily defended than abolished; once a constituency exists for a benefit, it tends to survive long after its rationale weakens. The result, over decades, is a steady accumulation of programmes that draw on the same finite tax base, eventually crowding out the genuinely productive uses of public money. *(Concrete example)* Several developed economies now spend a third of their budgets servicing legacy commitments — pensions, debt interest, structural subsidies — leaving comparatively little for forward-looking investment. *(Mini-conclusion)* This does not invalidate the case for active government, but it does mean that any defence of public spending must take seriously the question of how programmes are reviewed, narrowed or retired over time, rather than treating every existing line in the budget as untouchable.

## Common pitfalls / clichés to avoid

- "Government should help people." — Vague.
- "Money is the root of all evil." — Cliché, also a misquotation.
- "Politicians lie." — Sweeping and low band.
- Treating *the government* as a single agent that "decides" everything.
- Confusing *legislation* (laws) with *legalisation* (making something legal).
- "Democracy is the best system." — Assertion without argument.
- "Taxes are theft." — Polemical, unsuitable register.
- Misusing *public* (general public) and *the public sector* (state-funded services).
- "There are two sides to every coin." — Cliché.
- Pretending detailed knowledge of a foreign country's tax brackets.
