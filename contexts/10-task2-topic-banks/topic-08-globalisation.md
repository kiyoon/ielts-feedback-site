# Topic: Globalisation

## Sub-topics commonly seen on the IELTS exam

- International trade and the rise of multinational corporations.
- The dominance of the English language and its effect on local languages.
- Cultural exchange vs cultural homogenisation.
- Outsourcing of jobs from developed to developing economies.
- Whether globalisation widens or narrows global inequality.
- Mass migration as a feature of globalised labour markets.
- Global brands and the loss of local businesses.
- The environmental consequences of global supply chains.
- Whether national identity is weakened by globalisation.
- International cooperation on problems that cross borders.

## Sample real exam prompts

1. *Globalisation is positive for economies, but its negative sides should not be ignored. To what extent do you agree?*
2. *Some people believe that international tourism and trade have made the world more similar, while others argue that globalisation actually preserves cultural diversity. Discuss both views.*
3. *In some countries, the use of English is replacing local languages. Is this a positive or negative development?*
4. *Multinational corporations now operate across many countries. What advantages and disadvantages does this bring to the host nations?*
5. *Some argue that globalisation has widened the gap between rich and poor countries, while others believe it has helped reduce poverty. Discuss both views.*
6. *Many traditional skills and customs are being lost as a result of modernisation and globalisation. Why is this happening, and what can be done to address it?*
7. *Some people believe that international cooperation is essential for solving global problems, while others argue countries should focus on their own issues first. Discuss both views.*
8. *In a globalised economy, more and more people are working for international companies. Is this a positive or negative trend?*

## Core arguments FOR (e.g. globalisation lifts incomes, spreads ideas, expands choice)

| # | Argument | Supporting reason | Concrete example | Useful phrase |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Trade has lifted hundreds of millions out of extreme poverty | Export-led growth raised incomes especially in East Asia | World Bank data show extreme poverty falling sharply since 1990 | "lift incomes through export-led integration" |
| 2 | Multinationals transfer technology and managerial know-how | Local suppliers and workers learn modern practices | Foreign direct investment in Vietnam and Mexico illustrates this | "facilitate the diffusion of technology and skills" |
| 3 | Consumers benefit from wider choice and lower prices | Global supply chains keep clothing and electronics affordable | The cost of basic consumer goods has fallen in real terms over decades | "expand consumer choice at lower real prices" |
| 4 | Cultural exchange enriches everyday life | Music, food and film travel and combine | K-pop, anime and Latin American TV illustrate global cultural flows | "facilitate reciprocal cultural exchange" |
| 5 | A common language (English) lubricates international cooperation | Scientists, diplomats and pilots share a working tongue | International scientific publishing has been overwhelmingly anglophone | "function as a global lingua franca" |
| 6 | Global capital markets fund infrastructure in developing economies | Local savings alone cannot finance roads, ports, broadband | World Bank-rated bonds attract international investors | "channel international capital into infrastructure" |
| 7 | International cooperation tackles transboundary problems | Climate, pandemics and crime cross borders | The Montreal Protocol on ozone is the standout success | "co-ordinate action on global externalities" |
| 8 | Global standards raise quality and safety | Aviation, food and pharmaceutical standards converge upwards | ICAO, Codex Alimentarius and ICH are everyday examples | "harmonise standards in safety-critical industries" |
| 9 | Migration fills labour-market gaps in receiving countries | Healthcare and agriculture in many ageing economies depend on migrant workers | Several European health systems would collapse without migrant clinicians | "address structural labour-market shortages" |
| 10 | Cross-border firms create higher-paid jobs in host countries | Foreign-affiliate wages tend to exceed local averages | OECD data confirm this in most host markets | "deliver wage premia in host economies" |

## Core arguments AGAINST / counter (e.g. cultural homogenisation, exploitation, inequality, lost local industry)

| # | Argument | Supporting reason | Concrete example | Useful phrase |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Global brands erode distinctive local cultures | Identical chains, films and clothes everywhere | High streets globally now feature the same handful of brands | "promote a globalised commercial monoculture" |
| 2 | Outsourcing has hollowed out manufacturing in some developed regions | Industrial heartlands lost stable jobs | The US Rust Belt and parts of northern England illustrate the effect | "leave behind hollowed-out industrial communities" |
| 3 | Multinationals can extract more value than they leave behind | Profits go to headquarters; local taxes are minimised | Investigations into transfer-pricing arrangements have repeatedly shown this pattern | "extract value through transfer-pricing arrangements" |
| 4 | Global supply chains increase environmental footprint | Goods travel thousands of kilometres before consumption | Container shipping is itself a major emissions source | "embed long-distance emissions in everyday products" |
| 5 | Dominance of English squeezes out smaller languages | Children opt for English-medium schooling | UNESCO repeatedly warns of accelerating language extinction | "marginalise linguistic minorities at the expense of a global lingua franca" |
| 6 | Sweatshop conditions persist in some global supply chains | Race-to-the-bottom labour standards | Textile and electronics scandals recur with depressing regularity | "rest in part on the exploitation of low-wage labour" |
| 7 | Global integration transmits crises faster | A shock in one market hits all markets | The 2008 financial crisis spread within weeks | "transmit financial shocks across borders" |
| 8 | International institutions can override democratic preferences | Trade rules limit what national parliaments can do | Critics of the WTO and EU make this case | "constrain democratic choice within national jurisdictions" |
| 9 | Uneven gains have widened intra-country inequality | Skilled urban workers gain; unskilled rural ones lose | Comparative-political-economy research documents this clearly | "widen distributional gaps within nation states" |
| 10 | Global tourism can damage the very cultures it claims to celebrate | Authenticity gives way to performance for visitors | Several heritage sites now restrict visitor numbers | "commodify cultural traditions for global audiences" |

## Concession patterns (band-7 nuance)

1. *While globalisation has clearly lifted incomes in much of the developing world, the gains within developed economies have been distributed unevenly enough to fuel a backlash.*
2. *Granted that multinationals bring investment and technology, their long-term contribution depends critically on the quality of host-country institutions.*
3. *It is true that English functions usefully as a global lingua franca; this advantage, however, can come at the expense of smaller languages without deliberate protection.*
4. *Critics rightly note that global supply chains transmit crises rapidly, but the same chains have, on balance, expanded consumer welfare for decades.*
5. *Even those who celebrate cultural exchange tend to concede that exchange between unequal partners can edge towards homogenisation.*
6. *To be sure, no country can solve climate change alone, but international cooperation works best when it complements, rather than overrides, domestic political legitimacy.*
7. *Although outsourcing reduces costs, treating workers in host countries merely as a cost is incompatible with the longer-term standards most consumers say they support.*

## Ready-to-adapt thesis statements

1. **(Opinion)** *I largely agree that globalisation has, on balance, been a positive force for the world economy, although its costs — distributional and cultural — must be addressed rather than denied.*
2. **(Discuss both views)** *Although both views have merit, in my view the dominance of English is, on balance, a positive development, provided that deliberate policy is in place to preserve linguistic diversity.*
3. **(Advantages / disadvantages)** *While the operations of multinational corporations bring investment, jobs and technology to host countries, they also raise concerns about taxation and labour standards that, on balance, governments must actively manage.*
4. **(Problem / solution)** *The principal harms attributed to globalisation — unequal distribution of gains, cultural homogenisation and exploitation — are not inherent to trade itself but reflect the regulatory choices societies have made.*
5. **(Two-part question)** *This essay will first examine why globalisation has accelerated in recent decades and then consider whether its benefits genuinely outweigh its disadvantages.*

## Sample fully-developed body paragraph #1 (band 7+)

> *(Topic sentence)* The clearest benefit of economic globalisation has been its sheer scale of poverty reduction in the developing world. *(Explanation)* By integrating into international supply chains, countries that previously relied on subsistence agriculture have been able to specialise in manufacturing for export, creating millions of jobs that, while modestly paid by Western standards, represent a substantial improvement on the rural alternative. World Bank statistics on the share of the global population living in extreme poverty have shown a remarkable decline since 1990, almost entirely driven by Asia's integration into world trade. *(Concrete example)* Vietnam, for instance, transformed itself within a generation from one of the poorest countries in Asia to a middle-income economy after opening to foreign investment and export-led growth. *(Mini-conclusion)* Whatever the legitimate concerns about globalisation in wealthy economies, this developmental record is too consequential to be discounted in any honest assessment.

## Sample fully-developed body paragraph #2

> *(Topic sentence)* Even so, it is precisely in those wealthy economies that globalisation's costs have been concentrated, in ways that public policy has been slow to address. *(Explanation)* Where labour-intensive manufacturing has migrated to lower-wage economies, regions whose economies were built around such industries have struggled to find equivalently stable employment. The aggregate gains from cheaper consumer goods are real but diffuse, while the losses fall heavily on a smaller number of identifiable communities — and democratic politics is, predictably, more sensitive to the latter than to the former. *(Concrete example)* The pattern is visible in the post-industrial regions of the United States, the United Kingdom and France, where political volatility tracks closely with long-term industrial decline. *(Mini-conclusion)* Defending an open international economy therefore requires not denying these costs, but pairing trade openness with the adjustment, retraining and regional-investment policies that have too often been neglected.

## Common pitfalls / clichés to avoid

- "Globalisation has made the world a small village." — Cliché.
- "We live in a global village." — Cliché.
- "Globalisation is good / bad." — Empty without scope.
- Confusing *globalisation* with *internationalisation* with *Westernisation*.
- "All MNCs exploit workers." — Overstated.
- "English is killing local languages." — Hedge with *can / may*.
- Citing fabricated trade statistics.
- Sweeping claims about "developing countries" as a single bloc.
- "It is a coin with two sides." — Tired.
- Confusing *export* (verb stress on first syllable as noun, second as verb).
