# Topic: Sport and Leisure

## Sub-topics commonly seen on the IELTS exam

- Competitive vs recreational sport.
- The pay of professional athletes.
- Government funding of elite sport vs grassroots.
- Sport in schools: should it be compulsory?
- The role of dangerous or extreme sports.
- E-sports and whether they qualify as "real" sport.
- Sport and national identity.
- Doping and the integrity of competition.
- Spectator sport vs participation in sport.
- The role of leisure time in modern life.

## Sample real exam prompts

1. *Some people think that professional athletes earn too much money compared with people in other professions. To what extent do you agree?*
2. *Some people argue that governments should spend more money on training top athletes to win major competitions, while others believe that this money would be better spent on improving sports facilities for ordinary people. Discuss both views.*
3. *Some people argue that sports such as boxing or motor racing are too dangerous and should be banned. To what extent do you agree?*
4. *Some people believe that competitive sport at school is good for children, while others think it produces too much pressure. Discuss both views.*
5. *Some people argue that watching sport on television does more harm than good, since it encourages a sedentary lifestyle. To what extent do you agree?*
6. *In some countries, there is a growing debate about whether e-sports should be classified as a real sport. What is your view?*
7. *Some people think that taking part in international sporting events promotes peaceful relationships between countries, while others believe it merely fuels rivalry. Discuss both views.*
8. *Some people believe that sport should be compulsory in schools, while others argue it should be optional. Discuss both views and give your own opinion.*

## Core arguments FOR (e.g. funding sport, compulsory PE, pay reflects value, e-sports inclusion)

| # | Argument | Supporting reason | Concrete example | Useful phrase |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Sport in schools instils lifelong healthy habits | Active children are likely to become active adults | Several public-health bodies recommend daily PE | "embed habits of physical activity at a formative age" |
| 2 | Government funding of elite sport raises national morale and inspires participation | Successful athletes inspire children to take up sport | Olympic-host countries report participation surges | "produce inspirational effects across grassroots participation" |
| 3 | Athletes' high salaries reflect scarcity and revenue generation | The number of people who can win the Champions League is very small | Sponsorship and broadcast revenue justify market salaries | "reflect scarcity premia and revenue contribution" |
| 4 | Sport develops teamwork and resilience in ways the classroom cannot | Children learn to win, lose and cooperate | Educational research consistently links sport to non-cognitive skill | "build the non-cognitive skills classrooms cannot easily teach" |
| 5 | Investment in grassroots facilities widens access | Without local pitches, talent goes undiscovered | Many medal-winning athletes attribute success to local clubs | "broaden the talent base from which elite sport emerges" |
| 6 | E-sports demand genuine skill and discipline | Reaction time, strategy and team coordination are real | Major arenas now sell out e-sports events | "rest on demonstrable skill and sustained training" |
| 7 | International sport can ease political tension | Shared events bring rival nations into structured contact | Ping-pong diplomacy and the World Cup are routinely cited | "create structured channels for non-political contact" |
| 8 | Spectator sport drives public-health awareness | Major competitions push fitness into mainstream conversation | Hosting major events often catalyses public-health campaigns | "elevate physical-activity awareness in public discourse" |

## Core arguments AGAINST / counter (e.g. against high pay, against compulsory PE, dangerous sport bans)

| # | Argument | Supporting reason | Concrete example | Useful phrase |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Athletes' pay is hard to justify when contrasted with essential workers | Nurses, teachers, scientists earn a fraction | Public discontent recurs around top-flight transfer fees | "produce a stark contrast with socially essential occupations" |
| 2 | Government funding of elite sport diverts money from grassroots | Medals concentrate at the top; participation is what matters | Several audit reports raise this concern | "concentrate funding at the apex while neglecting the base" |
| 3 | Compulsory school sport can humiliate non-athletic children | Forced public competition damages confidence | Many adults trace lifelong sport-aversion to school PE | "produce lasting aversion in unathletic children" |
| 4 | Dangerous sports impose costs on public-health systems | Treating injuries falls on the taxpayer | Several jurisdictions debate liability for adventure sports | "externalise the costs of risk-taking onto public services" |
| 5 | Doping and corruption have hollowed out elite credibility | Repeated scandals erode trust | Major federations have faced sustained scrutiny | "erode the credibility on which sport's value depends" |
| 6 | E-sports can entrench sedentary behaviour in young players | Hours at a screen replace physical play | Health bodies have flagged this | "displace physical activity with extended screen time" |
| 7 | International sport can intensify nationalist rivalry rather than soothe it | Hooliganism and political instrumentalisation are recurring problems | Several major events have produced violent fan clashes | "intensify, rather than mitigate, nationalist antagonism" |

| 9 | Children specialised young in single sports risk injury and burnout | Year-round training on developing bodies is harmful | Sports-medicine bodies caution against early specialisation | "expose children to overuse injury through early specialisation" |
| 10 | Spectatorship can substitute for participation, not promote it | Watching is the opposite of playing | Surveys link heavy spectator-sport consumption with low personal activity | "substitute, rather than catalyse, personal participation" |

## Concession patterns (band-7 nuance)

1. *While professional athletes' salaries can seem disproportionate, the market they operate in is genuinely competitive at a global scale.*
2. *Granted that compulsory PE benefits most pupils, schools should be mindful of those for whom enforced public competition is genuinely damaging.*
3. *It is true that hosting major sporting events can foster civic pride; the costs to taxpayers, however, are rarely as well advertised as the benefits.*
4. *Critics rightly note that elite-sport funding can crowd out grassroots provision; the relationship, however, is not always as zero-sum as it is presented.*
5. *Even those who celebrate spectator sport tend to concede that watching cannot substitute for personal physical activity.*
6. *To be sure, dangerous sports involve real risk; banning them outright, however, would represent an unusual extension of state authority into adult choice.*
7. *Although e-sports demand genuine skill, it is reasonable to ask whether the benefits of physical sport can be retained alongside its rise.*

## Ready-to-adapt thesis statements

1. **(Opinion)** *I largely agree that the salaries of top-flight athletes are excessive when measured against socially essential occupations, although the underlying market explanation is harder to dismiss than is sometimes acknowledged.*
2. **(Discuss both views)** *Although both elite and grassroots funding have legitimate claims, in my view the bulk of public investment should flow to grassroots facilities, with elite sport benefiting indirectly from the broader participation base.*
3. **(Advantages / disadvantages)** *While compulsory school sport carries real risks for unathletic children, on balance its public-health and developmental benefits justify retaining it, with appropriate flexibility in how it is delivered.*
4. **(Problem / solution)** *Falling participation in sport in many countries reflects a confluence of facility closures, screen displacement and rising inequality; addressing it requires investment in local infrastructure alongside school-level reform.*
5. **(Two-part question)** *This essay will first examine why professional athletes earn so much before considering whether such salaries can be regarded as fair.*

## Sample fully-developed body paragraph #1 (band 7+)

> *(Topic sentence)* The most defensible argument for substantial government funding of sport is not the prestige of elite medals but the cumulative public-health value of widespread participation. *(Explanation)* Modern sedentary lifestyles, combined with calorie-dense food environments, have produced rising rates of cardiovascular disease, type-2 diabetes and adolescent mental ill-health — all of which exact a far greater fiscal cost over time than sustained investment in pitches, swimming pools and after-school clubs would. Investment in grassroots facilities is, in this sense, less a leisure subsidy than a long-horizon health-policy intervention. *(Concrete example)* Several Nordic countries explicitly justify their sport budgets in public-health terms, and their participation and population-fitness statistics tend to bear out the rationale. *(Mini-conclusion)* The standard framing — elite glory versus grassroots access — is therefore in part a false choice; the strongest case for public investment is the one that begins with the local sports hall.

## Sample fully-developed body paragraph #2

> *(Topic sentence)* The salaries of top professional athletes nevertheless demand a more honest discussion than the easy moralism that surrounds them. *(Explanation)* It is undoubtedly striking that a midfielder can earn in a week what a paediatric nurse earns in a year, and any account of social value will struggle to defend the gap. Yet within their own market, top athletes are paid as scarce talent in a global industry that generates extraordinary commercial revenue from sponsorship, broadcasting and merchandise — and these pay levels are the result of competitive bidding by buyers, not of any monopoly extraction by the players themselves. *(Concrete example)* The wages of leading footballers, basketballers and Formula One drivers track closely with broadcasting and sponsorship revenue in their respective sports, with several of these revenue streams growing substantially in the past decade. *(Mini-conclusion)* The honest objection, therefore, is not that any individual athlete is overpaid by their employer, but that the wider distribution of income in modern economies allows entertainment industries to outbid socially essential ones — a critique aimed not at sport, but at how we allocate resources more broadly.

## Common pitfalls / clichés to avoid

- "Sport is a universal language." — Cliché.
- "Health is wealth." — Cliché.
- "Mens sana in corpore sano." — Tired and showing off.
- Confusing *win* (a match) with *beat* (an opponent).
- Confusing *sport* (BrE uncountable) with *sports* (AmE plural usage).
- "Football is the most popular sport in the world." — True but not an argument.
- "Sport unites the world." — Sentimental.
- "Athletes should be role models." — Develop or cut.
- Listing types of sport without analysis.
- Confusing *amateur* (unpaid) with *poor quality*.
