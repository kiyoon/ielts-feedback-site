# Topic: Health

## Sub-topics commonly seen on the IELTS exam

- Public-health funding: prevention vs treatment, government vs individual responsibility.
- Lifestyle-related diseases (obesity, type-2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease).
- Exercise, diet and the role of schools and government in promoting health.
- Mental health: rising rates, stigma, access to care.
- Healthcare systems: free at the point of use vs private; inequality of access.
- Tobacco, alcohol and sugar: should governments tax or ban harmful products?
- The role of doctors in patient education.
- Traditional vs modern medicine; alternative therapies.
- Pandemic preparedness, vaccination and public-health communication.
- Ageing populations and the rising cost of geriatric care.

## Sample real exam prompts

1. *Prevention of health problems and illness is more important than treatment and medicine. Government funding should reflect this. To what extent do you agree?*
2. *Some people believe that public health is the responsibility of the government, while others argue that individuals should be responsible for their own health. Discuss both views and give your own opinion.*
3. *The number of people who are at risk of serious health problems due to being overweight is increasing. What are the causes, and what can be done to address this issue?*
4. *Some governments are introducing taxes on food and drinks high in sugar to reduce consumption. To what extent do you think this is an effective measure?*
5. *Mental-health problems are becoming increasingly common, especially among young people. What are the causes, and how can the issue be addressed?*
6. *Healthcare should not be provided for free regardless of a person's income. To what extent do you agree or disagree?*
7. *Some people argue that everyone should be required by law to do regular exercise. Do you agree or disagree?*
8. *Many people now suffer from health problems caused by a modern lifestyle that cannot be treated with conventional medicine. Should traditional medicine be encouraged?*

## Core arguments FOR (e.g. government should fund / regulate; preventive health; collective responsibility)

| # | Argument | Supporting reason | Concrete example | Useful phrase |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Prevention is far more cost-effective than treatment | Treating disease is many times more expensive than preventing it | WHO analyses repeatedly find that every pound spent on vaccination saves several in later treatment | "yields a substantial return on preventive investment" |
| 2 | Universal healthcare protects the most vulnerable | Without subsidy, the poor delay care and end up sicker | The British NHS is widely cited as a flagship of universal coverage | "ensure equitable access regardless of income" |
| 3 | Government regulation of harmful products has a track record of working | Smoking rates have fallen sharply where taxes and bans were imposed | Australia's plain-packaging policy reportedly reduced smoking among the young | "leverage taxation and regulation to shift behaviour" |
| 4 | Schools are the most efficient venue for instilling lifelong health habits | Children form habits early; reaching them is reaching tomorrow's adults | Many European countries now mandate compulsory PE and nutrition education | "embed healthy habits at a formative age" |
| 5 | Public-health campaigns can reshape cultural norms within a generation | Drink-driving stigma was created largely by sustained campaigns | The "Truth" anti-smoking campaign in the US is a textbook example | "reshape public attitudes through sustained campaigning" |
| 6 | Healthy populations are economically productive | Sick days, premature death and disability are macroeconomic problems | OECD data link healthier populations to higher labour-force participation | "underpin sustainable economic productivity" |
| 7 | Mental-health investment delivers high returns | Untreated mental illness drives unemployment, addiction and self-harm | The UK's IAPT programme is often cited as a cost-effective scaled intervention | "yield disproportionate returns on relatively modest investment" |
| 8 | Government can correct unhealthy market incentives | Junk food is heavily marketed; fresh produce is not | Restrictions on junk-food advertising to children have been adopted in several jurisdictions | "counterbalance asymmetric commercial incentives" |
| 9 | Public-health crises are inherently collective and require state action | Pandemics, anti-microbial resistance and pollution cross individuals | The COVID-19 response showed that only states can mobilise testing, vaccines and information at scale | "respond effectively to systemic health threats" |
| 10 | Investing in primary care relieves pressure on hospitals | Most conditions can be managed early at lower cost | Countries with strong primary care report lower hospital-admission rates | "reduce avoidable demand on acute services" |

## Core arguments AGAINST / counter (e.g. individual responsibility, against bans, against high taxation)

| # | Argument | Supporting reason | Concrete example | Useful phrase |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Adults should bear the consequences of their own lifestyle choices | Personal autonomy is a foundational liberal value | Sin taxes are sometimes criticised as paternalistic over-reach | "uphold individual autonomy in lifestyle decisions" |
| 2 | Universal free healthcare can lead to over-use and queues | When a service is free, demand is unlimited | Several European systems report long elective-surgery waiting lists | "give rise to capacity constraints and treatment delays" |
| 3 | Heavy taxation on unhealthy products is regressive | Lower-income households spend a higher share on these goods | Sugar taxes have been criticised for falling hardest on the poor | "regressive in their distributional impact" |
| 4 | Compulsory exercise infringes on personal liberty | Forcing adults to work out is unenforceable and authoritarian | No major democracy has tried it for adults | "represents an unjustifiable extension of state authority" |
| 5 | Lifestyle disease has many causes; blaming "poor choices" oversimplifies | Genetics, marketing and food deserts all contribute | Obesity rates correlate strongly with income and post-code, not just willpower | "obscures structural determinants of health" |
| 6 | Government health spending can crowd out other priorities | Money spent on hospitals cannot be spent on schools or housing | Several ageing societies are now wrestling with exactly this trade-off | "imposes opportunity costs on competing public priorities" |
| 7 | Education campaigns alone often have weak effects on behaviour | "Just say no" approaches have a poor track record | Several "eat-five-a-day" style messages have shown only marginal impact in evaluations | "deliver disappointing returns on awareness-only interventions" |
| 8 | Privatised insurance can drive innovation in diagnostics and treatment | Profit motive funds R&D | Many cutting-edge cancer therapies were developed in market-based systems | "stimulate medical innovation through competitive incentives" |
| 9 | Heavy regulation of food can be culturally insensitive | Traditional cuisines may not fit nutritionist templates | Some traditional diets are now being re-evaluated as healthier than once thought | "marginalise legitimate cultural food practices" |
| 10 | Excessive medicalisation of normal life events undermines resilience | Treating ordinary stress as illness can paradoxically worsen it | Some psychologists warn that over-diagnosis of anxiety disempowers young people | "risks pathologising ordinary human experience" |

## Concession patterns (band-7 nuance)

1. *While individuals undoubtedly bear some responsibility for their own health, this argument is too often used to obscure the structural drivers of poor outcomes.*
2. *Granted that universal healthcare can produce queues, the alternative — leaving the poor unable to afford care — strikes most observers as worse.*
3. *Sin taxes on sugary drinks are admittedly regressive in their incidence; their behavioural and fiscal effects, however, are now reasonably well evidenced.*
4. *To be sure, prevention cannot replace treatment for those already ill, but it can reduce the inflow of new chronic patients over time.*
5. *Even those who favour personal responsibility tend to concede that children require special protection from aggressive marketing of unhealthy products.*
6. *It is true that mental-health awareness campaigns can drift into over-diagnosis; this risk is, on balance, less serious than the under-treatment they seek to address.*
7. *Although market-based health systems have their successes, they tend to leave equity gaps that public systems are better placed to fill.*

## Ready-to-adapt thesis statements

1. **(Opinion)** *I largely agree that prevention should be prioritised over treatment, since the long-term economic and human costs of avoidable illness are demonstrably greater than the upfront cost of preventive measures.*
2. **(Discuss both views)** *Although both governments and individuals share responsibility, in my view the heavier obligation lies with governments, whose regulatory and fiscal powers far exceed what citizens can achieve alone.*
3. **(Advantages / disadvantages)** *While taxes on unhealthy foods carry distributional risks, on balance their effect on public health justifies their introduction, provided lower-income households are protected.*
4. **(Problem / solution)** *Rising rates of obesity reflect a confluence of sedentary work, calorie-dense food environments and aggressive marketing; addressing them will require coordinated action across regulation, education and urban planning.*
5. **(Two-part question)** *This essay will first analyse why mental-health problems among young people have risen so sharply before considering which interventions are most likely to be effective.*

## Sample fully-developed body paragraph #1 (band 7+)

> *(Topic sentence)* The strongest case for prioritising preventive over curative medicine rests on simple economics: it is far cheaper to prevent a chronic disease than to treat it for decades. *(Explanation)* Conditions such as type-2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease and certain cancers are largely products of modifiable risk factors — diet, smoking, inactivity — and once established, they require lifelong medication, repeat hospital admissions and, in many cases, expensive complications. By contrast, the upstream interventions that prevent them, including nutrition education, physical-activity initiatives and basic screening, are comparatively inexpensive. *(Concrete example)* WHO and OECD analyses repeatedly find that every pound invested in immunisation or smoking cessation returns several pounds in avoided treatment costs over a generation. *(Mini-conclusion)* It follows that, far from being a luxury, prevention is in fact the most fiscally responsible component of any modern health budget.

## Sample fully-developed body paragraph #2

> *(Topic sentence)* Critics, however, are right to point out that lifestyle-disease narratives can drift into a kind of moralism that ignores structural realities. *(Explanation)* People do not choose their food environments in a vacuum: those living in low-income neighbourhoods often face longer journeys to fresh produce, longer working hours and more aggressive marketing of cheap, calorie-dense alternatives. Telling such residents to "make better choices" is therefore as much a description of their disadvantage as a remedy for it. *(Concrete example)* Studies in major US and UK cities have repeatedly mapped obesity rates onto post-code, not personal character — a pattern that pure individual-responsibility models struggle to explain. *(Mini-conclusion)* Effective public-health policy must therefore combine personal advice with measures that change the environment in which choices are actually made.

## Common pitfalls / clichés to avoid

- "Health is wealth." — Tired proverb, low band.
- "Prevention is better than cure." — Cliché; rephrase as a specific argument.
- "Junk food is killing our children." — Sensational, hedge.
- Confusing *fewer* / *less* (fewer cases of diabetes, not less cases).
- Citing fabricated statistics ("70 % of teens are obese").
- Treating *fitness* and *health* as synonyms.
- "Mental illness is just stress." — Reductive.
- "We are what we eat." — Cliché.
- Endless lists of unhealthy foods without analysis.
- "Doctors should help patients." — Trivially true; develop or cut.
