# Topic: Work and Employment

## Sub-topics commonly seen on the IELTS exam

- Career choice: passion vs salary, vocation vs profession.
- Work-life balance and the rise of long hours.
- Remote and hybrid working.
- The gig economy and platform-based work.
- Automation and the future of employment.
- Retirement age and the ageing workforce.
- Job satisfaction and what makes a good job.
- Women in the workforce and the gender pay gap.
- Youth unemployment and graduate over-supply.
- The role of unions and worker rights.

## Sample real exam prompts

1. *Some people believe that working from home is more productive than working in an office, while others disagree. Discuss both views and give your own opinion.*
2. *Many young people today change jobs frequently throughout their careers. What are the reasons, and is this a positive or negative trend?*
3. *Some people believe that paid work is essential for personal happiness, while others think this is not necessarily true. Discuss both views.*
4. *Automation and artificial intelligence are increasingly replacing human workers. What are the consequences for employment, and what can be done?*
5. *Some people argue that the retirement age should be raised, while others believe it should remain the same. Discuss both views and give your own opinion.*
6. *In some countries, more and more people are working in the gig economy. What are the advantages and disadvantages of this development?*
7. *Some employers believe that academic qualifications are more important than life experience and personal qualities when employing people. To what extent do you agree?*
8. *Some people argue that high salaries should be limited by law. To what extent do you agree?*

## Core arguments FOR (e.g. flexibility, technology, raised retirement age, female participation)

| # | Argument | Supporting reason | Concrete example | Useful phrase |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Remote work improves productivity for many roles | Fewer interruptions and no commute time | Several large-firm studies report sustained or higher productivity post-pandemic | "deliver measurable gains in focused output" |
| 2 | Flexible arrangements widen workforce participation | Carers, disabled workers and parents can stay employed | Female workforce participation has risen where flexible work is normal | "broaden access to the workforce" |
| 3 | Automation removes the most dangerous and tedious tasks | Robots take roles that injure or bore | Industrial-injury rates have fallen sharply where automation is dense | "eliminate hazardous and repetitive work" |
| 4 | A higher retirement age is necessary as longevity rises | Pension systems were not designed for 30-year retirements | Most OECD countries have raised the state-pension age | "align retirement with rising life expectancy" |
| 5 | Job mobility allows workers to find better matches | Frequent moves can raise wages and skills | Tech-sector data show clear "job-hopping wage premia" | "facilitate optimal worker-employer matching" |
| 6 | Lifelong learning keeps workers employable through change | Retraining beats redundancy | Several Nordic states have well-funded retraining systems | "embed continuous reskilling in the labour market" |
| 7 | Diverse workforces produce better decisions | Mixed teams catch what homogeneous ones miss | Multiple consultancy reports link diversity with performance | "foster cognitive diversity in decision-making" |
| 8 | The gig economy gives flexibility to those who want it | Students, second-earners and creatives benefit | Many drivers and freelancers explicitly choose this model | "accommodate non-standard work preferences" |
| 9 | Higher minimum wages reduce in-work poverty | Wage floors lift the lowest-paid without strong job loss | Several jurisdictions have raised minimums with limited employment effects | "raise the wage floor without disproportionate displacement" |
| 10 | Women's full labour-force participation lifts GDP | Excluding half the talent pool wastes capacity | World Bank analyses repeatedly quantify the gain | "unlock substantial latent capacity" |

## Core arguments AGAINST / counter (e.g. remote-work problems, gig precarity, automation losses, raised retirement age)

| # | Argument | Supporting reason | Concrete example | Useful phrase |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Remote work erodes mentoring and informal learning | Junior staff lose absorbed-by-osmosis training | Many large firms have pulled back to hybrid for this reason | "weaken the informal transmission of professional skill" |
| 2 | Gig work strips workers of basic protections | No sick pay, holiday pay, or pension contributions | Multiple court cases have reclassified platform "contractors" as employees | "leave workers without statutory protections" |
| 3 | Automation displaces routine workers faster than retraining can absorb | The mismatch is temporal and geographical | Several declining industrial regions illustrate this | "displace workers more rapidly than they can be retrained" |
| 4 | Raising the retirement age is unfair to physically demanding occupations | A 67-year-old labourer is not a 67-year-old desk worker | Several unions have campaigned on this issue | "ignore differential physical demands across occupations" |
| 5 | Long-hours cultures damage wellbeing | Burnout, depression and even mortality | Several countries' "death by overwork" terms (Japan's *karoshi*) reflect this | "produce measurable harms to physical and mental health" |
| 6 | Frequent job-hopping can hurt long-term career building | Pension, training and tenure benefits accrue with stability | HR research notes diminishing returns to excessive churn | "forfeit the compounding benefits of tenure" |
| 7 | Excessive credentialism shuts out capable non-graduates | Routine roles increasingly demand a degree | Several economies are rolling back degree requirements | "exclude capable workers through unnecessary credentialism" |
| 8 | Capping high salaries can drive talent abroad | Tax-sensitive professionals are mobile | Several finance hubs lost senior staff after compensation regulations | "trigger talent migration to less restrictive jurisdictions" |
| 9 | "Work from home" widens inequality between knowledge and frontline workers | Some can work remotely; many cannot | The cleavage was sharpened during the pandemic | "deepen the gap between knowledge and frontline workers" |
| 10 | Persistent youth unemployment leaves long-term scars | Early periods out of work depress lifetime earnings | Several southern European cohorts illustrate the effect | "produce lasting scarring effects on early careers" |

## Concession patterns (band-7 nuance)

1. *While remote work has clear productivity advantages for many roles, it cannot fully replicate the informal mentoring that physical workplaces provide.*
2. *Granted that automation will displace some workers, history suggests that new occupational categories tend to emerge alongside the disappearing ones.*
3. *It is true that the gig economy offers flexibility; this flexibility, however, is rarely freely chosen by workers without alternatives.*
4. *Critics rightly note that raising the retirement age is harder on physically demanding occupations than on desk work — a distinction policy must respect.*
5. *Even those who favour higher salaries for executives concede that they need not be set without any reference to median pay within the firm.*
6. *To be sure, work confers identity and structure for many; equating self-worth entirely with paid employment, however, can deepen the harm of unemployment.*
7. *Although flexibility can boost wellbeing, "flexibility" used by employers to push risk onto workers is something quite different.*

## Ready-to-adapt thesis statements

1. **(Opinion)** *I largely agree that the retirement age should be raised in line with rising life expectancy, although the policy must accommodate the differential physical demands of different occupations.*
2. **(Discuss both views)** *Although both views have merit, in my view a hybrid model — combining the flexibility of remote work with the collaboration of office time — best matches the realities of most modern workplaces.*
3. **(Advantages / disadvantages)** *While the gig economy offers genuine flexibility, the precarity it imposes on workers without alternatives, on balance, calls for stronger regulatory protection.*
4. **(Problem / solution)** *Persistent youth unemployment is driven by a confluence of skills mismatch, weak job-creation and inadequate transition support; addressing it requires coordinated action across education, training and labour-market policy.*
5. **(Two-part question)** *This essay will first analyse why so many workers now change jobs frequently before considering whether this trend is, on balance, beneficial.*

## Sample fully-developed body paragraph #1 (band 7+)

> *(Topic sentence)* The most consequential effect of remote working has been to recast the everyday geography of professional life. *(Explanation)* In a conventional office model, where one lived was tightly constrained by where one worked; commute times of an hour or more each way absorbed a significant share of waking life and limited where families could realistically settle. With the normalisation of hybrid and remote arrangements, this constraint has loosened: workers can live further from city centres, parents can be present for school pick-ups, and carers can sustain employment that would previously have been impossible. *(Concrete example)* Several large-firm productivity studies conducted since 2020 report that, for knowledge-intensive roles, output has been at least maintained while measures of employee wellbeing have improved. *(Mini-conclusion)* This is not to claim that remote work suits every job — many do not — but the genuine gains for those who can take advantage of it should not be lost in the rush to bring people back to the office.

## Sample fully-developed body paragraph #2

> *(Topic sentence)* Equally, however, the rapid spread of platform-based gig work has exposed a less attractive face of the same flexibility. *(Explanation)* Where employees enjoy paid leave, sick pay, pension contributions and protection from arbitrary dismissal, gig workers — formally classified as self-employed contractors — typically enjoy none of these. The "flexibility" is real, but it often masks a transfer of risk from employer to worker, with the worker bearing the cost when work is scarce, when illness strikes or when retirement approaches. *(Concrete example)* Several recent court rulings in the UK and EU have reclassified platform drivers as workers entitled to basic protections, in tacit acknowledgement that the original "independent contractor" framing did not match the underlying reality. *(Mini-conclusion)* If the gig economy is to be a genuine source of opportunity rather than precarity, regulators will need to ensure that the flexibility it offers is accompanied by the basic safety net that all forms of work, in modern economies, are reasonably expected to provide.

## Common pitfalls / clichés to avoid

- "Work hard, play hard." — Cliché.
- "Time is money." — Tired.
- "All robots will replace humans." — Overstated.
- Confusing *employer* and *employee*.
- Confusing *job* (specific role) and *work* (uncountable in most senses).
- "Working from home is the future." — Hedge.
- "Money is not everything." — Trite.
- "A happy worker is a productive worker." — Sweeping.
- Using *salary* and *wage* interchangeably — they differ in register.
- Listing job perks without analysis.
