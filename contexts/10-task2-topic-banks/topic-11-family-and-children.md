# Topic: Family and Children

## Sub-topics commonly seen on the IELTS exam

- Parenting styles: strict vs permissive, traditional vs modern.
- Childcare: nursery vs grandparents vs stay-at-home parent.
- The role of grandparents in raising children.
- Ageing populations and care for the elderly.
- Family structure: nuclear vs extended, single-parent families.
- Discipline, punishment and reward in raising children.
- Balancing work and family responsibilities.
- The role of fathers in modern families.
- Falling birth rates in many developed economies.
- The role of childhood in shaping adult life.

## Sample real exam prompts

1. *Many families today have both parents working outside the home, so children spend less time with their parents. What problems does this cause, and what can be done to address them?*
2. *Some people believe that grandparents should help raise their grandchildren, while others think this is the parents' responsibility. Discuss both views and give your own opinion.*
3. *In many countries, the population is rapidly ageing. What problems does this cause, and how can society address them?*
4. *Some people argue that the best way to raise children is to be very strict, while others believe a more relaxed approach is more effective. Discuss both views and give your own opinion.*
5. *Some people say that children should be required to help with household chores. To what extent do you agree?*
6. *In many parts of the world, families are smaller now than they were in the past. What are the reasons, and is this a positive or negative development?*
7. *Some people argue that the role of fathers should be the same as that of mothers in raising children. To what extent do you agree?*
8. *In some countries, an increasing number of older people live alone. What are the reasons, and is this a positive or negative development?*

## Core arguments FOR (e.g. shared parenting, grandparental help, smaller families, modern parenting)

| # | Argument | Supporting reason | Concrete example | Useful phrase |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Working parents need affordable childcare to participate fully | Without it, one parent (usually the mother) is forced out of work | Subsidised public nurseries in France and Sweden are routinely cited | "underpin parental — particularly maternal — labour-force participation" |
| 2 | Grandparental involvement strengthens intergenerational bonds | Children gain history, language and emotional anchoring | Many extended families across Asia and southern Europe still operate this way | "transmit values across generations" |
| 3 | Smaller families allow parents to invest more per child | Education, time and resources stretch further | OECD data link smaller family sizes to higher per-child investment | "concentrate resources per child" |
| 4 | Active fathering improves child outcomes | Children with engaged fathers show measurable benefits | Studies repeatedly link father involvement with educational and emotional outcomes | "associated with measurable child-development benefits" |
| 5 | Authoritative (warm-but-firm) parenting is widely supported by research | Combines structure with emotional support | Parenting research since the 1960s consistently identifies this style as optimal | "balance warmth with consistent boundaries" |
| 6 | Childcare professionals provide structured early learning | Trained carers deliver developmental curricula | Many countries see early-years education as a public good | "deliver structured early-years development" |
| 7 | Sharing chores teaches children responsibility | Practical contributions develop competence and self-worth | Cross-cultural research links chore participation to later wellbeing | "instil practical responsibility from an early age" |
| 8 | Multi-generational households can support ageing populations | Care is provided informally without state cost | Several Mediterranean and Asian societies still operate this model | "absorb care-giving demand within the family unit" |
| 9 | Public investment in early childhood yields high social returns | Outcomes flow into education, work and crime statistics | Several Nobel-laureate economists have made this case | "deliver high social returns on early-years investment" |
| 10 | Flexible parental leave normalises both parents' caring role | Use-it-or-lose-it paternity leave shifts behaviour | Iceland and Sweden are widely cited examples | "redistribute care responsibilities across both parents" |

## Core arguments AGAINST / counter (e.g. against grandparent care, against full-time nurseries, concerns about ageing, against permissive parenting)

| # | Argument | Supporting reason | Concrete example | Useful phrase |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Heavy reliance on grandparents can be unfair to them | Older people deserve their retirement | Many grandparents have explicitly raised this concern | "impose unrecognised burdens on the older generation" |
| 2 | Long hours in childcare may weaken parent-child bonds | Time together is irreplaceable | Some attachment research raises this concern | "displace the parent-child time on which secure attachment depends" |
| 3 | Smaller families and falling birth rates threaten pension systems | Fewer workers per retiree | Several developed economies are openly grappling with this | "narrow the demographic base on which welfare systems rest" |
| 4 | Permissive parenting can leave children without self-discipline | Without structure, children struggle later | Comparative parenting research raises this caveat | "fail to provide the boundaries children require" |
| 5 | Excessive academic pressure on children damages childhood | Hyper-competitive cultures produce burned-out adolescents | Several East Asian societies have begun to roll back tutoring requirements | "subject children to chronic academic pressure" |
| 6 | Ageing populations strain healthcare and public finances | Costly care needs concentrate at end of life | Most OECD analyses identify this as a structural fiscal challenge | "exert sustained pressure on health and pension systems" |
| 7 | Single-parent households face acute time and income pressures | Two roles fall on one shoulder | Poverty rates among single-parent households are notably higher | "multiply the structural pressures on single-parent households" |
| 8 | Outsourcing too much child-rearing to institutions undervalues family | Some intangible aspects cannot be professionalised | Religious and traditional commentators routinely raise this | "professionalise dimensions of family life that resist professionalisation" |
| 9 | Older generations' practical knowledge can be lost as households separate | Cooking, repair and crafts pass down through living together | Many traditional skills have visibly declined within a generation | "lose intergenerational transmission of household competence" |
| 10 | Children of dual-earner households can experience time poverty | Schedules are overbooked rather than spacious | Parenting commentators have flagged this consistently | "impose a kind of scheduled, time-poor childhood" |

## Concession patterns (band-7 nuance)

1. *While grandparental involvement clearly benefits children in many ways, it should not be quietly assumed as a given when both parents work full-time.*
2. *Granted that smaller families allow more investment per child, falling birth rates also pose serious long-term challenges to pension systems.*
3. *It is true that nurseries provide structured early learning; they cannot, however, fully substitute for the secure attachment formed at home.*
4. *Critics rightly note that strict parenting can produce conscientious adults; equally, it can also produce anxious or rebellious ones.*
5. *Even those who celebrate the nuclear family tend to concede that it places considerable weight on a small number of relationships.*
6. *To be sure, an ageing population places strain on public services; with appropriate policy, however, longer lives are surely something to celebrate rather than dread.*
7. *Although fathers are increasingly expected to share child-rearing equally, the household-level data suggest there remains a gap between aspiration and practice.*

## Ready-to-adapt thesis statements

1. **(Opinion)** *I largely agree that fathers and mothers should share child-rearing responsibilities equally, since the benefits — for children, parents and the wider economy — substantially outweigh the costs of cultural change.*
2. **(Discuss both views)** *Although both views have merit, in my view grandparental involvement should complement rather than replace direct parental care, with the precise balance depending on family circumstances.*
3. **(Advantages / disadvantages)** *While the trend towards smaller families has clear benefits in terms of per-child investment, the demographic costs that follow, on balance, call for active policy responses.*
4. **(Problem / solution)** *The challenges of an ageing population stem from a confluence of longer lifespans, lower birth rates and rising health costs; addressing them requires reforms to pensions, immigration policy and care provision.*
5. **(Two-part question)** *This essay will first examine why dual-earner households have become the norm before considering what problems this poses for parent-child relationships and how they might be mitigated.*

## Sample fully-developed body paragraph #1 (band 7+)

> *(Topic sentence)* The most pressing reality of modern family life is that, in most middle-income households, both parents now work outside the home — and the institutions surrounding the family have been slow to adjust. *(Explanation)* Subsidised early-years care, flexible working hours, and properly paid parental leave are features of only a handful of advanced economies; in the rest, families improvise with grandparents, expensive private childcare or one parent's reduced career. The cumulative effect is that responsibility for the under-fives often falls on whoever can absorb it, with significant consequences for women's careers, household finances and child development alike. *(Concrete example)* Countries such as Sweden, France and Iceland that have invested systematically in public childcare and shared parental leave consistently report higher female labour-force participation alongside reasonable family stability. *(Mini-conclusion)* Treating childcare as a public-policy concern, rather than a private family puzzle, is therefore not a matter of ideology but a precondition for the dual-earner household to function fairly.

## Sample fully-developed body paragraph #2

> *(Topic sentence)* Equally, however, the heavy reliance on grandparents that has emerged in many societies as a stop-gap deserves a more honest reckoning than it usually receives. *(Explanation)* Grandparents who step in to provide childcare do something genuinely valuable, and many do so willingly; nevertheless, this arrangement quietly transfers a significant cost — of time, energy and forgone retirement — onto a generation that is also expected to remain economically active for longer than its predecessors. Where this informal support is taken for granted, the policy conversation about formal childcare can stall, leaving families with no real alternative. *(Concrete example)* Surveys of grandparents across several European countries have repeatedly found high levels of unrecognised caring effort, particularly among women in their sixties. *(Mini-conclusion)* Recognising grandparental contributions, rather than relying on them by default, is therefore an essential part of any serious response to the modern childcare puzzle.

## Common pitfalls / clichés to avoid

- "Family is the most important thing in life." — Trite.
- "Mother is the best teacher." — Sentimental.
- "Charity begins at home." — Cliché.
- Treating "the family" as a single universal form.
- Assuming all grandparents are willing and available.
- "Old people are wise." — Stereotyped.
- Claiming statistics ("80 % of children in nurseries are unhappy").
- Confusing *raise* (children, plants) and *rise* (intransitive).
- "Children are our future." — Empty filler.
- Assuming all parents have the same options regardless of income.
