# IELTS General Training Task 1 — Letter Types Overview

This guide summarises the **tone, register, vocabulary and structural conventions** for each of the three letter types tested in IELTS GT Task 1: formal, semi-formal, and informal. Use it together with the band-graded samples in this folder.

---

## 1. Identifying the Letter Type from the Prompt

| Recipient (in the prompt) | Letter type | Sign-off |
|---|---|---|
| "the manager", "the director", "the company", "the editor of a newspaper" | **Formal** | "Yours faithfully," |
| "your boss", "a colleague", "a landlord", "a tutor / teacher you don't know well", "a tour operator", "your neighbour you don't know well" | **Semi-formal** | "Yours sincerely," / "Kind regards," |
| "a friend", "your cousin", "a family member", "your pen friend" | **Informal** | "Best wishes," / "Take care," / "Love," |

**Rule of thumb:** if the prompt **names** the recipient (e.g. "Dear John") OR uses words like "friend / family / cousin", it's informal. If it says "Dear Sir/Madam" or names a job title, it's formal.

---

## 2. Universal Structure (all three types)

1. **Greeting**: "Dear Sir/Madam," (formal, no name) | "Dear Mr Smith," (formal, name) | "Dear Mr Smith," / "Dear Mrs Lee," (semi-formal) | "Dear John," / "Hi Sarah," (informal)
2. **Opening line / reason for writing** (1 sentence)
3. **Bullet point 1 paragraph** (2–4 sentences)
4. **Bullet point 2 paragraph** (2–4 sentences)
5. **Bullet point 3 paragraph** (2–4 sentences)
6. **Closing line** (1 sentence — looking forward / hope to hear / can't wait)
7. **Sign-off + name**

Target: **160–190 words**. Below 150 is penalised. Cover **all three bullet points** — missing one caps Task Achievement at 5.

---

## 3. Formal Letter

### When to use
- Writing to a manager / director / editor / unknown company / official body.
- Recipient title only — no first name basis.

### Tone
Polite, professional, impersonal. **No contractions** ("I am", not "I'm"). **No emojis / exclamation marks**. **No phrasal verbs** where Latin-rooted alternatives exist ("request" not "ask for", "obtain" not "get").

### Opening (formal)
- "I am writing to inform you that…"
- "I am writing in connection with…"
- "I am writing to lodge a formal complaint regarding…"
- "I am writing to apply for the position of…"
- "I am writing to express my concern about…"

### Body language
- Use the **passive voice** strategically: "It would be appreciated if…", "the matter could be resolved if…".
- Use **modal verbs** for politeness: "I would be grateful if you could…", "could you kindly…", "would it be possible to…".
- Avoid weak hedging ("maybe", "perhaps", "I'm not really sure") — these damage formal register.

### Closing (formal)
- "I look forward to hearing from you at your earliest convenience."
- "I trust this matter will be resolved promptly."
- "Thank you for your attention to this matter."

### Sign-off (formal)
- "Yours faithfully," (when recipient name unknown)
- "Yours sincerely," (when name known)
- Then your **full name**.

### Common formal-letter purposes
- **Complaint** (faulty product, poor service, neighbour noise via authority)
- **Application** (job, course, scholarship, membership)
- **Request** (information, action, intervention)
- **Inquiry** (about a tour, a service, a product)

### Formal vocabulary cheat-sheet
- "ask for" → "request"
- "tell" → "inform / advise / notify"
- "get" → "receive / obtain / acquire"
- "buy" → "purchase"
- "give back" → "return / refund"
- "look at" → "examine / consider"
- "sorry about" → "I would like to apologise for"
- "I want" → "I would like to / I would appreciate"

## 4. Semi-formal Letter

### When to use
- Writing to people in a **defined role** but with whom you have some relationship: a landlord, a tutor you know, a colleague, a tour operator, a head of school you have met.
- Recipient is named (Mr/Mrs/Dr) but not a friend.

### Tone
Polite, respectful, slightly warmer than formal. **Limited contractions** (avoid them mostly). Some personal references are appropriate.

### Opening (semi-formal)
- "I am writing to enquire about…"
- "I hope this email finds you well. I am writing to…"
- "Thank you for your letter dated… I am writing back regarding…"

### Body language
- Mostly formal structures, but a few personal touches are fine.
- Modal politeness: "could", "would", "might".
- Some personal opinion is acceptable ("I really enjoyed", "I was delighted").

### Closing (semi-formal)
- "I look forward to your reply."
- "Thank you for your time and consideration."
- "I would appreciate it if you could get back to me."

### Sign-off (semi-formal)
- "Yours sincerely," / "Kind regards," / "Best regards,"
- Then your full name (or first + last).

### Common semi-formal purposes
- **Complaint to landlord** about a flat / equipment.
- **Request to a colleague / tutor** for help / information.
- **Inquiry to a tour operator / hotel manager**.
- **Reply to a manager / official** with explanation.

---

## 5. Informal Letter

### When to use
- Writing to a friend, family member, cousin, pen-friend.
- Prompt uses first names.

### Tone
Warm, personal, conversational. **Use contractions** ("I'm", "can't", "won't", "we'll"). **Idioms and exclamations welcome** ("can't wait", "over the moon", "guess what!"). Personal anecdotes appropriate.

### Opening (informal)
- "Hi Sarah! How are you?"
- "Hope you're doing well!"
- "It's been ages since I last wrote — sorry for the delay!"
- "I was so happy to get your letter last week."

### Body language
- Contractions throughout.
- Phrasal verbs ("come over", "drop by", "catch up", "hang out").
- Casual sentence starters ("Anyway,…", "By the way,…", "Oh, and…").
- Exclamations to show enthusiasm.

### Closing (informal)
- "Can't wait to hear from you!"
- "Looking forward to seeing you soon."
- "Give my love to your family."
- "Drop me a line whenever you can."
- "Take care!"

### Sign-off (informal)
- "Best wishes," / "Take care," / "Lots of love," / "Cheers,"
- Then **first name only**.

### Common informal purposes
- **Invitation** to visit / event / wedding.
- **Apology** for delay / missed event.
- **Advice** on a problem / decision.
- **News update** about your life.
- **Thank you** for a gift / hospitality / favour.
- **Asking a favour** (look after pet, help with arrangement).

### Informal idioms (use sparingly — 1–2 per letter)
- "I'm over the moon" (very happy)
- "Keep me in the loop" (keep updating)
- "It's been ages" (a long time)
- "Drop by" (visit briefly)
- "Catch up" (talk after a while)
- "Hit the ground running" (start strongly)
- "On the same page" (in agreement)

## 6. Tone Mistakes That Cost Marks

The single biggest GT Task 1 mistake is **mixing registers**. Examples:

- Using "I am writing to inform you" in a letter to a friend → too formal.
- Using "Hi! Hope you're well!" in a complaint to a manager → too informal.
- Ending a complaint with "Thank you so much for your kindness, you're amazing!" → tone slip kills the score.
- Using contractions ("I'm", "don't") in a job application → register breach.

**One sustained register throughout the letter is more important than fancy vocabulary.** Examiners can move you from Band 6 to Band 7 simply by maintaining consistent register.

---

## 7. Band 6 → Band 7 Upgrade Checklist (letters)

| Feature | Band 6 typical | Band 7 target |
|---|---|---|
| Bullet coverage | All three covered, but one is brief | All three developed evenly |
| Tone | Generally appropriate but slips at points (esp. closing) | Sustained register throughout |
| Opening | Generic ("I am writing this letter to…") | Purpose-specific ("I am writing to lodge a formal complaint regarding…") |
| Vocabulary | Repeats basic verbs ("ask", "tell", "want") | Uses register-appropriate verbs ("request", "inform", "would appreciate") |
| Grammar | Several errors in articles, agreement, prepositions | Mostly accurate; minor errors only |
| Linkers | "and / but / so / also" | "Furthermore / In addition / However / As a result / Moreover" (formal) or "Anyway / By the way / Plus" (informal) |
| Closing | Abrupt ("Thank you. Bye.") | Polite and purposeful ("I look forward to your prompt response.") |
| Sign-off | Wrong type for register | Correct (Yours faithfully / sincerely / love) + name |

---

## 8. Quick Don'ts

- Do **not** use bullet points or numbered lists in the letter body — write in paragraphs.
- Do **not** use SMS abbreviations ("u", "thx", "pls", "btw").
- Do **not** invent the recipient's name unless asked. Use "Dear Sir/Madam,".
- Do **not** sign off with both "Yours sincerely" AND "Yours faithfully" (pick one based on whether the name is known).
- Do **not** copy more than 4 consecutive words from the prompt.
- Do **not** write under 150 words — Task Achievement is capped at 5.
- Do **not** end an informal letter with "Yours faithfully" — wrong register.
