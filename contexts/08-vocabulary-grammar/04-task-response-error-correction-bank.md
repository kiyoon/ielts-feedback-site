# Task Response Error Correction Bank — Atomic Reference (Band 6 → 7)

> Atomic table reference for Task Response (TR, Task 2) and Task Achievement (TA, Task 1) errors. **TR is 25 % of the score** — failing to address every part of the prompt or shifting position is the single most common reason a fluent essay still scores 6. Pair with `contexts/05-band-6-to-7/task-response-upgrades.md` (strategy) and `contexts/09-instructor-tips/06-task2-sentence-frames.md` (drop-in frames).
>
> **Reason** tags: `[scope]` = not addressing the full prompt; `[pos]` = position issue; `[dev]` = underdevelopment; `[mem]` = memorised/template; `[t1]` = Task 1-specific; `[reg]` = register.

---

## 1. Not addressing all parts of the prompt

> Common failure: a "two-part" or "discuss both views and give your opinion" prompt is treated as if it had only one part. Examiners deduct sharply.

| # | Prompt type / issue | Failing essay opener (band 5–6) | Improved opener (band 7+) | Reason |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | "Discuss both views and give your opinion." Essay only argues one side. | "I strongly believe X, and in this essay I will explain why." | "While some argue X, others believe Y. **This essay will examine both views before arguing that** X is more compelling." | [scope] Must cover both + own view. |
| 2 | Two-part question (*What are the causes? What measures can be taken?*). Essay only lists causes. | "There are several causes of obesity, such as fast food and lack of exercise…" | "Obesity stems from **two principal causes — dietary and lifestyle factors — and can be addressed through both individual and policy-level measures**, as this essay will outline." | [scope] Must address causes AND measures. |
| 3 | "To what extent do you agree?" — essay never states extent. | "Many people think X. Some agree, some disagree." | "I **largely / partially / strongly** agree with this view, although significant reservations apply." | [pos] Must take a degree-of-agreement position. |
| 4 | "Advantages and disadvantages — do the advantages outweigh the disadvantages?" Essay lists pros & cons but never weighs. | Lists 2 advantages and 2 disadvantages, then ends. | "**On balance, the advantages outweigh the disadvantages**, primarily because …" | [scope] Must answer the weighing question. |
| 5 | "Why is this happening? What can be done?" — essay only answers Why. | Two body paragraphs both about causes. | Body 1: Why this is happening. Body 2: What can be done. | [scope] One body per part. |
| 6 | Prompt mentions a *specific group* (e.g., children) — essay discusses adults. | "Many adults today are addicted to social media…" | Stay on **children** as the prompt requires. | [scope] Off-target group. |
| 7 | Prompt asks about a *specific country* — essay generalises globally. | "In the modern world, …" | Stay focused on the country named in the prompt. | [scope] Off-target setting. |
| 8 | "Some people think X, others think Y." Essay only says X. | Only argues X for 250 words. | Must give roughly equal weight to both views before stating position. | [scope] Balance required. |
| 9 | Prompt has *and* (two questions joined). Writer misses the second. | "Why do people work long hours?" → essay only on causes. | Re-read prompt for *and*; identify both halves. | [scope] Missed conjunction. |
| 10 | Two-part: ignores the "what measures" half because writer doesn't have ideas. | Pads cause analysis to 250 words. | At least **one body paragraph (~80 words) per part** — even if briefer than ideal. | [scope] Coverage > depth here. |

## 2. Position not clear / shifting position

| # | Issue | Failing example | Improved example | Reason |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Position absent. | "Some people think X, others Y. Both have merit. In conclusion, both have merit." | "I take the view that **X is the more compelling position**, although Y has some validity." | [pos] No position. |
| 2 | Position only in conclusion. | First three paragraphs balanced; conclusion drops opinion. | State position in **introduction**, defend through body, restate in conclusion. | [pos] Late position. |
| 3 | Position shifts between body paragraphs. | Body 1: "X is correct." Body 2: "Y is correct." | Choose one and stick with it; Body 2 should reinforce, not contradict. | [pos] Inconsistency. |
| 4 | Hedged into meaninglessness. | "Maybe X, but maybe Y, it depends." | Hedge **degree** (*partially / largely*), not direction. | [pos] Unclear. |
| 5 | Position contradicts the body's main argument. | Position: "X is bad." Body: lists three benefits of X. | Align body with thesis. | [pos] Contradiction. |
| 6 | Uses "I think" once but never returns. | "I think X." → 200 words of neutral analysis. | Reinforce with phrases like *as argued above*, *for the reasons given*, *I therefore maintain that…* | [pos] Weak commitment. |
| 7 | Two opposing claims in introduction. | "X is the best solution. However, X has limits." | Make the dominant claim primary and the qualification subordinate. | [pos] Mixed signal. |
| 8 | Conclusion reverses introduction. | Intro: "X is correct." Conclusion: "Y is correct." | Conclusion = restatement + synthesis, never reversal. | [pos] Reversal. |
| 9 | "It depends" without specifying conditions. | "It depends on the situation." | Specify: "**X holds in most contexts**, **but Y is more appropriate when** specific conditions Z apply." | [pos] Vague. |
| 10 | Position changes register from confident to tentative mid-essay. | Body 1: "X is undeniably correct." Body 2: "Maybe X is sometimes okay." | Maintain consistent confidence level. | [pos] Inconsistent stance. |

## 3. Off-topic body paragraphs

| # | Issue | Off-topic example | Improved example | Reason |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Body wanders from topic in the topic sentence. | Prompt: school uniforms. Body 2: "School lunches are also bad." | Stay on uniforms. Add a uniforms-related sub-aspect (cost, individuality, equality). | [scope] Off-topic. |
| 2 | Body discusses a related but distinct issue. | Prompt: online learning. Body: "Traditional classrooms have advantages." | Either tie back ("Online learning, **unlike traditional classrooms**, …") or drop. | [scope] Tangent. |
| 3 | Body answers a different prompt the writer prepared. | Memorised tech essay used for an environment prompt. | Write fresh for the actual prompt. | [scope] Memorised content. |
| 4 | Body lists generic ideas with no link to the prompt. | "Money is important. People want money. Money brings success." | Anchor every claim to the prompt's specific question. | [scope] Padding. |
| 5 | Body shifts from cause analysis to solution analysis when prompt is "Why?". | Prompt: Why are cities polluted? → "We can plant trees…" | Stick to causes; mention solutions only if prompt asks. | [scope] Wrong question. |
| 6 | Body discusses the writer's personal life in detail. | "When I was young, I…" + 5 sentences of biography. | One brief example is OK; don't make the body autobiographical. | [scope] Excess personal narrative. |
| 7 | Body argues against the writer's own thesis (steel-man without rebuttal). | Position: X. Body 2: 100 words for Y. No rebuttal. | If presenting the counter-view, **rebut** it: "However, this argument fails to account for …" | [scope] Unfinished counter-argument. |
| 8 | Two body paragraphs that say the same thing. | Body 1: X is good for kids. Body 2: X benefits children. | Each body paragraph must have a **distinct** main idea. | [dev] Redundancy. |
| 9 | Body cites unrelated current event. | Prompt: education. Body: discusses the latest celebrity scandal. | Use only relevant, generalisable examples. | [scope] Off-topic example. |
| 10 | Body slips into a different sub-topic (e.g., from "should" to "what is"). | Prompt asks "should governments…" → Body answers "Governments do…" | Match modal to the prompt: *should / could / must*. | [scope] Modal mismatch. |

## 4. Underdeveloped main ideas (claim with no support)

| # | Issue | Underdeveloped example | Improved example | Reason |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Topic sentence followed by no support. | "Technology is helpful. It saves time." *(2 sentences total)* | Topic + reasoning + concrete example + brief analysis + link back (4–6 sentences). | [dev] Underdeveloped. |
| 2 | No example. | "Education improves life quality." | "Education improves life quality. **For instance,** OECD data show that university graduates earn 56 % more on average over their careers." | [dev] No support. |
| 3 | No reasoning between claim and example. | "Pollution is bad. Look at Beijing." | "Pollution is bad **because it causes respiratory disease and shortens life expectancy**, as seen in heavily industrialised cities like Beijing." | [dev] Causal gap. |
| 4 | One-clause body paragraph. | "I think school is important because we learn." | Expand: claim + reason + example + counter / qualification + link. | [dev] One-clause. |
| 5 | Listing reasons without elaborating. | "X is bad because of A, B, and C." *(no expansion)* | Pick **2 reasons and develop each**, rather than listing 5 unsupported ones. | [dev] List vs depth. |
| 6 | Example without analysis. | "Look at smokers — many get cancer." | Add analysis: "**This pattern shows that** habitual exposure to risk factors compounds over time, …" | [dev] No analysis. |
| 7 | Generic example anyone could give. | "For example, in many countries, people use technology." | Specific: "**For example, in South Korea, smartphone penetration has reached 95 % among teenagers**, which has reshaped …" | [dev] Generic. |
| 8 | Repeats the topic sentence as the conclusion of the paragraph. | "Education matters. … Therefore, education matters." | Conclude with **implication**: "Therefore, sustained investment in education is essential to long-term mobility." | [dev] Loop. |
| 9 | Hypothetical example without grounding. | "If governments did X, then Y would happen." *(only)* | Pair the hypothetical with a real instance or evidence. | [dev] Speculative only. |
| 10 | Body paragraph under 50 words. | Two short sentences. | Aim for ~80–110 words per body paragraph in a 250-word task. | [dev] Volume. |

## 5. Weak conclusions (just a repeat)

| # | Issue | Weak example | Improved example | Reason |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Conclusion is one short sentence. | "In conclusion, technology is good." | 2–3 sentences: synthesis + final judgement + (optional) implication. | [dev] Underdeveloped. |
| 2 | Conclusion repeats the introduction verbatim. | Same sentence as the thesis. | **Synthesise** body arguments + restate position in different words. | [reg] Repetition. |
| 3 | Conclusion introduces a new argument. | "In conclusion, another reason is …" | Conclusions don't add new content. | [reg] New idea late. |
| 4 | Conclusion lists what the body said. | "I talked about X, Y, and Z." | Don't narrate; **judge**. | [reg] Meta-narration. |
| 5 | Conclusion contradicts position. | Body argues X; conclusion says Y. | Stay consistent. | [pos] Reversal. |
| 6 | Conclusion uses cliché. | "All in all, every coin has two sides." | "**On balance, …**" + specific judgement. | [reg] Cliché. |
| 7 | Conclusion is a question. | "So what should we do?" | End with a statement, not a rhetorical question. | [reg] Rhetorical. |
| 8 | Conclusion only restates the prompt. | "Many people debate X." | State **your** answer, not the question. | [reg] No position. |
| 9 | Conclusion uses memorised closer. | "Hopefully the world becomes a better place." | End with a precise, prompt-anchored judgement. | [mem] Cliché closer. |
| 10 | Conclusion missing entirely. | Essay stops mid-thought. | Always include 2–3 sentence conclusion. | [dev] Missing. |

## 6. Memorised / template phrases examiners deduct for

| # | Memorised phrase (band 5–6) | Replace with (band 7+) | Reason | Frequency |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | In the modern era of globalisation, … | In recent years, / At present, | [mem] Cliché opener. | H |
| 2 | It is a controversial topic in modern society. | This issue continues to provoke debate. | [mem] Cliché opener. | H |
| 3 | With the development of modern technology, … | As technology advances, / Driven by technological change, | [mem] Cliché opener. | H |
| 4 | This essay will discuss both views and give my opinion. | This essay will examine both perspectives **before arguing that …**. *(or just present the thesis directly)* | [mem] Mechanical signposting. | H |
| 5 | Every coin has two sides. | Each policy presents both advantages and drawbacks. | [mem] Cliché. | H |
| 6 | A double-edged sword | This brings both benefits and costs. | [mem] Memorised idiom. | H |
| 7 | As we all know, … | It is widely acknowledged that … *(or delete entirely)* | [mem] Padding. | H |
| 8 | From my point of view, … | I take the view that … / I would argue that … | [mem] Overused. | H |
| 9 | Last but not least, … | Finally, … / A further point is that … | [mem] Cliché. | H |
| 10 | All in all, … | On balance, / Taking everything into account, | [mem] Cliché. | M |
| 11 | In a nutshell, … | In summary, / To summarise, | [mem] Cliché. | M |
| 12 | Without a shadow of a doubt, … | Undeniably, / It is clear that, | [mem] Cliché. | M |
| 13 | This is a hot topic. | This topic has attracted considerable attention. | [mem] Cliché. | M |
| 14 | Since the dawn of time, … | Throughout history, / For centuries, *(or drop)* | [mem] Cliché. | M |
| 15 | At the end of the day, … | Ultimately, / In the final analysis, | [mem] Cliché. | M |
| 16 | Each and every | Every / All | [mem] Redundant. | M |

## 7. Task 1 (Academic): Missing or weak overview

| # | Issue | Weak example | Improved example | Reason |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | No overview at all. | Essay describes data but never summarises main trends. | Add an **overview paragraph** stating 2 main trends/features (no figures). | [t1] No overview = max 5 for TA. |
| 2 | Overview just lists data. | "There were 5, 10, 15, 20 students." | "**Overall, the figure increased steadily across the period, with the largest gains observed in the final decade.**" | [t1] Overview = trends, not numbers. |
| 3 | Overview has specific figures. | "Overall, the figure was 20 % in 2020." | Save figures for the body; overview is high-level. | [t1] Wrong content. |
| 4 | Overview hidden mid-essay. | Overview placed after body paragraphs. | Place after intro **or** at the end as final synthesis — never buried. | [t1] Visibility. |
| 5 | Overview repeats the introduction. | Same sentence as paraphrase of prompt. | Overview = **summary of trends**, not paraphrase of question. | [t1] Repetition. |
| 6 | Overview only mentions one feature when multiple are key. | "The line rose throughout." | Mention **2 main features**: peak/trough, biggest changes, comparisons. | [t1] Coverage. |
| 7 | Overview is a single short sentence. | "Overall, sales went up." | 2 sentences with 2 features. | [t1] Underdeveloped. |
| 8 | Overview uses *I think* / *In my opinion*. | "I think the data show…" | Task 1 is descriptive — no opinion. | [t1] Register. |
| 9 | Overview missed the "wow" feature (the most striking). | Mentions minor changes; ignores 5× increase. | Lead with the **most striking** change. | [t1] Selection. |
| 10 | Overview labelled awkwardly. | "Overview: …" *(label-style)* | Use "**Overall, …**" or "**To summarise, …**". | [t1] Style. |

## 8. Task 1: Listing data without grouping / comparison

| # | Issue | Weak example | Improved example | Reason |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Sentence per data point. | "A was 10. B was 20. C was 30." *(repeated)* | Group: "**A and B remained relatively stable at 10 and 20, while C surged to 30.**" | [t1] No grouping. |
| 2 | No comparison between categories. | All categories described independently. | Compare: "**X was twice as high as Y**" / "**X exceeded Y by 15 percentage points**". | [t1] No comparison. |
| 3 | All sentences use *was / were* + figure. | Repetitive sentence structure. | Vary verbs: *rose to, fell to, peaked at, declined sharply, plateaued, fluctuated*. | [t1] Verb variety. |
| 4 | No use of *while / whereas* to contrast. | Two paragraphs with no contrast. | "**While X rose, Y fell over the same period.**" | [t1] Contrast linker. |
| 5 | Reports every data point in the chart. | 12 sentences for 12 bars. | Select **the most significant** points; group similar ones. | [t1] Over-detail. |
| 6 | No sense of magnitude. | "X went up." | "X rose **steadily / sharply / gradually / dramatically**." | [t1] Adverb of degree. |
| 7 | Misses the highest/lowest. | Discusses middle data only. | Lead with **highest, lowest, biggest change**. | [t1] Selection. |
| 8 | No grouping by trend (similar movements). | Describes A, B, C, D individually. | "**A, B, and C all increased**, whereas D declined." | [t1] Grouping. |
| 9 | Repeats *increase / decrease* every sentence. | "X increased. Y increased. Z decreased." | Vary: *climb, surge, soar, plummet, dip, dwindle, plateau*. | [t1] Vocabulary range. |
| 10 | Forgets units. | "X reached 50." | "X reached **50 %** / **50 million**." | [t1] Units. |

## 9. Task 1: Wrong tense for chart timeframe

| # | Issue | Wrong example | Improved example | Reason |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Past chart described in present. | "The graph shows that sales **increase** between 1990 and 2000." | "The graph shows that sales **increased** between 1990 and 2000." | [t1] Past timeframe. |
| 2 | Future chart described in past. | "By 2050, sales **rose** to 100 m." | "By 2050, sales **will rise / are projected to rise** to 100 m." | [t1] Future projection. |
| 3 | Present chart in past. | "The pie chart **showed** today's distribution." | "The pie chart **shows** today's distribution." | [t1] Present-time chart. |
| 4 | Mixed tenses with no time marker. | "Sales rose, and demand increases." | Use one tense per timeframe; align with the chart. | [t1] Inconsistency. |
| 5 | Forecasts described in simple future. | "Sales **will be** higher in 2050." *(if data is "projected")* | "Sales **are projected to be / are expected to reach** higher levels in 2050." | [t1] Hedging future. |
| 6 | Process diagram in past instead of present passive. | "The water **was filtered**, then **was pumped**." | "**The water is filtered**, **then pumped** through the system." *(present simple passive)* | [t1] Process tense. |
| 7 | Map description in wrong tense. | Past map: "The town **has changed**." | Past map: "The town **changed** between 1980 and 2020." | [t1] Map past. |
| 8 | Present map described in past. | Current map: "The school **was located** near the river." | Current map: "The school **is located** near the river." | [t1] Map present. |
| 9 | Generic "the chart shows" repeated. | Every sentence starts with *The chart shows*. | Vary: *The data indicate…, According to the figures…, As can be seen,…* | [t1] Variety. |
| 10 | "From the chart" as a clause-opener every time. | Mechanical linker. | Use sparingly; vary opener. | [t1] Mechanical. |
