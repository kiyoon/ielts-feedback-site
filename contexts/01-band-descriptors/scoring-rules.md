# IELTS Writing — Scoring Rules

This document covers everything an LLM marker needs to know about how IELTS Writing band scores are calculated, rounded, weighted, and penalised. All rules are taken from official IELTS documentation (ielts.org, takeielts.britishcouncil.org) and corroborated by widely cited examiner sources.

---

## 1. The four criteria are equally weighted within each task

Each task (Task 1 and Task 2) is assessed on four criteria, each worth 25% of that task's score:

| Task | Criterion 1 | Criterion 2 | Criterion 3 | Criterion 4 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Task 1** (AC and GT) | Task Achievement | Coherence and Cohesion | Lexical Resource | Grammatical Range and Accuracy |
| **Task 2** (AC and GT) | **Task Response** | Coherence and Cohesion | Lexical Resource | Grammatical Range and Accuracy |

Each criterion is rated on a whole-band scale 0–9. There are **no half-band ratings on individual criteria**; half-bands only emerge from averaging.

### Task-level score

The four criterion ratings are averaged to a Task score. The Task score may end in .25, .5, or .75 — a rounding step then applies to produce a half-band Task score (see Section 3).

Worked examples (per task):

| Criterion 1 | Criterion 2 | Criterion 3 | Criterion 4 | Average | Task score |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 6 | 6 | 6 | 6 | 6.00 | **6.0** |
| 7 | 6 | 6 | 6 | 6.25 | **6.5** (rounds up) |
| 7 | 7 | 6 | 6 | 6.50 | **6.5** |
| 7 | 7 | 7 | 6 | 6.75 | **7.0** (rounds up) |
| 7 | 7 | 7 | 7 | 7.00 | **7.0** |
| 8 | 7 | 6 | 5 | 6.50 | **6.5** |

> **Rounding convention used by IELTS:** Average ending in **.25 rounds up** to .5; average ending in **.75 rounds up** to the next whole band. Some unofficial sources claim IELTS rounds *down*; the official IELTS scoring page uses round-up to nearest half-band, and this is the standard interpretation. (See "Rounding" section for nuance.)

## 2. Task 2 carries more weight than Task 1

> "Each task is assessed independently. The assessment of Task 2 carries more weight in marking than Task 1." — IELTS Writing Key Assessment Criteria

The widely cited (and unofficially confirmed) formula:

```
Overall Writing Band = (Task 1 score + 2 × Task 2 score) / 3
```

This means **Task 2 is worth 2/3 (≈67%)** of the writing score and **Task 1 is worth 1/3 (≈33%)**.

> Note: IELTS does not formally publish the exact weighting; it is computed by an internal algorithm. The 1:2 ratio is the consensus interpretation used by most major test-prep sources.

### Worked examples — Overall Writing Band

| Task 1 | Task 2 | Weighted average | Overall Writing |
|---|---|---|---|
| 6.0 | 6.0 | 6.00 | **6.0** |
| 6.0 | 7.0 | 6.67 | **6.5** |
| 7.0 | 6.0 | 6.33 | **6.5** |
| 7.0 | 7.0 | 7.00 | **7.0** |
| 6.5 | 6.5 | 6.50 | **6.5** |
| 7.0 | 6.5 | 6.67 | **6.5** |
| 6.5 | 7.0 | 6.83 | **7.0** (rounds up from .83) |
| 5.0 | 7.0 | 6.33 | **6.5** |

The weighting means **a strong Task 2 can pull a weak Task 1 up** more than the reverse — strategic implication: candidates should not skimp on Task 2 to perfect Task 1.

## 3. Rounding rules (overall band score)

> "The overall band score is the average of the four section band scores rounded to the nearest half band." — IELTS official scoring page

The four sections are Listening, Reading, Writing, Speaking. The same rounding logic applies *within* Writing when averaging task scores.

**Rounding rules:**

- Average ending in .25 → **rounds up** to next .5 (e.g. 6.25 → 6.5)
- Average ending in .75 → **rounds up** to next whole band (e.g. 6.75 → 7.0)
- Anything strictly less than .25 → **rounds down** to whole band (e.g. 6.10, 6.20 → 6.0)
- Anything from .25 up to but below .75 → rounds to .5 (e.g. 6.30, 6.40, 6.50, 6.60, 6.70 → 6.5)

| Calculated average | Reported band |
|---|---|
| 6.00 – 6.24 | 6.0 |
| 6.25 – 6.74 | 6.5 |
| 6.75 – 7.24 | 7.0 |
| 7.25 – 7.74 | 7.5 |

> **Conflicting source note:** A handful of test-prep sites (including the cited *blog.myieltsclassroom.com*) state IELTS "rounds down, not up" for writing specifically. This contradicts the official IELTS scoring page, which uses the standard round-up rule. The official rule is what examiners and the IELTS computer system use; the "rounds down" claim is a misreading. **Use the round-up rule above.**

## 4. Word count rules

### Minimum word counts (official, fixed)

| Task | Minimum |
|---|---|
| Task 1 (Academic and GT) | **150 words** |
| Task 2 (Academic and GT) | **250 words** |

### Penalty regime (current)

**Pre-2018:** Going under the minimum triggered an automatic 1-band deduction on Task Achievement / Task Response. (Some sources state Band 5 was the cap.)

**Post-2018 (current):** The **automatic word-count penalty has been removed**. Examiners no longer deduct a fixed amount.

**However**, going under the minimum still hurts in two indirect but powerful ways:

1. **Task Achievement / Task Response is mathematically harder to satisfy.** The descriptors at Band 6+ require all main parts to be addressed and ideas to be extended/supported. With fewer than 250 words, you cannot extend ideas adequately — so TR is capped at Band 5 or 4 *by content*, not by rule.
2. **Lexical Resource and Grammatical Range descriptors at Band 3** explicitly say: "the resource is inadequate (which may be due to the response being **significantly underlength**)" and "**length may be insufficient to provide evidence of control of sentence forms**." Significantly underlength scripts can be capped at Band 3 on LR or GRA.

### Practical guidance

- **Task 1 target:** 160–180 words (a small buffer above 150).
- **Task 2 target:** 260–290 words (a small buffer above 250). Some examiners suggest 270–290 as the safe sweet spot.
- **There is no upper limit**, but writing too much wastes time and increases error density. Going past 320 words on Task 2 rarely helps and usually hurts grammar accuracy.

### How examiners count

- Every word counts: articles, prepositions, conjunctions, numbers (numerals or written-out) — all count as one word each.
- Hyphenated compound words (*twenty-one*, *part-time*) generally count as **one** word.
- Contractions (*don't*, *I'll*) count as **one** word each.
- Date / number expressions: *15th May 2023* would typically count as 3 words.
- The examiner does not count words by hand for every script in the post-2018 era; they look at length holistically and use their judgement on whether the script appears too short to satisfy the criteria.

### 20-word floor

> **Responses of 20 words or fewer are rated at Band 1.** This is hard-coded into all four criteria at Band 1.

## 5. Other penalties and limiters

### Plagiarism / memorised content

> "Scripts may be penalised if they are **partly or wholly plagiarised**." — IELTS Key Assessment Criteria

> "Band 0 should only be used … where there is proof that a candidate's answer has been **totally memorised**."

If an examiner believes a script has been memorised or copied:
- Partial: TR/TA is heavily reduced; LR may also be hit ("possible over-dependence on memorised language").
- Total: Band 0.

The Band 4 LR descriptor explicitly mentions "inappropriate use of lexical chunks (e.g. memorised phrases, formulaic language and/or language from the input material)" — i.e. importing whole phrases verbatim from the prompt or from rote-learned templates is a Band 4 lexical limiter.

### Off-topic / wholly unrelated content

| Severity | Effect |
|---|---|
| Some irrelevant detail | Detracts from TR/TA at Band 5 or 6 |
| Tangential answer | Band 4 ("answer is tangential") |
| Content barely related | Band 2 |
| Wholly unrelated | Band 1 |

### Format / non-text responses

> "Scripts may be penalised if they are … **not written as full, connected text** (e.g. using bullet points in any part of the response, or note form, is not appropriate, etc.)."

A script written as bullet points or notes — even if otherwise good — is penalised. Format is also explicitly part of TR/TA.

### Copied rubric

> "Any copied rubric must be discounted." — Band 1 descriptor for all four criteria.

If a candidate copies the prompt into their answer, those words **don't count toward the word total**, and any band ≥ 2 requires content that is the candidate's own.

### Use of a language other than English

A response written in any other language throughout = Band 0.

## 6. The "fully fit" rule (how examiners apply descriptors)

> "A script must **fully fit the positive features** of the descriptor at a particular level. **Bolded text** indicates negative features that will limit a rating."

Implications for an LLM marker:

1. **Best-fit, not average.** The examiner picks the band whose descriptor best matches the script holistically, not by averaging features.
2. **Negative features are limiters.** If any **bolded** negative feature is present, the script cannot exceed that band on that criterion. Examples:
   - "**Paragraphing may be inadequate or missing**" → cannot exceed Band 5 on CC.
   - "**Subordinate clauses are rare and simple sentences predominate**" → cannot exceed Band 4 on GRA.
   - "**The format may be inappropriate**" → cannot exceed Band 4 on TR.
   - "**The entire response may be off-topic**" → cannot exceed Band 2 on CC.
   - "**Unrelated to the task**" → cannot exceed Band 4 on LR.
3. **Borderline cases.** When a script has features from two adjacent bands, examiners pick the band where most features fit; they do not split the difference.

---

## 7. Independent assessment of criteria (no compensation)

Each of the four criteria is rated independently. Strong vocabulary cannot rescue weak organisation; great structure cannot rescue grammar errors. The only place "compensation" happens is in the average — a high score on one criterion can lift the task average — but the criterion ratings themselves do not influence each other.

---

## 8. Summary cheat-sheet for an LLM marker

```
1. Score each criterion 0-9 (whole numbers only) using "best fit".
2. Check for any bolded negative limiters → cap that criterion accordingly.
3. Average the four criterion scores → Task score.
4. Round Task score: .25 → .5, .75 → next whole band.
5. Compute Overall Writing = (Task1 + 2*Task2) / 3.
6. Round Overall the same way.
7. Apply hard caps:
     - Under 20 words: Band 1.
     - Wholly off-topic: Band 1 on TR.
     - All in non-English / proven memorised: Band 0.
     - Significantly under length (e.g. <100 Task 1, <150 Task 2): consider Band 3 LR/GRA caps.
8. Note any penalty flags: plagiarism suspicion, bullet-point format,
   copied rubric (don't count those words), tone violations (GT only).
```
