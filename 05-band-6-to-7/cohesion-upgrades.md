# Cohesion Upgrades — Beyond Firstly, Secondly, Finally

> The biggest single Band 6 marker is **mechanical cohesion**: linkers stamped at the start of paragraphs and sentences in fixed positions. Band 7 cohesion is **flexible** and built into the sentences themselves through referencing, lexical chains, and varied paragraphing — not bolted on with conjunctive adverbs.

---

## 1. The official descriptor language

| Aspect | Band 6 | Band 7 |
|---|---|---|
| Cohesive devices | "uses cohesive devices effectively, but cohesion within and/or between sentences may be **faulty or mechanical**" | "uses a range of cohesive devices **appropriately, although there may be some under-/over-use**" |
| Referencing | "may not always use referencing clearly or appropriately" | (implicit) referencing handled accurately |
| Paragraphing | "uses paragraphing, but not always logically" | "presents a clear central topic within each paragraph" |

The two key words: **mechanical** (Band 6) → **appropriate** (Band 7).

---

## 2. The mechanical-linker trap

### What it looks like (Band 6)

> **Firstly,** education is important. **Moreover,** it provides knowledge. **Furthermore,** it gives jobs. **In addition,** it helps society.
>
> **Secondly,** technology is also useful. **Moreover,** it saves time. **Furthermore,** it connects people.
>
> **Finally,** in conclusion, both are important.

Every sentence is fronted with a discourse marker. Paragraphs are introduced with sequencers. Every "addition" function uses a different word, but they're all in the same slot. The examiner sees a **linker per sentence** pattern — Band 6.

### Why this caps the score

- Cohesion is built **into** sentences in good writing, not bolted on.
- Repeating the same function (addition) with rotating words is overuse.
- Sequencers like *Firstly/Secondly/Finally* signal listing, which is the lowest-rigour rhetorical mode.
- The flow becomes predictable, robotic.

### Band 7 alternative

> Education plays a fundamental role in shaping life outcomes. By equipping individuals with both technical knowledge and critical-thinking skills, it opens doors to employment that would otherwise remain closed. This expansion of opportunity, in turn, lifts entire communities out of poverty.
>
> Technology, meanwhile, complements rather than competes with education. The internet has made high-quality learning materials universally accessible, narrowing the gap between students in well-funded schools and those in remote areas.
>
> Taken together, the two forces — education and technology — are most powerful when they reinforce each other, with technology amplifying the reach of effective teaching.

Notice: **zero** of the Band-7 paragraphs starts with a sequencer. Cohesion is achieved through:
- Pronouns (*it*)
- Demonstratives (*this expansion*)
- Repetition of the topic (*Education... Technology... the two forces*)
- Lexical chains (*opportunity → poverty → reach*)
- Phrases that signal relationships without flagging them: *in turn, meanwhile, taken together*

## 3. The four pillars of Band 7 cohesion

### Pillar 1 — Reference (pronouns and demonstratives)

Pointing back to earlier nouns without repeating them.

| Tool | Example |
|---|---|
| *it / they / them* | *Pollution is rising. **It** affects everyone.* |
| *this / that / these / those* | *Pollution is rising. **This trend** must be reversed.* |
| *such* | *Pollution is rising. **Such contamination** has long-term consequences.* |
| *the former / the latter* | *Cars and factories both pollute, though **the former** are now more strictly regulated.* |
| *one / ones* | *Affordable cars are needed; electric **ones** in particular.* |

**Band 6 → Band 7 by referencing:**

| Band 6 (repeats nouns) | Band 7 (uses references) |
|---|---|
| *Computers are useful. Computers help students. Computers are everywhere.* | *Computers have become indispensable in modern classrooms; **they** support learning in ways that were unimaginable a generation ago, and **such tools** are now found in nearly every school.* |

### Pillar 2 — Substitution and ellipsis

Substituting a word for an earlier one, or omitting a word the reader can recover.

| Tool | Example |
|---|---|
| *do so / do this* | *Governments must act, and they must **do so** quickly.* |
| Verb ellipsis | *Some countries have invested heavily in renewables; others have not [invested].* |
| *one* / *ones* substitute | *The traditional approach is failing; a new **one** is needed.* |

### Pillar 3 — Lexical chains and synonymy

Sustaining a topic across a paragraph by varying the words for the same idea.

**Theme: pollution**
> *Pollution* → *contamination* → *emissions* → *environmental damage* → *the problem* → *this contamination* → *such hazardous output*

**Theme: education**
> *education* → *schooling* → *the learning process* → *academic instruction* → *this system* → *the classroom*

**Theme: technology**
> *technology* → *digital tools* → *innovation* → *these advances* → *modern devices* → *such innovations*

This is one of the highest-yield improvements: it lifts both lexical resource AND cohesion simultaneously.

### Pillar 4 — Conjunction (used, not over-used)

Conjunctions and conjunctive adverbs are still allowed — just **flexible** in placement and **appropriate** in function.

| Function | Range to draw from |
|---|---|
| Addition | *moreover, furthermore, in addition, what is more, beyond this, not only... but also* |
| Contrast | *however, nevertheless, that said, on the other hand, conversely, by contrast, while, whereas* |
| Cause | *because, since, as, given that, owing to, due to* |
| Effect | *therefore, consequently, as a result, hence, thus, accordingly, for this reason* |
| Concession | *although, even though, despite, in spite of, while it is true that* |
| Example | *for instance, for example, to illustrate, a case in point, namely* |
| Sequence (rare in Band 7) | *firstly... secondly... — used at most ONCE in the essay* |
| Reformulation | *in other words, that is to say, put differently, namely* |
| Emphasis | *in particular, especially, notably, most importantly* |
| Summary | *on balance, in sum, taken together, to summarise* |

## 4. Linker placement variety

Don't always front-load. Move linkers around for natural flow.

| Always front (Band 6) | Mid-sentence variety (Band 7) |
|---|---|
| ***However**, technology has drawbacks.* | *Technology, **however**, is not without drawbacks.* |
| ***Therefore**, governments must act.* | *Governments, **therefore**, must act.* |
| ***Moreover**, recycling is essential.* | *Recycling is, **moreover**, essential to the solution.* |
| ***For example**, in Korea, …* | *In Korea, **for example**, …* |

## 5. Paragraph-level cohesion: the topic-sentence pattern

Every body paragraph should open with a clear claim. The rest of the paragraph elaborates on, supports, and connects back to that claim.

### Band 6 paragraph (no clear topic sentence)

> *In some countries, like Japan and Korea, students study many hours. They go to private schools after school. This makes them tired. Their parents pay a lot of money. Some students get good results.*

Reader can't tell what the paragraph's central claim is.

### Band 7 paragraph (topic sentence + development + example + link)

> *The intense academic pressure faced by students in many East Asian countries comes at a substantial human cost.* (topic sentence) *Beyond the long official school day, pupils in Japan and Korea typically attend private cram schools, or **juku** and **hagwon**, late into the evening, leaving little time for sleep, social development, or non-academic pursuits.* (explanation + concrete example) *This relentless schedule has been linked, over time, to rising levels of student burnout and mental-health concerns — costs that arguably outweigh the marginal academic gains.* (consequence + link)

## 6. Inter-paragraph cohesion (linking paragraph to paragraph)

How a Band 7 writer transitions between body paragraphs without saying "Secondly":

| Technique | Example transition sentence |
|---|---|
| Echo a key noun from the previous paragraph | *While these economic pressures are significant, the **environmental** cost is arguably even more severe.* |
| Contrast | *In stark contrast to these advantages, however, lies a less obvious set of drawbacks.* |
| Build | *These pressures are not the only factor at work; a second, equally powerful, force is at play.* |
| Limit | *Although the case made above is compelling, it is not without exceptions.* |
| Pivot | *The picture changes considerably, however, when we shift focus from individuals to society as a whole.* |

## 7. Sentence-level cohesion: 5 quick techniques

| Technique | Band 6 | Band 7 |
|---|---|---|
| Combine with a relative clause | *Pollution is rising. It harms public health.* | *Pollution, **which is rising rapidly**, harms public health.* |
| Combine with a participle clause | *Pollution is rising. It harms public health.* | ***Rising rapidly**, pollution harms public health.* |
| Combine with subordination | *Pollution is rising. It harms public health.* | ***Because pollution is rising rapidly**, public health is suffering.* |
| Replace second mention with pronoun + demonstrative | *Pollution is rising. Pollution is dangerous.* | *Pollution is rising; **this trend** is alarming.* |
| Use a colon to expand | *Pollution is rising. There is one cause.* | *Pollution is rising for one main reason**:** unchecked industrial expansion.* |

## 8. Sequencers — when (rarely) to use Firstly/Secondly

Use them at most **once** in the essay, ideally not at the start of a paragraph. Better alternatives:

| Instead of *Firstly* | Try |
|---|---|
| *Firstly, …* | *One key reason is that…* / *A primary factor is…* / *To begin with, …* |
| *Secondly, …* | *A second consideration is…* / *Equally important is…* / *Another factor at play is…* |
| *Finally, …* | *Lastly, …* / *A final point worth mentioning is…* / *Beyond these factors, …* |
| *In conclusion, …* | *On balance, …* / *Weighing these arguments, …* / *To sum up, …* (still acceptable) |

---

## 9. Cohesion errors to eliminate

| Error | Example | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Same linker repeated in a paragraph | *Moreover... Moreover... Moreover...* | Use synonymic range: *moreover, what is more, beyond this, in addition* |
| Wrong linker for the function | *Pollution is rising. **Moreover**, it harms health.* (this is consequence, not addition) | *Pollution is rising; **as a result**, it harms health.* |
| Pronoun without clear antecedent | *They use them every day.* | *Adolescents use these platforms every day.* |
| Demonstrative without anchor | *This is a serious problem.* (which "this"?) | *This widespread littering is a serious problem.* |
| Missing topic sentence | Paragraph opens with example | Open with claim, then example |
| One-sentence paragraph | A claim with no support | Develop into a full paragraph (5–7 sentences) |
| Repetition of full noun phrase | *Air pollution... air pollution... air pollution...* | Build a lexical chain |

---

## 10. Self-test — does my paragraph have Band 7 cohesion?

For each body paragraph, check:

- [ ] Topic sentence states the paragraph's central idea
- [ ] At most one explicit linker fronted at the start of a sentence
- [ ] Pronouns and demonstratives are used (and clearly refer back)
- [ ] The same key noun is paraphrased rather than repeated
- [ ] At least one cause/effect, contrast, or example is signalled within sentences (not always at the start)
- [ ] Final sentence connects back to the claim or transitions to the next paragraph
- [ ] No *Firstly / Secondly / Moreover / Furthermore* fronting every sentence

If 6/7 boxes are ticked, the paragraph is at Band 7 cohesion.
